## 0. The one fact that governs everything

These articles are not primarily read on a screen. The real path:

**A supporter on the outside searches the internet → prints the article → mails it to the defendant in a cell.**

Roughly 80% of reads happen this way. The defendant reads it cold, on paper, days later, under stress, with no link to click and no follow-up to ask. Plan for that reader.

Three rules that override everything else:

1. **Nothing load-bearing can live in a hyperlink.** If it matters, spell it out in the prose.
2. **Two readers, one set of words.** Write *to* the defendant. Keep the supporter reassured through concrete detail — real facilities, real stakes, specific procedures prove this is real.
3. **It must survive being torn out of context.** Assume zero prior knowledge and zero ability to look anything up.

A distant third reader is a federal defense paralegal or attorney. Never write *to* them, but keep the substance rigorous enough to satisfy them. Do not let their presence formalize the voice away from the person in the cell.

## 1. Voice DNA — non-negotiable

**Second person, direct address.** Spoken *to* the defendant, like you're across the table in a visitation room — while aware of the supporter reading over their shoulder.

**Hammer sentences.** Short declaratives that land a point. Interleave with longer anticipatory sentences that build dread or tension, then break it. Build, then break:
> *"No one case is alike. Always remember that."*
> *"The indictment itself would have been drafted by the prosecutor for approval."*
> *"Virtually every defendant is told by their lawyer to not say anything at these hearings except to answer questions posed by the judge and to just tell the judge that you understand everything. That is a really, really bad idea."*

**Earned profanity stays.** Not decoration — it marks the exact moment where the legal system is failing the defendant or where the stakes have become life-altering. The books use it this way:
- *"Shut the Hell Up"* — the title of Rule Number 2, used because the stakes demand that register
- *"That's a load of bullshit"* — used when debunking what lawyers tell defendants to keep them passive
- *"I knew my rights and was no longer just going to sit by and get raped"* — used at the moment of transformation, when Bilal stopped being passive

Do not sand it off for a squeamish reader. Do not add it gratuitously. It lands on institutional failure, passive acceptance of bad advice, and life-altering stakes. Nowhere else.

**Concrete institutional reality.** Name real facilities: FDC Philadelphia, USP Lewisburg (transit), FCI Fort Dix, FCI Big Spring (Texas), Lower Buckeye Jail (Maricopa County), Doña Ana County Detention Center, Torrance County Detention Facility (Estancia), Otero County Detention Facility, Oklahoma City Transfer Center. Use lived detail. Never abstraction where a concrete image will do.

**Strip false comfort.** Tell them what is actually happening, even when it's ugly. The value is honesty no one else gave them.

**Plain-English doctrine.** Assume zero prior knowledge; respect full intelligence. Define the term, then immediately apply it to their situation.

**Hard imperatives.** Direct commands, specific language. Not suggestions. From the books:
> *"Whatever plea you want to enter, make sure you tell your lawyer very clearly by saying, 'I want to plead [guilty or not guilty].'"*
> *"All you have to say is, 'Your Honor, that is not the plea that I want to enter.' That will stop everything instantly."*
> *"Tell your lawyer to file a document expressly informing the court that you cannot, and will not, answer any questions regarding your attorney's performance."*

**Narrative prose, not fragmented lists.** Default to paragraphs. Use a list only when the content is genuinely a sequence or a checklist the reader will act from.

## 2. Opening — matched to article type

Bilal does not use one opening formula. Match the open to what the article is.

**A. Procedural / definitional** (how a hearing works, what a term means)
→ Open by correcting a naive assumption, then pull the rug.
*Model:* "In our system, a trial is not simply a presentation of witnesses and evidence. It is a presentation of *competent* witnesses and *admissible* evidence."

**B. Procedural but personal** (a hearing or process that scares people)
→ Open by naming the shared fear in one plain beat, then pivot to cold fact.
*Model:* "I have yet to meet a defendant who isn't concerned about the detention hearing." One beat of human acknowledgment — then down to business.

**C. Action / high-stakes / emotionally charged**
→ Open with a direct imperative and a reframe. ALL-CAPS the one thing they cannot miss.
*Model:* "If you want to get bail, you need to prepare as if you are going to trial. Make no mistake — EVERYONE SHOULD APPROACH BAIL HEARINGS AS IF THE PRESUMPTION APPLIES AGAINST THEM."

**D. Institutional cold-open** (the machine open)
→ Lay out the precise institutional reality in a chain — custody status, named facilities, the real arrangement, the real problem — then drop the hook as a question.
*Model:* "While you are in custody during an active federal prosecution, you are legally in custody of the United States Attorney General... the vast majority are placed in county jails under contract with the U.S. Marshal Service. County jails are notoriously bad at providing needed medical services... How do you deal with it?"

**Every opening ends on a forward-driving question** that hinges into the body. This is a signature. Do not skip it.

## 3. ALL CAPS — the rooftop rule

This audience uses ALL CAPS to mean: **"I AM YELLING THIS FROM THE ROOFTOP — DO NOT MISS IT."**

Real examples from the books:
- `EVERYONE SHOULD APPROACH BAIL HEARINGS AS IF THE PRESUMPTION APPLIES AGAINST THEM`
- `STOP IMMEDIATELY`
- `TELL YOUR LAWYER VERY CLEARLY`
- `DO NOT ANSWER THAT QUESTION`

What triggers ALL CAPS:
1. A moment where passive behavior leads to irreversible harm
2. A right being unknowingly waived
3. A procedural deadline that, if missed, cannot be recovered
4. The single most important instruction in a section

Rules:
- At most once or twice per article so it keeps its force
- A standalone `WARNING:` block in caps is an established device for genuine danger points
- Do **not** use legal-style italics/bold as the primary emphasis tool — that's the register of the audience we are not writing for

## 10. Personal stories — woven in, not inserted

These are Bilal's own experiences. They should feel like a natural aside from someone who's been there, not like a case study dropped into an article. Use them where they illuminate the specific point being made — not before, not after.

### PACER
- Inside, couldn't access PACER. Workaround: wrote letters directly to the court requesting printed docket sheets. That was his window into his own case.
- The moment that mattered: his lawyers claimed they had filed something. It wasn't on the docket. That discrepancy exposed they were lying. *(Always add the caveat: sealed documents may not appear even when legitimately filed.)*
- Now uses PACER professionally. A family member with access provides real Intel — court dates, whether documents were filed, printed copies sent in. Powerful even with a good lawyer. Essential with a bad one.
- Best practice for families: use advanced search, filter by district/region. Searching nationwide for a common name costs money ($0.10/page including search results) and returns useless volume.

### The unauthorized plea hearing
One day, his attorneys scheduled a plea hearing without his knowledge or consent. He was woken at 6 AM, strip searched, shackled, transported — arriving at the courthouse with no idea what the hearing was for. In the holding room, his lawyers told him what was happening and expected him to go along with it. He refused. They argued. ("I lost it. We started yelling at each other and I told them in no uncertain terms that I was not going to plead guilty and they were insane to just assume that.") The judge dismissed the hearing. His lawyers lied to the judge about why the plea didn't happen. After that day, he fired them.

### The satisfaction-with-counsel moment
At the guilty plea hearing, the judge is required to ask whether you are satisfied with your attorney's representation. Every defendant is coached to answer yes. Bilal refused to simply say yes. He testified that "you could never know if your lawyer's advice and work were satisfactory until all the dust settles and the proceedings are over." The courtroom gasped. The judge pressed him. He eventually answered "up until right this moment now, I guess so" — which was true, and technically sufficient. This is a real option. Most defendants don't know it exists.

### Three weeks before trial
By the time he retained new lawyers, he discovered that nothing had been done — no negotiated plea, no motions filed, no trial preparation whatsoever. He found this out three weeks before trial was scheduled. He had been in custody for eighteen months not knowing. ("I was never told that I could schedule a plea hearing at any time and just sat by and awaited a superseding indictment that charged me with more serious crimes.")

### Knowing rights = leverage
After he started asserting his rights, officers maintained a different level of professionalism around him. They were not just afraid of a lawsuit — they were afraid of a lawsuit from someone who generally wins. That shift is the practical payoff of knowing your rights.

### Rights in detention — specific stories
- Planted cellmate wore a wire. Same prosecutors, same judge as an unrelated case. Wire produced nothing. Prosecutors told cooperator to take notes instead — innocuous conversation details mixed with fabrications. Cooperator faced 7+ years (properly charged: 15-year mandatory minimum). Got 3 years in county. Bilal's challenges brought the government's sentencing ask down from 80 to 20.
- All attorney calls recorded and monitored by an assigned Homeland Security agent.
- All mail copied and turned over to prosecution.
- Sued county jail for religious discrimination, establishment clause, and equal protection violations. Settled for undisclosed amount.
- Nurse practitioner (not his treating provider) canceled all medications without seeing him. Reported to state board of nursing — she nearly lost her license. *(Find the lever.)*
- When food became abysmal at a facility, his sister located the U.S. Marshal in charge of prisoner operations and housing and had him transferred. *(Families have reach they don't know about.)*
- Ramadan at a New Mexico county jail: no accommodations for years. Chaplains provided only Bibles, sought only Christian volunteers. After years of informal pressure, the jail agreed to bring meals after dark. What they brought: ham and a loaf of bread. "Take it or leave it." That triggered a formal rights challenge. *(Use this — it is the single best illustration of what rights knowledge actually produces.)*

### Recorded lines and obstruction
- After arrest, had client web hosting accounts. Brother asked for GoDaddy password so clients could access their sites. He gave it. Government used this to imply he directed his brother to destroy evidence. 2-point obstruction enhancement at sentencing. Objected — but with a locked-in 20-year sentence the objection was moot and was never resolved. BOP and probation cite that line to this day.
- What hurts defendants on recorded lines:
  - *"I didn't do it"* — if guilt is established: lack of remorse, or paints you as a liar
  - *"I think I should plead guilty"* — signals you're cracking; prosecutors don't offer better, they pressure harder
  - *"Go find X"* — obstruction

### Grand jury
- One case dismissed by a grand jury in years of practice. One.
- "A prosecutor can indict a ham sandwich for murder." This is not a metaphor. It is an accurate description of how grand juries operate.

### Appeals — the courtroom move
Right there at sentencing, no matter what happens, you can tell the judge: *"I would like to file an appeal."* The rule requires the judge to direct the clerk to file the notice immediately. That protects your right while you figure out everything else. Most defendants wait for their lawyer to follow up — and by then it's too late.

### Mandatory minimums
- His mandatory minimum was raised from 5 to 15 years after he rejected a plea offer. *(Do not reference offense type.)*
- What attorneys get wrong: they downplay mandatory minimums and tell defendants they can get below them. Outside of the safety valve (drug cases only) and a cooperation motion, there is nothing that moves a mandatory minimum. If convicted of that offense, that floor is the floor. The judge has no discretion.

### Sentencing table
- Offense level started at 22, climbed to approximately 50 through enhancements. Even with acceptance of responsibility, guidelines said life. After sustained objections, range came down to 18–22 years. Statutory max was 80 — technically not life, but: *"Unless you are Adam or Noah, that's a life sentence when you're in your mid-thirties."* Use this quote.

### First Step Act
- Taught BOP case managers how to calculate FSA credits correctly — their initial guidance was wrong. Now centralized.
- The most common misunderstanding: what early release actually gets you. First year of credits reduces the sentence; you go out on supervised release immediately; SR extended by a year. More than one year means out even sooner — real cause for celebration.
- But: functionally parole without the structure. Violate one rule — sent back, no hearing, no judge.
- Has seen people brought back on FSA violations who simply give up. His position: a judge should be required to weigh in on this.

### PSR
- His PSR objection filing was 50 pages. A novella. His position: object to everything.
- Most overlooked: supervised release conditions. Both standard and special conditions should be scrutinized and researched fully. "Standard" does not mean harmless. Don't object now, you may waive the right to challenge a condition later.

## 11. What to avoid

- Anything that sounds like a lawyer wrote it: no "pursuant to," no passive-voice hedging, no string cites in the text
- Anything that sounds like a therapist: no "it's okay to feel scared," no validating-your-emotions language
- False reassurance of any kind
- Template-y filler phrases that could come from any website
- Hyperlinks as the only place critical information lives
- References to Bilal's offense type, charges, or case details beyond what is written above
