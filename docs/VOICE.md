## 0. The one fact that governs everything

These articles are not primarily read on a screen. The real path:

**A supporter on the outside searches the internet → prints the article → mails it to the defendant in a cell.**

Roughly 80% of reads happen this way. The defendant reads it cold, on paper, days later, under stress, with no link to click and no follow-up to ask. Plan for that reader.

Three rules that override everything else:

1. **Nothing load-bearing can live in a hyperlink.** If it matters, spell it out in the prose.
2. **Two readers, one set of words.** Write *to* the defendant. Keep the supporter reassured through concrete detail — real facilities, real stakes, specific procedures prove this is real.
3. **It must survive being torn out of context.** Assume zero prior knowledge and zero ability to look anything up.

A distant third reader is a federal defense paralegal or attorney. Never write *to* them, but keep the substance rigorous enough to satisfy them. Do not let their presence formalize the voice away from the person in the cell.

## 1. Voice DNA — non-negotiable

**Second person, direct address.** Spoken *to* the defendant, like you're across the table in a visitation room — while aware of the supporter reading over their shoulder.

**Hammer sentences.** Short declaratives that land a point. Interleave with longer anticipatory sentences that build tension, then break it. Build, then break.

**Earned profanity stays.** Not decoration — it lands a point and it matches how people in the system actually talk. Use it where it hits, not on a quota. Don't sand it off for a squeamish reader; the audience is already screaming.

**Concrete institutional reality.** Name real facilities: FDC Philadelphia, MDC Brooklyn, FCI Fort Dix, FCI Big Spring, Lower Buckeye Jail, FDC Estancia (Torrance County), Doña Ana County Detention Center, Otero County Detention Facility, Oklahoma City Transfer Center. Use lived detail — the belly chain, the dog kennel, the stench. Never abstraction where a concrete image will do.

**Strip false comfort.** Tell them what is actually happening, even when it's ugly. The value is honesty no one else gave them.

**Plain-English doctrine.** Assume zero prior knowledge; respect full intelligence. Define the term, then immediately apply it to their situation.

**Hard imperatives.** "Just be clear." "Slow down." "Don't get creative." "Shut the Hell Up."

**Narrative prose, not fragmented lists.** Default to paragraphs. Use a list only when the content is genuinely a sequence or a checklist the reader will act from.

## 2. Opening — matched to article type

Bilal does not use one opening formula. Match the open to what the article is.

**A. Procedural / definitional** (how a hearing works, what a term means)
→ Open by correcting a naive assumption, then pull the rug.
*Model:* "In our system, a trial is not simply a presentation of witnesses and evidence. It is a presentation of *competent* witnesses and *admissible* evidence."

**B. Procedural but personal** (a hearing or process that scares people)
→ Open by naming the shared fear in one plain beat, then pivot to cold fact.
*Model:* "I have yet to meet a defendant who isn't concerned about the detention hearing." One beat of human acknowledgment — then down to business.

**C. Action / high-stakes / emotionally charged**
→ Open with a direct imperative and a reframe. ALL-CAPS the one thing they cannot miss.
*Model:* "If you want to get bail, you need to prepare as if you are going to trial. Make no mistake — EVERYONE SHOULD APPROACH BAIL HEARINGS AS IF THE PRESUMPTION APPLIES AGAINST THEM."

**D. Institutional cold-open** (the machine open)
→ Lay out the precise institutional reality in a chain — custody status, named facilities, the real arrangement, the real problem — then drop the hook as a question.
*Model:* "While you are in custody during an active federal prosecution, you are legally in custody of the United States Attorney General... the vast majority are placed in county jails under contract with the U.S. Marshal Service. County jails are notoriously bad at providing needed medical services... How do you deal with it?"

**Every opening ends on a forward-driving question** that hinges into the body. This is a signature. Do not skip it.

## 3. ALL CAPS — the rooftop rule

This audience uses ALL CAPS to mean: **"I AM YELLING THIS FROM THE ROOFTOP — DO NOT MISS IT."**

- Reserved for the **single most important do-or-die point** in a section.
- Use it **at most once or twice per article** so it keeps its force.
- A standalone **WARNING:** block in caps is an established device for genuine danger points.
- Do **not** use legal-style italics/bold as the primary emphasis tool.

## 9. Personal stories and interview material

These are Bilal's own experiences. Weave them in at the right moment — don't force them, don't over-explain them. They should feel like a natural aside from someone who's been there, not like inserted case studies.

### PACER
- Inside, Bilal couldn't access PACER. His workaround: write letters directly to the court requesting printed docket sheets. That was his window into his own case.
- The moment that mattered: his lawyers claimed they had filed something — it wasn't on the docket. That discrepancy told him they were lying. *(Always add the caveat: sealed documents may not appear even when legitimately filed.)*
- Now uses PACER professionally to track ongoing cases and court dates. A family member with access can provide real Intel — court dates, filed documents, printed copies sent in. Powerful even with a good lawyer. Essential with a bad one.
- Best practice for families: use the advanced search and filter by district/region. Searching nationwide for a common name costs money ($0.10/page including search results) and returns useless volume.

### Rights in pretrial detention / recorded communications
- Cellmate was a federal cooperator planted in his cell to wear a wire. Same prosecutors, same judge as an unrelated case. Wire produced nothing useful because the target information was fabricated. Prosecutors told the cooperator to take notes instead. Innocuous conversation details were mixed with fabrications. Cooperator faced 7+ years (properly charged: 15-year mandatory minimum). Got 3 years in county for the cooperation. Bilal fought back — challenged everything, attempted judge recusal (failed), but the cumulative pressure dropped the government's ask from 80 years to 20.
- All attorney calls were recorded and monitored by an assigned Homeland Security agent.
- All mail was copied and turned over to the prosecution.
- Sued a county jail for religious discrimination (Muslim), establishment clause, and equal protection violations. Settled for an undisclosed amount.
- When a nurse practitioner — not even his treating provider — canceled all his medications without seeing him, he reported her to the state board of nursing. She nearly lost her license. *(This is the model: find the lever.)*
- When food at a facility became abysmal, his sister located the U.S. Marshal in charge of prisoner operations and housing and had him transferred to a better facility. *(Families have more reach than they think.)*
- Ramadan at a New Mexico county jail: no accommodations for years. Chaplains provided only Bibles, sought only Christian volunteers. After years of informal pressure, the jail agreed to bring meals after dark during Ramadan. What they brought: ham and a loaf of bread. "Take it or leave it." That triggered a formal rights challenge. *(Use this — it's the best single illustration of what knowing your rights actually produces.)*
- Knowing his rights early would have meant 9–11 years instead of 20. Not knowing them, and not keeping his mouth shut at the police station, cost him nearly a decade of his life.

### Recorded calls and obstruction
- After his arrest, he had web hosting accounts with digital assets for clients. His brother asked for the GoDaddy password so clients could access their sites. He gave it. The government used that to imply he directed his brother to destroy evidence. He received a 2-point obstruction of justice enhancement. He objected at sentencing, but with a locked-in 20-year sentence the objection was moot and was never resolved. BOP and probation still cite that line today.
- What people say on recorded lines that destroys them:
  - *"I didn't do it"* — if guilt is established, shows lack of remorse; paints you as a liar ("if he can lie so easily to his own mother, why should we believe him today?")
  - *"I think I should plead guilty"* — signals to prosecutors that you're cracking; they pressure harder, not offer better
  - *"Go find X"* — painted as obstruction

### Grand jury
- One case dismissed by a grand jury in years of practice. Just one.
- "A prosecutor can indict a ham sandwich for murder." Not a metaphor. A reality.
- In the state system, Bilal has seen defendants convince themselves they can walk into a grand jury and talk their way out. They can't. And whatever they say can and will be used against them later.

### Federal appeals
- At sentencing, no matter what happens, you can tell the judge right there in the courtroom: *"I would like to file an appeal."* The rule requires the judge to direct the clerk to file the notice immediately. That protects you while you figure out the rest. Most people don't know this move exists.
- Most defendants wait for their lawyer to follow up — and by then it's too late. If your lawyer doesn't talk to you about appeal within a day of sentencing, notify the court yourself. The lawyer's silence may be ineffective assistance, but why fight that battle when one sentence at the sentencing hearing prevents it entirely?

### Mandatory minimums
- *(Mandatory minimum raised from 5 to 15 years after rejecting a plea offer. Case type is kept private — do not reference the offense or hint at it.)*
- What attorneys get wrong: they downplay mandatory minimums and convince defendants they can get below them. With the exception of the safety valve (drug cases only) and a cooperation motion, there is nothing that moves a mandatory minimum. If you are convicted of that offense, that floor is the floor. The judge has no discretion.

### Sentencing table
- Offense level started at 22, ended at approximately 50 through enhancements. Even with 3 points for acceptance of responsibility, the guidelines initially said life. After sustained objections, the range came down to 18–22 years. Statutory max was 80 years — technically not life, but: *"Unless you are Adam or Noah, that's a life sentence when you're in your mid-thirties."* *(Use this quote.)*

### First Step Act
- Spent significant time teaching BOP case managers how to calculate FSA credits correctly — their initial guidance was wrong. Calculations are now centralized.
- The most common misunderstanding: what early release actually gets you. The first year of credits reduces the sentence and places you on supervised release; SR is extended by a year. More than one year of credits means you're out even sooner — real cause for celebration.
- But: this is functionally parole without the structure of parole. Violate one rule and you can be sent directly back — no hearing, no judge. The system is too new to know what it looks like in practice.
- Has seen people brought back in on FSA violations who simply give up. His position: a judge should be required to weigh in on this.

### PSR
- His PSR objection filing was 50 pages. A novella. His position: object to everything.
- Most overlooked piece: supervised release conditions. Both standard and special conditions should be scrutinized and researched fully. "Standard" does not mean harmless. If you don't object at sentencing, you may waive the right to challenge a condition later — and those conditions govern years of your life after release.

## 10. What to avoid

- Anything that sounds like a lawyer wrote it: no "pursuant to," no passive-voice hedging, no string cites in the text
- Anything that sounds like a therapist: no "it's okay to feel scared," no validating-your-emotions language
- False reassurance of any kind: "everything will be okay," "your lawyer has this," "don't worry"
- Template-y filler phrases that could come from any article on any website
- Hyperlinks as the only place critical information lives
