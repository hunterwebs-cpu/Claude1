## 2. Platform Rules (Research-Backed — Do Not Guess)

These come from analysis of 94,000+ Substack posts and the Marshall Project's "Life Inside" editorial standard — the closest professional analog to this column.

### Length
- **Target: 900–1,400 words.** The top-50 Substack publications average 871 words.
- The Marshall Project's first-person criminal justice dispatch standard is 1,000–1,400 words. That is the benchmark.
- 1,600–1,800 words is defensible for a genuinely high-stakes piece. Not a regular habit.

### Title
- **First-person, 13–17 words, no question marks.**
- Titles with "I" or "My" outperform all others by ~30%.
- Titles of 13–17 words outperform short titles by ~27%.
- Question marks suppress subscriber conversion (they drive comments, not applause).
- Model: *"I Reported the Condemned Building to an Associate Warden. She Agreed. The Bleachers Are Still There."*

### Subtitle (6–10 words)
- This is not decoration. **The subtitle becomes the email preview text** — the second line every subscriber sees in their inbox before deciding to open.
- It should add urgency or context the title couldn't carry.
- Model: *"Nine years inside Fort Dix. Here's what federal neglect actually looks like."*

### Opening
- **Single vivid scene. In medias res. No preamble.**
- One sentence or a short 2-sentence paragraph. Nothing before the scene.
- Significant reader drop-off happens at the 25% scroll mark when the opening fails. The opening must carry the reader past that.
- Never: "In this piece I want to talk about..." Never: background before the scene.

### Paragraphs
- **Short. 2–3 sentences maximum. One sentence is fine.**
- On mobile (where most email is read), long paragraphs look like walls and trigger abandonment.
- One idea per paragraph. Front-load the point.

### Subheadings
- For pure narrative first-person writing (the dispatch format): **no subheadings — use horizontal dividers (`---`) to mark section transitions.**
- Subheadings are acceptable for the actionable section only (e.g., **What you can actually do about this**).
- Never more than 2–4 total if used at all.

### Ending
1. A literary close that echoes something from the opening
2. A direct question to the comment section (drives engagement in first hours after publish)
3. The standard sign-off: `Be Well, Stay Safe, and Survive the Feds, ~Bilal`
4. The standard bio block (see §6)

### Platform mechanics to respect
- Write for **email first**. The majority of subscribers read in their inbox and never click to the web.
- **No load-bearing hyperlinks.** If information matters, it lives in the prose. Readers on mobile, and any reader who prints the piece, cannot click.
- Substack's open rate is ~44–45% (roughly 2x the email industry average). The audience is intentional. Treat them accordingly.
- **Restacks are the most algorithmically powerful engagement signal.** Write something worth sharing.

## 3. Voice

The full voice specification lives in `docs/VOICE.md`. Read it before writing. The adaptations for the Substack dispatch format are:

**First-person throughout.** Unlike the survivingthefeds.com articles (which write TO the reader), dispatches are written FROM inside — Bilal as narrator, observer, and guide. The reader sees the world through his eyes first. The pivot to "you" happens in the actionable section.

**Earned profanity.** Same rule as in VOICE.md — it marks the exact moment of institutional failure or life-altering stakes. Not decoration. Not omitted for squeamishness.

**ALL CAPS: once per article, maximum.** On the one procedural point that, if missed, causes irreversible harm. Almost always a filing deadline or a rights waiver. Format: `WARNING: [STATEMENT IN FULL CAPS]`

**Specificity is the credibility.** Name the facilities. Name the forms (BP-8, BP-9, BP-10, BP-11). Name the laws and program statements. "Prison is dehumanizing" lands nowhere. "The count hits at 4 a.m. and the CO never makes eye contact" lands everywhere.

**No false comfort.** If something is hard, say it is hard. If the system's answer to a problem is worse than the problem, say that.

**Strip abstraction.** Every time the draft reaches for a general claim, replace it with the specific instance.

## 5. Interview Protocol — Conduct Before Every Article

Do not skip this. The article's credibility lives in the specific details Bilal provides. Generic prison writing is everywhere. This column's value is that it comes from someone who is there.

**Conduct the interview in rounds of 5–7 focused questions.** Do not send a list of 20 questions at once.

**Round 1 — Story sourcing:**
- What specifically happened? Where, when, who was involved?
- How do you know what you know? (direct observation, inmate accounts, staff statements, documents?)
- Is there anything you cannot or do not want to include on a recorded line?

**Round 2 — Credibility anchors:**
- Is there a personal experience from your history (facility, case, litigation) that speaks directly to this topic?
- Has any official — BOP staff, administrator, court — made a statement (written or verbal) relevant to this?
- Have you seen this pattern at other facilities?

**Round 3 — Actionable layer:**
- What can an inmate or family member actually do about this?
- What is the specific procedure, form number, program statement, or law?
- What happens if they miss the deadline or skip the process?

**Round 4 — Corrections gate:**
- Before writing, confirm any numbers, dimensions, dates, or official statements for accuracy
- Flag anything that is inmate rumor vs. documented fact — the article must distinguish between them

## 6. Article Structure

Every dispatch follows this structure. Do not add elements. Do not remove elements.

```
[TITLE — first-person, 13–17 words, no question mark]
[SUBTITLE — 6–10 words, adds urgency]

By Bilal Khan
*Surviving the Feds: Dispatches from the Inside*

---

[OPENING — single vivid scene, 1–2 short paragraphs, ends on tension]

---

[BODY SECTIONS — narrative prose, dividers between major sections]
[2–5 sections depending on scope]
[Each section: short paragraphs, specific detail, no abstraction]

---

[ACTIONABLE SECTION — headed **What you can actually do about this**]
[Specific procedures, form numbers, deadlines]
[WARNING: [ALL CAPS DEADLINE OR CRITICAL POINT]]
[Program Statement or regulation reference where applicable]

---

[LITERARY CLOSE — 2–4 short paragraphs echoing the opening]

---

[COMMENT PROMPT — italic, one question to the audience]

Be Well, Stay Safe, and Survive the Feds,
~Bilal

---

[BIO BLOCK — see standard below]
```

## 8. Delivery Workflow

After the article passes the quality checklist:

**Step 1: Save the source file**
```
content/substack/[article-slug].md
```

**Step 2: Generate the .docx**
```bash
python3 scripts/substack_to_docx.py content/substack/[article-slug].md
```
This produces `content/substack/[article-slug].docx` — the file delivered to Doug.

**Step 3: Verify the .docx structure**
```bash
python3 -c "
from docx import Document
doc = Document('content/substack/[article-slug].docx')
for i, p in enumerate(doc.paragraphs[:15]):
    if p.text.strip(): print(f'{i:03d} | {repr(p.text[:70])}')
"
```
Confirm: cover checklist at top (PUBLISHER CHECKLIST header visible), title and subtitle both present, article begins cleanly after the separator.

**Step 4: Commit and push**
```bash
git add content/substack/[article-slug].md content/substack/[article-slug].docx
git commit -m "Add Substack dispatch: [short description]"
git push
```

**Step 5: Deliver the .docx to the user** via SendUserFile.
