# Practice Reference — Criminal Law (Defense-Oriented)

Loaded by the legal-research skill for criminal defense matters — suppression motions,
discovery disputes, sentencing advocacy, and post-conviction claims. Federal constitutional
floor is set out below; state constitutions and statutes can be more protective — always
check the state analog before relying solely on the federal standard.

---

## Fourth Amendment — Search & Seizure

- **Reasonable suspicion (stop/frisk)**: *Terry v. Ohio*, 392 U.S. 1 (1968)
- **Probable cause**: totality-of-circumstances, *Illinois v. Gates*, 462 U.S. 213 (1983)
- **Exclusionary rule / good-faith exception**: *United States v. Leon*, 468 U.S. 897 (1984)
  — evidence obtained via objectively reasonable reliance on a warrant later found invalid
  is not excluded
- **Fruit of the poisonous tree**: *Wong Sun v. United States*, 371 U.S. 471 (1963);
  attenuation doctrine narrowing it — *Utah v. Strieff*, 579 U.S. 232 (2016)
- **Search incident to arrest — vehicles**: *Arizona v. Gant*, 556 U.S. 332 (2009) — limited
  to the arrestee's reaching distance or where it's reasonable to believe the vehicle
  contains evidence of the offense of arrest
- **Cell phones**: *Riley v. California*, 573 U.S. 373 (2014) — warrant generally required
  to search a cell phone incident to arrest; *Carpenter v. United States*, 585 U.S. 296
  (2018) — warrant required for historical cell-site location information

## Fifth Amendment

- **Custodial interrogation warnings**: *Miranda v. Arizona*, 384 U.S. 436 (1966)
- **Voluntariness**: totality of circumstances; requires police coercion, not just internal
  pressure — *Colorado v. Connelly*, 479 U.S. 157 (1986)
- **Invocation must be unambiguous**: right to counsel — *Davis v. United States*, 512 U.S.
  452 (1994); right to remain silent — *Berghuis v. Thompkins*, 560 U.S. 370 (2010)

## Sixth Amendment

- **Ineffective assistance of counsel**: *Strickland v. Washington*, 466 U.S. 668 (1984) —
  two prongs: (1) deficient performance (below an objective standard of reasonableness),
  (2) prejudice (reasonable probability the result would have been different)
- **Speedy trial**: *Barker v. Wingo*, 407 U.S. 514 (1972) four-factor balancing (length of
  delay, reason for delay, assertion of the right, prejudice); federal statutory clock —
  Speedy Trial Act, 18 U.S.C. § 3161 (generally 70 days from indictment/appearance to trial)
- **Confrontation Clause**: *Crawford v. Washington*, 541 U.S. 36 (2004) — bars testimonial
  hearsay absent unavailability + prior cross-examination opportunity
- **Counsel of choice**: *United States v. Gonzalez-Lopez*, 548 U.S. 140 (2006) — erroneous
  deprivation is structural error, no prejudice showing required

## Discovery Obligations

- **Exculpatory evidence**: *Brady v. Maryland*, 373 U.S. 83 (1963)
- **Impeachment evidence** (witness deals, credibility): *Giglio v. United States*, 405 U.S.
  150 (1972)
- **Witness statements**: Jencks Act, 18 U.S.C. § 3500 — producible after direct testimony
- **General scope**: Fed. R. Crim. P. 16

## Sentencing

- **Guidelines are advisory, not mandatory**: *United States v. Booker*, 543 U.S. 220 (2005)
- **§ 3553(a) factors**: nature/circumstances of the offense; history and characteristics of
  the defendant; need for the sentence to reflect seriousness, promote respect for law,
  provide just punishment, afford deterrence, protect the public, and provide correctional
  treatment; the guideline range itself; avoidance of unwarranted sentencing disparities;
  restitution
- **Departures** (within the Guidelines Manual's own authorized grounds) vs. **variances**
  (§ 3553(a)-based, post-*Booker*) — argue both where applicable, they're independent bases
- **Safety valve**: 18 U.S.C. § 3553(f) — relief from certain mandatory minimums for
  qualifying low-criminal-history, non-violent offenders

## Motion Practice Quick Reference

- **Suppression**: Fed. R. Crim. P. 12(b)(3) — must be raised pretrial or is waived absent
  good cause
- **Dismissal of indictment**: defects in institution of the prosecution, defects in the
  indictment itself (duplicity/multiplicity), statute of limitations
- **Motion in limine**: pretrial evidentiary gatekeeping

## Burden Allocation

- **Suppression**: defendant bears the burden to establish standing/a reasonable
  expectation of privacy; once a warrantless search is shown, the government bears the
  burden to justify it under a recognized exception, by a preponderance of the evidence
- **Voluntariness of a confession**: government's burden, preponderance (*Lego v. Twomey*,
  404 U.S. 477 (1972))
- **Competency to stand trial**: preponderance standard, raisable by either party or the
  court sua sponte (*Dusky v. United States*, 362 U.S. 402 (1960))

## Suggested Track Breakdown for a Criminal Motion Practice Matter

| Track | Scope |
|---|---|
| 1 | Judicial profile — judge's sentencing/suppression track record, prior rulings |
| 2 | Primary suppression/dismissal issue (4th/5th/6th Amendment as applicable) |
| 3 | Discovery compliance (Brady/Giglio/Jencks) — has the government met its obligations? |
| 4 | Sentencing exposure — guideline calculation, § 3553(a) mitigation record |
| 5 | Factual record reconstruction — police reports, body-cam, 911 calls, timeline |
| 6 | Prior-conviction / criminal-history verification (affects both guidelines and impeachment exposure) |
