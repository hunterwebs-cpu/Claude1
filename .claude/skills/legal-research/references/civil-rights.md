# Practice Reference — Civil Rights (§ 1983 / Bivens / Prison Civil Rights)

Loaded by the legal-research skill for civil rights matters against state actors (§ 1983),
federal actors (Bivens), or conditions-of-confinement claims. Because the client roster
includes correctional/reentry work, this module pays particular attention to prison
civil-rights doctrine (PLRA exhaustion, deliberate indifference, contractor-vendor issues).

---

## Section 1983 Elements (42 U.S.C. § 1983)

1. Conduct committed by a **person**
2. Acting **under color of state law** (state-action requirement — includes private actors
   performing a traditionally exclusive state function, or acting in concert with state
   officials)
3. That deprived the plaintiff of a **right secured by the Constitution or federal law**

## Bivens (Federal-Actor Analog)

- *Bivens v. Six Unknown Named Agents*, 403 U.S. 388 (1971) — originally recognized an
  implied damages remedy for a Fourth Amendment violation by federal agents
- Severely narrowed since: *Ziglar v. Abbasi*, 582 U.S. 120 (2017) (two-step test — is this
  a "new context," and if so, are there "special factors" counseling against extension);
  *Egbert v. Boule*, 596 U.S. 482 (2022) (further narrowing — if there is *any* reason to
  think Congress, not the courts, is better positioned to weigh the costs and benefits, no
  extension)
- Only three confirmed contexts remain reliably viable: Fourth Amendment unreasonable
  search (*Bivens* itself), Fifth Amendment gender discrimination (*Davis v. Passman*, 442
  U.S. 228 (1979)), Eighth Amendment deliberate indifference to serious medical need by
  federal prison officials (*Carlson v. Green*, 446 U.S. 14 (1980)) — but even *Carlson*-type
  claims are being narrowed post-*Egbert* in some circuits. [VERIFY the controlling
  circuit's current treatment before pleading a Bivens claim against BOP officials or BOP
  contractor employees — contractor employees in particular may not be "federal officials"
  for Bivens purposes at all, and a state-law tort or § 1983-against-the-contractor theory
  may be the better vehicle depending on the contractor's role.]

## Qualified Immunity

- Two-step analysis, order discretionary per *Pearson v. Callahan*, 555 U.S. 223 (2009):
  (1) was a constitutional right violated; (2) was the right "clearly established" at the
  time such that every reasonable official would have understood the conduct was unlawful
  (*Saucier v. Katz*, 533 U.S. 194 (2001), origin of the test)
- "Clearly established" requires existing precedent placing the constitutional question
  "beyond debate" — *Ashcroft v. al-Kidd*, 563 U.S. 731 (2011); cannot be defined at a high
  level of generality — *White v. Pauly*, 580 U.S. 73 (2017) (per curiam)
- Qualified immunity does not apply to private contractors performing traditional
  government functions in some circuits — [VERIFY circuit split] relevant if a contractor
  employee (not a BOP officer) is the named defendant

## Monell Municipal Liability (§ 1983 only, not Bivens)

- *Monell v. Dep't of Soc. Servs.*, 436 U.S. 658 (1978) — no respondeat superior; must show
  a municipal **policy or custom** caused the violation
- Theories: (1) formal written policy, (2) widespread custom/practice, (3) a single
  decision by an official with final policymaking authority, (4) failure to train amounting
  to deliberate indifference (*City of Canton v. Harris*, 489 U.S. 378 (1989))

## Excessive Force

- *Graham v. Connor*, 490 U.S. 386 (1989) — objective reasonableness under the totality of
  the circumstances, judged from the perspective of a reasonable officer on the scene
  (not 20/20 hindsight); factors: severity of the crime, immediate threat posed, active
  resistance or flight
- Post-conviction claims arise under the **Eighth Amendment**, not the Fourth — "malicious
  and sadistic" standard, *Whitley v. Albers*, 475 U.S. 312 (1986)
- Pretrial detainees: **Fourteenth Amendment** objective-reasonableness standard —
  *Kingsley v. Hendrickson*, 576 U.S. 389 (2015)

## Eighth Amendment — Conditions of Confinement

- *Farmer v. Brennan*, 511 U.S. 825 (1994) — deliberate indifference standard: (1) an
  objectively serious risk of harm, (2) the official subjectively knew of and disregarded
  the risk
- *Estelle v. Gamble*, 429 U.S. 97 (1976) — deliberate indifference to serious medical needs
- **Contracted-service disputes (commissary, tablets, communications vendors)**: generally
  do NOT sound in the Eighth Amendment unless the conduct amounts to a denial of "life's
  necessities" or reflects punitive intent — that's a high bar. Overcharging, deceptive
  pricing, or unmet service promises by a commissary/tablet vendor more often sound in:
  - **State consumer-protection law** (deceptive/unfair trade practices statutes) if the
    vendor's conduct reaches consumers (inmates' families funding accounts) outside the
    government-contract relationship
  - **False Claims Act**, 31 U.S.C. § 3729 et seq., if the vendor made false certifications
    to the government to obtain or retain the contract or payment
  - **Breach of contract / third-party beneficiary theory** if the contract's terms
    (deliverables, pricing caps) were meant to benefit the inmate population and were
    breached
  - **State bar / attorney general consumer-protection referral** as a practical parallel
    track, independent of litigation
  This is the framework to reach for when evaluating a Keefe/ATG-style commissary-fraud
  fact pattern — the constitutional claim is usually the weakest vehicle, not the strongest.

## PLRA Exhaustion (42 U.S.C. § 1997e(a))

- The Prison Litigation Reform Act requires exhaustion of **all available** administrative
  remedies before filing suit over prison conditions
- Exhaustion is an **affirmative defense**, not a pleading requirement — *Jones v. Bock*,
  549 U.S. 199 (2007); the plaintiff need not plead exhaustion in the complaint
- **"Availability" exception**: a remedy is not "available" if (1) it operates as a simple
  dead end, (2) it is so opaque that no ordinary prisoner can discern or navigate it, or
  (3) officials thwart use through intimidation or misrepresentation — *Ross v. Blake*,
  578 U.S. 632 (2016)
- **BOP administrative remedy program**: 28 C.F.R. §§ 542.10–542.19 — informal resolution,
  then BP-9 (institution), BP-10 (regional), BP-11 (Central Office/General Counsel) — must
  exhaust through BP-11 before filing a federal civil suit over conditions

## Access to Courts

- Requires a showing of **actual injury** — a nonfrivolous claim was frustrated or
  impeded — not an abstract right to a law library or legal materials: *Bounds v. Smith*,
  430 U.S. 817 (1977), narrowed by *Lewis v. Casey*, 518 U.S. 343 (1996)

## Burden Allocation

- Plaintiff bears the burden on each § 1983/Bivens element, preponderance of the evidence
- Qualified immunity: once raised, burden shifts to the plaintiff to show the right was
  clearly established
- PLRA exhaustion: defendant's burden to prove failure to exhaust (affirmative defense)
