# Practice Reference — Family Law

Loaded by the legal-research skill for custody, support, and divorce matters. Family law is
overwhelmingly **state-statutory** — treat every substantive standard below as a structural
map, not a citable rule, until checked against the specific state's current family code and
local court rules. Uniform acts (UCCJEA, UIFSA) give a common jurisdictional skeleton across
states, but even those have state-by-state codification quirks.

---

## Jurisdictional Threshold

- **UCCJEA** (Uniform Child Custody Jurisdiction and Enforcement Act, adopted in nearly all
  states) — "home state" jurisdiction generally means the state where the child has lived
  with a parent for the 6 consecutive months immediately before filing (or since birth, if
  under 6 months old). Establishes exclusive continuing jurisdiction in the decree state
  for modification purposes.
- **UIFSA** (Uniform Interstate Family Support Act) — governs interstate child-support
  jurisdiction, establishment, and enforcement; the issuing state generally retains
  continuing, exclusive jurisdiction over the support order.
- [VERIFY the requester's state's specific UCCJEA/UIFSA codification section numbers and
  any non-uniform local amendments before relying on either framework.]

## Custody — Best Interests of the Child

Most states use a multi-factor "best interests" test; the exact statutory factor list
varies by state (VERIFY the local statute), but commonly includes:

- Each parent's fitness, stability, and history of caregiving
- The child's relationship with each parent, siblings, and community
- The child's own preference (weight increases with age/maturity; some states set an age
  threshold for formal consideration)
- History of domestic violence or substance abuse
- Each parent's willingness to foster the child's relationship with the other parent
- Geographic proximity / practicality of a shared-time arrangement
- Any history of abuse or neglect

## Custody Modification

- Majority standard: a **material/substantial change in circumstances** since the prior
  order, *and* modification serves the child's best interests. Exact phrasing, evidentiary
  burden, and any presumption against modification varies by state.
- Relocation requests often carry a **heightened standard** or a distinct relocation
  statute separate from the general modification standard — check for one specifically.

## Child Support

- Two dominant models: **Income Shares** (support approximates what the combined parental
  income would have produced for the child in an intact household) and **Percentage of
  Income** (support is a flat percentage of the noncustodial parent's income, adjusted for
  number of children).
- The state's guideline amount is presumptively correct; deviation requires written
  findings explaining why the guideline amount is unjust or inappropriate.
- Modification standard: substantial change in circumstances — many states set a bright-line
  percentage-change threshold (e.g., a specified percentage swing in the guideline
  calculation) that triggers a presumption of modifiability; [VERIFY the state's threshold].

## Divorce / Property Division

- **Equitable distribution states** (majority): marital property is divided *fairly*, not
  necessarily equally, weighing factors like length of the marriage, each spouse's economic
  and non-economic (e.g., homemaking) contributions, each spouse's post-divorce economic
  circumstances, and any dissipation of marital assets.
- **Community property states** (AZ, CA, ID, LA, NV, NM, TX, WA, WI, + Puerto Rico): marital
  property is presumptively divided 50/50; separate property (pre-marital assets, gifts,
  inheritance received individually) is excluded from division.
- [VERIFY which regime governs before drafting any property-division argument.]

## Domestic Violence / Protective Orders

- **Full Faith and Credit for Protection Orders**, 18 U.S.C. § 2265 — a valid DV protective
  order is enforceable across state lines without separate registration in most
  circumstances.
- **Federal firearms prohibitor**, 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(8)–(9) — certain qualifying DV
  protective orders and misdemeanor DV convictions bar firearm possession under federal
  law; *United States v. Rahimi*, 602 U.S. 680 (2024) upheld § 922(g)(8) against a Second
  Amendment challenge.
- A protective order almost always interacts with a pending custody case — check the
  state's statute for any automatic effect on custody/parenting-time determinations.

## Burden Allocation

- Initial custody determination: no presumption favoring either parent in most modern
  statutes (a few states retain vestigial tender-years or maternal-preference language —
  VERIFY it has not been struck as unconstitutional or repealed).
- Modification: movant bears the burden to show a substantial change in circumstances.
- Support modification: movant's burden to show the change meets the state's threshold.

## Suggested Track Breakdown for a Family Law Matter

| Track | Scope |
|---|---|
| 1 | Jurisdictional threshold — UCCJEA home-state analysis, prior orders from other states |
| 2 | Primary substantive issue (custody factors, support calculation, or property division) |
| 3 | Domestic violence / protective order history and its effect on the primary issue |
| 4 | Factual record reconstruction — parenting timeline, financial disclosures, communications |
| 5 | Opposing party's filings — errors, mischaracterizations, undisclosed income/assets |
