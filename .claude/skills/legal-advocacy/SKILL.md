# Legal Advocacy Skill — Motion and Brief Drafting, Any Subject Matter

Use this skill whenever drafting an adversarial legal filing — a motion, opposition,
brief, or advocacy letter — regardless of subject matter (supervised release,
bankruptcy, family court, civil litigation, agency proceedings). It captures durable
lessons about *how to advocate*, not doctrine specific to one area of law. For
subject-matter-specific research architecture (e.g., bankruptcy opposition papers),
see the sibling `legal-research` skill and adapt its subagent/memo structure to the
new subject matter; this skill governs how the actual advocacy gets built once the
research is in hand.

---

## 1. Core Philosophy: Chess, Not Checkers

Two things are simultaneously true, and neither excuses the other:

1. **Cite something for every conclusion.** Advocacy is not "I believe X is true" —
   it is "X is true because [case/statute/canon] says so, and here is why this fact
   pattern falls inside it." A sentence that states a legal conclusion without a
   citation, or that cites a case without explaining *why* the case controls this
   fact pattern, is a bare conclusion — an unforced error, not a stylistic choice.

2. **Advocacy is not neutral research.** The job is not to help the tribunal or the
   opposing party reach the "right" answer — it is to advance the client's position,
   affirmatively, everywhere the record and the law permit it. This is a game of
   chess, not checkers: the opening brief should be built to withstand the opponent's
   most likely attack, and where possible, to make that attack land in a direction
   that helps the client — immediately, or on reply.

**Concrete drafting consequence:** for every argument section, ask two questions
*before* moving on:
- "What case or canon makes this conclusion, and not just my opinion?"
- "What is the single strongest thing the other side will say back, and does my
  draft already make that reply harder to make, or land somewhere useful if made?"

If either answer is missing, the section is not done.

## 2. Voice and Register — Respectful, Blunt, Candid, Concrete

Drawn from a real body of the drafter's own filings (a Third Circuit appellate
response, a § 2241 habeas reply and supporting declaration, a § 3582(c)(2)
sentence-reduction reply, an FTCA opposition to a motion to dismiss, and an advocacy
letter to a non-court third party) — not generic "sound persuasive" advice, but
specific, repeatable techniques actually used across these documents.

### Respectful to the tribunal, unbashful about the opponent
Every filing stays formally respectful to the court while being direct — sometimes
blunt — about the opposing party's conduct. Calling out misconduct is not hedged into
passive voice:

> "This appears to be a deliberate attempt to mislead the Court."
> "The government's attempt to rely on this inaccurate information exemplifies its bad faith."
> "To suggest that [the cited case] resolves [this question] is not merely misleading; it is a profound fabrication of the record."
> "The Government handed this Court documents that confirm the Plaintiff's account while arguing they prove the opposite."

The register: state the accusation as a factual finding the record supports, not as a
rhetorical flourish. Never insult; always indict with specifics.

### Give the other side its due
The same drafter who accuses the government of bad faith also concedes cleanly,
without hedging, when a point is not winnable:

> "Plaintiff concedes one point only: the discretionary function exception bars the
> negligent supervision and training theory within Count II. That concession is the
> beginning and end of the Government's meritorious arguments."

A narrow, explicit concession, immediately bounded ("the beginning and end"), is more
credible than silence on a weak point — it signals the rest of the brief can be
trusted because the drafter is willing to fold where folding is correct.

### Never hide bad facts about your own client
Full candor about the client's own unflattering facts is a feature, not a risk to
manage. Client's own instruction, verbatim, describing a supervised-release
recidivist:

> "[He] is a recidivist, plain and simple, and his conditions should be more strict
> than previously — but not at the expense of living."

The model: concede the aggravating fact plainly (no minimizing, no euphemism),
concede it justifies a real consequence (stricter conditions), then draw the actual
line the brief is fighting for (stricter, but not to the point of destroying a
livelihood). A brief this candid about bad facts earns more trust on everything else
in it than one that avoids the word "recidivist" and hopes the court doesn't notice
the record. Compare *United States v. Grober*'s contact/non-contact distinction
(§ 6, below): same technique — state the true, checkable category the client falls
in, including the unflattering parts, and argue from there, not around it.

### Predictive framing, then self-vindication on reply
The sharpest structural technique in this body of work: in an opening filing,
predict specifically what the opponent will do — in the opponent's own likely
words — then, if the opponent does exactly that, quote the prediction back to the
court in the reply.

> Petition (opening): "The Bureau will inevitably attempt to remedy this facial
> deficiency — through post-hoc declarations from staff claiming that they
> 'considered' the factors in their minds. This Court must reject such retroactive
> justifications. The administrative record must stand on its own."
>
> Reply (four months later, after the government did precisely that): "On May 8,
> 2026 — four months later, and precisely on schedule — the government filed the
> Declaration of Scott Tedesco. The prediction was correct. The government has done
> exactly what Petitioner warned it would do."

This makes the opponent's move look institutionally predictable and cynical rather
than responsive, and lets the reply open with vindication instead of bare rebuttal.
Build this in deliberately: identify the opponent's most likely procedural or
evidentiary move in the *opening* brief, name it explicitly, and explain in advance
why it should fail — so the reply, if the prediction lands, becomes a victory lap.

### Turn the opponent's own remedy into proof of the violation
When the opponent files something *to fix* a defect, argue that the filing itself
proves the defect existed:

> "The government needed a declarant because the Progress Report does not say what
> the government needs it to say. That need proves the deficiency, not the remedy."

And, turning the opponent's own documents against their own argument — quote the
opponent's own exhibit, not a paraphrase of it, so the court sees the contradiction
in the opponent's own words or data:

> "The Government submitted those records. The Government relied on the fill-date
> column to argue that Mr. Khan received his medications. The Government did not
> mention the distribution column. That column ... shows either 'Not Distributed,' a
> blank field... The Government handed this Court documents that confirm the
> Plaintiff's account while arguing they prove the opposite."

### Aphoristic reductio — short, declarative, hard to argue with
Complex statutory arguments compress well into a short, near-proverbial sentence a
judge can quote back in an opinion:

> "A portion cannot be zero. Zero is not a part of a whole — it is the absence of a
> part."
> "Duration. Not eligibility."

These work because they are simple enough to be self-evidently true and specific
enough to resolve the exact dispute — not because they are clever. Reach for this
compression *after* the doctrinal argument is fully built, as the sentence that
distills it, not as a substitute for the doctrinal work.

### Direct, numbered questions posed to the tribunal
Rather than only asserting conclusions, pose the court's actual legal question as an
explicit, numbered question when the argument's core is a discrete question of law
(as opposed to a multi-factor balancing test):

> "1. Does 18 U.S.C. § 3624(c)(1)'s command that the Director 'shall ensure' a
> prisoner spends 'a portion' of final months in pre-release custody permit the BOP
> to award zero days via administrative fiat?
> 2. Do the § 3621(b) factors govern duration or eligibility?
> 3. Does § 3624(c)(6)(B)'s command that decisions be 'determined on an individual
> basis' permit categorical exclusions?"

This frames the court's job as answering a discrete question, not wading through
prose to extract one.

### Lists only in service of an advocacy purpose — never as filler
Numbered/lettered structure appears throughout this body of work, but always doing
rhetorical work, never as a substitute for connected prose:
- "on four independent grounds" / "on five independent grounds" — signals that any
  one basis alone would suffice, so the court doesn't have to accept every point to
  rule for the client.
- Relief-requested lists (lettered a–h) — a different genre entirely (a court order
  needs discrete, separately-orderable findings), not a stylistic default.
- The numbered legal questions above — posed because the court must answer discrete
  questions, not because numbering looks organized.

Never break connected legal reasoning into bullet fragments merely to make a page
look organized. If a list doesn't change what the court has to decide, and doesn't
let the court act on any one item independently, it should be prose instead.

## 3. The Reductio / Absurdity Technique

One of the sharpest tools available: when the opponent's own conduct or position
demonstrates that a broad literal reading is untenable, **use their own confusion as
the proof**, then generalize past the immediate dispute to show the reading is
untenable everywhere, not just here.

**Worked example** (from the Bernecky CIMP/employer dispute): a supervised-release
condition required device monitoring on any "internet capable device... operated by
the defendant." The defendant's employer read this literally and grew alarmed about
its own compliance systems. Rather than treat the employer's confusion as a mere fact
to be resolved, the advocacy move was to treat it as *Exhibit A for the absurdity of
the literal reading* — and then extend the same logic outward:

> Consider his CPAP machine. It is, in every literal sense, an "internet capable
> device." It is also a single-purpose medical instrument with no web browser, no
> app store, and no capacity whatsoever to display an image of any kind... Consider,
> too, the paradigmatic thing a person "operates": not a laptop, not a phone — a
> vehicle. One *uses* a laptop; one *operates* a car... The government cannot have
> it both ways.

This works because:
- It does not ask the tribunal to invent a new rule — it asks the tribunal to avoid
  an absurd application of the *existing* rule, which courts are already inclined
  to do (see, e.g., *United States v. Kunz*, 68 F.4th 748, 754-55, 764-65 (2d Cir.
  2023): "we think it would be absurd to interpret [the condition] as purporting to
  require [defendant] to ask permission every time..."; general absurdity canon:
  *Public Citizen v. U.S. Dep't of Justice*, 491 U.S. 440, 454-55 (1989); *Griffin v.
  Oceanic Contractors*, 458 U.S. 564, 571 (1982)).
- It is not confined to the immediate dispute (the employer) — by reaching CPAP
  machines and cars, it forces the opponent to either concede the narrowing principle
  or defend an application so absurd that defending it costs them credibility on
  every other point in the brief.
- It converts a defensive posture ("please clarify this doesn't apply to my client's
  employer") into an offensive one ("the literal reading is absurd on its face, and
  here is proof from three unrelated domains").

**When to reach for this tool:** whenever a condition, statute, or contract term uses
a broad general word (like "operate," "any," "device," "communication") that would,
read completely literally, sweep in something obviously unintended. Find the
sharpest, most sympathetic, most unrelated-to-the-dispute example first (the thing
least like the actual controversy), because that is the example that best proves the
reading is wrong *in general*, not just inconvenient here.

## 4. The Holistic-Drafting Rule (Never Patchwork-Edit)

**The single most important process lesson from this practice:** a finished or
near-finished document cannot be safely edited by isolated patches. Every edit to one
section has ripple effects — a changed framing in the introduction that isn't carried
into the statement of facts; a new section inserted that isn't reflected in the
conclusion's numbered list or the cross-references in earlier sections; a hedge
("ambiguous") removed from one place but left standing in three others; a fact
correction applied to one paragraph but not to the summary that repeats it.

> "One of the issues with patchwork editing is the rest of the document suffers.
> You can't just take out and put in without analyzing the entire thing. Otherwise
> we end up with a patchwork of garbage, not a beautiful piece."

**Protocol:**
1. After any round of substantive edits — and *always* before final delivery — do a
   **full, linear, front-to-back re-read** of the entire document, not just the edited
   sections.
2. While re-reading, grep/search for anything the edit should have changed elsewhere:
   the old framing word ("ambiguous," "may," a superseded case name), stale counts
   ("eight groups of conditions" — a number that will drift every time a section is
   added or removed; better to delete the count than to maintain it), stale
   cross-references (section letters/numbers after an insertion or deletion),
   and anything the outline promised that the draft never actually delivered.
3. Confirm every planned-but-not-yet-drafted piece from the outline actually made it
   into the document — a full re-read is often the only way an omitted section (not
   just a wrong one) gets caught.
4. Only after the full re-read is clean does the document go out. A patch that "looks
   right in isolation" is not evidence the whole document is coherent.

This rule applies with equal force to formatting: if headings, spacing, indentation,
or citation style are fixed in one place, confirm the fix propagates everywhere the
same pattern occurs, not just the example the reviewer happened to point at.

## 5. Research Rigor and Gap-Honesty

Advocacy without rigor is just assertion with adjectives. Every citation needs a
verification status, and every gap in the law needs to be disclosed as a gap, not
papered over with confident prose.

### Verification tiers
- **CONFIRMED** — read in full from a primary source (official reporter, court's own
  PACER docket, or a verified opinion PDF). Safe to quote and pincite directly.
- **[VERIFY]** — reconstructed from secondary sources (WebSearch summaries, treatise
  quotations, another opinion's own quotation of it). Usable in a draft, but flagged
  visibly, and must be resolved (Westlaw/Lexis/PACER) before filing.
- **DO NOT CITE** — identified as unreliable, unlocatable across multiple passes, or
  contradicted by a primary-source read. Keep it in the tracking file specifically so
  it does not get silently reintroduced later.

### Honest-gap disclosure
When no case addresses the precise question (a common and *fine* outcome — most
sharp arguments are partly synthesis), say so explicitly rather than implying a
square holding exists. A synthesis argument built candidly on adjacent doctrine
("no case names this exact rule, but it follows from A, B, and C, each independently
confirmed") is stronger than an overclaimed one that a clerk can debunk with one
search. Likewise, when adverse authority exists (a case that cuts against the
client's position), disclose and distinguish it affirmatively in the brief — do not
wait for the opponent to raise it. A judge who finds the adverse case herself, after
the brief went silent on it, will discount everything else in the brief.

### Factual corrections
When a client corrects a factual premise the draft got wrong, don't just silently fix
the sentence — log the correction (what was wrong, what the actual fact is, and
where the draft needs to change) in a durable client-directive record before editing,
so the same error can't quietly resurface in a later draft or a different section
that references the same fact.

## 6. Style Calibration — What Precedent Briefs Teach

Before drafting in a new register (new court, new subject matter, new client voice),
read 2-3 of the drafter's own prior filings that succeeded, and extract *technique*,
not boilerplate. (For the specific rhetorical techniques found this way — predictive
framing, aphoristic reductio, candor about bad facts, and the rest — see § 2 above;
this section is about the *process* of extracting them, not the catalog itself.)

- **Categorical honesty as a persuasion tool.** A compassionate-release motion for a
  CP-possession client opened not by minimizing the offense, but by immediately and
  precisely placing the client outside a worse category: "[Client] is neither a child
  molester nor a contact offender" — drawing directly on *United States v. Grober*,
  624 F.3d 592, 598 (3d Cir. 2010)'s own distinction between contact and non-contact
  offenders. This is more persuasive than euphemism because it is falsifiable and
  true — it invites the court to check the distinction rather than take the drafter's
  word for sympathy.
- **Fact-first sympathy, still filtered through legal categories.** Specific,
  checkable facts (voluntary disclosure to arresting agents, offering a polygraph
  unprompted, documented community involvement) do the work that adjectives cannot —
  but only when immediately tied to the legal category the court actually has to rule
  on (e.g., the § 3553(a) factors, not just "he's a good person").
- **The advocacy letter as a genre of its own.** A letter to a non-court third party
  (e.g., a client's employer, explaining that a condition does not reach them and
  instructing them to route further contact to the court rather than comply
  administratively) still needs the same cite-and-attack discipline as a brief: state
  the rule, apply it to the specific document in the third party's hands, and give
  concrete instructions for what to do next. It should never read as a request for
  the third party's discretion — it should read as a legal conclusion the third party
  can safely rely on.

Do this style pass *before* drafting, not as a post-hoc polish — voice and structure
choices made on page one constrain what page ten can credibly do.

## 7. Track-Record Awareness — Learn From the Same Court's Own History

Before drafting a motion type that has likely been filed before (in the same
district, on the same condition type, by the same client or firm), search for prior
examples and their **actual outcomes** — not just the arguments made, but which ones
won and which lost, and why. A won/lost scorecard against a real judgment is worth
more than another hour of law-review reading, because it tells you which version of
an argument a real court actually accepted.

See `reference/preston-field-case-study.md` for a full worked example: a W.D.N.Y.
supervised-release modification motion with a documented scorecard (six wins, one
partial win, four losses across eleven requested modifications) and a diagnosis of
*why* each loss lost — in every case, a correct instinct and a real citation, but no
argument connecting the two to the specific fact pattern in a way that pre-empted the
government's obvious reply. That diagnosis is the single most reusable lesson in this
skill: **a citation is necessary, but it is not the argument. The argument is why
this citation, applied to these facts, forecloses that reply.**

When using a prior client's file this way:
- Extract structure, technique, and outcome — never import the prior client's facts,
  admissions, or violations into the current client's motion.
- Check whether a losing (or winning) argument's fact pattern would have covered the
  prior client's own later conduct, if any is in the file — an argument that would not
  have prevented a client's own subsequent violation is a warning sign that the theory
  is drawn too narrowly, or that the government's reply "this wouldn't have helped
  anyway" needs to be addressed head-on.
- Confirm any factual claim recovered this way (e.g., "this district's probation
  office actually uses CVSA exams in practice") independently before relying on it —
  a single prior case file is corroboration, not proof.

## 8. Anticipating the Reply — Structural Sequencing

Order arguments so the strongest, most procedurally prior point leads (a threshold
defect that resolves the whole condition beats a proportionality argument that only
narrows it). Within each argument section, structure to survive the predictable
counter:

- If the tribunal is likely to ask "why not read this narrowly instead of striking
  it," have the narrowing answer ready in the same section, not held in reserve for
  reply.
- If an adverse but distinguishable case exists, distinguish it in the opening brief.
  Never let the first time a court sees the adverse case be in the opponent's papers.
- If relief has a "fallback" version the client would accept but does not actually
  want, do not draft it as an alternative unless the client has said so explicitly —
  presenting an unwanted fallback in the papers can be read by the tribunal as the
  outer bound of what the client is asking for, foreclosing the stronger ask. (E.g.:
  a client who wants a categorical "no employer monitoring, period" ruling should not
  see a backup "employer consents to monitoring instead" option drafted into the
  motion — that fallback becomes the ceiling, not the floor.)

## 9. Client-Directive Logging Convention

Maintain a running `client-directives.md` (or equivalent) for any matter with more
than one drafting round. Every substantive correction, reframe, or new instruction
from the client gets logged — verbatim or near-verbatim — with:
- what was wrong or missing in the prior draft,
- the corrected fact/framing/instruction,
- what changed as a result.

This is not bureaucratic overhead — it is what prevents a corrected error from
silently reappearing three drafts later when a different section touches the same
fact, and it gives whoever picks up the file next (including a future version of the
same drafter) a full account of *why* the document looks the way it does.

## 10. Pre-Filing Quality Checklist

### Argument Construction
- [ ] Every legal conclusion has a citation; every citation has an explanation of why
      it controls *these* facts (no bare conclusions)
- [ ] Every section's strongest predictable counter-argument is addressed inline, not
      left for reply
- [ ] Adverse authority is disclosed and distinguished affirmatively, not omitted
- [ ] No unwanted fallback relief is drafted in without explicit client sign-off
- [ ] Reductio/absurdity examples (if used) reach beyond the immediate dispute to the
      sharpest, most unrelated illustration available

### Voice and Register
- [ ] Any accusation against the opposing party is stated as a specific, record-backed
      finding, not a hedge or an unsupported adjective
- [ ] Any losing or weak point is conceded explicitly and narrowly, not left silent
- [ ] Any unflattering fact about the client is stated plainly, not minimized or
      euphemized, and is turned into the actual line the brief is drawing
- [ ] Lists are used only where they do genuine advocacy work (independent grounds,
      separately-orderable relief, a discrete legal question) — not as organizational
      filler for prose that should just be prose

### Document-Wide Consistency (Holistic Pass)
- [ ] Full front-to-back re-read completed after the last round of edits — not just a
      diff of the changed sections
- [ ] No stale framing language left over from a superseded theory (e.g., "ambiguous"
      surviving in the introduction after the body was rewritten to argue "no
      restriction exists at all")
- [ ] No hardcoded counts/lists that will drift as sections are added or removed
- [ ] Every section the outline promised is actually present in the draft
- [ ] All cross-references (section letters/numbers, page counts) updated after any
      insertion, deletion, or relettering
- [ ] Formatting fixes (fonts, spacing, indentation, citation style) verified to
      propagate everywhere the same pattern occurs, not just the flagged example

### Research and Citations
- [ ] Every citation's verification status is current (CONFIRMED / [VERIFY] / DO NOT
      CITE) in the tracking file
- [ ] No [VERIFY]-flagged citation is quoted with a pincite as if independently
      confirmed
- [ ] Every genuine gap in the law is disclosed as a gap, not implied to be resolved
- [ ] Every client factual correction has been propagated to every place the old fact
      appeared, and logged in the client-directive record

### Client Intent
- [ ] Every explicit client instruction (removed argument, reframed theory, changed
      relief) has actually been carried out — check the current draft against the
      directive log line by line
- [ ] The requested relief matches what the client actually wants, not a hedged or
      narrowed version arrived at for drafting convenience

## 11. Formatting Pipeline — Markdown to Court-Ready Document

For a polished, reviewable deliverable (docx for tracked-changes review, PDF for
filing), do not rely on a single conversion tool's defaults — they leak inconsistent
styling (theme colors, non-standard fonts, stray horizontal rules from markdown `---`).

**docx pipeline (pandoc + python-docx force-formatting):**
1. Strip all markdown `---` horizontal-rule lines from the source before conversion
   (pandoc renders them as bordered paragraphs, which read as unwanted lines between
   sections).
2. Convert via `pandoc input.md -o output.docx --reference-doc=reference.docx`, where
   `reference.docx` defines the target Normal/Heading styles (e.g., Times New Roman
   12pt, double-spaced, zero extra paragraph spacing).
3. **Always follow with a direct python-docx post-processing pass** that force-sets,
   on every run and paragraph, regardless of what the reference doc or pandoc did:
   - font name/size/color on every run (do not trust inherited styles)
   - removal of any `w:pBdr` border element (kills leftover horizontal-rule borders)
   - double line-spacing with zero before/after paragraph spacing
   - a first-line indent on body paragraphs (heuristically: non-heading paragraphs
     over some minimum character length)
4. Verify: count total runs vs. non-conforming runs (wrong font/size/color) after
   processing — zero non-conforming runs is the bar, not "looks right on screen."

**PDF pipeline (pandoc + pdflatex via a custom template):** see the sibling
`legal-research` skill's `gen_pdf.py` and `templates/legal-memo.tex` for a working
implementation (cover page, Table of Authorities, Unicode-safe LaTeX substitutions,
formatting decisions table). Adapt the cover-page fields and citation-extraction
regexes for the new matter rather than rebuilding the pipeline from scratch.

## 12. Scanned-Document OCR Workflow

Court dockets and Drive uploads are frequently scanned PDFs with no usable text
layer — a Drive `read_file_content` or `download_file_content` call will return only
page-header bates stamps, not the substantive text. When this happens:
1. Download the PDF (`download_file_content`, then extract the base64 payload with
   Python if the tool result is saved to a file rather than returned inline).
2. Render each page to a PNG with PyMuPDF (`pip install pymupdf`; `fitz.open(...)`,
   `page.get_pixmap(matrix=fitz.Matrix(2,2))`, `pix.save(...)` — 2x scale keeps OCR
   accuracy reasonable without huge files).
3. OCR each page with `tesseract` (`apt-get install -y tesseract-ocr`; `pip install
   pytesseract` if a Python API is preferred over shelling out).
4. Read the OCR'd text output directly — expect occasional character-level OCR noise
   (e.g., "1" misread as "l", stray symbols in signature blocks) and sanity-check any
   number, date, or citation against context before relying on it.
5. Do not commit the raw scratch PNGs/PDFs generated for OCR into the repository —
   they are working artifacts. Keep only the synthesized markdown analysis.
