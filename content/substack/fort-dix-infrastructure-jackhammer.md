# Fort Dix Rented a Jackhammer for a Month. Here's What They Built.

**By Bilal Khan**
*Surviving the Feds: Dispatches from the Inside*

---

There is a hole in the sidewalk directly in front of my housing unit at FCI Fort Dix. Not a crack, not a dip — an actual hole. They put an orange cone on it.

The cone is still there.

Here's how we got here.

---

## The Setup

A few weeks ago, a work crew showed up with a jackhammer truck, made their marks on the pavement, and tore up a section of sidewalk between two housing units. The project, as I understand it from inmates who've been tracking this, was to replace the entire stretch of deteriorating sidewalk — the kind of repair that's been needed for years. The paint marks were laid out. The scope was clear. They had the equipment.

They jackhammered for two days. They completed roughly one-eighth of the marked project.

Then the crew left.

That was nearly three weeks ago.

Here is the part that makes your head hurt: according to inmates familiar with the procurement, that jackhammer truck was rented for an entire month.

One month. Two days of use. And a hole with a cone on it.

---

## The Hole They Didn't Fill

It gets better.

Right there, a few inches from where the crew left off, they were actively pouring fresh concrete. They had the material. They had the tools. They had the crew on-site. And there was a pre-existing hole in the sidewalk that predated the whole project — not the section they jackhammered, a separate hazard that had been sitting there, unaddressed.

They put an orange cone on it and kept moving.

Or rather, stopped moving. Then left entirely.

I want to be precise here because this is not just an inconvenience story. Fort Dix has a population with significant medical needs. There are inmates here who use wheelchairs. Inmates on crutches. Inmates who have had recent surgeries and have mobility restrictions. The sidewalk between housing units is not a optional path — it's the main route people travel every day to get to chow, to medical, to programming.

The BOP, at least officially, claims compliance with the Rehabilitation Act and the Americans with Disabilities Act. There is a process for accommodations. There is language in policy about accessible facilities.

What there is not, apparently, is any requirement that when you are actively pouring concrete a few inches from a hazard, and inmates with crutches are walking by that hazard every single day, you fill the damn hole.

They put a cone on it.

---

## What This Is, Exactly

I've been through Lewisburg USP, Oklahoma City FTC, FCI Big Spring, and now I'm back at Fort Dix — eight months across four facilities. I've seen a lot of institutional dysfunction. Most of it is forgettable. But this particular situation crystallizes something that I think matters for anyone trying to understand how federal prison infrastructure actually works.

It is not a resource problem. The money was there — a month-long equipment rental is not cheap. The materials were there. The labor was there. Two days of work on an eight-day job, and then nothing for three weeks while the truck presumably sat somewhere burning through its rental period.

This is what a broken accountability structure looks like from the inside.

Nobody got fired for stopping in the middle of the project. Nobody got written up for leaving the hazard. Nobody had to answer the question: *why did we rent the equipment for four times longer than we needed it?* The facility's infrastructure budget absorbed whatever this cost, and the sidewalk sits half-done, and the hole has its cone, and the inmates with mobility issues navigate around it every day.

That is not incompetence. That is an institution that has learned it doesn't have to answer for any of this.

---

## What You Can Do About It

If your family member is at a facility with infrastructure hazards that are creating genuine safety risks, here's the actual path:

**Document everything.** Any inmate at risk due to a physical hazard should file a BP-8 immediately — the informal resolution form. Describe the hazard specifically: location, nature of the hazard, how long it has been present, any near-misses or injuries. Names of witnesses help. Dates matter.

**If you have a mobility-related medical condition**, the ADA and Rehabilitation Act apply to you inside federal prison. A documented failure to maintain accessible pathways is not just a complaint — it's a civil rights issue with a paper trail. File the administrative remedy. Get the denial in writing. That denial is your ticket to the next level, and eventually, if necessary, to court.

**If someone gets hurt**, that orange cone becomes evidence. It shows the institution knew about the hazard and chose to leave it rather than fix it. That's the legal standard for deliberate indifference, and it matters.

**For families on the outside**: BOP facilities are subject to oversight, at least theoretically. Congressional representatives' offices have constituent services staff. A facility with documented, ongoing safety hazards is exactly the kind of thing a constituent services inquiry can surface — even if slowly.

WARNING: THE ADMINISTRATIVE REMEDY PROCESS HAS STRICT DEADLINES. A BP-8 must be filed within 20 days of the incident. Miss that window and you may lose the right to pursue the claim further. File first, figure out the details second.
