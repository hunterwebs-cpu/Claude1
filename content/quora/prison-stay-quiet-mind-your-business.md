Sort of — but "mind your business" is the wrong instruction, and that's why it half-works and half-doesn't. The instruction we actually use inside is "stay in your lane," and it's not the same thing.

Here's the difference. "Mind your business" sounds passive — put your head down, don't look, don't engage. That's not enough on its own, and in some institutions it'll get you smashed. "Stay in your lane" is active: worry about the traffic in front of you and behind you, not what that traffic is doing inside their cars. You're going to see guys do drugs, use phones, fight, even have sex. None of that is your business. But if you're "minding your business" while your face is turned toward someone else's cell, you're still bothering them. You're bothering someone the moment you're watching them do something against the rules or outright illegal — whether you meant to or not.

You also have to think of yourself like a financial statement. You're either an asset to the inmate community or a liability. Guys who withdraw completely — bury themselves in religion, sports, music, and nothing else — often get looked at like they're going to snitch. Doing minor stuff yourself (a cigarette, a little liquor, a phone) buys trust. You don't have to do it, but total abstinence isn't the safe, invisible choice people assume it is.

And staying quiet doesn't protect you from getting dragged into something that has nothing to do with you. I've watched a guy get sent to the SHU because his cellmate kept a shank in the vent — a shared, common part of the cell. Officer finds it, cellie doesn't step up and claim it, you're both writing an incident report and taking a walk to the SHU. You did nothing. Depending on the facility you might be able to talk to your cellie about it, switch cells, or go through your car leader — but sometimes none of that works and you're just fucked.

Visibility can do it too. I know a guy who landed in his first unit and immediately started baking and selling cakes. He was a sex offender, and he looked like an easy target — turned out he wasn't. An opportunist told him to hand over the money or else. He refused, stood his ground, and got hit with a padlock so bad he ended up in the hospital, then months in the SHU for his own protection. Did he stay in his lane? Not really — he started making money in a building with a high rate of guys who specifically target sex offenders, before he'd gotten a read on where he'd landed. He made excellent cakes. That was the problem. Prison has plenty of criminals, and a lot of them are jealous ones.

Sometimes you break the rule by accident. A guy who'd been a nurse on the outside took a job as an orderly in health services. Someone asked who'd grabbed a box of gloves, and he answered without thinking — didn't register it as snitching, just answered a question. The guy he named lost his job, came straight to the building, and pulled a knife demanding payment. A friend of mine talked it down, but that nurse was in real danger and had to check in for his own protection.

Posture matters more than people think. Head up, shoulders back, eye contact when you talk to someone — respectful, not confrontational. Guys who walk around with their heads down looking ashamed become the easy mark for whoever's opportunistic that week. Prison is a lot like showing up to a new junior high: everybody's sizing you up while you find your place.

I learned the "stay in your lane" lesson on day one, very green, in county. I was standing near two guys talking about their case — not looking at them, just close enough to hear. One walked over: "Can I help you?" Unfriendly posture. That told me everything. I turned my hearing off right there, and I've kept it off since — I could walk past a fight in a hallway now and be genuinely oblivious to it.

I've spent my BOP time in low security, and I've never been in a fight — but I'm not a pushover, and being a jailhouse lawyer who actually gets results makes me an asset in other guys' eyes, which lets me talk to different groups freely. I still stay in my lane. That's the whole trick: not invisibility, just knowing exactly where your business ends and everyone else's begins.
