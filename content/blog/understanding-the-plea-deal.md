More than 97 percent of federal convictions don't come from a jury — they come from a signature on a plea agreement. That number should make clear how central the plea is to the federal system. And yet most families don't fully understand what a plea agreement actually is, what it costs, and what it doesn't fix — until after the deal is done.

This is not legal advice. Your attorney is the only person who can tell you whether a specific offer makes sense in your specific case. What follows is the framework for understanding what you're looking at.

## What a plea agreement actually is

A plea agreement is a contract between the defendant and the government. The defendant agrees to plead guilty to specific charges; the government agrees to certain terms in return. What those terms look like varies widely — and that variation matters enormously.

A plea can include:
- **Dropping or not bringing certain charges** — the government may agree to dismiss counts or agree not to bring additional charges it could have filed
- **A stipulated sentencing range** — both sides agree on where the guidelines should land, which limits the judge's range
- **A cap on the recommended sentence** — the government agrees not to recommend above a certain number
- **Cooperation provisions** — more on this below
- **Factual admissions** — the defendant agrees to the government's version of the facts, which becomes part of the court record

What a plea does *not* fix: civil liability, immigration consequences, firearm rights, professional licenses, and other collateral effects. Those exist independently of the criminal sentence and often survive even after time is served.

## The factual basis matters as much as the charge

Every plea requires the defendant to admit to a set of facts — often called the "factual basis" or "statement of facts." These aren't just formalities. They become the foundation of the **Presentence Report**, which drives the guidelines calculation, which shapes the sentence.

Admitting to a larger quantity of drugs, a higher loss amount, a more serious role in the offense — any of these can add years to the outcome. A defendant who pleads guilty without carefully reviewing what they're admitting to can inadvertently give away the very sentencing fight they thought the plea was resolving.

## Cooperation: the most misunderstood piece

Many pleas involve what's called a **cooperation agreement** — the defendant agrees to assist the government in other investigations or prosecutions. In return, the government may later file a **5K1.1 motion** (for pre-sentencing cooperation) or a **Rule 35 motion** (for post-sentencing cooperation), which allow the judge to sentence below the mandatory minimum.

Cooperation is not for everyone, and it comes with real risks:
- You must tell the truth — even if that means implicating people you care about
- The government decides whether the cooperation was substantial enough to reward; you have no guaranteed return
- Safety risks vary by case type and environment
- Once you cooperate, there is generally no going back

The decision to cooperate — or not — is one of the most consequential a defendant makes, and it deserves careful, candid discussion with the attorney.

## Why pleading is often strategic even without a strong case against you

Federal prosecutors have enormous resources and a near-infinite supply of pressure. The trial penalty — the difference in sentence between losing at trial and accepting a plea — is real, documented, and substantial. Studies consistently show that defendants who go to trial and lose receive sentences significantly longer than those who pleaded to similar conduct.

That doesn't mean you should always plead. It means the decision has to account for:
- The strength of the evidence
- The applicable guidelines and mandatory minimums
- What the government is actually offering versus what a conviction at trial would likely produce
- The realistic odds at trial in that jurisdiction with that judge

Your attorney's job is to help you weigh all of that honestly.

## Acceptance of responsibility: the three-point reduction

If a defendant clearly accepts responsibility for their offense, the guidelines allow for a **two-level reduction** in the base offense level, with a **third level** available if the defendant timely notified the government of their intent to plead. That three-point reduction can translate to a meaningful difference in years — and it disappears if you go to trial and lose.

This is one reason many defense attorneys counsel against going to trial in cases with overwhelming evidence: not because fighting is wrong, but because the mathematics of the guidelines, combined with the trial penalty, make the costs of losing extremely high.

## What to do when an offer comes

Don't accept or reject a plea on the day it's presented. Push for time — and use it. Questions worth asking your attorney:

- What are the sentencing guidelines if I plead to this? What are they if I go to trial and lose?
- What am I actually admitting to in the factual basis, and how will that affect the PSR?
- What charges are being dropped — and would they have stuck at trial anyway?
- Is the government offering cooperation, and what does that mean in this case?
- What's our realistic shot at trial?

A good attorney will walk you through all of this without rushing you toward a decision. Be cautious of anyone who pushes you to sign without fully explaining what you're giving up.
