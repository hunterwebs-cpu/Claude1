People say you should hire a lawyer to watch your lawyer. Almost nobody can afford that. So the next best thing is knowing what to look for yourself — because in federal court, the gap between a prepared attorney and a coasting one can be measured in years of someone's life.

Here are seven questions worth asking. None of them require a law degree to understand the answer.

## 1. "How many federal cases like mine have you actually tried?"

Note the two key words: *federal* and *tried*. Plenty of capable state attorneys are out of their depth in federal court, which runs on different rules, different sentencing, and different stakes. And many lawyers settle everything — ask how many they've taken to trial. You're not necessarily looking for a trial; you're looking for someone the government knows *can* go to trial.

## 2. "Who will do the actual work on my case?"

It's common to meet the senior partner at the consultation and then never hear from them again. Ask plainly: who reviews the discovery, who writes the motions, who shows up at hearings? If the answer is an associate you haven't met, that's not automatically bad — but you deserve to know.

## 3. "What's your honest read on my exposure?"

A good federal attorney can speak intelligently about the sentencing guidelines, potential enhancements, and realistic ranges early on. Beware two extremes: the lawyer who promises you'll walk, and the lawyer who can't or won't give you any framework at all. You want sober realism, not a sales pitch or a shrug.

## 4. "What's the plan for the next 30 days?"

Federal cases reward early, deliberate work — reviewing discovery, identifying issues, preserving arguments. If the only "plan" is to wait and see what the prosecutor offers, that's a passive defense. You're paying for strategy, not a mailbox.

## 5. "How and when will you communicate with us?"

One of the most common complaints from families is silence. Set the expectation up front: how often will you get updates, who do you call, and what's a realistic response time? A lawyer who won't define this now usually won't deliver it later.

## 6. "Have you read everything the government has given you?"

This sounds basic. It is not always true. Discovery in federal cases can be enormous, and a defense built on a skim is a weak defense. You want to hear specifics about the evidence, not generalities.

## 7. "If this were your family member, what would you do?"

It cuts through the posturing. The answer — and how honestly it's given — tells you whether you're talking to an advocate or a vendor.

## Red flags that should give you pause

- Guarantees of a specific outcome.
- Pressure to decide immediately or pay everything up front with no clarity on scope.
- Dismissiveness when you ask reasonable questions.
- No working knowledge of the sentencing guidelines.
- You can never reach them and never meet the person doing the work.

---

You will spend a great deal of money on this. Spend a little attention first. The right questions, asked early, are some of the cheapest leverage you will ever have.
