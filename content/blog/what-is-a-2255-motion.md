A federal conviction feels final. Often it isn't — not completely. One of the most important tools available afterward is a motion under **28 U.S.C. § 2255**, and most people have never heard of it until they desperately need it. This is a plain-language introduction to what it is, who it's for, and why the deadlines will quietly destroy a good claim if you let them.

This is educational only. A 2255 is a serious, technical filing, and the stakes are high. Treat what follows as a map, not a substitute for counsel.

## What a 2255 motion actually is

A § 2255 motion asks the federal court that sentenced you to **vacate, set aside, or correct** the sentence. It is not a second appeal and it is not a do-over of the trial. It's a targeted challenge that says, in effect: *something about my conviction or sentence violated my rights, and here's why.*

Common grounds include:

- **Ineffective assistance of counsel** — your lawyer's performance fell below a constitutional minimum and it affected the outcome. This is the most frequently raised ground.
- **Constitutional violations** that weren't or couldn't be raised on direct appeal.
- **Lack of jurisdiction** or a sentence that exceeds what the law allows.
- **New, retroactive changes in the law** that affect your case.

## Who can file one

Generally, a person **in federal custody** under a federal sentence can file a 2255 in the sentencing court. There are important limits — for example, you usually must have finished or exhausted your direct appeal first, and there are strict rules about filing more than one.

## The deadline that ends cases before they start

This is the part people miss. There is generally a **one-year statute of limitations** for a 2255 motion. The clock usually starts when your conviction becomes final — but it can also run from other trigger dates (such as the recognition of a new, retroactive right).

Miss it, and even a strong claim can be barred forever. If you take one thing from this article, take this: **find out your deadline early, and treat it as immovable.**

## "Ineffective assistance" is a specific legal test — not just disappointment

People often feel their lawyer let them down. That feeling, by itself, isn't enough. Courts apply a two-part test (commonly called the *Strickland* standard):

1. **Deficient performance** — counsel's work fell below an objective standard of reasonableness, and
2. **Prejudice** — there's a reasonable probability the outcome would have been different but for those errors.

Both parts matter. A claim that only complains about strategy, without showing it actually changed the result, usually fails.

## Why this gets its own handbook

Filing a 2255 well means identifying the right grounds, marshaling the record, respecting the deadlines, and writing it so a judge takes it seriously. It is genuinely hard — which is exactly why so few resources explain it for the people who need it most. That gap is the reason **The 2255 Motion Handbook** exists: to walk a non-lawyer through the exact steps to file, argue, and fight.
