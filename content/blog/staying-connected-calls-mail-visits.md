One of the hardest parts of a federal case for families is the communication — not just how little of it there is, but the constant awareness that every word is being monitored. This isn't paranoia. It's the law, and it's the practice. Understanding how each channel works — and where the limits are — lets you stay close without accidentally making things worse.

## Phone calls: the most immediate — and the most recorded

Almost every call from a federal detention facility or BOP institution is **recorded and made available to prosecutors**. This is not a secret; detainees are reminded of it at the start of each call. And yet families lose ground every day on recorded lines.

**The rule is simple: never discuss the facts of the case on a jail or prison call.** Not the charges. Not what happened. Not what someone else said or did. Not your legal strategy. And not anything that sounds like coordinating stories — that can become obstruction.

Practical setup:
- The facility will give a pin number or call setup process for outgoing calls
- Most federal facilities use contracted services like **Securus** or **GTL/ViaPath** (now Aventiv) — you'll typically set up a prepaid account on their platform to receive calls or have funds ready
- Calls are often limited in duration (often 15 minutes) and the per-minute cost can be significant — keeping money in the account matters

What you *can* discuss: how everyone is doing emotionally, logistics (books on the account, upcoming visits, status of family life), encouragement, love. Keep it real. Keep it human.

**Attorney calls are different.** Calls between a defendant and their attorney are supposed to be confidential and are not recorded — but only if the call is properly designated as an attorney-client call. This is not automatic. Make sure the attorney knows to call the facility's designated legal line.

## Physical mail: slower, but often more meaningful

**BOP mail is routinely read.** Facilities scan, read, and sometimes copy inmate mail. Assume everything you send in writing will be reviewed. The same content rule applies: no case facts, no strategy, nothing that looks like you're arranging witnesses or coordinating accounts.

That said, mail matters deeply to people inside. Cards, letters, photos, articles — they are a lifeline. Many people in federal custody report that mail is the thing they look forward to most.

Practical notes:
- Address clearly: use the full name and register number (the BOP inmate number), the facility name, and the correct mailing address
- Facilities often have rules about what can be enclosed: no loose glitter, no staples, sometimes no colored envelopes. Check the specific facility's rules on the BOP website
- Photos are usually allowed but must be non-Polaroid prints (the facility may reject instant-film photos)
- Hardcover books typically must be sent directly from a publisher or retailer (like Amazon), not from home; paperbacks may vary by facility

**Legal mail** — mail to or from an attorney clearly marked as such — is treated differently. It can only be opened in the presence of the inmate, not read. This protection has limits and can be abused, but it exists.

## Email via Corrlinks

The Bureau of Prisons operates **Corrlinks**, an email system that connects family members to federal inmates. It is not instant messaging. It's an asynchronous system where each person sends a message and the other reads and replies when they have access.

How it works:
- The inmate initiates the Corrlinks connection from their end, sending a request to your email address
- You'll receive an invitation to set up a Corrlinks account
- Once linked, you can exchange messages through the platform
- Messages are subject to review — the same content rules apply

Corrlinks accounts have a small per-message cost charged to the inmate's account, so having money on the books matters for this too.

Not all facilities or all inmates have consistent Corrlinks access. Early in custody (especially during pretrial detention), access may be limited or absent.

## Putting money on the books

"Books" refers to the inmate's commissary account — the account they use to buy food, hygiene items, postage, phone time, and email credits beyond what the facility provides.

Common methods:
- **JPay** (for many BOP facilities) or the facility's specific designated service
- **Western Union** or **MoneyGram** (at certain facilities)
- **Corrlinks/TRULINCS** account — some email credits come from a separate trust fund account
- Some facilities allow online deposits directly through their own portal

An initial deposit of around **$100–$150** usually provides a reasonable cushion for the first few weeks. It won't last forever — phone time alone can be significant — but it's a solid starting point.

## Visitation

Visiting a federal inmate requires advance approval. Every visitor must be on the **approved visitor list**, and the process for getting on that list varies by facility.

General steps:
- The inmate submits your name for approval
- Some facilities require you to complete a visitation form and submit identification
- A background check is standard — federal visitors are screened, and prior felony convictions may complicate or prevent approval
- Once approved, you schedule a visit according to the facility's specific visiting days and hours (these are published on the BOP website at bop.gov)

What to know about visiting:
- Dress codes are enforced — typically no clothing that resembles inmate uniforms (khaki, green, orange), no revealing clothing, no clothing that matches officer uniforms
- You'll go through security screening (metal detectors, sometimes a pat-down)
- Contact visits are typical in most BOP facilities but the extent varies
- Video visits are available at some facilities — check via bop.gov for that specific institution

If the person is in a county jail awaiting a federal hearing (common early on), the visitation rules are the county jail's rules, not BOP's — these vary widely.

## What never to say

To repeat what matters most: **do not discuss the facts of the case on any recorded channel.** That is every phone call, every email, every physical letter. The only truly privileged communication is with the attorney, and only if that communication is properly protected.

Families that lose the most ground lose it not because they didn't care — but because they cared so much that they tried to help without understanding the risks.

---

Connection is possible. It takes more effort than it should, costs more than it should, and requires more caution than feels natural. But it's there — and for someone sitting in federal custody, hearing a familiar voice or reading a letter from home can matter more than anything else happening in that case.
