When the federal government arrests someone, the clock doesn't wait for you to catch your breath. The first 48 hours feel like chaos, but they are not random. There is a process, and the people running it do this every single day. You don't have to. That asymmetry is exactly why knowledge matters more in the beginning than at almost any other point in the case.

This is not legal advice. It's a survival map drawn from lived experience. Use it to ask better questions of a licensed attorney — not to replace one.

## 1. Say less than you think you should

The single most common, most damaging mistake is talking. Agents are trained, patient, and friendly on purpose. "We just want to clear a few things up" is not an invitation to help yourself — it's an invitation to build the government's case for them.

You have the right to remain silent. Use it. Politely, clearly, and completely:

> "I'm going to remain silent and I want a lawyer."

Then stop. Don't explain. Don't negotiate. Don't try to talk your way out. There is no version of that conversation that helps you and many versions that hurt.

## 2. Assume every call is recorded — because it is

Jail and detention calls are recorded and routinely reviewed by prosecutors. So are visits, in many facilities. Families lose ground every day by discussing the case on these lines: admitting facts, coordinating stories (which can become obstruction), or simply saying something that sounds worse out of context.

The rule is simple: **do not discuss the facts of the case on any monitored line.** Talk about how everyone is doing. Talk about logistics. Save the substance for a confidential conversation with the attorney.

## 3. Understand what's about to happen procedurally

In the first couple of days you'll likely encounter:

- **Initial appearance** — formal notice of the charges and your rights.
- **Detention / bond hearing** — whether the defendant is released or held pending trial. This matters enormously and moves fast.
- **Appointment or retention of counsel** — court-appointed or private.

Most families don't realize the detention decision is often where the case's trajectory quietly bends. Showing up unprepared here is costly.

## 4. Choose the attorney deliberately, not in a panic

You are about to make one of the most important hires of your life in one of the worst emotional states of your life. Slow down enough to ask real questions: How many federal cases like this have you handled? What's your read on the detention posture? Who actually does the work — you or an associate?

A future article covers this in depth, but the principle now is: **don't confuse confidence for competence, and don't confuse a high retainer for a good outcome.**

## 5. Protect the family's stability

Federal cases can last months or years. The family is now part of the case whether they want to be or not — managing finances, communication, kids, and their own fear. Decide early who is the point person, keep records organized, and protect your own health. You cannot help anyone from a state of collapse.

---

The federal system is built to feel overwhelming. It is not built to be understood by the people caught inside it. But the first 48 hours reward the prepared and punish the panicked — and preparation is something you can start right now.
