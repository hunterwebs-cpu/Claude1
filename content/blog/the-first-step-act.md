# The First Step Act: What It Actually Gets You

**By Bilal Khan** | [HEADSHOT: Bilal Khan]

---

In December 2018, Congress passed the most significant federal criminal justice reform in a generation. The First Step Act became law. The Bureau of Prisons was required to implement it. Inmates were going to be able to earn time off their sentences by participating in programs. Everyone celebrated.

Then the BOP had to actually figure out how to do it.

I was inside when implementation began. I spent significant time sitting with BOP case managers, walking them through the calculations, correcting the math. Their initial guidance was wrong. The system has since been centralized — case managers have less direct role in calculating your credits now, it flows through a national database — but the confusion that surrounded the rollout never fully went away. And the confusion that lives in people's minds about what the law actually does is, in many ways, worse.

Most people who are eligible for First Step Act credits know they are earning them. Very few understand what those credits actually buy. The difference between what people think is happening and what is actually happening is significant enough to reshape your entire strategy — and to determine whether you survive what comes after release.

So what does the First Step Act actually get you — and what does it not get you?

---

## What the Law Is

The First Step Act was enacted December 21, 2018.¹ It has two major components, and they are separate enough that you need to understand each on its own terms.

The first is the retroactive application of the Fair Sentencing Act of 2010. That earlier law reduced the crack-to-powder cocaine sentencing disparity — before it passed, you needed 100 times as much powder cocaine as crack to trigger the same mandatory minimum sentence. The First Step Act made those reductions retroactive, meaning people sentenced before August 3, 2010 for certain crack cocaine offenses can petition their sentencing court for a reduction.² I'll come back to that.

The second component — and the one that affects the largest number of people currently incarcerated in the federal system — is the earned time credit system. This is what most people mean when they say "the First Step Act." This is the part I want to spend real time on, because this is the part that is almost universally misunderstood.

---

## How Earned Time Credits Work

Under 18 U.S.C. § 3624(g), federal inmates can earn time credits by completing two categories of programming: Evidence-Based Recidivism Reduction programs, known as EBRR programs, and Productive Activities, known as PAs.³

The rate is this: for every 30 days of successful participation in EBRR programs or PAs, you earn 10 days of credit. If you are assessed as minimum or low risk of recidivism — the BOP uses a risk assessment tool called PATTERN to make that determination — you can earn 15 days of credit for every 30 days of programming.⁴

That math compounds. If you are consistently participating in eligible programming and you are minimum or low risk, you can accumulate substantial credits over a multi-year sentence.

Good conduct time is separate. Under 18 U.S.C. § 3624(b), eligible inmates can earn up to 54 days per year of the sentence *imposed* — not served — for good behavior.⁵ Good conduct time and earned time credits are calculated independently. They stack, but they are not the same thing.

The BOP is supposed to track your credits in your Individualized Needs Plan — your INP. This is a document your case manager maintains. It should reflect your programming participation and your credit accumulation. In theory, it updates as you complete programs. In practice, you should check it, because errors happen and they do not always get corrected on their own.

---

## What "Early Release" Actually Means

Here is where most people have it wrong.

When people hear "earned time credits," they assume it works like this: you rack up credits, you knock that time off your sentence, you walk out earlier than your release date. Clean and simple.

It is not that clean.

The law distinguishes between two things credits can do, and they do not do the same thing.

The **first year** of accumulated credits — once you have earned at least 365 days — is applied to move your release date forward and place you directly onto **supervised release**. You do not go to a halfway house first. You go out on supervised release, and your supervised release term is extended by that period. This is a real reduction. Getting out a year early matters. But understand what it is: you are moving onto supervised release, not into freedom. The conditions of your supervision apply from day one.⁶

When you earn **more than one year** of credits, those additional credits become eligible for a different use: placement in a **Residential Reentry Center** — a halfway house — or **home confinement**, earlier than you would have otherwise qualified for. This is genuinely worth something. A halfway house is not prison. Home confinement is not prison. Getting there months earlier than your scheduled date is a real and meaningful thing. Families who have been waiting years need to understand this is achievable.

But here is the part that does not get said plainly enough.

---

## This Is Functionally Parole Without the Protections of Parole

Federal parole was abolished for offenses committed after November 1, 1987.⁷ There is no federal parole board. There is no structured release mechanism with years of institutional knowledge and, at minimum, a formal revocation process with a hearing before a neutral body.

What we have instead — when you are released early on supervised release through accumulated FSA credits, or placed in a halfway house or home confinement — is something that functions like parole in every practical sense. You have conditions. You have a supervision officer. You can be sent back.

But you do not have the protections that even the old parole system provided.

IF YOUR SUPERVISED RELEASE IS REVOKED FOR A VIOLATION — ANY VIOLATION, INCLUDING TECHNICAL VIOLATIONS THAT ARE NOT NEW CRIMES — YOU CAN BE SENT DIRECTLY BACK TO A FEDERAL FACILITY. NO JUDGE REQUIRED. NO HEARING BEFORE REVOCATION.

I want to be precise here because the revocation process for supervised release does technically involve a court — a revocation hearing can be requested.⁸ But the practical reality I have watched play out is this: people who earned their way out under the First Step Act, who came home after years in a federal facility, who violated a condition — sometimes a minor one — and found themselves back inside. Not always because a judge reviewed the facts and decided that was the appropriate outcome. Because the system moved them back before anyone pushed back, and they accepted it.

I have seen multiple people brought back on FSA release violations who simply gave up. They stopped fighting. They accepted reincarceration as if there were nothing to do about it. Let me be honest with you: that is bullshit. Someone who earned their way out through years of programming and good behavior, who came home, who was trying — losing all of that without any meaningful judicial review of whether the revocation was even appropriate? That should not be accepted. Not by them. Not by you.

My position, stated plainly: a judge should be required to weigh in before someone who earned their way out under the First Step Act is sent back. The current mechanism does not guarantee that in any meaningful practical sense. That is a problem. It is the kind of problem that gets litigated, and it should be. If you or someone you know is facing revocation of FSA-credited release, do not accept it passively. That is a legal question worth examining, and there are lawyers who fight it.

## Who Is and Is Not Eligible

Not every person in federal custody can earn First Step Act time credits. Congress excluded specific offense categories from eligibility.

The statutory exclusions under 18 U.S.C. § 3632(d)(4)(D) include, among others: terrorism-related offenses, sex offenses requiring registration under the Sex Offender Registration and Notification Act, certain high-level drug trafficking offenses (including offenses where the defendant was found to have been a leader, organizer, manager, or supervisor), and certain firearms offenses.⁹

The BOP determines eligibility based on the offense of conviction. If the BOP has told you or your family member that they are ineligible, and you believe that determination is wrong — whether because the offense categorization is incorrect or because the exclusion is being applied too broadly — that is a legal question worth examining. Eligibility disputes have been litigated in federal court through 28 U.S.C. § 2241 habeas petitions filed in the district of confinement.¹⁰

This is worth knowing: the charge of conviction matters, not what you were accused of, not what the government would have preferred to prove. If your plea resulted in conviction for a charge that is *eligible* for FSA credits, that eligibility does not evaporate because the underlying conduct was more serious. I have seen plea negotiations where this distinction mattered enormously. In *Surviving Pretrial*, I walk through the mechanics of how charge selection in a plea agreement can meaningfully affect time credit eligibility — and by extension, years of your life.

## The Retroactive Fair Sentencing Act Provision

If you are serving a sentence for a crack cocaine offense that was **imposed before August 3, 2010**, the First Step Act may give you a path to petition the sentencing court for a sentence reduction.

This operates under Section 404 of the First Step Act in conjunction with 18 U.S.C. § 3582(c)(1)(B).¹¹ It is not automatic. You must file a motion with the court that sentenced you. The court has discretion — it can grant or deny. But for people who were sentenced under the old crack-to-powder disparity, when crack cocaine sentences were dramatically harsher than comparable powder sentences, this is a real mechanism for sentence relief.

The filing is made in the sentencing district. If you are close to completing your sentence, speed matters — a motion filed after you are released may be dismissed as moot. If this applies to you or to someone you love, move on it. Talk to a lawyer who understands post-conviction practice. This is exactly the kind of claim that the *2255 Motion Handbook* addresses in terms of the procedural framework for getting back before a sentencing court.

## Compassionate Release: A Brief Note

Separate from time credits entirely, the First Step Act also dramatically expanded the compassionate release statute: 18 U.S.C. § 3582(c)(1)(A).¹²

Before 2018, a defendant who wanted to seek compassionate release had to convince the BOP to file the motion on their behalf. The BOP rarely did it. The First Step Act changed that — it now allows inmates to file the motion directly with the sentencing court after exhausting BOP administrative remedies. Courts have used this expanded provision to grant relief based on serious medical conditions, age and deterioration, severe family circumstances, and in some cases based on the harshness of conditions of confinement.

Compassionate release is not the same as earned time credits. They operate under different statutes, through different processes, and based on different criteria. But for people who have not explored whether they have an extraordinary and compelling circumstance that a court might recognize, it is worth a hard look. The 2255 Motion Handbook addresses the procedural mechanics of these filings and what courts have actually been willing to find extraordinary and compelling in the years since the First Step Act passed.

## What to Do Right Now

If you are eligible for First Step Act time credits, or if you are uncertain whether you are eligible, here is where to start.

**Request your Individualized Needs Plan.** The INP is the BOP document that tracks your programming participation, your risk assessment, and your time credit accumulation. Ask your case manager for a current copy. Review it carefully. If credits appear to be miscalculated, undercounted, or missing entirely, document the discrepancy.

**Use the administrative remedy process.** If your credits are wrong, you have to pursue the BOP's internal grievance system before you can take the dispute to a federal court. That process runs: BP-8 (informal resolution at the unit level), then BP-9 (to the Warden), then BP-10 (to the Regional Office), then BP-11 (to the Central Office in Washington).¹³ Every step matters. Every step has a deadline. Missing a deadline or skipping a step can cost you the ability to bring the claim in court at all.

WARNING: DO NOT SKIP STEPS IN THE ADMINISTRATIVE REMEDY PROCESS. COURTS REQUIRE EXHAUSTION. IF YOU FILE A 2241 PETITION WITHOUT COMPLETING BP-8 THROUGH BP-11, THE COURT WILL DISMISS YOUR PETITION AND YOU WILL HAVE TO START OVER.

Keep copies of everything. Every BP form you submit, every response you receive, every date. The record is your ammunition if this ends up in court.

**Families on the outside: you can help.** Research the specific EBRR programs and Productive Activities offered at the facility. Confirm the person is enrolled in eligible programming — not just any class, but programming the BOP has approved for credit. Facilities vary in what they offer. If a facility has limited programming, that is worth knowing and potentially worth raising through counsel.

**If the BOP says you are ineligible and you think they are wrong:** get a lawyer. This is litigable. Courts have reviewed BOP eligibility determinations in habeas proceedings, and the BOP does not always get it right.

---

## The Bigger Picture

The First Step Act is real. The credits are real. Getting out of a halfway house earlier than you otherwise would — that is real and it matters. For people who have been sitting in a cell for years watching a release date that felt permanently distant, earning your way to an earlier placement is meaningful. Do not let anyone tell you it is not worth pursuing.

But go in with your eyes open.

The law was written to create incentives, and it does that. What it did not build — what it still does not have — is robust judicial protection for the people who earn their way out and then face being sent back in. That gap exists. People are falling into it. And the ones who accept it without a fight are the ones who end up losing what they earned.

You worked for those credits. If someone tries to take them back from you, or if the system tried to erase them or miscalculate them or declare you ineligible when you are not — fight it. Document it. Exhaust your remedies. Get a lawyer who knows this area of law. And do not accept it passively.
