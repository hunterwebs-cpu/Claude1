# The First Step Act: What It Actually Gets You

**By Bilal Khan**

---

In December 2018, Congress passed the most significant federal criminal justice reform in a generation. The First Step Act became law. The Bureau of Prisons was required to implement it. Inmates were going to be able to earn time off their sentences by participating in programs. Everyone celebrated.

Then the BOP had to actually figure out how to do it.

I was inside when implementation began — only six years after it passed — and it was a disaster. Even though I didn't personally qualify for the time off, I spent significant time sitting with BOP case managers, walking them through the calculations, correcting the math. Their initial guidance was wrong. The system has since been centralized — case managers have less direct role in calculating your credits now, it flows through a national system — but the confusion that surrounded the rollout never fully went away. And the confusion that lives in people's minds about what the law actually does is, in many ways, worse.

Most people who are eligible for First Step Act credits know they are earning them. Very few understand what those credits actually buy. The difference between what people think is happening and what is actually happening is significant enough to reshape your entire strategy — and to determine whether you survive what comes after release.

So what does the First Step Act actually get you — and what does it not get you?

---

## What the Law Is

The First Step Act — pushed into law by President Trump during his first term — was enacted December 21, 2018.¹ It has two major components that directly affect those doing time, and they are separate enough that you need to understand each on its own terms.

The first is the retroactive application of the Fair Sentencing Act of 2010. That earlier law reduced the crack-to-powder cocaine sentencing disparity. The First Step Act made those reductions retroactive for certain crack cocaine offenses sentenced before August 3, 2010.² At this point — more than eight years after passage — nearly everyone who would have qualified for that retroactive relief has already pursued it. If it applies to you or someone you know and it hasn't been addressed, move on it now.

The second component — and the one that affects the largest number of people currently incarcerated in the federal system — is the earned time credit system. This is what most people mean when they say "the First Step Act." This is the part I want to spend real time on, because this is the part that is almost universally misunderstood.

---

## How Earned Time Credits Work

Under 18 U.S.C. § 3624(g), federal inmates can earn time credits by completing two categories of programming: Evidence-Based Recidivism Reduction programs, known as EBRR programs, and Productive Activities, known as PAs.³

The rate works like this: for every 30 days of successful participation in EBRR programs or PAs, you earn 10 days of credit. If you are assessed as minimum or low risk of recidivism, you can earn 15 days of credit for every 30 days of programming — but only after you have been through your Program Review ("team") at least twice at that low or minimum recidivism risk level.⁴

The BOP uses a risk assessment tool called PATTERN to make that recidivism determination. Here is something almost nobody gets told: **your PATTERN score is not the same as your security classification score.** They are two separate systems, scored differently, measuring different things. Someone who scores high risk on the PATTERN recidivism tool can be housed in a camp or a low-security facility. Someone with a high security designation will generally be in a USP or medium. These are parallel tracks. Knowing your PATTERN score matters for your time credit earning rate. Knowing your security designation matters for where you are housed. Do not confuse them.

That math compounds. If you are consistently participating in eligible programming and you are minimum or low PATTERN risk, you can accumulate substantial credits over a multi-year sentence.

**Good conduct time is separate — and nearly universal.** Under 18 U.S.C. § 3624(b), every federal inmate not serving a life sentence can earn up to 54 days per year of the sentence *imposed* — not served — for good behavior.⁵ The First Step Act actually changed this: it increased the effective annual good conduct time from roughly 47 days per year to 54 days per year across the board. Good conduct time and First Step Act earned time credits are calculated independently. They stack, but they are not the same thing.

Here is what often goes unspoken: **68 categories of offenses are excluded from FSA earned time credits.** Good conduct time, on the other hand, applies to virtually everyone not serving a life sentence, regardless of the offense. If your family member has been told they cannot earn any time credits, confirm whether that statement refers to FSA credits specifically, or whether it is also meant to include good conduct time. These are different programs. That distinction matters.

The BOP is supposed to track your FSA credits in your Individualized Needs Plan — your INP. This is a document your case manager maintains and is required to give you at each of your team meetings. You may need to remind them to provide a copy. Your INP should reflect your programming participation, your credit accumulation, and any inoperative time — periods such as time spent in the SHU that do not count toward credit accrual.

If you are just arriving at a facility after sentencing, the BOP typically takes approximately 90 days to develop these documents. Your case manager may not have them available at your first meeting — that first meeting generally happens within your first month, and the documents follow later. Once they exist, check them at every team. Errors happen and they do not correct themselves.

**What to request:** Ask your case manager to print your complete team packet. That packet should include all of the following:

- Individualized Needs Plan — Program Review (your INP)
- FSA Recidivism Risk Assessment (your PATTERN score)
- FSA Needs Assessment
- Sentence Monitoring Computation Data (your sentence computation)
- Custody Classification Form (your security designation)

Review each one carefully.

## What "Early Release" Actually Means

Here is where most people have it wrong.

When people hear "earned time credits," they assume it works like this: you rack up credits, you knock that time off your sentence, you walk out earlier than your release date. Clean and simple.

It is not that clean.

The law distinguishes between two things credits can do, and they do not do the same thing.

The **first year** of accumulated credits — once you have earned at least 365 days — can be applied to move your release date forward and place you directly onto **supervised release**, bypassing the halfway house entirely.⁶ You go out on supervised release, and your supervised release term is extended by that period. This is a real reduction. Getting out a year early matters.

**But here is the critical point almost nobody explains:** this path only exists if the sentencing judge imposed a term of supervised release. Recent updates to the Sentencing Guidelines have pushed judges toward not imposing supervised release in certain cases. If your judge did not impose a supervised release term — or if supervised release simply does not apply to your sentence — **those credits do not give you an early exit onto supervision. Instead, all of your earned credits go toward prerelease custody only** — meaning earlier placement in a halfway house or home confinement, not early release from custody.

When you earn **more than one year** of credits, those additional credits become eligible for placement in a **Residential Reentry Center** — a halfway house — or **home confinement**, earlier than you would have otherwise qualified for. This is genuinely worth something. A halfway house is not prison. Home confinement is not prison. Getting there months, or even years, earlier than your scheduled date is a real and meaningful thing. Families who have been waiting need to understand this is achievable.

But here is the part that does not get said plainly enough.

---

## Prerelease Custody: This Is Functionally Parole Without the Protections

Federal parole was abolished for offenses committed after November 1, 1987.⁷ There is no federal parole board. What replaced it — for people in prerelease custody under the First Step Act — is something that functions like parole in every practical sense, except it does not come with parole's protections.

The two FSA release paths are fundamentally different when something goes wrong, and you need to understand that difference before you get there.

**If your credits move you to early supervised release:** that is actual supervised release. If the BOP revokes your supervised release for a violation, that process goes before a federal judge. The government must prove the violation by a preponderance of the evidence. You have procedural rights under Fed. R. Crim. P. 32.1, including the right to a hearing.⁸ There is meaningful judicial oversight. That is a real protection.

**If your credits move you to prerelease custody** — a halfway house or home confinement — **the situation is entirely different.** You have conditions. You have supervision in some form, whether that is a BOP case manager, a halfway house employee, or a federal probation officer depending on the arrangement. And you can be sent back to prison for any violation of the rules.

**IF YOUR FSA PRERELEASE CUSTODY IS REVOKED FOR A VIOLATION — ANY VIOLATION, INCLUDING TECHNICAL VIOLATIONS THAT ARE NOT NEW CRIMES — YOU CAN BE SENT DIRECTLY BACK TO A FEDERAL PRISON. NO JUDGE REQUIRED. NO HEARING BEFORE REVOCATION. AT THAT POINT, IT IS ENTIRELY AT THE DISCRETION OF THE BOP.**

Do not confuse this with supervised release revocation. SR revocation is a very different process with very different protections. This is prerelease custody revocation — and it is handled differently.

I have watched people who earned their way out under the First Step Act come home after years in a federal facility, violate a condition — sometimes a minor one — and find themselves back inside. And then they sat there every day, complaining about it, and not doing a single thing to fight it. They just accepted it. Bitched and moaned every day they were back in, but never did anything. That is not an option you should choose.

The only recourse when FSA prerelease custody is revoked is to work the administrative remedy process — BP-8 through BP-11 — and eventually file a habeas action in U.S. District Court under 28 U.S.C. § 2241. Nobody I have personally seen challenged their revocation through that process. They accepted it. While no one challenges this discretionary authority, it remains the reality you may face.

My position, stated plainly: everyone in prerelease custody should be entitled to some form of administrative hearing, a real second chance to prove themselves in the community, and a meaningful determination of whether revocation is warranted before being sent back. Currently, there is no mechanism requiring any level of proof before return, and nothing guarantees that unused credits will be restored. That is a problem. It is the kind of problem that deserves to be litigated — and it will not be until someone who is revoked stands up and demands a full review.

If you or someone you know is facing revocation of FSA prerelease custody, do not accept it passively. Get a lawyer who understands this area of law. I get nothing out of recommending him — no referral fees, no kickbacks, nothing. I recommend him because he fights and he is genuinely excellent at this work. Whoever you choose, choose someone. This is worth fighting.

## Who Is and Is Not Eligible

Not every person in federal custody can earn First Step Act time credits. Congress excluded specific offense categories from eligibility.

The statutory exclusions under 18 U.S.C. § 3632(d)(4)(D) include, among others:⁹

- Terrorism-related offenses
- *Most* sex offenses requiring registration under the Sex Offender Registration and Notification Act — but note that coercion, enticement, and transportation offenses under Chapter 117 of Title 18 remain fully eligible
- Certain drug trafficking offenses, including offenses where the defendant was found to be a leader, organizer, manager, or supervisor; offenses involving fentanyl; and offenses where a death was associated with the drug charge
- Certain firearms offenses — 18 U.S.C. § 924(c) is excluded, but § 924(j) and § 922(g) are not
- Federal reentry offenses, and many others

The BOP determines eligibility based primarily on the offense of conviction. Drug offenses may also be affected by certain Guidelines applications — such as a leadership role finding. If the BOP has told you or your family member that they are ineligible, and you believe that determination is wrong, that is a legal question worth pursuing through the administrative remedy system. Eligibility disputes have been litigated in federal court through 28 U.S.C. § 2241 habeas petitions, and the BOP does not always get it right.¹⁰

**The charge of conviction matters — not what you were accused of, not what the government would have preferred to prove.** If your plea resulted in conviction on a charge that is eligible for FSA credits, that eligibility does not evaporate because the underlying conduct was more serious. I have seen plea negotiations where this distinction mattered enormously — charge selection in a plea agreement can meaningfully affect time credit eligibility, and by extension, years of someone's life.

**One more thing that is critical and almost never said directly: if you were convicted on multiple charges and any one of them is an ineligible offense, you are not eligible for FSA time credits.** The presence of a single ineligible count renders the entire program unavailable to you. The BOP applies the statute exactly this way. It is not theoretical.

## What to Do Right Now

If you are eligible for First Step Act time credits, or uncertain whether you are, here is where to start.

**Request your complete team packet.** Ask your case manager to print all five documents: your INP — Program Review, your FSA Recidivism Risk Assessment (PATTERN score), your FSA Needs Assessment, your Sentence Monitoring Computation Data, and your Custody Classification Form. If you are newly arrived, expect that these take approximately 90 days to develop from the point of designation. You will see your case manager within the first month — but the documents may not be ready until the follow-up meeting.

Review these documents fully. If credits appear to be miscalculated, undercounted, or missing entirely, it is your responsibility to pursue corrections.

**Use the administrative remedy process.** If your credits are wrong, you have to exhaust the BOP's internal grievance process before you can take the dispute to a federal court. That process runs: BP-8 (informal resolution at the unit level), then BP-9 (to the Warden), then BP-10 (to the Regional Office), then BP-11 (to the Central Office in Washington).¹¹ Every step matters. Every step has a deadline.

**WARNING: DO NOT SKIP STEPS IN THE ADMINISTRATIVE REMEDY PROCESS. COURTS REQUIRE *PROPER* EXHAUSTION. IF YOU FILE A 2241 PETITION WITHOUT COMPLETING BP-8 THROUGH BP-11, THE COURT WILL GENERALLY DISMISS YOUR PETITION AND YOU WILL HAVE TO START OVER.**

Keep copies of everything. Every BP form you receive or submit, every response, every date. The record is your ammunition if this ends up in court.

**If the BOP says you are ineligible and you think they are wrong:** exhaust all administrative remedies first, pointing out precisely why the BOP's determination is wrong at each step. Then get a lawyer. This is litigable. Courts have reviewed BOP eligibility determinations in habeas proceedings across the country, and the BOP does not always get it right. If you need a recommendation, Doug Passon handles this area of law and handles it well.

## The Bigger Picture

The First Step Act is real. The credits are real. Getting to a halfway house or home confinement earlier than you otherwise would — that is real and it matters. For people who have been sitting in a cell for years watching a release date that felt permanently distant, earning your way to an earlier placement is meaningful. Do not let anyone tell you it is not worth pursuing.

But go in with your eyes open.

The law was written to create incentives, and it does that. What it did not build — what it still does not have — is robust judicial protection for the people who earn their way out and then face being sent back in. That gap exists. People are falling into it. And the ones who accept it without a fight are the ones who end up losing what they earned.

You worked for those credits. If someone tries to take them back from you, or if the system tries to erase them or miscalculate them or declare you ineligible when you are not — fight it. Document it. Exhaust your remedies. Get a lawyer who knows this area of law. And do not accept it passively.
