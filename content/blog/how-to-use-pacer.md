> "It is clear that the courts of this country recognize a general right to inspect and copy public records and documents, including judicial records and documents."
>
> — Justice Potter Stewart, *Nixon v. Warner Communications, Inc.*, 435 U.S. 589, 597 (1978) [VERIFY]

---

Right now, somewhere on a federal court server, there is a file. It contains every document ever filed in your case — every motion, every government response, every order the judge has signed, every hearing notice, every plea agreement, every sentencing memorandum. It is indexed, numbered, and time-stamped. It is a public record. Anyone with an internet connection can register to view it for ten cents a page.

Your lawyer has access to it. The prosecutor has access to it. The court clerk maintains it in real time. Journalists, researchers, and curious members of the public can pull it up right now and see exactly where your case stands.

You, sitting in a federal detention center or county jail awaiting your day in court, cannot touch it.

That gap — between the public record that exists and the defendant who can't see it — is one of the more quietly devastating realities in the federal system. It means your lawyer can tell you anything and you have no independent way to check. It means deadlines can pass without your knowledge. It means motions can go unfiled, hearings can get scheduled, and the trajectory of your case can shift in ways you will only find out about when it's too late to do anything.

There is a system that closes that gap. It is called PACER. Your family can use it right now, for almost nothing, to verify everything your lawyer tells you. So how do you get your hands on what's in that system when you're locked away from it — and what do you do once you have it?

---

## What PACER Is

PACER stands for Public Access to Court Electronic Records. It is run by the federal judiciary — not the Department of Justice, not the Bureau of Prisons, not any law enforcement agency. The federal courts themselves maintain it at pacer.gov.

What it contains is simple: virtually every document filed in virtually every federal case in the country. Criminal cases, civil cases, bankruptcy cases. District courts, circuit courts of appeal, the bankruptcy courts. If it was filed in a federal court, it is almost certainly in PACER. If it was filed and *sealed*, it will appear as an entry on the docket — but the document itself won't be readable. More on sealed documents in a moment.

This is a public system. That is not a figure of speech. The Supreme Court has recognized the right of public access to judicial records going back decades.[^1] PACER is the federal judiciary's implementation of that principle in the electronic age. What that means for you is that your case is not a private arrangement between your lawyer and the government. It is a public proceeding. The record of it belongs to the public. And that includes the people who love you.

## The Access Gap — and How to Work Around It

Here is the reality most defendants never figure out: you may not be able to access PACER from inside, but there is still a way to get the information from it. As a rule, jail facilities don't provide internet terminals for defendants to pull up court records. For most inmates, the only connection to what's happening in their case is what their lawyer tells them. Don't let that be you.

Marines have a saying that should always be your guide: "Inspect, Don't Expect." If you just believe your lawyer you are putting an enormous amount of trust there. It is easy for a lawyer to fib when nobody is looking. But if you conduct spot inspections, then the lawyer has nowhere to run. Either they tell you the truth or you will likely fire them.

I know this firsthand. I spent years in federal custody during an active prosecution, moving through facilities from Doña Ana County in New Mexico to FDC Philadelphia to FCI Fort Dix and beyond. The one thing I couldn't do was sit down and look up my own docket — and until I figured out a workaround, my lawyers lied, lied, and lied some more. What to do?

So I wrote letters.

The Clerk of the Court in every court in the country (state, federal, municipal) maintains a "docket." The docket is simply an index of everything filed and scheduled on a case. Thus, if your lawyer says "I am going to file [x]" and there is never an entry on the docket, it probably wasn't filed.

Here is exactly what to do. Write a letter addressed to:

**Clerk of Court**  
**United States District Court**  
**[Name of Your District — e.g., Southern District of New York]**

If you don't know the address, usually you can get it from the jail's law librarian. But if they aren't helpful, you can ask a family member or other supporter to look it up for you. And if you don't have anyone to help you, then you can ask a cellmate to ask their supporters for you (getting an address is very different from disclosing intimate details about your case).

In the letter, keep it simple. All you need to say is: *"I am the defendant in the above-captioned case. I would like to request that a copy of the current docket sheet be mailed to me at the following address."* Then give your name, your case number, and your mailing address at the facility. Nothing more. No legal argument, no explanations, no demands. Just a clear, specific request from a party to the case.

The case number is on every document you've ever received from the court or your lawyer. It looks something like this: 1:23-cr-00456-ABC. That is your case identifier in the federal system. If you don't know your case number, provide the clerk with all case information that you do know (i.e. the judge's name, your lawyer's name, a partial case number, your date of birth with your name, when you last went to court, and even the name of one of your charges). Do not mention anything about the facts of your case or anything you and your lawyer discussed. Just mention data points that will allow the clerk to find your case specifically.

Some clerks will send it immediately. Some will ask you to pay a small copying fee (although this is very rare during an active case). Some will drag their feet. Write the letter again. Keep a copy of everything you send. The point is that this channel exists, it is yours to use, and it does not require your lawyer's cooperation.

That docket sheet — even a single one, even weeks old by the time it reaches you — is intelligence. It tells you what was filed. It tells you when. It tells you what the judge has signed. If your lawyer has been telling you things happened that didn't happen, the docket will tell you.

## How to Get Into PACER

The more powerful channel is the one available to people on the outside. There are two ways you can access court records electronically. The first is to go to the United States District Court clerk's office and use the "public access terminal" which is free for searches and you only pay for printing. The second way is to use the PACER Case Locator web portal.

Anyone can register for a PACER account. The registration itself is free. Step-by-step, here's how you get an account:

Go to pacer.uscourts.gov or do a Google search for "pacer case locator" and click the result. Only click a site that has .gov in the URL. There are sites out there that will provide you with PACER materials for more money than is charged by PACER itself. Don't trust them.

![Google search results for PACER — only use the official pacer.uscourts.gov result](assets/img/pacer/pacer_google_result.jpg)

Next, click on Menu — the blue box in the upper right corner of the page. The dropdown has "Registration Wizard." Click that.

![PACER homepage menu dropdown showing the Registration Wizard option](assets/img/pacer/pacer_menu_options.jpg)

In the registration screen, fill out all information asked of you. You will be asked for a credit or debit card number. You don't have to provide one if you do not want to, but you will not be provided instant access. Rather, PACER administrators will send mail to the address you listed with specific instructions on how to proceed without a card — this is basically an address verification to reduce fraud. If you put in a credit or debit card you will NOT be charged. PACER only charges your card after you give express authorization to charge your card, which only is necessary after a bill issues.

![PACER Registration Wizard — fill out all fields to create your free account](assets/img/pacer/pacer_registration_wizard.jpg)

After you complete all the steps, you are ready to start using PACER. I will explain exactly how to use the system below. Before I get to that, let me explain PACER's fee structure because it is complicated (of course it is, the government made it!).

## Fees

The web version does have a fee structure that will charge you for searches and document retrieval. Here is the cost structure, and you need to understand it exactly so you don't accidentally waste your money.

PACER generally charges ten cents per page, up to a $3 maximum (except for transcripts) to view or download a document. That includes the search results page itself — so even running a search costs money even if it doesn't return results (a no-result is considered one page). The ten-cent charge applies to every page you load, whether it's a single-page order or a hundred-page suppression motion. If you load three documents totaling 100+ pages each, you will be charged $9. But if you download 10 documents that total 50 pages each you will be charged $15. When it comes to transcripts, it is $0.10 per page for every page regardless of length, so a 200-page transcript will cost $20 as opposed to $3 like other long documents. When you run searches for case files, you will not be asked if you accept the charges. The moment you click submit, you will be charged $0.10 per page up to $3 for that. For case documents, you will be asked if you want to view or download the document and the screen will show you the cost that will be charged.

![PACER fee confirmation screen — the system shows you the exact cost before you download any document](assets/img/pacer/pacer_run_report.jpg)

There is some good news: if your total charges in a given quarter (three-month billing period — Jan–Mar, Apr–Jun, Jul–Sep, Oct–Dec) are thirty dollars or less, those charges are waived entirely. That means your bill will be zero. For anyone tracking one case and being thoughtful about what they pull up, it is entirely realistic to stay under thirty dollars a quarter. So long as you are only looking at one case, most months you won't reach that threshold.[^2]

The billing is quarterly. You register your payment method when you create the account, and PACER bills you at the end of each quarter — but only if you exceeded the thirty-dollar threshold. It does not charge your card automatically. When you are billed, you have to log in to your account and specifically click to pay. PACER does allow you to also pay by check and money order and will provide such directions on the bill they send you.

## Effective Searching

Searching on PACER is a little nuanced with a learning curve. The system was designed mostly by and for legal professionals so the fields are built on what they are used to dealing with. With these instructions, it shouldn't take you too long to learn how to be a PACER master.

PACER has a tool called the PACER Case Locator. The PCL is the easiest way to find cases. It searches across all federal courts nationwide based on the criteria a user inputs. Unfortunately, the basic search will return thousands of results when you search for relatively common names. The way to keep the cost down is to use the Advanced Search tool — the last thing we want is to generate PACER bills for fruitless searches. Here's the step-by-step process I use for finding particular cases.

When you first enter the PACER Case Locator page it will have a simple screen that has a list of Quick Search links. You want to click "Find Parties (Advanced)."

![PACER Quick Search screen — click Find Parties (Advanced) to get the full filter options](assets/img/pacer/pacer_quick_search_screen.jpg)

The Advanced Party Search screen has a whole list of variables for you to select.

![PACER Advanced Party Search form — use these filters to narrow your search by district, case type, and date range](assets/img/pacer/pacer_advanced_party_search.jpg)

The first section is where you are going to put the defendant's name. In the first line you will put their last name (if it is a corporation or other entity, that is the name you put). The search field is not case sensitive.

Just below the last name you put the first name. You can also enter a middle name but I don't usually do it because the court clerk may not have recorded it. If multiple defendants with the same name pop up and any of them have middle initials, it may help you to isolate the one you are looking for, but I generally don't need to rely on it.

Clicking "Exact Matches Only" is a personal preference but is not necessary. The system is only going to provide results for your spelling anyway. If your spelling is wrong, it isn't going to try and figure it out.

The next major section is for case information which, at the beginning, has a dropdown for "Court Type." The list offers:

- Appellate
- Bankruptcy
- Civil
- Criminal
- Multidistrict

As we are dealing with the pretrial phase of a criminal proceeding, you want to click "Criminal."

The next block, immediately below the court type is "Case" and has the fields "Number," "Title," and "Type." Leave all three of these blank.

Next is "Case Status." Generally, the case you are looking up will be "Open" so you can isolate your search to only open cases. In my profession I leave it at "All" because there could be oddities in the clerk's office that produced case numbers that have been dismissed or converted. For instance, if the defendant in the case you are looking up has just been arrested but no indictment has been issued, they will have a case number with the letters "MJ" in it but once they are indicted it will be converted to "CR." The MJ case number still exists but will be set to closed while the CR number is open. In my own case there would be two MJ numbers and one CR number because the government was playing fast and loose with speedy trial rights.

Then you have "Region" — a dropdown of all federal circuits and districts in the country. Generally, the district is named after the state. For instance you have the District of Massachusetts, the Northern District of California, and the Eastern District of Virginia to name but three of the 98 federal districts. Your goal is to isolate your search to, at maximum, one specific state where the case is. If you know the precise district, like the Southern District of New York, you can drill your search even more specifically. Most people don't know the precise district but know what state the defendant is going to court in. So click the dropdown and select an appropriate value.

The last two search criteria available to you are the dates the case was filed and closed, and you can define how you want your list sorted. I will put in a date range of three months before I know the case started to the present date. But if I don't know within a reasonable degree, I will leave it blank. I don't ever use the sort function, but if you like organizing your lists, go for it. Hopefully though you get a list of no more than 20 names. Given that we are isolating a specific case based on the region, you shouldn't see too many options.

Below is a screenshot of the search results for a search I ran for Brian Walker — a completely random name — without isolating to any state or district.

![PACER nationwide search for Brian Walker returns 142 results — expensive and useless without a region filter](assets/img/pacer/pacer_142.jpg)

![Same search restricted to Florida returns 2 results — both case numbers belong to the same person: one MJ, one CR](assets/img/pacer/pacer_2.jpg)

As you can see, a national unrestricted search returned 142 results. But when I restricted the search to Florida, it provided two case numbers which were incidentally for the same person — an MJ case number and a CR case number. If there is a CR case number for your result, that's the one you want to click. If only the MJ number is available, that is the one you want to click.

In the case of Mr. Walker, I clicked the CR case number and a new screen appears. That screen provides a number of options. You want to select: Docket Report. As you click it, yet another screen will appear where you can isolate the entries you want to see. For instance, let's say you got the full docket three months ago and you only want to see the entries since that date to save money. When dockets get very long — and they can get extremely long — this can save a tremendous amount of money over the long run. On the flip side, you may not remember where your printout is and may want the whole thing again. It's up to you. Before this moment, I have never seen the Brian Walker docket, so I am just going to click ok. I leave the page output to HTML because that page can be printed to PDF anyway and it allows me to navigate through documents. That will prevent you from being double charged. In any event, now I click "Run Report" and the docket sheet immediately appears.

![PACER docket report options — select a date range to pull only new entries and keep costs down on long cases](assets/img/pacer/pacer_docket_selector.jpg)

Now I'm not in the business of putting people's information out there and it won't be done today, so I have blacked out Mr. Walker's specific case information such as his charges and sentence. But this should also tell you just how easy it is to look up literally anyone in the system.

![A real federal docket sheet — case details redacted. Every filing is numbered, dated, and described. This is the record your lawyer works from.](assets/img/pacer/pacer_docket_sheet.jpg)

Once you find the case, bookmark it. The case number is unique. From that point forward, you can go directly to the case docket with no searching required — and no search charges.

## How to Read and Utilize a Federal Docket Sheet

The docket sheet provides several pieces of information. First, it tells you the case number, the defendants' names, the attorneys on the case including the U.S. Attorneys assigned, the current charges filed and, if the case is completed, the sentence imposed. Second, it generally contains a numbered list of every entry in the case, usually in date-chronological order. Every item has three basic components: a number, a date, and a description. Here is what each means.

The **docket entry number** is simply a counter. Entry 1 is the first thing filed. Entry 47 is the forty-seventh. Cases that have been going on for years may have hundreds of entries. That counter tells you two things: how active the case has been, and whether anything was filed between the last docket you reviewed and this one.

The **date** is the date the document was docketed — meaning the date the court received and entered it into the system. In most cases, that is the same day it was filed. In electronic filing, it is often the same minute.

The **description** tells you what was filed. "Indictment" is the charging document. "Order Setting Conditions of Release" means bail was set. "Motion to Suppress" means a motion challenging evidence was filed. "Sentencing Memorandum — Defendant" means your lawyer submitted an argument to the judge before sentencing.

You do not need to read every document to monitor a case effectively. What you are looking for, at minimum, is whether the things your lawyer says are happening are actually appearing on that docket.

Here are the entries worth paying particular attention to:

The **criminal complaint** or **indictment** is entry 1 in most criminal cases, or close to it. The underlying document will tell you exactly what charges were brought and how many counts. If you never received this document from your lawyer, you need to demand they give them to you. If your lawyer refuses to provide it to you through legal mail channels, then you can have your loved ones send it to you, but only as a last resort. While the document is legally public, the jail staff don't usually know what's in it and if it comes through regular mail they are going to read it. When you do get it, read it carefully. If there is a superseding (meaning a newer) indictment later in the docket — an amended or expanded charging document — that is the current charging document; all others before it mean absolutely nothing.

The **initial appearance** entry records when the defendant first appeared before a magistrate judge. That hearing is where you were told the charges, where the question of detention was first raised, and where the clock on certain deadlines began.

The **detention order or release order** tells you the official ruling on whether the defendant stays in custody pending trial. If there was a detention hearing, those proceedings are usually documented here as well.

The **scheduling order** sets the deadlines for the entire case — when discovery closes, when motions are due, when the trial is scheduled. Every date in that order is a real deadline with real consequences for missing it.

**Discovery motions and responses** tell you whether your lawyer is actually litigating the case. A defense that files nothing during the discovery phase is a defense that is either planning to plead guilty or planning to fail at trial. Either way, you want to know which.

The **PSR** — the Presentence Report — will appear on the docket after conviction and before sentencing. It is NOT publicly accessible but it may appear as an entry. The date it was filed matters because there are deadlines for objecting to it. If it appears on the docket and your lawyer hasn't told you about it, that is a serious problem. *(The PSR is one of the most consequential documents in your entire case. The full breakdown — what it contains, why it matters, and exactly what to object to — is covered in depth in* Surviving Pretrial.*)* 

The **sentencing hearing date** will typically be set in a scheduling order or a minute entry. Know this date. Your family should know this date. It is one of the hardest deadlines in the federal system to reschedule once it is set. It is usually issued as a "Text Order" meaning there is no underlying document — the relevant record is the docket entry itself.

If you see docket entry numbers that skip — a jump from 34 to 38, for example — that means entries 35, 36, and 37 MAY exist but are under seal. (I have had it happen that the clerk misnumbered a document and accidentally skipped a number, but it is pretty rare.) They are not visible to the public and may not be visible to you on PACER. If it is visible on PACER but is under seal, when you click the document number a screen will say "You are not authorized to view this document."

Being under seal is not necessarily a problem. Certain government filings are routinely sealed: cooperator agreements, sensitive law enforcement material, and presentence reports are always under seal and unsealing them is almost impossible. But most documents will be filed publicly, as you have a Constitutional right to a public court proceeding. If you're seeing large clusters of sealed entries that your lawyer has not explained to you, ask about them directly — there could be things being filed that you have problems with.

## The Moment That Mattered

I want to tell you exactly why all this matters, because abstract advice about record-keeping doesn't land the same way a specific story does.

At a certain point in my case, my lawyers told me they had filed a particular document — a lot of particular documents. I thought I had no way to verify that from inside. They were my lawyers. I had to take them at their word and I believed them when they said they did it.

I eventually found out that writing the court is an effective way to get information on my case so I did. I now have the address for my courthouse memorized because I have asked for docket sheets in my own case many many times. As it happened back then, I got a copy of my docket sheet and it revealed some giant issues.

That document along with others were not on it.

My lawyers were lying through their teeth about what they had filed on my behalf. That discrepancy — between what they told me and what the public record showed — was one of the clearest signals I ever got that my legal representation was not working in my interest. I eventually fired them. But the damage was done. I found out they lied about a lot of things, and one of their lies convinced me to eventually plead guilty to a 15-year minimum charge that I insisted I was innocent on. Such is life for me, but hopefully you don't make the same mistake as I did: Inspect your Lawyer's Work.

Now, here is the caveat I want you to hold onto alongside that story: sealed documents sometimes do not appear on the docket even when they were legitimately filed. A document filed under seal may show up as a sealed entry, or in some circumstances, may not appear at all depending on the court's procedure. So the absence of something on the docket is not always proof that it was not filed. It is, however, a discrepancy that demands an explanation. You are entitled to ask your lawyer: show me the filing receipt. Show me the CM/ECF confirmation. If they filed it, that record exists. If they cannot produce it, that tells you something.

IF YOUR LAWYER SAYS SOMETHING WAS FILED AND IT IS NOT ON THE DOCKET, YOU HAVE FOUND A DISCREPANCY THAT DEMANDS AN IMMEDIATE EXPLANATION. Get that explanation in writing.

## Using PACER as an Accountability Tool

The practical use of this system is not complicated. A family member sets up the account. They check the docket once a week — or after any conversation with the lawyer in which the lawyer claims to have done something. They print the relevant pages and mail them to the defendant.

The defendant, reading that docket in their cell, can now compare it against what the lawyer has been saying.

This works with a bad lawyer — obviously. A lawyer who is lying to you, charging you for work they aren't doing, or simply coasting on a passive defense will be exposed by their own absence from the docket. The filings aren't there. The motions aren't there. The activity that should be there, isn't.

But it also works with a good lawyer. Even a competent attorney can drop a ball. Deadlines get missed, documents get misfiled, hearings get rescheduled without adequate notice to the client. A family monitoring the docket is a check on honest human error, not just deliberate misconduct. You are not accusing your lawyer of anything by monitoring your case. You are participating in your own defense.

I use PACER now professionally. Every case I work on, every client I advise, I am in PACER tracking what's happening in real time. It is the single most useful publicly accessible tool in the federal system, full stop. The entire official record of a federal case — the charges, the filings, the rulings, the schedule — is right there for ten cents a page. There is no excuse for not using it.

## Putting the Printed Docket to Work

When the docket sheet arrives in the mail, here is how to use it.

First, look at the most recent entries. Compare the date on the docket against the date of the last conversation you had with your lawyer. If your lawyer told you on a certain date that they filed a motion, look at the entries on that date and the few days around it. Is it there?

Second, look at the entry numbers. Are they sequential, or are there gaps? Make a note of any gaps.

Third, look at the scheduling order entries, if you haven't already seen them. Write down every deadline date. If the trial date has moved or a hearing has been added, you need to know that.

Fourth, if a document is available — meaning it was filed publicly and not under seal — your family can download it directly through PACER and mail you a copy. The charging indictment. A motion the government filed. The judge's order on a contested issue. These are documents your lawyer may or may not have shared with you in full. The public record version is the authoritative one.

Communicate back to your family with specific questions. Not just "what's new" — but "was there an entry on or around [date] for a suppression motion?" Give them something concrete to look for, and they will be much more effective than if they're browsing without direction.

## What This Gives You

The federal system is designed around the assumption that defendants are passive. That they will wait for their lawyer to bring information, accept what they're told, and surface at sentencing with whatever outcome their attorney negotiated without their meaningful input.

That assumption is wrong. And the people who get the best outcomes are, without exception, the ones who refused to be passive — who monitored their own cases, who demanded documentation, who asked hard questions backed by specific knowledge.

PACER is one of the sharpest tools for that. It is not glamorous. It is a government database of court filings, and reading docket entries is not anyone's idea of a good afternoon. But the information is there, it is current, it is accurate, and it belongs to you. Your family can have it in their hands by tomorrow.

Do not leave the record of your own case to someone else to interpret for you.

---

## How to Get Started — Summary

If you are inside and have no family support right now, write the letter. Address it to the Clerk of Court for your district. Give your case number and your facility address. Request a copy of the docket sheet. That is all.

If you have a family member on the outside: direct them to pacer.gov. Register a free account. Use a credit or debit card that has a low balance or set a spending alert — the costs are manageable, but it is good practice. Search within your specific district rather than running a nationwide search. Find the case, bookmark it, and check it regularly. Print the docket and the documents that matter. Mail them.

And when something on the docket doesn't match what your lawyer told you: ask. Put the question in writing. Keep a copy of what you sent and what you received back.

That discrepancy — if it exists — is information you need. The docket sheet is how you find it.
