# Your Rights in Pretrial Federal Detention

> "The presumption of innocence, although not articulated in the Constitution, is a basic component of a fair trial under our system of criminal justice. Yet the presumption, like most legal principles, must be balanced against other considerations. It has never meant that a person accused of crime cannot be confined pending trial."

---

While you are in custody during an active federal prosecution, you are in a building run by someone who answers to someone else, who answers to the U.S. Marshal Service, who answers to the Attorney General of the United States. In most cases, you are not sitting in a federal detention center. You are sitting in a county jail — Doña Ana County, Torrance County, Otero County, Lower Buckeye, some other place with a contract and a bed count — facilities the federal government rents on your behalf for a fee per day. The people running those buildings have their own administrators, their own rules, their own budgets, and their own institutional indifference. And now you're inside one of them, waiting on a case that could take years to resolve.

Most people sitting in those facilities believe the same thing: that they have no rights. That the guards can do whatever they want. That the food is whatever it is, the medical care is whatever it is, and there is nothing to be done about any of it except wait.

That is wrong. Dangerously wrong.

So what does the government actually owe you while you're sitting in that building — and how do you make them deliver it?

---

## The Constitutional Foundation: You Have Not Been Convicted

This is not a philosophical point. It is a legal one with teeth.

The Eighth Amendment — the one that prohibits cruel and unusual punishment — applies to people who have been convicted of a crime. You have not been convicted. The government cannot punish you under the Eighth Amendment because there is no punishment yet. You are, legally speaking, innocent.

What governs your treatment is the Fifth Amendment's due process clause. The Supreme Court made this explicit in *Bell v. Wolfish*: pretrial detainees are protected against conditions that amount to punishment. The government can detain you pending trial. It can restrict your movement, monitor your communications, and subject you to the institutional reality of incarceration. What it cannot do is punish you. Because punishment requires a conviction first, and you don't have one.[^1]

That distinction — Eighth Amendment for convicts, Fifth Amendment due process for pretrial detainees — is not academic. It is the legal foundation for every claim you might ever make about the conditions inside that facility. Know it cold.

The practical standard the courts apply: conditions of confinement are unconstitutional if they amount to punishment, or if they are imposed for a punitive purpose, or if they are excessive in relation to any legitimate governmental interest. That gives you a real legal line. It is not a comfortable line — the government has a lot of room before it crosses it — but it exists, it is enforceable, and courts have found it violated.

I spent years inside facilities in New Mexico, Arizona, Oklahoma, Pennsylvania, and Texas. I know what the inside of that standard looks like from the living side of it. Most people don't fight because they don't know they can. The ones who know they can often don't know how. This article is the how.

---

## What They Are Required to Give You

There is a floor. It is higher than most people realize.

**Food.** The facility is required to provide you with food that meets basic nutritional standards. Not good food. Not food you'll enjoy. Not food that reflects any meaningful understanding of what human beings need to function. But food that clears the constitutional minimum — adequate calories, nutritional sufficiency, safe preparation.[^2] When that standard isn't being met, it is documented and it is actionable.

**Medical care.** You have a constitutional right to medical care for serious medical needs. A serious medical need is one that has been diagnosed by a physician as requiring treatment, or one that even a layperson would recognize as needing a doctor's attention — untreated infections, unmanaged chronic conditions, emergencies, dental conditions causing serious pain.[^3] The keyword is "serious." Not every ailment rises to the constitutional threshold. But if you have a documented condition — diabetes, heart disease, seizure disorders, HIV, mental illness — and they are not managing it, that is not just bad medical care. That is a constitutional violation.

**Safe housing.** The government must protect you from known dangers. If a facility knows you face a serious threat — a rival, a documented enemy, an officer who has made credible threats — and does nothing, that is deliberately indifferent to your safety, and that matters legally.[^4] This applies to excessive violence in the facility, known gang conflicts, and threats staff were made aware of.

**Sanitation and hygiene.** You are entitled to basic sanitation. Functioning toilets, access to showers, hygiene supplies. Again — not comfort. Not privacy. Not anything that looks like the standard any free person would recognize. But the constitutional floor is there.

**Access to your attorney.** This one is absolute. You have a Sixth Amendment right to counsel, and that right means nothing if the facility can prevent you from actually communicating with your lawyer. Attorney-client calls must be unmonitored. Attorney-client mail — properly marked — must be treated as privileged and opened only in your presence for contraband inspection, not read.[^5] If you are being denied private communication with your lawyer, that is a serious constitutional violation. Assert it immediately and document it.

**Access to the courts.** You have the right to meaningful access to the courts — to file documents, pursue legal claims, and participate in your own defense. Facilities are required to provide a law library or its equivalent, or to provide legal assistance. They cannot functionally cut off your ability to litigate.

---

## What They Are NOT Required to Provide

Comfort. That's it. That's the list.

Pretrial detention is supposed to be bad. Not cruel. Not punitive. But bad. The system was not designed to feel like anything other than incarceration. The food is going to be poor. The mattress is going to be poor. The smell, the noise, the cold or heat, the institutional rhythm of lockdowns and counts — all of it is going to be exactly as miserable as you're imagining.

The floor is "not punishment." And that floor, as low as it sounds, is enforceable. But never confuse the floor with a livable standard. The government is complying with the Constitution in conditions that would horrify any normal person. Both of those things are true at the same time.

What you are doing by knowing your rights is not making detention comfortable. You are making it survivable. You are drawing the line at the actual constitutional threshold and holding them to it. That is a meaningful thing.

## Religious Rights: The Ramadan Story

The First Amendment protects your right to practice your religion. Inside a federal pretrial facility, that protection is reinforced by a federal statute called RLUIPA — the Religious Land Use and Institutionalized Persons Act — which provides that the government cannot impose a substantial burden on your religious exercise unless it can show a compelling interest and uses the least restrictive means of furthering that interest.[^6] That is a high standard. It gives religion-based claims inside detention real force.

Let me tell you what that looks like in practice.

I spent pretrial time in county jails in New Mexico. I am Muslim. For years — years — there were no accommodations for Ramadan. The chaplains in those facilities provided Bibles. They sought out Christian volunteers. When inmates of other faiths asked about their own religious materials and practices, the answer was silence or indifference. I pushed, formally and informally, for a long time.

Eventually, the facility agreed to bring meals after dark during Ramadan. That was the accommodation.

What they brought: ham. And a loaf of bread.

"Take it or leave it."

Ham. To a Muslim during Ramadan. After years of pressure. After years of legal framework building, formal requests, documented assertions of rights. The facility's response to the religious practice of its Muslim pretrial detainee was to hand him food that his faith explicitly prohibits, at a time when he was already fasting, and tell him to make do.

That is a hell of a way to tell someone that his faith is a nuisance to be handled rather than a right to be respected.

That triggered a formal legal challenge — religious discrimination, establishment clause violation, equal protection. We sued. The case settled for an undisclosed amount.

The point is not the settlement. The point is what knowing your rights actually produces. Not comfort. Not an apology. Not a facility that magically changes its culture. What it produces is a legal record, a formal challenge, and — eventually — accountability. The settlement was real money that came directly from the government's failure to honor a constitutional right. That is what knowing the law and asserting it gets you.

If you are Muslim, Jewish, Buddhist, Hindu, Sikh, or practicing any faith other than what the facility caters to by default, do not accept "we don't do that" as an answer. It is not a legally sufficient answer. Document every request. Document every refusal. Cite RLUIPA. Cite the First Amendment. File formal grievances. And if it escalates, pursue it — because the law supports you.

## Medical Rights: Find the Lever

Here is something they don't tell you about asserting your rights inside a facility: the lever is not always inside the facility.

A nurse practitioner — not my treating provider, not a physician who had ever evaluated me, not anyone who had reviewed my chart in any meaningful sense — canceled all of my medications. Without seeing me. Without examining me. She simply canceled them because she could.

I did not just file a grievance. I reported her to the state board of nursing.

She nearly lost her license.

The state board of nursing does not work for the facility. It does not take direction from the county administrator or the U.S. Marshal or anyone with a stake in how that jail is run. It investigates licensure violations. And canceling a patient's medications without examination, without review, without any clinical basis, is a licensure violation. The facility could absorb a grievance. They could not absorb a licensing investigation from an external regulator.

That is the point. When you are inside a facility, you are surrounded by people who answer to the people who are holding you. The power dynamic is designed to make you feel like there is nowhere to go. But many of the people inside that facility are licensed professionals — nurses, physicians, mental health practitioners — who are licensed by a state authority that has nothing to do with the facility and is not subject to its control. Every licensed medical professional inside that building has an external regulatory body that is capable of investigating them and removing their license.

That external body is afraid of nothing inside that facility. It operates under a completely different set of rules and priorities. And it responds to complaints.

State boards of nursing, state medical boards, state licensing authorities for mental health practitioners — these are real levers that facility administrators fear in a way they will never fear an internal grievance. Because an internal grievance stays inside. A licensing complaint goes to an external authority with the power to end someone's career.

Know which professional is doing what to you. Know what license they hold. Know what state board governs them. If they violate their professional obligations to you, that board is your lever.

Document everything. Dates, names, what was done or refused, who was present, what was said. That documentation is the foundation of any external complaint. Without it, it is your word against theirs. With it, you have a record.

## Your Communications: The Truth About Privacy

Let me be plain about this so there is no confusion.

All calls from the jail — except properly designated legal calls — are recorded. All mail — except properly marked legal mail — can be read. Visits can be observed. Your communications inside a federal pretrial facility exist under the assumption that the government can access them, and in practice the government does.

My attorney calls were monitored by an assigned Homeland Security agent. Over a hundred calls between me and my lawyers were recorded. The prosecution had access to my legal strategy, my attorneys' thinking, the details of what I was considering doing next. The judge acknowledged the objection and did not take meaningful action.

My mail was copied and turned over to the prosecution.

This is the reality. Not theory. Not a risk. Reality. Treat every communication accordingly.

Now — attorney-client calls are constitutionally protected. The facility is required to provide you with access to an unmonitored line to speak with your lawyer. In practice, setting up those calls is a bureaucratic nightmare. Case managers are busy, phones are shared, and the temptation to just call your lawyer on the regular recorded line because it's easier is real. Do not do it. Insist on the legal call designation. Assert the privilege explicitly. And if the facility is recording your attorney calls anyway — as happened to me — document it, object to it in court, and pursue it as the serious constitutional violation it is.[^7]

Legal mail — mail clearly and properly marked as attorney-client correspondence — has additional protections. It must be opened in front of you for contraband inspection only. Staff are not permitted to read it.[^8] If they are, that is a violation. Assert it. Document it.

For everything else: operate as if every word you say or write is going directly to the prosecutor. Because it might be.

## What Your Family Can Do

Families have reach they don't know about. Most families feel as trapped as the defendant — they don't know who to call, they don't know what they're allowed to do, and they don't know how far their reach actually extends.

Here is one thing most people don't know: the supervising United States Marshal is the federal official responsible for the conditions in which pretrial federal defendants are held in the contract county jails. That is a specific person. That person has a specific office. That office has a phone number.

When food at a facility I was in became genuinely inadequate — not "bad" but functionally insufficient — my sister located the U.S. Marshal in charge of prisoner operations and housing and called his office. She made clear, calmly and persistently, that there was a documented problem with the conditions of confinement. She got me transferred to a facility with better conditions.

Families are not helpless. The U.S. Marshal Service has a direct accountability relationship with the facilities it contracts with. When someone with standing calls that office and documents a legitimate concern about conditions of confinement, it moves through a different chain of accountability than an internal facility grievance. Not always. Not fast. But it moves.

Beyond that: document everything from the outside too. If you are receiving letters that describe medical care being denied, food being inadequate, safety concerns, or religious rights being violated — keep those letters, date them, and document them carefully. If this escalates to litigation, that outside record becomes evidence. You are not just a support system. You are part of the paper trail.

Know the facility's name and contract number if you can get it. Know the name of the administrator. Know which federal district the facility is contracted through. These are not just administrative details. They are the identifiers that connect that facility to the oversight structure that governs it.

## How to Enforce These Rights

There is a sequence here, and it matters.

**Step one: the internal grievance process.** File it. Document it. Keep a copy.

Not because it works. It probably won't. Not because the facility will suddenly change its behavior. It probably won't. You file the internal grievance because if this ever goes to federal court, the Prison Litigation Reform Act requires that you exhaust all available administrative remedies before a court will even hear your claim.[^9]

IF YOU SKIP THE GRIEVANCE PROCESS, A FEDERAL COURT WILL THROW YOUR CASE OUT BEFORE IT STARTS — NO EXCEPTIONS.

It doesn't matter how strong your constitutional claim is. It doesn't matter how obvious the violation is. It doesn't matter that the grievance process inside the facility is a performance with a predetermined outcome. Courts require exhaustion. Do it. Keep every response you get. If they don't respond within the required timeframe, document that too — non-response is itself part of the record.

**Step two: external levers.** These are where real pressure comes from.

The supervising U.S. Marshal — for conditions in contract facilities holding federal pretrial detainees, this office has direct accountability over the facility. Families can contact it. Lawyers can contact it. Document the contact.

State licensing boards — for any licensed professional who has violated their professional obligations. Nurses, physicians, mental health professionals. These boards operate independently of the facility and have authority the facility cannot override.

State oversight agencies — many states have a county detention oversight office, a department of corrections oversight function, or a state inspector general with authority over jails. Research what exists in the state where you're held.

The facility's contract oversight office — the federal contract between the U.S. Marshal Service and the county facility has compliance provisions. There are contract officers and oversight staff who monitor whether facilities are meeting the terms of those contracts. A documented complaint filed through a lawyer to that oversight function is a different category of pressure than an internal grievance.

**Step three: civil rights litigation.** If the internal process fails and external pressure fails, you have legal claims.

Against state actors — including county jail employees — claims flow under 42 U.S.C. § 1983, the federal civil rights statute that allows people to sue state and local government officials for constitutional violations.[^10] Against federal actors — federal employees acting under color of federal law — the claim flows under *Bivens v. Six Unknown Named Agents*, which allows similar claims against federal officials.[^11]

These are real lawsuits with real remedies — damages, injunctive relief, and attorney's fees where warranted. They are not easy. They are slow. They are expensive to pursue, particularly pro se. But they exist, and facilities know they exist, and that knowledge is itself a lever.

## The Strategic Reality

There is something I want to be direct about, because it is not written anywhere in any legal doctrine and it is more practically true than almost anything I could tell you.

Facilities maintain a different level of professionalism around people they know will fight back.

After I started asserting my rights — formally, legally, persistently — the tone inside those buildings changed around me. Not because the officers suddenly became decent people. Not because the administration had a change of heart. Because they understood that I knew what I was doing, and that a lawsuit from me was a lawsuit they were likely to lose. They weren't afraid of a lawsuit from someone who didn't know his rights. They were afraid of a lawsuit from someone who generally wins.

That shift is real. It is not justice. It is not the way a system based on presumptive innocence should operate. But it is the truth about how these places actually work.

You are not required to be passive inside a federal pretrial facility. You are not required to accept what is given to you without question. You are not required to eat the ham.

The law gives you a floor. Knowing it, documenting it, asserting it formally — that is what raises you from the floor to something survivable. That is what sends the message that you are a person who knows what you're owed and who intends to be paid.

That message changes things.
