You found the right place

Someone you love was just arrested. Breathe — start here.

Free. No judgment. No money needed. Just the real, calm answers you need in this exact moment.

It doesn't matter who took them — local police, state troopers, the FBI, Homeland Security, or any other agency. It doesn't matter whether there's already been an arrest or just a search warrant and the fear that one is coming. And it doesn't matter what they're accused of. You are welcome here, there is nothing to be ashamed of, and you don't need a dollar to get real help. Take a breath. Here is what to know, right this second.

What to know right now

The things that matter most, first.

1

You never have to talk to them. Ever.

Nobody is obligated to speak with a government agent — not the person arrested, and not you as the mother, wife, husband, child, or grandparent. Police, FBI, Homeland Security, state or local: it makes no difference. Being polite is fine. Answering their questions is not required, and doing so almost always carries one very real risk — hurting the case.

The only words you need: "I am going to remain silent, and I want a lawyer." Then stop talking.

2

Who actually needs a lawyer right now.

Here's where you can slow down a little. If you are the person charged, you will be given a lawyer no matter what — even if you can't afford one. If you are the family, you may choose to hire a lawyer just to help guide you through the process, but you are not obligated to.

And if any agent tells you "only guilty people need a lawyer," ignore it completely. Plenty of innocent people have been locked away for exactly one reason: they talked to the police without one.

3

You don't have to let them search.

If they do not have a warrant, you can say it plainly and calmly: "I do not consent to a search." If they do have a warrant, don't physically interfere — ask to see it and read what it actually covers — but you still don't have to answer questions or explain anything. Consenting can give away protections you'll wish you had kept.

4

Assume everything is recorded.

Every jail phone call and nearly every visit is recorded and later reviewed by the prosecution. Never discuss the facts of the case on a jail line. Keep those calls about love and logistics — "are you okay," "here's what's happening at home." Save anything about the case for a private conversation with the lawyer.

5

Write everything down — right now, while it's fresh.

Under this kind of stress, memory fades fast. Write down which agency showed up, the agents' names and badge numbers, exactly what was said, what they took, and the times. These notes can become some of the most valuable material your lawyer ever gets.

6

You have more time than it feels like.

It feels like you must do something enormous this second. You don't. The gears of justice grind slowly — these cases take months, often longer. Don't empty your savings or make huge decisions today out of fear. The most powerful thing you can do right now is stay calm and get informed.

This is general information shared by someone who lived through it — a starting point, not legal advice, and not a substitute for a lawyer who knows your specific case. But these things hold true in the first moments of almost any case, no matter the agency.
