01 — First

Go make your favorite drink.

The case is not going anywhere in the next ten minutes, and neither is this page. Getting comfortable matters more than it sounds.

Make no mistake: the United States Attorney assigned to this case is perfectly comfortable — sitting in a nice chair, sipping a latte, reading the file at their leisure. You are allowed to take a breath too.

Then remind yourself of something that is actually freeing: nothing you do in this exact moment is going to change what happens next. If an arrest is going to happen, it will happen. If it already happened, the procedure from here is the same in every case. You cannot make a wrong move by slowing down.

You could have Jesus himself appear and swear you're innocent — and history reveres him, across every belief, as the most honest man who ever lived — and it still would not stop the engine of justice today. If even he can't get you out this minute, you may as well get comfortable.
02 — Right now

Find them. Reach them. Support them.

1

Find out where they are.

If a family member was just arrested, start by calling the police or the local jail to see if they're there. Don't panic if you're told they aren't. It often takes hours for a jail to register that someone has been processed. Give it time and call back.

2

If you can't find them, use PACER.

If you're sure it's a federal case and still can't locate them, look up the case yourself on PACER — the federal courts' public records system. It shows whether charges have been filed, when hearings are scheduled, and who has been appointed as the lawyer. Federal defendants are often held out of state, so contacting that assigned attorney is frequently the fastest way to learn where they are.

How to search PACER

  1. Go to pacer.uscourts.gov and register for a free account.
  2. Open the PACER Case Locator and search by your loved one's name.
  3. Open the matching case to see the docket — charges, hearings, and the assigned attorney.

Registration is free. Searches cost about ten cents a page, and fees are waived entirely if you stay under a small quarterly amount.

3

Wait for their call — and keep it safe.

Most people know at least one phone number by heart. Your loved one may call collect to tell you where they are. Accept the charges, get the essentials, and keep it short. Remember: that line is recorded. Keep it to where and how they're doing — never the facts of the case.

4

Put a little money on their "books."

Once you know where they are, ask the facility how to put money on their account — their "books." This lets them buy the basics the jail doesn't really provide: soap, paper, pens, stamps, snacks. (Yes, a jail must provide soap — but it's the cheap kind, and it doesn't go far.) An initial deposit of around $100 usually gets them situated for the first week or two.

About being held: in federal cases, until a detention hearing is held, your loved one will most likely remain in custody. That hearing is one of the most important early moments in the entire case — Surviving Pretrial dedicates an entire chapter to it. See the book →

03 — Protect the case

Three rights nobody can take.

You don't have to talk.

No one — not the person arrested, not family — must speak with any government agent. "I am going to remain silent, and I want a lawyer." Then stop.

A lawyer is coming regardless.

If you're the one charged, you'll be given a lawyer no matter what. Ignore anyone who says "only the guilty ask for one" — innocent people are convicted for talking without one.

You can refuse a search.

No warrant? "I do not consent to a search." If they have one, don't interfere — but you still don't have to answer questions or explain anything.

04 — Don't let fear lie to you

Myths that hurt families — and the truth.

Myth

"If we just explain everything, this will get cleared up."

The truth

Talking to agents almost never clears anything up. You can't talk your way out — only into. Silence is what protects the case.

Myth

"Only guilty people ask for a lawyer."

The truth

Innocent people are convicted all the time — often because they spoke without a lawyer. Asking for one is what smart, innocent people do.

Myth

"A court-appointed lawyer means a second-rate lawyer."

The truth

Many federal defenders are deeply experienced in exactly this kind of case. Judge the lawyer, not the label.

Myth

"They'll post bail and be home by morning, like on TV."

The truth

Federal release isn't automatic. It usually turns on a detention hearing — and many remain in custody until then.

Myth

"If they're innocent, the truth will protect them."

The truth

The system runs on procedure and evidence rules, not innocence alone. You have to engage it correctly for the truth to matter.

Myth

"A federal case will be over quickly."

The truth

Most take many months — often more than a year. It's a marathon, and pacing yourself is part of winning.

This is general information shared by someone who lived through it — a starting point, not legal advice, and not a substitute for a lawyer who knows your specific case.

05 — You are not alone

This feels like the end. It isn't.

Thousands of families have stood exactly where you are standing right now — blindsided, terrified, certain their world had just ended. It hadn't. There is a way through this, and it begins the moment you trade fear for understanding.
