You found the right place

Your first step is simple. Get comfortable.

Free. No judgment. No money needed. Make your favorite drink and settle in — the case isn't going anywhere, and neither are we.

Here is your actual first step: go make yourself your favorite beverage — coffee, tea, whatever steadies you. The case is not going anywhere in the next ten minutes, and neither is this page.

Getting comfortable matters more than it sounds. Make no mistake: the United States Attorney assigned to this case is perfectly comfortable — sitting in a nice chair, sipping a latte, reading the file at their leisure. You are allowed to take a breath too.

Then remind yourself of something that is actually freeing: nothing you do in this exact moment is going to change what happens next. You could have a dozen witnesses swear, under oath, that you or your loved one is completely innocent — and it would not stop the engine of justice today. If an arrest is going to happen, it will happen. If it already happened, the procedure from here is the same in every case. Which means you cannot make a wrong move by slowing down — and you have time to get this right.

First things first

Find them, reach them, support them.

1

Find out where they are.

If a family member was just arrested, you'll likely start by calling the police or the local jail to see if they're there. Don't panic if you're told they aren't. It often takes hours for a jail system to register that someone has been processed. Give it time and call back.

2

If you can't find them, use PACER.

If you're sure it's a federal case and still can't locate them, you can look up the case yourself on PACER — the federal courts' public records system. It shows whether formal charges have been filed, when hearings are scheduled, and who has been appointed as the lawyer. Federal defendants are often held out of state or far from home, so if you can't find your person, contacting that assigned attorney is frequently the fastest way to learn where they are.

How to search PACER

  1. Go to pacer.uscourts.gov and register for a free account.
  2. Open the PACER Case Locator and search by your loved one's name.
  3. Open the matching case to see the docket — charges, scheduled hearings, and the assigned attorney.

Registration is free. Searches cost about ten cents a page, and fees are waived entirely if you stay under a small quarterly amount.

3

Wait for their call — and keep it safe.

Most people know at least one phone number by heart. Your loved one may call collect to tell you where they are. Accept the charges, get the essentials, and keep it short. Remember: that line is recorded. Keep the call to where and how they're doing — never the facts of the case.

4

Put a little money on their "books."

Once you know where they are, find out that facility's procedure for putting money on their account — their "books." This lets them buy the basics the jail doesn't really provide: soap, paper, pens, stamps, snacks. (Yes, a jail must provide soap — but it's the cheap kind, and it doesn't go far.) An initial deposit of around $100 is usually enough to get them situated for the first week or two.

About being held: in federal cases, until a detention hearing is held, your loved one will most likely remain in custody. That hearing is one of the most important early moments in the entire case — Surviving Pretrial dedicates an entire chapter to it. See the book →

What to know right now

The rights that protect the case.

A

Nobody has to talk to them. Ever.

No one is obligated to speak with a government agent — not the person arrested, and not you as the mother, wife, husband, child, or grandparent. Police, FBI, Homeland Security, state or local: it makes no difference. Being polite is fine. Answering questions is not required, and doing so almost always carries one very real risk — hurting the case.

The only words you need: "I am going to remain silent, and I want a lawyer." Then stop talking.

B

Who actually needs a lawyer right now.

Here's where you can slow down. If you are the person charged, you will be given a lawyer no matter what — even if you can't afford one. If you are the family, you may choose to hire a lawyer to help guide you, but you are not obligated to. And if any agent tells you "only guilty people need a lawyer," ignore it completely — plenty of innocent people have been locked away for exactly one reason: they talked to the police without one.

C

You don't have to consent to a search.

If they don't have a warrant, you can say it plainly: "I do not consent to a search." If they do have a warrant, don't physically interfere — ask to see it and read what it covers — but you still don't have to answer questions or explain anything.

This is general information shared by someone who lived through it — a starting point, not legal advice, and not a substitute for a lawyer who knows your specific case. But these things hold true in the first moments of almost any case, no matter the agency.

Don't let fear lie to you

Myths that hurt families — and the truth.

The Myth The Truth
Myth

"If we just explain everything, this will get cleared up."

Fact

Talking to agents almost never clears anything up. You can't talk your way out — only into. Silence is what protects the case.

Myth

"Only guilty people ask for a lawyer."

Fact

Innocent people are convicted all the time — often because they spoke without a lawyer. Asking for one is what smart, innocent people do.

Myth

"A court-appointed lawyer means a second-rate lawyer."

Fact

Many federal defenders and appointed attorneys are deeply experienced in exactly this kind of case. Judge the lawyer, not the label.

Myth

"They'll post bail and be home by morning, like on TV."

Fact

Federal release isn't automatic. Whether someone is held usually turns on a detention hearing — and many remain in custody until then.

Myth

"If they're innocent, the truth will protect them."

Fact

The system runs on procedure and evidence rules, not on innocence alone. You have to engage it correctly for the truth to matter.

Myth

"A federal case will be over quickly."

Fact

Most take many months — often more than a year. It's a marathon, and pacing yourself is part of winning.
