You're in the right place

Someone you love was just arrested by the feds.

Breathe. Read this before you spend a dollar or say a word.

Right now it feels like the entire weight of the United States government just landed on your family in a single instant — and the ground is gone. That feeling is normal. But hear this clearly: this is not the end, and the most damaging mistakes happen in the first 48 hours, out of fear. This page is triage. The few things that matter most, right now, in order.

Do these two things first

Before anything else.

1
Rule Number 1

Slow Down!

My brother once told me "the gears of justice grind slowly." He couldn't have been more right. They grind so slowly that you can take your time with everything. In fact, take up knitting. You now have the time to do it!

The prosecutor's biggest weapon is knowledge. You can dull that weapon by taking your time, learning all the material, and making good decisions. This is the long game. A criminal prosecution can take many months to finish. Don't start throwing money at lawyers until you decide that is what is right for you.

2
Rule Number 2

Shut the Hell Up!

People are out to get you. And no, I'm not being paranoid. Do not talk to anyone about your case. If you are in jail and asked, just say, "I'm fighting it and that's all I can say about my case." Only a snitch would be upset that you don't talk about your case.

Don't talk with anyone. Not friends, not family, and definitely not other inmates. Your life literally depends on it. Only speak with your lawyer in a private area where nobody else can hear you. In fact, act like an NFL coach who hides his mouth when talking. Don't fall victim to the many opportunists out there who don't care if you go away forever — and protect your family and real friends by not telling them anything either.

In the first 48 hours

What not to do.

These are the mistakes that quietly do the most damage — and they almost always happen in the first days, out of panic.

  • Don't discuss the case on jail calls or visits.Every jail phone call and most visits are recorded and reviewed by the prosecution. Keep calls to "how are you" — never the facts of the case.
  • Don't talk to agents or investigators without a lawyer.They are trained and patient. "I'm going to remain silent and I want a lawyer." Then stop. You cannot talk your way out — only into.
  • Don't post anything about the case online.No social media, no group chats, no "venting." It can and will be read in the worst possible light.
  • Don't panic-hire the first expensive lawyer you find.You will be appointed counsel no matter what — even with money. There is time to choose well. Fear is a terrible negotiator.
Right now, you're asking

Your first questions, answered.

My family member was just arrested by the federal government. What do I do first?

Slow down and stop talking about the case. Those are the first two rules above, and they matter more than anything else in the first days. You do not need to make big decisions — or hire anyone — today. Protect the case by saying nothing, and give yourself time to learn before you act.

Should we talk to the investigators or agents to "clear things up"?

No. Agents are friendly and patient on purpose. There is no version of that conversation that helps — only versions that hurt. The words are simple: "I'm going to remain silent and I want a lawyer," and then stop. This applies to the defendant and to family members.

Are jail phone calls really recorded?

Yes. Jail and detention calls are recorded and routinely reviewed by prosecutors, and so are most visits. Never discuss the facts of the case on any monitored line. Talk about how everyone is holding up — save the substance for a confidential conversation with the attorney.

Do we have to hire a lawyer immediately?

No. Finding a lawyer feels like the most urgent thing in the world; it isn't. If your loved one is brought to court, they will be appointed a lawyer no matter what — even if there is money. That gives you time to choose deliberately instead of in a panic. Learn what to look for before you spend.

What happens next — will they be released?

Generally, soon after arrest there is an initial appearance, and then a detention or bond hearing where a judge decides whether your loved one is released or held while the case proceeds. This moves fast and matters enormously. Federal cases themselves often take many months to over a year — it is a marathon, not a sprint.

This page is general information from lived experience — not legal advice, and not a substitute for a licensed attorney. Every case is different. But these fundamentals hold true in the first days of almost every federal case.
