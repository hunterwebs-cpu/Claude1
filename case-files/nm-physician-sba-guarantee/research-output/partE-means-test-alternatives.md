## 1. The Means Test Is Very Likely Irrelevant Here — Two Independent Reasons

### 1(a). Social Security income is excluded from "current monthly income" entirely

11 U.S.C. § 101(10A) defines "current monthly income" (CMI) — the figure that drives the § 707(b)(2) means test — and § 101(10A)(B) excludes from that definition:

> "benefits received under the Social Security Act" (and certain other narrow categories: payments to victims of war crimes/crimes against humanity/terrorism, and Armed Forces/DHS combat-related casualty payments).

**Effect:** A debtor whose *only* income is Social Security has **CMI of $0** for means-test purposes, regardless of the actual dollar amount received and regardless of age or disability status. This is a categorical statutory exclusion — Social Security dollars are not "income" as the Bankruptcy Code defines that term for means-test purposes, even though the same dollars will still appear on Schedule I and matter for the "totality of circumstances" / good-faith analysis under § 707(b)(3) if the means test were ever reached (it should not be — see 1(b) and 4, below). This exclusion is also why Social Security is not counted as "projected disposable income" in Chapter 13.

```
Statute: 11 U.S.C. § 101(10A)(B)
Text (as returned by search of Cornell LII / house.gov): current monthly income "does not include benefits received under the Social Security Act, payments to victims of war crimes or crimes against humanity on account of their status as victims of such crimes, payments to victims of international terrorism ... or domestic terrorism ... on account of their status as victims of such terrorism, or payments by the United States Government to eligible individuals under the Radiation Exposure Compensation Act."

### 1(b). The § 707(b) presumption-of-abuse framework applies only to debtors whose debts are "primarily consumer debts" — an SBA business guaranty is not consumer debt

11 U.S.C. § 707(b)(1) — the operative dismissal/conversion provision — is expressly limited in scope:

> The court "may dismiss a case filed by an individual debtor under this chapter *whose debts are primarily consumer debts* ... if it finds that the granting of relief would be an abuse of the provisions of this chapter."

The means test and its presumption of abuse live in § 707(b)(2), which is triggered only within a § 707(b)(1) proceeding — i.e., **only if the debtor's debts are primarily consumer debts in the first place**. If a debtor's debts are primarily *non-consumer* (business) debts, § 707(b) does not apply to the case at all — no means test, no presumption of abuse, no totality-of-circumstances abuse inquiry under § 707(b)(3). This is a threshold gate, not merely a rebuttable factor.

```
Statute: 11 U.S.C. § 707(b)(1)
Text: "After notice and a hearing, the court, on its own motion or on a motion by the United States trustee, trustee (or bankruptcy administrator, if any), or any party in interest, may dismiss a case filed by an individual debtor under this chapter whose debts are primarily consumer debts, or, with the debtor's consent, convert such a case to a case under chapter 11 or 13 of this title, if it finds that the granting of relief would be an abuse of the provisions of this chapter."
Source: https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/11/707 ; https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid%3AUSC-prelim-title11-section707&num=0&edition=prelim

**Application to this debtor.** An SBA-guaranteed PPP or EIDL loan is, by definition, a loan made to and used by the LLC for business operating purposes (payroll, rent, and other business expenses for the medical practice). A guaranty is generally treated, for classification purposes, as taking on the character of the underlying obligation it secures — i.e., a personal guaranty of a business loan is itself classified as a business (non-consumer) debt, not a "debt incurred by an individual primarily for a personal, family, or household purpose" under § 101(8). Secondary sources uniformly describe this as the prevailing/majority view ("If an individual guaranteed a debt for a business obligation, that personal guaranty would be deemed business debt"; "personal guarantees of business debts are, by definition, non-consumer"), and it follows directly from the § 101(8) text — the guarantor did not incur the debt "for a personal, family, or household purpose"; he incurred it to backstop his practice's SBA financing.

If the SBA guaranty debt (potentially a substantial sum, likely six figures given PPP/EIDL practice-level borrowing) is compared against whatever residual personal/consumer debt he may separately carry (credit cards, a car loan not tied to the practice, etc.), it appears highly likely that the guaranty debt would constitute *more than half the dollar amount of his total debt* — placing him outside "primarily consumer debts" and therefore outside § 707(b) altogether. **This is a fact-specific, arithmetic determination that counsel must run on his actual debt schedule (all debts, consumer and non-consumer, listed by dollar amount) before filing** — but on the facts as given (SBA guaranty as the dominant liability, Social Security as the only income source with no other apparent major consumer debt), the conclusion that this is a primarily-non-consumer-debt case, taking it outside the means test and the presumption of abuse entirely, is well supported.

**Practical bottom line on Part 1:** Even setting aside the § 101(8)/§ 707(b)(1) business-debt argument, the means test independently produces a $0 CMI because Social Security is excluded from CMI by statute. Either ground alone defeats a presumption-of-abuse problem; together they make this about as clean a Chapter 7 eligibility case as exists.

## 2. Confirming § 101(8) "Consumer Debt" and Its Inapplicability to a Business Guaranty

Restated for clarity and cross-reference to Track topics: § 101(8) requires that the debt be "incurred by an individual primarily for a personal, family, or household purpose." A guaranty signed by the physician to enable his LLC (the medical practice) to obtain PPP and EIDL financing does not meet that definition — the purpose was to fund the *practice's* payroll/operations/disaster-relief needs, not the guarantor's personal consumption. This is consistent with the general rule that business-purpose loans, and guaranties of them, are non-consumer debt regardless of the individual's status as guarantor rather than primary obligor. See authorities cited in Part 1(b) above (Stewart, Kelly, Booth, and the secondary-source consensus on guaranty classification following the character of the underlying debt).

One caution for the drafting team: courts do sometimes scrutinize *why* an individual is the guarantor and whether there was any personal-consumption angle (e.g., if loan proceeds were diverted to personal use). Nothing in the facts as given suggests that; the loans were used for legitimate practice operations. Track B/D materials on the LLC's own bankruptcy filing and use of PPP/EIDL proceeds should be cross-checked to make sure there is no fact pattern (e.g., commingling, personal draws framed as payroll) that would complicate the "business purpose" characterization.

## 3. New Mexico Venue for the Individual Case — 28 U.S.C. § 1408 — and the Filing-Venue vs. Exemption-Choice Distinction

```
Statute: 28 U.S.C. § 1408
Text (as returned by search of Cornell LII/house.gov): venue for a bankruptcy case filed by an individual is proper in a district "(1) in which the domicile, residence, principal place of business in the United States, or principal assets in the United States, of the person ... that is the subject of such case have been located for the one hundred and eighty days immediately preceding such commencement, or for a longer portion of such one-hundred-and-eighty-day period than [in] any other district; or (2) in which there is pending a case under title 11 concerning such person's affiliate, general partner, or partnership."
Source: https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/28/1408 ; https://www.govinfo.gov/app/details/USCODE-2015-title28/USCODE-2015-title28-partIV-chap87-sec1408
Verification Status: VERIFY BEFORE FILING (snippet-derived; confirm exact codified text and current subsection numbering)
```

**Application:** The physician resides in New Mexico. Under § 1408(1), the Bankruptcy Court for the District of New Mexico is the proper venue for his individual Chapter 7, based on ordinary residence/domicile — no special durational-residency threshold beyond having been domiciled/resident there for the 180 days preceding filing (which, on these facts, is not in question — he is a longstanding New Mexico resident/physician). Additionally, § 1408(2) independently supports New Mexico venue because there is a pending Title 11 case concerning his "affiliate" — the wholly-owned LLC — which itself is filing (or has filed) its Chapter 7 in New Mexico. Both prongs point to the same result: D.N.M. is proper venue for the individual case.

**Filing venue vs. exemption-choice residency — do not conflate.** This § 1408 venue analysis (where he may *file*) is a distinct question from *which state's exemption scheme applies* to protect his homestead, vehicle, and other property once the case is filed. The latter is governed by 11 U.S.C. § 522(b)(3)(A), which looks to the debtor's domicile for the 730 days (2 years) before filing, and, if he was domiciled in more than one state during the 180 days before that 2-year period, the state where he was domiciled for the greater part of that 180-day window. Track C's memo covers the § 522(b)(3)(A) 730/180-day exemption-choice analysis in detail and should be relied on for that question; this memo notes only that venue-to-file (§ 1408) and law-to-apply-for-exemptions (§ 522(b)(3)(A)) are governed by different statutes with different lookback periods, and satisfying one says nothing about the other. On these facts, since the physician appears to be a longtime New Mexico domiciliary, both analyses should independently point to New Mexico (District of New Mexico as venue; New Mexico exemption law as applicable law) — but the two questions should not be merged or treated as interchangeable in the memo's exemption discussion.

## 4. Chapter 7 vs. Chapter 13 — Chapter 7 Is the Natural Fit Here

Chapter 13 eligibility requires an "individual with regular income":

```
Statute: 11 U.S.C. § 109(e)
Text (as returned by search; current version reflecting the post-BTATCA reversion): "Only an individual with regular income that owes, on the date of the filing of the petition, noncontingent, liquidated, unsecured debts of less than [statutory unsecured cap, inflation-adjusted] and noncontingent, liquidated, secured debts of less than [statutory secured cap, inflation-adjusted] ... may be a debtor under chapter 13."
Source: https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/11/109 ; https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid%3AUSC-prelim-title11-section109&num=0&edition=prelim ; https://www.delawarebankruptcyblog.com/2021/12/chapter-13-analysis-of-the-debt-limit-under-section-109e-of-the-bankruptcy-code/
Verification Status: VERIFY BEFORE FILING — the dollar figures here are in flux. A temporary unified debt limit of $2,750,000 (enacted by the Bankruptcy Threshold Adjustment and Technical Corrections Act) applied to cases filed before it expired (June 21, 2024, per multiple secondary sources); after expiration, the law reverted to the pre-2020 structure of separate, inflation-adjusted secured/unsecured caps. One secondary source (Middlebrooks Shapiro) reports the reverted figures, effective April 1, 2025, as unsecured debts of less than $526,700 and secured debts of less than $1,580,125. Given the multiple legislative changes in this area in recent years, counsel MUST confirm the exact figures in effect on the actual filing date directly from the U.S. Courts website (which publishes the triennial inflation adjustments) before relying on any number here.
```

Regardless of the precise current dollar cap, debt-limit eligibility is not the binding constraint for this debtor — feasibility is. Chapter 13 requires a confirmable repayment plan funded from the debtor's "projected disposable income" (11 U.S.C. §§ 1322, 1325(b)). Two structural problems make Chapter 13 a poor (likely infeasible) fit here:

- **No surplus income to fund a plan.** His only income is Social Security, and by his own account that income is already fully committed to his mortgage payment. There is no evident disposable income left over to fund plan payments to unsecured creditors (including the SBA-guaranty deficiency claim) over a 3-5 year commitment period. A plan that proposes $0 (or near-$0) monthly payments to unsecured creditors is sometimes confirmable in narrow circumstances, but it gains him nothing that Chapter 7 does not already accomplish faster and more simply — and risks dismissal for lack of good faith or infeasibility if the numbers don't work.
- **Social Security is statutorily excluded from "disposable income" calculations** in the same manner it is excluded from CMI (courts, including recent Tenth Circuit authority, have held Social Security benefits are not counted in the means test or projected-disposable-income analysis for Chapter 13 purposes — see the ABI summary of the "10th Circuit rules Social Security not considered in means test or good faith in chapter 13" line of authority flagged in research). That reinforces that a Chapter 13 plan here would have essentially no funding source beyond whatever non-Social-Security assets/income he might have — which, on these facts, appears to be none.

**Practical conclusion:** Given (a) his age (82), (b) fixed, already-committed Social Security income, and (c) the absence of business debt reasons to prefer Chapter 13 (no need to catch up a home mortgage arrearage or save a non-exempt asset that Chapter 7 would otherwise force liquidation of, assuming his home equity and vehicle fall within New Mexico exemption limits — see Track C), Chapter 7 is clearly the appropriate chapter if he files at all. Chapter 7 liquidates non-exempt assets (likely none or minimal, given Social-Security-only income and probable full exemption coverage of homestead/vehicle under New Mexico law) and discharges the guaranty debt in a matter of months, without requiring him to commit fixed income he does not have to a multi-year repayment plan.

## 5. Non-Bankruptcy Alternatives

### 5(a). SBA Offer in Compromise (OIC) for individual guarantors

- **Regulatory framework:** SBA debt collection generally, including offset and administrative wage garnishment, is governed by 13 C.F.R. Part 140 ("Debt Collection"). Compromise authority specifically draws on the government-wide Federal Claims Collection Standards (FCCS) at 31 C.F.R. Parts 900–904, and in particular 31 C.F.R. Part 902 ("Standards for the Compromise of Claims").

```

Text/summary (search-derived): agencies may compromise a debt where, among other bases, "[t]he debtor is unable to pay the full amount in a reasonable time" or "[t]he Government is unable to collect the debt in full within a reasonable time by enforced collection proceedings." This last point is directly useful to the "why bother filing bankruptcy at all" analysis in Part 5(b): the federal government's own compromise standards instruct the agency to account for the debtor's available state and federal exemptions when deciding how hard to pursue collection — which, for an elderly guarantor whose only income is exempt Social Security and whose home/vehicle equity likely falls within New Mexico's exemption amounts, points toward minimal realistic recovery for SBA/Treasury even outside of bankruptcy.

  - **PPP** is a 7(a)-program loan; unforgiven PPP balances are serviced/liquidated under the SOP governing 7(a) and 504 loans.   - **EIDL** (a disaster loan) is serviced/liquidated under **SOP 50 52 (Disaster Loan Servicing and Liquidation)**, current version referenced in search results as **SOP 50 52 2**, with SBA policy notices extending/revising COVID-EIDL-specific servicing and liquidation policy layered on top of the base SOP.

This should be flagged to the client as a real but not-guaranteed-to-be-fast option, and **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING]** given how quickly SBA administrative posture on COVID-EIDL collections has been shifting.

### 5(b). "Wait and see" / judgment-proof analysis — is personal bankruptcy even necessary?

Two independent legal protections support treating this client as largely "judgment proof" against SBA/Treasury collection even *without* filing personal bankruptcy:

**Social Security benefits are protected from ordinary creditor process (including by the federal government itself, subject to the TOP carve-out addressed in Part 6):**

```

Source: https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/42/407 ; https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title42-section407&num=0&edition=prelim

**Synthesis:** If, as the facts suggest, essentially all of this client's assets and income (Social Security + a modest homestead + one vehicle) would be fully exempt in a Chapter 7 case anyway, the *same* categories of assets are largely unreachable by an ordinary judgment creditor **outside** bankruptcy too — Social Security by federal anti-attachment law (§ 407(a)), and homestead/vehicle equity by New Mexico's own execution-exemption statutes (which apply independent of any bankruptcy filing). That raises a legitimate strategic question for the memo to flag explicitly: **is a personal Chapter 7 filing strictly necessary, or would simply doing nothing (and asserting available state-law exemptions if and when the SBA/Treasury ever actually attempts collection) achieve a functionally similar result at lower cost/effort?**

Arguments favoring filing anyway, despite this "judgment proof" baseline, that the drafting team should weigh (not fully researched here — Track A/F scope, flagged for cross-reference):
- Bankruptcy provides an affirmative discharge (a permanent bar to future collection and to future changes in his exemption profile or asset base), whereas relying on "judgment proof" status is a case-by-case defensive posture that must be re-asserted every time collection is attempted, and offers no protection if his circumstances change (e.g., inheritance, home sale/equity increase above the exemption cap, non-exempt asset acquired later).
- The TOP offset exposure discussed in Part 6 below is a partial, real chink in the "fully judgment proof" picture — Social Security is not 100% unreachable by the federal government specifically (as opposed to private judgment creditors), which is the key factual nuance cutting toward filing being more protective than simply waiting.
- A discharge removes the psychological/administrative burden of an outstanding federal debt and eliminates TOP exposure prospectively and permanently (once discharged, the debt is no longer "legally enforceable" for TOP purposes, subject to the usual bankruptcy-discharge caveats).

### 5(c). Statute of limitations on SBA guaranty collection

```
Statute: 28 U.S.C. § 2415(a)
Text: "[E]very action for money damages brought by the United States or an officer or agency thereof which is founded upon any contract express or implied in law or fact, shall be barred unless the complaint is filed within six years after the right of action accrues or within one year after final decisions have been rendered in applicable administrative proceedings required by contract or by law, whichever is later." The statute also provides that partial payment or written acknowledgment of the debt restarts the six-year clock as of the date of the payment/acknowledgment, and that a timely-filed action later dismissed without prejudice may be recommenced within one year of dismissal notwithstanding the general bar.
Source: https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/28/2415 ; https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid%3AUSC-prelim-title28-section2415&num=0&edition=prelim
Verification Status: VERIFY BEFORE FILING
```

Well-established, non-controversial background principle: the United States is not bound by state statutes of limitations or subject to the defense of laches when enforcing its own contract rights (multiple secondary sources, consistent with black-letter federal sovereign-immunity/limitations doctrine, e.g., the reasoning underlying *United States v. Summerlin*, 310 U.S. 414 (1940), and its progeny — not independently re-verified in this session but consistent with universally reported doctrine). Accrual under the companion provision, 28 U.S.C. § 2416, generally runs from when facts material to the right of action are known (or reasonably should be known) to a responsible government official — not simply from the date of default — so counsel should not assume the SBA's window necessarily started ticking on the exact default date without confirming when the guaranty claim was formally referred/assigned for collection.

**Application:** Depending on when the LLC defaulted and when (if ever) SBA/Treasury formally demanded payment from the guarantor or referred the guaranty claim for collection, the six-year § 2415(a) clock may or may not have meaningfully run yet on these COVID-era (2020-2021 origination) loans — this is a fact-intensive accrual question that the drafting team should pin down with actual dates before relying on a limitations defense. Do not assume the SOL has run without confirming the accrual date and checking for any tolling/restart events (partial payments, written acknowledgments, administrative proceedings).

## 6. FLAGGED PROMINENTLY: Treasury Offset Program (TOP) Exposure on Social Security — He Is NOT Fully Judgment-Proof Against the Federal Government

This is the single most important nuance in this memo and should be surfaced prominently in the final synthesized memorandum: **although Social Security benefits are broadly protected from execution/levy/garnishment by ordinary creditors under 42 U.S.C. § 407(a) (Part 5(b), above), that protection is not absolute against the federal government itself for federal non-tax debts.** Section 407(b) allows Congress to override § 407(a)'s protection by *express reference* to that section, and Congress did so for administrative offset of federal benefit payments to collect delinquent federal non-tax debt.

```
Statute: 31 U.S.C. § 3716 (Administrative offset), esp. § 3716(c)(3)(A)
Text/summary (search-derived): Congress authorized offset of certain "covered benefit payments" (including Social Security old-age, survivors, and disability insurance benefits) to collect delinquent nontax debt owed to the United States, "[n]otwithstanding any other provision of law (including sections 207 and 1631(d)(1) of the Social Security Act)" — i.e., an express override of the § 407(a) anti-attachment shield, satisfying § 407(b)'s "express reference" requirement. The statute protects a floor: "[a]n amount of $9,000 which a debtor may receive under [covered] Federal benefit programs within a 12-month period shall be exempt from offset," prorated across the debtor's periodic benefit payments during that 12-month period.
Source: https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/31/3716 ; https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid%3AUSC-prelim-title31-section3716&num=0&edition=prelim
Verification Status: VERIFY BEFORE FILING (confirm current codified subsection numbering and exact current dollar figure — this figure has been reported as fixed at $9,000/12-month period rather than automatically inflation-indexed, but confirm whether any more recent statutory or regulatory amendment has changed it)
```

```
Regulation: 31 C.F.R. § 285.4 (Offset of Federal benefit payments to collect past-due, legally enforceable nontax debt)
Text/summary (search-derived): implements 31 U.S.C. § 3716 (and § 3720A, and 26 U.S.C. § 6402) as applied to Social Security and other covered federal benefit payments. The regulation caps the amount that may be offset from a monthly benefit payment at the **lesser of 15% of the monthly benefit, or the amount by which the benefit exceeds $750/month** — i.e., the first $750 of a monthly Social Security benefit is protected from offset under this nontax-debt regime (the $750/month figure is the monthly-equivalent expression of the $9,000/12-month statutory floor in § 3716(c)(3)(A)). Example given in secondary sources: on a $900/month benefit, the maximum offset is $150 (the amount above the $750 floor), not $135 (15% of $900), because the floor-based cap is lower in that scenario and the regulation applies whichever cap is more protective of the beneficiary. This $750 floor / 15% cap combination applies specifically to nontax federal debt collection under § 285.4; it is distinct from, and more protective than, the rule for federal *tax* debts, where the IRS can levy 15% of Social Security benefits under the Federal Payment Levy Program without a $750 floor protection.
Source: https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-31/subtitle-B/chapter-II/subchapter-A/part-285/subpart-A/section-285.4 ; https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/31/285.4
Verification Status: VERIFY BEFORE FILING — confirm the $750/month and 15% figures are still current (they derive from the $9,000/12-month statutory floor enacted via the Debt Collection Improvement Act of 1996 and have reportedly not been inflation-adjusted since; confirm no more recent rulemaking has changed either number as of the actual analysis/filing date), and pull the live eCFR text directly.
```

**What this means for this client specifically:** An SBA loan guaranty is a federal non-tax debt. If SBA refers the guaranty deficiency to Treasury for collection (which is the standard path once an agency debt becomes "delinquent" and is not otherwise resolved), the debt becomes eligible for the Treasury Offset Program, and TOP can administratively offset up to 15% of his monthly Social Security retirement (OASDI) benefit, subject to the $750/month floor protecting his baseline benefit from being reduced below that level. **This is a real, non-bankruptcy collection exposure that keeps him from being fully "judgment proof"** notwithstanding the broad general anti-attachment protection of § 407(a) discussed in Part 5(b) — because Congress specifically carved federal-debt TOP offset out of that protection via the express-reference mechanism in § 407(b)/§ 3716(c).

Given that his Social Security income is, per the facts, already fully committed to his mortgage payment, even a 15%-of-benefit-above-$750 offset could meaningfully threaten his ability to keep making mortgage payments — directly implicating his stated fear of "losing the house," not through a homestead-exemption failure but through **income-side attrition of the very funds he uses to pay the mortgage**. This is an important, non-obvious point for the memo: the TOP offset risk is not primarily an asset-seizure risk, it is a cash-flow risk that could indirectly cause a mortgage default and foreclosure even though the home itself would otherwise be safe (whether via NM homestead exemption outside bankruptcy or a Chapter 7 exemption inside bankruptcy).

**This TOP exposure is precisely the kind of ongoing, prospective risk that a bankruptcy discharge eliminates** (once the guaranty debt is discharged, it is no longer "legally enforceable" and should no longer be TOP-offset-eligible, subject to the caveat that any pre-petition offset/setoff already effectuated before filing is a separate, harder question not addressed here and warranting cross-reference to Track A/F on setoff-versus-discharge timing issues). It is the strongest affirmative argument, on this record, for why the physician may want to file personal Chapter 7 notwithstanding the "he's probably judgment-proof anyway" analysis in Part 5(b) — the "wait and see" approach leaves him exposed to a specific, quantifiable, recurring monthly offset against the income he needs for his mortgage, whereas Chapter 7 discharge forecloses that exposure permanently and relatively quickly (a matter of months, not years).

## Bottom-Line Recommendations for the Synthesized Memo

1. **Means test is not a real barrier.** Social Security produces $0 CMI, and the SBA guaranty debt is very likely non-consumer debt that takes the case outside § 707(b) altogether (subject to confirming the actual consumer/non-consumer debt split on his real Schedule E/F once filed).
2. **Venue in D.N.M. is proper and essentially unquestionable** under § 1408, reinforced by the pending affiliate (LLC) case; keep this analytically separate from the Track C exemption-domicile question.
3. **Chapter 13 is not a realistic or advisable alternative** — no disposable income to fund a plan, and Social Security is excluded from disposable-income calculations anyway. If he needs to file, Chapter 7 is the right chapter.
4. **He may not be strictly required to file at all** — Social Security and (likely) his home/vehicle equity are largely protected from ordinary creditor process even outside bankruptcy under § 407(a) and NM's own execution-exemption statutes — but this "judgment proof" baseline has one real hole.
5. **The hole is Treasury Offset Program exposure**: up to 15% of his monthly Social Security benefit (above a $750/month floor) can be administratively offset for this federal debt without going through court at all, and because his Social Security is fully committed to his mortgage, that offset risk could precipitate a mortgage default even though the home itself is otherwise protected. This is the strongest reason to actually file rather than simply wait, and should be highlighted prominently to the client.
6. **Non-bankruptcy alternatives (OIC, waiting out the SOL) exist but are uncertain in practice** — reported SBA sluggishness on COVID-EIDL compromises, and unresolved SOL-accrual-date facts — and do not eliminate the TOP exposure the way a discharge would.
