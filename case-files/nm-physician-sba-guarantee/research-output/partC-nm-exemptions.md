## 1. Is New Mexico an "Opt-Out" State? — CORRECTION TO WORKING ASSUMPTION

**Finding: New Mexico is NOT an opt-out state.** This corrects the premise in the task assignment. New Mexico has never enacted a statute "specifically" denying its residents the federal bankruptcy exemptions, so under 11 U.S.C. § 522(b)(2)–(3), a New Mexico debtor may elect **either**:

- the federal exemptions under 11 U.S.C. § 522(d); **or**
- the New Mexico state-law exemptions under NMSA 1978 §§ 42-10-1 through 42-10-13 (as made available to the debtor via 11 U.S.C. § 522(b)(3)(A)).

11 U.S.C. § 522(b)(2) (verbatim, as confirmed by multiple independent lookups):
> "Property listed in this paragraph is property that is specified under subsection (d), unless the State law that is applicable to the debtor under paragraph (3)(A) specifically does not so authorize."

Because New Mexico has no such "specifically does not so authorize" opt-out statute, the federal § 522(d) exemptions remain available to NM debtors as an alternative, in addition to the state exemptions. New Mexico is consistently listed (alongside Alaska, Arkansas, Connecticut, Hawaii, Kentucky, Massachusetts, Michigan, Minnesota, New Hampshire, New Jersey, New York, Oregon, Pennsylvania, Rhode Island, Texas, Vermont, Washington, West Virginia, Wisconsin, D.C., Puerto Rico, and the Virgin Islands) among the minority of jurisdictions that give the debtor a choice.

There is **no old "opt-out" provision at NMSA 1978 § 42-10-2** — that section (titled "Exemptions of persons who support only themselves") was itself **repealed** as part of the sweeping 2023 rewrite of Article 10 (Laws 2023, ch. 104, eff. July 1, 2023) and its content folded into the rewritten § 42-10-1. It was never an opt-out statute; it was a substantive exemption-amount provision for unmarried/single-earner debtors that predates the 2023 consolidation.

**Practical consequence for the client:** Because a debtor must elect one system or the other (no mixing), and because — as shown below — New Mexico's 2023-enhanced state exemptions (especially the $150,000 homestead) are now dramatically more generous than the federal § 522(d) exemptions (currently roughly $27,900 homestead per debtor, plus an "unused homestead" wildcard of roughly $13,950, for cases filed April 1, 2025–March 31, 2028 — **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING against the current Judicial Conference dollar-adjustment notice, next scheduled April 1, 2028]**), the client should almost certainly elect the **New Mexico state exemptions**, not the federal set. This is a strategic choice to be made on Schedule C, not a mandatory result — counsel should still run both sets of numbers against the client's actual equity before electing.

## 2. New Mexico Homestead Exemption — NMSA 1978 § 42-10-9

### Current dollar amount
- **$150,000** — the standard homestead exemption amount for a person claiming a homestead exemption in a domicile that is that person's primary residence.
- **$300,000** — if the claimant's spouse died within the two years preceding the claim, and the deceased spouse would independently have qualified for the homestead exemption had he/she survived.

Language repeatedly and consistently returned from the current codification (law.justia.com's 2024 and 2025 New Mexico Statutes pages, which republish NMSA 1978 § 42-10-9 as amended):
> "A person shall have a homestead exemption in a domicile or land owned by the person that is the primary residence of the person. Such homestead is exempt from attachment, execution or foreclosure by a judgment creditor and from any proceeding of receivers or trustees in insolvency or bankruptcy proceedings and from executors or administrators in probate. ... The amount of the homestead exemption is: (1) one hundred fifty thousand dollars ($150,000); or (2) three hundred thousand dollars ($300,000) if the spouse of the person claiming the exemption died within two years prior to the date of claiming the homestead exemption and if the deceased spouse would have been able to claim the homestead exemption had the deceased spouse survived until the date of claiming the homestead exemption."

The 2023 amendment (Laws 2023, ch. 104, eff. July 1, 2023) also **added a broad definition of "domicile"**:
> "'Domicile' means any shelter or dwelling used by the person as a primary residence and may include a mobile home, trailer, recreational vehicle, outbuilding or other similar shelter, regardless of whether such dwelling complies with relevant housing or building regulations."

and clarified that the homestead exemption **does not** apply to defeat (a) taxes, (b) garnishment, or (c) recorded/perfected liens of mortgagees, lessors, or laborers/materialmen, or **properly perfected liens of secured creditors** generally. This matters for Section 6 below.

### Amendment history
- Pre-2007: $30,000.
- 2007 amendment (eff. June 15, 2007): increased to $60,000.
- **2023 amendment (Laws 2023, ch. 104, eff. July 1, 2023, enacted as Senate Bill 216, signed by Gov. Lujan Grisham, passed the Legislature March 16, 2023):** increased from $60,000 to **$150,000** (with the $300,000 recent-widow(er) enhancement newly added). This was part of a comprehensive rewrite of essentially all of Article 10.
- **CPI inflation adjustment:** The statute (and its 2023 companion amendments to §§ 42-10-1, 42-10-4, and 42-10-10) provides for automatic inflation indexing. Search results are **inconsistent on the precise cadence** — some describe adjustment "every two-year interval ending on July 1" starting July 1, 2023; others describe adjustment "every odd-numbered year on July 1, beginning in 2025." Since July 1, 2025 has already passed as of this memo's July 11, 2026 preparation date, **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING]** whether the $150,000/$300,000 figures have already been adjusted upward by CPI as of a July 1, 2025 index date, and confirm the exact current figure directly from the statute or NM Compilation Commission before relying on it in a filing. Do not assume $150,000 is still precisely accurate without this check — it is very likely slightly higher now (CPI-adjusted), which only helps the client.

### Per person / per debtor / per property; joint ownership
Older (pre-2023) secondary summaries describe: "If the homestead is owned jointly by two persons, each joint owner is entitled to an exemption of sixty thousand dollars ($60,000)" — i.e., doubling for jointly-owned homesteads under the *old* $60,000 regime (producing $120,000 total for a married couple who jointly owned and jointly filed). **I could not confirm, from the sources accessible this session, the verbatim post-2023 text of any analogous joint-owner doubling clause at the current $150,000 level.** The exemption is described in the current statutory text as available to "a person" claiming it in his/her primary residence, which is consistent with a per-person entitlement, and NM bankruptcy practice under the old regime allowed joint owners/joint filers to each claim the exemption (stacking to $120,000 under the old $60,000 cap). **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING]: confirm whether jointly-owned homesteads still allow each of two joint owners/joint debtors to claim a separate $150,000 (potentially $300,000 total for a married couple who both hold title and both file), or whether the 2023 rewrite capped the exemption at $150,000 per property regardless of the number of owners.** This distinction is likely moot for this client if he is the sole title-holder or if only he (not his wife, if he has one) is filing personally — confirm current title/vesting on the residence.

### Durational residency requirement — 11 U.S.C. § 522(b)(3)(A)
Federal law — not New Mexico law — governs *which state's* exemption scheme applies when a debtor has moved between states. 11 U.S.C. § 522(b)(3)(A) provides that a debtor may claim exemptions available under the law of the place where the debtor's domicile has been located for the **730 days** (2 years) immediately preceding the petition date; if the debtor's domicile was not in a single state for that full 730-day period, the applicable law is that of the state where the debtor's domicile was located for the **180 days** immediately preceding the 730-day period, or for the longer portion of that 180-day period. This rule exists to prevent forum-shopping via last-minute relocation to a more generous exemption state.

**Application to this client:** Per the case background, the client is described as a longtime New Mexico resident (an established local physician). Assuming no interstate relocation within the last ~2+ years, the 730/180-day rule is **not an issue** and New Mexico exemptions are squarely available. **Flag for completeness/confirmation only:** counsel should verify with the client (a) how long he has continuously been domiciled in New Mexico, and (b) whether he has any second home or recent change of domicile that could complicate this analysis — but on the facts given, this should be a non-issue.

## 3. New Mexico Vehicle (Motor Vehicle) Exemption

### Current statute and amount
Post-2023, the motor vehicle exemption is codified within the **rewritten NMSA 1978 § 42-10-1** ("Exemptions") — the 2023 amendment (Laws 2023, ch. 104, eff. July 1, 2023) "completely rewrote" this section and consolidated it with what had been § 42-10-2's vehicle provision (§ 42-10-2 was repealed).

- **Current amount: $10,000** — "a person's aggregate interest in motor vehicles is not exceeding ten thousand dollars ($10,000) in value" (as consistently reported from the current codified § 42-10-1 text).
- Prior (pre-July 1, 2023) amount: **$4,000**, under old NMSA 1978 § 42-10-2.

Companion increases in the same 2023 rewrite of § 42-10-1 (useful context, not squarely part of the vehicle question but relevant to protecting the client's other personal property, e.g., medical equipment, furnishings, and personal effects):
- Household goods and furnishings: increased to **$75,000** aggregate.
- Jewelry (beyond wedding/engagement rings, which are separately, unconditionally exempt): increased to **$5,000**.
- Tools of the trade / professional equipment, books, instruments, inventory, supplies reasonably necessary for the debtor's trade or profession: increased to **$15,000**. (This could matter if the physician still holds personal medical instruments/equipment used in practice, as opposed to LLC-owned practice assets, which would be assets of the separately-filing LLC bankruptcy estate, not his.)

As with the homestead figure, these § 42-10-1 dollar amounts are subject to the same CPI-indexing mechanism described in Section 2 above, so the true current (July 2026) figures may be modestly higher than $10,000/$75,000/$5,000/$15,000. **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING].**

## 4. New Mexico Wildcard Exemption — NMSA 1978 § 42-10-10 ("Exemption in Lieu of Homestead")

**Critical clarification of how this exemption actually works (this differs from how the task framed it):** NMSA 1978 § 42-10-10 is not a general-purpose "wildcard" stackable on top of the homestead exemption for any debtor who chooses to leave equity in the homestead exemption unused. Rather, by its terms it is available **only to a resident who does not own a homestead**:

> "Any resident of this state who does not own a homestead shall, in addition to other exemptions, hold exempt real or personal property in the amount of fifteen thousand dollars ($15,000) [previously five thousand dollars ($5,000), pre-2023] in lieu of the homestead exemption."

Because this client **does own** his single-family residence (his homestead), he does not fit the predicate for § 42-10-10 — he takes the (far larger) $150,000/$300,000 homestead exemption under § 42-10-9 instead, not the $15,000 in-lieu-of-homestead exemption. **This is actually favorable for the client** ($150,000+ dwarfs $15,000), but counsel should not plan around "stacking" a § 42-10-10 wildcard on top of the homestead exemption for a homeowner-debtor — that is not how the statute is written. The § 42-10-10 exemption exists so that renters/non-homeowners are not left without any analogous protection.

Also note: New Mexico does not appear to have a separate, freestanding, unconditional general "wildcard" (of the type found in, e.g., the federal § 522(d)(5) "any property" exemption) available to homeowners in addition to the itemized exemptions in §§ 42-10-1 and 42-10-9. Instead, New Mexico's 2023 rewrite substantially raised the itemized category caps ($75,000 household goods, $15,000 tools of trade, $10,000 vehicle, $5,000 jewelry, $150,000/$300,000 homestead) as its mechanism for expanding debtor protection, rather than adding a large discretionary wildcard for homeowners.

**Bottom line for this client:** No separate wildcard is likely needed or available to protect the vehicle further — the $10,000 motor-vehicle exemption under § 42-10-1 should be evaluated on its own against the car's actual equity value; if the car is worth less than $10,000 in equity (very likely for an elderly physician's ordinary personal vehicle, especially if it is not new/luxury and/or has a loan against it), it is separately, fully protected without needing to invoke any wildcard.

## 5. Social Security Income — Protected From Creditors and Excluded From the Means Test

This is one of the strongest, cleanest points in the client's favor, and it is federal law that applies **regardless of which state's exemptions are used**.

### (a) Anti-attachment: 42 U.S.C. § 407(a)
Verbatim text (confirmed via multiple consistent independent source lookups, including Cornell LII and the U.S. Code itself):

> "The right of any person to any future payment under this subchapter shall not be transferable or assignable, at law or in equity, and none of the moneys paid or payable or rights existing under this subchapter shall be subject to execution, levy, attachment, garnishment, or other legal process, or to the operation of any bankruptcy or insolvency law."

42 U.S.C. § 407(b) additionally provides that no other federal or state law may be construed to limit, supersede, or otherwise modify § 407 "except to the extent that it does so by express reference to this section" — a "super-preemption" clause that has repeatedly been read by courts (including the Supreme Court in *Philpott v. Essex County Welfare Board*, 409 U.S. 413 (1973), and *Bennett v. Arkansas*, 485 U.S. 395 (1988) (per curiam)) to make Social Security benefits essentially untouchable by creditors, garnishors, and (per the statute's own express text) bankruptcy/insolvency law. This means Social Security funds are protected **independent of any state exemption statute or dollar cap** — they simply are not "property of the estate" reachable by a Chapter 7 trustee or a judgment creditor such as the SBA/guaranty creditor, so long as the funds are reasonably traceable as Social Security proceeds (commingling in a bank account with other funds can create tracing complications the client should be advised to avoid — e.g., keep Social Security deposits in a segregated account if possible).

### (b) Exclusion from the Chapter 7 means test: 11 U.S.C. § 101(10A)(B)
"Current monthly income" (CMI) is the term used to run the means test under 11 U.S.C. § 707(b). Verbatim/near-verbatim text of § 101(10A), confirmed via multiple consistent independent lookups:

> "The term 'current monthly income' — (A) means the average monthly income from all sources that the debtor receives (or in a joint case the debtor and the debtor's spouse receive) without regard to whether such income is taxable income, derived during the 6-month period ending on— (i) the last day of the calendar month immediately preceding the date of the commencement of the case if the debtor files the schedule of current income required by section 521(a)(1)(B)(ii); or (ii) the date on which current income is determined by the court for purposes of this title if the debtor does not file the schedule of current income required by section 521(a)(1)(B)(ii); and (B) includes any amount paid by any entity other than the debtor (or in a joint case the debtor and the debtor's spouse), on a regular basis for the household expenses of the debtor or the debtor's dependents ..., but excludes benefits received under the Social Security Act, ..."

**This is the critical point for this client's means test:** because his only income is Social Security, and Social Security is statutorily excluded from "current monthly income" under § 101(10A)(B), his CMI for means-test purposes should be **at or near $0**. This places him well under the applicable median-income threshold for a household of his size in New Mexico, meaning the § 707(b) means-test presumption of abuse should not arise, and he should qualify for Chapter 7 on income grounds without needing to run the full Form 122A-2 disposable-income calculation. (Official Form 122A-1 does still require him to *list* Social Security income for informational purposes, but it is excluded from the CMI figure that drives the means test.)

**Practical synthesis:** Social Security income is (1) not reachable by creditors at all (42 U.S.C. § 407(a)), and (2) does not count against him in qualifying for Chapter 7 (11 U.S.C. § 101(10A)(B)). Both protections are independent of the New Mexico exemption scheme and would apply even if he elected federal § 522(d) exemptions instead of state exemptions.

**Verification Status: CONFIRMED** — both 42 U.S.C. § 407(a) and 11 U.S.C. § 101(10A)(B) text were consistently returned, word-for-word, across multiple independent searches including Cornell LII, and the exclusion of Social Security from the means test is black-letter, uncontroversial bankruptcy law with a very large body of confirming case law (e.g., *Anderson v. Cranmer* (In re Cranmer), 697 F.3d 1314 (10th Cir. 2012) (Social Security income excluded from CMI and generally not part of projected disposable income analysis) — 10th Circuit, **BINDING** on New Mexico; cite/verify precise holding before filing).

## 6. Interaction of Homestead/Vehicle Exemptions With the Unsecured SBA Guaranty Debt

**Assumption flagged for client verification:** This analysis assumes the SBA-guaranty debt is a **general unsecured claim** against the client personally — i.e., that neither the SBA nor its loan servicer/successor holds a mortgage, deed of trust, judgment lien, or UCC lien against the client's personal residence or personal vehicle securing the guaranty. Based on general SBA COVID-era EIDL lending practice, loans **above $200,000** typically required (i) a UCC-1 blanket lien against the *business's* assets and (ii) an **unsecured** personal guaranty from ≥20% owners — meaning the personal guaranty itself is ordinarily not secured by a lien on the guarantor's personal residence or vehicle unless the SBA separately required and recorded additional personal collateral (which does happen in some larger EIDL/7(a) files). **This must be verified directly against the actual loan/guaranty documents and any UCC or real property records before finalizing this analysis** — if a lien was in fact recorded against the client's home, the exemption analysis changes materially (a secured creditor's perfected lien generally survives even an otherwise-applicable homestead exemption, subject to any available lien-avoidance remedy under 11 U.S.C. § 522(f) if the lien is a *judicial* lien, as opposed to a mortgage).

**Assuming the guaranty debt is indeed unsecured**, the mechanics are straightforward:

1. In a Chapter 7 case, the trustee's job is to identify non-exempt assets and liquidate them for the benefit of unsecured creditors (which would include the SBA/guaranty creditor, pari passu with any other unsecured creditors).
2. If the client's **equity** in the homestead (fair market value minus valid liens, e.g., any remaining mortgage balance) is at or below the applicable NM homestead exemption ($150,000, or more if CPI-adjusted, or $300,000 if the recent-widower provision applies), that equity is **fully exempt** and the trustee cannot sell the house or otherwise use it to pay unsecured creditors, including the SBA guaranty claim. The trustee would have no incentive to administer an over-encumbered or fully-exempt asset.
3. Likewise, if the client's equity in his vehicle (value minus any auto loan lien) is at or below $10,000 (subject to CPI adjustment), the car is **fully exempt** and equally unreachable by the trustee or by the SBA/guaranty creditor.
4. Because the underlying SBA guaranty debt is (by assumption) unsecured, it has **no independent lien** against either asset — it is simply a claim that shares in whatever non-exempt assets exist in the estate. If there are no (or minimal) non-exempt assets after the homestead and vehicle exemptions are applied, this would functionally be a "no-asset" Chapter 7 case as to the guaranty creditor, and the debt would be discharged (assuming no dischargeability objection is sustained, e.g., under 11 U.S.C. § 523, which is a separate analysis outside this track's scope) without the SBA/guaranty creditor recovering anything from the house or car.
5. Social Security income, per Section 5 above, is also entirely unreachable by the SBA/guaranty creditor both inside and outside bankruptcy, and does not inflate the means-test income figure that could otherwise push the case toward Chapter 13 or dismissal for abuse.

**Net effect:** on the facts as currently understood (unsecured guaranty; ordinary home and car; Social-Security-only income), New Mexico's 2023-enhanced exemption scheme is very likely sufficient to fully protect both the house and the car in a personal Chapter 7 filing, and the client's Social Security income both cannot be reached by the SBA and does not count against him on the means test. The single largest open variable is whether the SBA guaranty is, in fact, wholly unsecured as assumed — this must be confirmed from the loan documents before finalizing any recommendation.

## Key Takeaways for the Broader Memo

1. New Mexico is **not** an opt-out state — the client (or his counsel) will need to affirmatively elect NM state exemptions over federal § 522(d) exemptions on Schedule C; state exemptions are almost certainly the better choice given the 2023 increases.
2. The current NM homestead exemption ($150,000, possibly CPI-adjusted upward as of July 2025) should be more than sufficient to protect a typical physician's personal residence equity, especially net of any remaining mortgage.
3. The current NM motor vehicle exemption ($10,000) should be sufficient to protect an ordinary personal vehicle.
4. The NM "wildcard" (§ 42-10-10, $15,000) is available only to non-homeowners "in lieu of" the homestead exemption — it is not an additional stackable cushion for this client, but he does not need it given the much larger homestead exemption he qualifies for instead.
5. Social Security income is untouchable by the SBA/guaranty creditor (42 U.S.C. § 407(a)) and does not count against the client on the Chapter 7 means test (11 U.S.C. § 101(10A)(B)) — this should make Chapter 7 eligibility straightforward on the income side.
6. The linchpin factual assumption — that the SBA guaranty debt is unsecured against the client's personal home and car — must be verified against the actual guaranty and any recorded liens before this analysis can be treated as final.
