## 1. Which COVID-era SBA loan programs required a personal guarantee, and at what thresholds?

### 1A. PPP (Paycheck Protection Program) — CARES Act § 1102; 15 U.S.C. § 636(a)(36)

**Conclusion: PPP loans generally did NOT require a personal guarantee or collateral, and were
non-recourse against individual shareholders/members/partners of the borrower — but only if loan
proceeds were used for eligible purposes and the borrower was in fact eligible.**

- The CARES Act added a new subsection (36) to the Small Business Act's 7(a) loan authority, 15
  U.S.C. § 636(a)(36), to create the PPP. Subsection (36)(F)(v) (as summarized in search results
  discussing the interim final rules) is described as **"the CARES Act's nonrecourse
  provision, 15 U.S.C. § 636(a)(36)(F)(v), [which] limits SBA's recourse against individual
  shareholders, members, or partners of a PPP borrower for nonpayment of a PPP loan only if the
  borrower is an eligible recipient of the loan."**
  [VERIFY BEFORE FILING — pull 15 U.S.C. § 636(a)(36)(F)(v) directly from uscode.house.gov or
  govinfo.gov; this agent could not directly fetch the statutory text due to 403 blocks].

- SBA's first PPP interim final rule, *Business Loan Program Temporary Changes; Paycheck
  Protection Program*, 85 Fed. Reg. 20811 (Apr. 15, 2020) (SBA Docket No. SBA-2020-0015), is
  the operative IFR implementing this. Secondary-source characterization: **"The SBA
  guarantees 100% of PPP loans, and the loans are non-recourse provided that the funds are used
  for authorized purposes (payroll, healthcare, etc.), and no personal guarantees or collateral
  are required."**
  Source: https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2020/04/15/2020-07673/business-loan-program-temporary-changes-paycheck-protection-program
  [VERIFY BEFORE FILING — this agent could not directly open the Federal Register page (403);
  confirm the "no personal guarantee, no collateral" language against the actual IFR text,
  which should appear in the FAQ/Q&A portion of the rule].

- **Important limitation on non-recourse protection — this matters for the physician-client's
  risk assessment:** the non-recourse shield is conditioned on (1) borrower eligibility at the
  time of application, and (2) proper use of proceeds. Per secondary-source summary: **"If one
  of your shareholders, members, or partners uses PPP funds for unauthorized purposes, SBA will
  have recourse against the shareholder, member, or partner for the unauthorized use,"** and
  **"if a shareholder, member or partner misuses funds, the nonrecourse provision of the CARES
  Act will no longer apply to such shareholder, member or partner."** This means the physician's
  personal exposure on any PPP loan turns on (a) whether the LLC was eligible for PPP in the
  first place, and (b) whether PPP proceeds were used exclusively for payroll and other
  permitted costs. If the LLC's PPP loan(s) were used properly and the LLC was eligible, the PPP
  debt itself should **not** be a source of personal guaranty exposure regardless of whether the
  physician signed a certification as an authorized representative of the LLC (a certification
  of authority is not the same thing as a personal guaranty of repayment).
  [VERIFY BEFORE FILING — confirm exact "misuse" standard and its interaction with the
  non-recourse provision in the IFR/SOP text.]

- **Practical implication:** Unless the physician separately and explicitly signed a personal
  guaranty specifically covering the PPP loan (unusual, and inconsistent with the program's
  design), PPP debt should not by itself create personal liability. Counsel should verify the
  actual PPP loan documents (SBA Form 2483/2483-SD and lender note) to confirm no personal
  guaranty rider was attached — some lenders reportedly still asked for personal guaranty
  language notwithstanding SBA guidance (an area worth factual confirmation with the client).

### 1B. COVID-19 EIDL (Economic Injury Disaster Loan) — Small Business Act § 7(b)(2); 15 U.S.C. § 636(b)

**Conclusion: A personal guarantee WAS required for COVID-19 EIDL loans and advances exceeding
$200,000. Below that threshold, no personal guaranty was required (though collateral — a UCC
blanket lien on business assets — was typically required above roughly $25,000).**

Multiple independent secondary sources converge on the same figures:

- **Under $25,000**: no collateral, no personal guarantee.
- **$25,001–$200,000**: collateral (UCC lien on business assets) required, but no personal
  guarantee required.
- **Over $200,000**: personal guarantee required, from any individual or entity owning 20% or
  more of the applicant business (consistent with SBA's general 20%-ownership guaranty rule
  applicable across SBA loan programs, discussed in Part 2 below).

Representative secondary-source quotations:
- **"According to SBA's own COVID EIDL program guidelines, personal guarantees were only
  required for loan amounts over $200,000."**
  Source: https://sba-attorneys.com/sba-updates/the-200-000-threshold-how-your-eidl-loan-size-affects-personal-liability
- **"The CARES Act specifically authorized the SBA to waive personal guarantees on EIDL loans
  and advances of $200,000 or less."**
  Source: https://getoutofdebt.org/248676/eidl-personal-guarantee-under-200k
- **"In response to the COVID-19 pandemic and an increase in demand for EIDL, the CARES Act
  authorized the SBA Administrator to waive any rules related to the personal guarantee on
  advances and loans of not more than $200,000."**
  (search-engine synthesis of SBA COVID EIDL procedural notices)
- **"Personal guarantees were required for SBA COVID EIDLs over $200,000, and any individual or
  entity that owned 20% or more of the applicant business was required to sign a guarantee. This

  Source: https://www.firstcard.app/learn/are-eidl-loans-personally-guaranteed

**Distinction from the pre-COVID/general disaster-loan rule:** The general (non-COVID) SBA
disaster loan regulation, 13 CFR Part 123, ties collateral requirements to a $10,000+ threshold
for physical disaster business loans (13 CFR § 123.202, discussing when collateral — as opposed
to personal guaranty specifically — becomes required), which is a materially lower and different
regime than the COVID-19 EIDL $200,000 personal-guaranty threshold. This confirms the task's
premise that the COVID EIDL program used a *relaxed, COVID-specific* personal-guarantee
threshold (raised to $200,000) rather than the ordinary disaster-loan collateral rule. Counsel
should not conflate the two regimes. [VERIFY BEFORE FILING — confirm 13 CFR § 123.202 language

**Bottom line for the physician-client:** Whether he has personal EIDL guaranty exposure turns
entirely on the face amount of each COVID-19 EIDL loan taken by the LLC. If any single COVID-19
EIDL loan (loans are not aggregated the same way physical disaster loans are, though counsel
should confirm this) exceeded $200,000, a personal guaranty from the physician as 100% owner was
required by SBA policy and — per the facts stated — he "personally signed guarantees on at least
some of these loans," which is consistent with an EIDL loan or loans above $200,000. Any EIDL
loan at or under $200,000 should have carried no personal guaranty; if the physician's loan
documents show a signed guaranty on a sub-$200,000 EIDL loan, that is worth flagging as a
potential lender/SBA overreach (see the "missing guarantee" issue noted in Part 3 below) —
though as a practical matter, this is more relevant to a defense/negotiation posture than to
whether personal bankruptcy is needed.

### 1C. SBA Debt Relief Program — CARES Act § 1112 (Subsidy for Certain Loan Payments)

**Conclusion: Section 1112 did NOT create any new personal guaranty. It simply had SBA pay six
months of principal, interest, and fees (later extended/modified in duration and amount by the
Economic Aid Act and Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2021) on qualifying PRE-EXISTING 7(a),
504, and Microloan loans. It piggybacked entirely on whatever guaranty already existed on the
underlying pre-COVID loan — it did not add, expand, or independently trigger any guaranty
obligation.**

- **"Under Section 1112 of the CARES Act, the SBA is required to subsidize certain existing and
  new SBA loans by paying the principal, interest and any associated fees on such loans for a
  six (6) month period."**
  Source: SBA Procedural Notice 5000-20041, *Further Guidance on the Implementation of Section
  1112 of the CARES Act, Subsidy for Certain Loan Payments, for the 7(a) Loan Program*,
  https://www.sba.gov/document/procedural-notice-5000-20041-further-guidance-implementation-section-1112-cares-act-subsidy-certain-loan-payments-7a-loan-program
  [VERIFY BEFORE FILING — direct fetch blocked; confirm via PACER/Westlaw or a successful direct
  pull].
- **"Qualified SBA loans include existing qualified SBA loans (made before March 27, 2020),
  including those that are in deferment and those that are not in deferment,"** and **"Section
  1112 requires the SBA to pay the principal, interest and any associated fees that are owed on
  certain existing SBA loans for a six-month period beginning with the next payment due on these
  loans,"** and **"The payments to be made by the SBA are not deferments. These payments will be
  forgiven, and borrowers will not be expected to make these payments later."**
  Source: https://www.ccsb.com/cares-act-provides-subsidies-for-loan-payment-relief-on-certain-sba-loans/
- No source located — after multiple targeted searches — describes Section 1112 as creating,
  modifying, or requiring execution of a new personal guaranty instrument. The § 1112 subsidy is
  structured as a payment by SBA directly to the lender on the borrower's behalf on the loan that
  already existed; it is not a new extension of credit and does not appear on SBA Form 148 or any
  new guaranty document. **This is consistent with the task's working assumption**, but this
  agent could not find an SBA source that affirmatively states "no new guaranty is required for
  § 1112" — the absence of any such requirement in any procedural notice, IFR, or secondary
  source is the basis for this conclusion, which should be treated as a negative inference rather
  than an affirmatively confirmed rule. [VERIFY BEFORE FILING — if the LLC had a pre-existing
  7(a) or 504 loan (with the physician's original personal guaranty from origination) that
  received § 1112 subsidy payments, pull the ORIGINAL loan's guaranty document (not a § 1112
  document, because none should exist) to confirm the physician's personal exposure traces back
  to the original 7(a)/504 guaranty, not to anything created by § 1112.]

**Practical implication:** If the LLC's debt stack includes a pre-existing 7(a)/504 loan that
received § 1112 subsidy payments, the physician's personal guaranty exposure on that loan is
whatever guaranty he signed when that loan originated (pre-COVID) — governed by the same SBA
Form 148 doctrine discussed in Part 2. The § 1112 subsidy simply reduced (via forgiveness) six-plus
months of payments; it does not create an independent, additional layer of personal liability,
and by the same token it also does not extinguish or modify the original guaranty's terms.

## 2. Legal effect and enforceability of an SBA personal guaranty — contract/suretyship doctrine

### SBA Form 148 (Unconditional Guarantee) / Form 148L (Unconditional Limited Guarantee)

SBA Form 148 is the SBA's standard personal-guaranty instrument used across its 7(a) loan
programs (and, per multiple secondary sources, the same form/format used for COVID-19 EIDL
guaranties above the $200,000 threshold). Key characteristics reported in search results
(pending direct-document verification):

- **General guaranty threshold, 13 CFR § 120.160**: **"Holders of at least a 20 percent
  ownership interest generally must guarantee the loan,"** and SBA (or a delegated SBA Lender)
  **"may require other appropriate individuals or entities to provide full or limited
  guarantees of the loan without regard to the percentage of their ownership interests."**
  Source: https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-13/chapter-I/part-120/subpart-A/subject-group-ECFRa096ec067b9c6cf/section-120.160
  [VERIFY BEFORE FILING — direct fetch of eCFR/Cornell LII text blocked in this session; confirm
  the exact regulatory text, and confirm whether 13 CFR § 120.160 governs 7(a)/504 loans
  specifically as opposed to the separate COVID-19 EIDL guaranty policy discussed in Part 1B,
  which arises under the disaster-loan authority (15 U.S.C. § 636(b)) rather than § 636(a)].
  As the physician is described as the 100% sole member of the LLC, he unambiguously falls within
  any ownership-percentage guaranty trigger regardless of the exact threshold used.

- **Independent, unconditional obligation.** Standard commercial-guaranty doctrine — applicable
  to SBA Form 148 — treats the guaranty as **a contract separate and independent from the
  underlying note.** Secondary-source summary: **"A key characteristic of a guaranty is that,
  legally, the contract of guaranty is deemed to be separate and independent from the contract
  made by the borrower in signing a promissory note. While the guaranty and the promissory note
  are clearly part of the same business transaction, from a legal perspective they are
  technically two different, albeit related, obligations."** This is the doctrinal basis for the
  central point of concern in this engagement: **the LLC's Chapter 7 filing and discharge does
  NOT discharge the physician's personal guaranty obligation**, because (a) the LLC's Chapter 7
  discharges the LLC's debts, not the guarantor's separate contractual obligation, and (b) even
  if it did, only individuals (not LLCs) receive a personal discharge under Chapter 7, 11 U.S.C.
  § 727(a)(1) (limiting discharge to individual debtors) — this statutory point is more properly
  developed in another research track but is noted here because it flows directly from the
  "independent obligation" doctrine of guaranty law.

- **Waiver of defenses.** SBA Form 148 contains a broad waiver-of-defenses section. Secondary
  source description: **"SBA Form 148 contains a section on 'Rights, notices, and defenses that
  guarantor waives,' with the guarantor waiving them to the extent permitted by law,"** including
  waivers of defenses based on: the lender's failure to obtain, perfect, or maintain a security
  interest; improper valuation or inspection of collateral; changes in collateral value or
  neglect of collateral; the lender's failure to make claims upon the borrower's death or
  disability; overstatement of the borrower's financial condition; lender errors in loan
  documentation or administration; the lender's failure to seek payment from the borrower or
  other sources first (i.e., no exhaustion-of-remedies requirement); and impairment of the
  guarantor's suretyship rights.
  Source (form text, described in secondary sources):
  https://www.sba.gov/document/sba-form-148-unconditional-guarantee and
  https://www.sba.gov/document/support-148-148l-inst-instructions-use-sba-form-148-unconditional-guarantee-and-sba-form-148l-unconditional-limited
  [VERIFY BEFORE FILING — direct PDF fetch was blocked (403) in this session for both the SBA
  Form 148 PDF itself (https://www.sba.gov/sites/sbagov/files/2020-09/sba_elending_clc_forms148-508.pdf)
  and the instructions document; counsel must pull the actual form text and, critically, the
  actual EXECUTED guaranty document(s) the physician signed, since lenders occasionally deviate
  from or supplement the standard form].

- **Federal choice-of-law / preemption clause.** Secondary-source description: **"When SBA is
  the holder, the Note and this Guarantee will be construed and enforced under federal law,
  including SBA regulations. ... Guarantor may not claim or assert any local or state law against
  SBA to deny any obligation, defeat any claim of SBA, or preempt federal law."** This matters
  because it forecloses reliance on state-law suretyship defenses (e.g., state anti-deficiency
  statutes, state guaranty-specific consumer protections) once SBA itself is the holder of the
  guaranty (as opposed to a private lender holding the unguaranteed/non-SBA portion). Note that
  while the loan is held by a private lender (pre-purchase by SBA), state law and the lender's
  own guaranty form may still govern; the federal-law/preemption clause is triggered specifically
  when SBA has purchased the guaranteed portion and become holder.

- **Attorneys' fees and costs.** Secondary-source description: the guaranty **"obligates the
  guarantor to reimburse every dollar the lender spends enforcing the instrument."**
  [VERIFY BEFORE FILING].

- **General suretyship-law backdrop (non-SBA-specific, standard commercial law, useful as
  interpretive gloss):** **"Because of the independence of a guaranty from the underlying
  promissory note, it is important that the guaranty be an 'absolute and unconditional' one and
  that it contain a waiver of defenses arising from the lender's interactions with the
  borrower,"** and **"a waiver of defenses in a commercial guaranty is enforceable"** as a general
  matter, subject to some limits (e.g., a waiver of "absolute and unconditional" boilerplate does
  not necessarily waive a statute-of-limitations defense, per general commercial guaranty
  caselaw discussed in secondary sources such as Law.com's "Waivers of Defenses in Guaranties:
  Unconditional or Unenforceable?" (Feb. 2023),
  https://www.law.com/newyorklawjournal/2023/02/01/waivers-of-defenses-in-guaranties-unconditional-or-unenforceable/).
  This is general commercial law, not SBA-specific caselaw, and is offered as background
  doctrine, not as binding authority on an SBA guaranty. [VERIFY BEFORE FILING].

**Net conclusion on Question 2:** If the physician signed an SBA Form 148 (or equivalent
lender-form) guaranty on the EIDL loan(s) exceeding $200,000, or on a pre-existing 7(a)/504 loan
now subsidized under § 1112, that guaranty is very likely enforceable as a broad, independent,
largely defense-free obligation under federal law, separate from the LLC's own liability and
NOT discharged by the LLC's Chapter 7. This is the central fact pattern driving the "does the
physician need to file personal bankruptcy too" question and should be the headline finding of
this track for the synthesis memo.

## 4. SBA/Treasury practice of pursuing individual guarantors after the business entity fails or files bankruptcy

**Conclusion: Yes — SBA/Treasury has a documented, active (if administratively troubled)
practice of pursuing defaulted COVID-19 EIDL debt, including from personal guarantors, primarily

### Statutory/regulatory framework — 31 U.S.C. § 3711 and the Federal Claims Collection Standards

- **31 U.S.C. § 3711** (Collection and Compromise) is the general statute authorizing (and in
  some circumstances requiring) federal agencies to attempt collection of, compromise, or refer
  delinquent debt, implemented jointly by Treasury and DOJ regulations known as the Federal
  Claims Collection Standards (31 C.F.R. Chapter IX / former 4 C.F.R. Parts 101–105).

  Cornell LII text blocked in this session].
- **Referral timing**: **"Under 31 U.S.C. § 3711, federal agencies must refer delinquent nontax
  debt to Treasury for collection once the debt reaches 180 days past due and internal resolution

### Effect of the LLC's own Chapter 7 filing on SBA's ability to pursue the guarantor

No source located in this research track states or implies that a Chapter 7 filing by the
primary borrower (here, the LLC) in any way limits, stays, or extinguishes SBA's or Treasury's
ability to pursue a non-debtor personal guarantor. This is consistent with black-letter
bankruptcy law: the automatic stay under 11 U.S.C. § 362(a) protects the debtor (the LLC) and
property of its estate, not non-debtor guarantors, and a corporate/LLC Chapter 7 discharge under
11 U.S.C. § 727 is in any event only available to individual debtors — an LLC does not receive a
discharge at all in Chapter 7 (it is liquidated and dissolved), which independently means there
is no discharge that could ever reach the guarantor's separate obligation. (This last point is a
straightforward statutory conclusion, not dependent on any COVID-specific source, and is flagged
for cross-reference with whichever track is covering core Chapter 7/discharge mechanics.)

**Bottom-line answer to the client's question, based on this track's research alone:** The LLC's
Chapter 7 filing will not, by itself, protect the physician from SBA/Treasury collection efforts
on any personal guaranty he validly signed (most likely on an EIDL loan or loans exceeding
$200,000, and/or on a pre-existing 7(a)/504 loan that received § 1112 subsidy payments). SBA and
Treasury have real, currently-escalating (as of 2026) tools to pursue him personally — most
prominently non-judicial Treasury offset against Social Security and other federal payments,
administrative wage garnishment, and credit reporting, with DOJ civil litigation a less-used but
available backstop. Whether personal bankruptcy is advisable for him is a separate
cost-benefit/discharge-scope analysis properly addressed by the tracks covering individual
Chapter 7 eligibility, means testing, and the dischargeability of guaranty debt — but this
track's research confirms the predicate: the personal guaranty exposure is real, is legally
independent of the LLC's bankruptcy, and SBA has both the authority and (increasingly, in 2026)
the practical apparatus to collect on it.
