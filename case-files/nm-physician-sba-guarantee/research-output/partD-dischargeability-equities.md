# Track D: Dischargeability of SBA COVID Debt in Chapter 7 and Physician/COVID Equitable Considerations

**Prepared for:** Preliminary bankruptcy research memo — 82-year-old physician, personal guarantor of LLC's PPP/EIDL loans, LLC filing Chapter 7, physician-guarantor may need to file personal Chapter 7
**Scope:** Pure legal research (no drafting of pleadings). Answers questions 1–5 as assigned.

---

## BOTTOM LINE UP FRONT

1. **There is no special "physician COVID hardship" discharge doctrine.** Extensive searching (CourtListener-indexed opinions, Google Scholar-indexed opinions, published and unpublished bankruptcy decisions, secondary sources) turned up **no case in which a court recognized, applied, or even seriously entertained** a special leniency rule, an undue-hardship-style standard, or an equitable exception to dischargeability because a debtor (a) took out COVID-era SBA debt, or (b) is/was a physician or healthcare provider who used the money to keep a practice open. This should be reported to the client plainly: it does not exist as a recognized doctrine, and counsel should not represent to the client that it does.
2. **Ordinary Chapter 7 dischargeability analysis governs**, full stop. SBA guaranty debt (PPP, EIDL) is a normal unsecured debt. It is discharged unless it fits one of the enumerated exceptions in 11 U.S.C. § 523(a) — most realistically here, §§ 523(a)(2)(A)/(B) (fraud / false financial statement) or, in more unusual fact patterns, § 523(a)(4) (fiduciary fraud/defalcation, embezzlement, larceny). Absent actual misrepresentation in the loan application or loan agreement, the debt is dischargeable regardless of the debtor's motives, occupation, age, or good character.
3. **Where the reported cases do exist, they run in exactly the opposite direction from what the referring attorney is hoping for**: every located case involving EIDL/PPP nondischargeability litigation is a fraud case — the debtor made an affirmative misrepresentation or omission on the loan application or loan agreement (misuse of funds, undisclosed contingent liabilities, false certifications). None of them credit the debtor's COVID-era motives as a defense; if anything, the COVID emergency purpose of the funds becomes evidence *against* the debtor when the money is diverted to something else. This is useful for the memo precisely because it show what *does* generate a nondischargeability fight (actual misrepresentation) versus what does not (ordinary inability to repay a loan taken out in good faith to keep a practice running).
4. **Section 707(b) is not a live threat here.** The presumption-of-abuse means test under § 707(b)(2) applies only when "current monthly income" (which by definition excludes Social Security benefits, 11 U.S.C. § 101(10A)(B)) triggers it, and § 707(b)(1) itself only reaches cases where the debtor's debts are "primarily consumer debts." A guaranty of a business's PPP/EIDL debt is non-consumer, business-purpose debt. If that guaranty debt (plus any other business debt) exceeds 50% of total debt, § 707(b) presumptively does not apply at all, and the physician's good motives become irrelevant to a threshold question that never arises.
5. The physician's story is not wasted, however — it has a real, but different, role: (a) backstop mitigating context if a § 707(b)(3) "totality of the circumstances" argument is ever raised by a trustee or UST despite the above (unlikely on these facts), (b) leverage in any pre-bankruptcy settlement/Offer-in-Compromise negotiation with the SBA/lender, and (c) reassurance to an anxious client that nothing about what he did was wrongful — dischargeability doesn't require the debtor to prove his conduct was noble, it only requires that the debt not fall within § 523(a)'s exceptions.

## 1. Threshold Point: § 523(a) governs; there is no general "undue hardship" standard for SBA debt

### 1.1 The statutory framework

Under 11 U.S.C. § 727, an individual Chapter 7 debtor receives a discharge of all pre-petition debts **except** those described in 11 U.S.C. § 523(a). Section 523(a) is a closed, enumerated list — courts do not import equitable exceptions or add categories to it. The relevant sub-paragraphs, confirmed against multiple sources (Cornell LII text as reflected in secondary-source quotations, FindLaw's codification, and bankruptcy-court opinions quoting the statute) include, among others:

- **§ 523(a)(1)** — certain taxes and customs duties
- **§ 523(a)(2)(A)** — debts obtained by "false pretenses, a false representation, or actual fraud, other than a statement respecting the debtor's or an insider's financial condition"
- **§ 523(a)(2)(B)** — debts obtained by use of "a statement in writing — (i) that is materially false; (ii) respecting the debtor's or an insider's financial condition; (iii) on which the creditor to whom the debtor is liable for such money, property, services, or credit reasonably relied; and (iv) that the debtor caused to be made or published with intent to deceive"
- **§ 523(a)(4)** — "fraud or defalcation while acting in a fiduciary capacity, embezzlement, or larceny"
- **§ 523(a)(5)** — domestic support obligations
- **§ 523(a)(6)** — "willful and malicious injury by the debtor to another entity or to the property of another entity"
- **§ 523(a)(7)** — certain fines, penalties, and forfeitures payable to a governmental unit
- **§ 523(a)(8)** — educational loans/benefit overpayments, discharged only on a showing of "undue hardship"
- **§ 523(a)(9)** — DUI-related death/injury debts
- **§ 523(a)(13)** — restitution ordered under Title 18 (criminal restitution)
- **§ 523(a)(19)** — securities-fraud-related debts

### 1.2 There is no "undue hardship"/motive-based exception for ordinary unsecured debt, including SBA guaranty debt

The "undue hardship" standard is a creature of § 523(a)(8) **only**, and it applies **only** to student loans and other qualifying educational obligations. The two competing judicial tests are:

- **Brunner test** (majority approach, three-part): (1) the debtor cannot maintain a "minimal" standard of living for herself and dependents if forced to repay; (2) additional circumstances exist indicating this state of affairs is likely to persist for a significant portion of the loan repayment period; and (3) the debtor has made good-faith efforts to repay.
- **Totality-of-the-circumstances test** (minority approach): looks at the debtor's ability to maintain a minimal standard of living while repaying, without requiring a separate "good faith" showing, considering factors like the ratio of student debt to total debt, disability, efforts to defer, and whether discharging the student loan was the dominant purpose of the filing.

```
Doctrine: Brunner test / totality-of-circumstances test for § 523(a)(8) undue hardship
Source: American Bar Association, "Elements of Undue Hardship Discharge of Student Loans Checklist," https://www.americanbar.org/groups/gpsolo/resources/ereport/2026-january/elements-undue-hardship-discharge-student-loans-checklist/ ; U.S. Dep't of Education, "Undue Hardship Discharge of Title IV Loans in Bankruptcy Adversary Proceedings" (Updated Aug. 5, 2024), https://fsapartners.ed.gov/knowledge-center/library/dear-colleague-letters/2023-10-19/undue-hardship-discharge-title-iv-loans-bankruptcy-adversary-proceedings-updated-august-5-2024
Verification Status: VERIFY BEFORE FILING (secondary sources; confirm current DOE guidance and any post-2024 case law shifts against Brunner, e.g., trend toward the totality test after DOE's 2022 guidance)
```

**Critically, nothing in § 523(a)(8) or its case law extends this "undue hardship" concept to SBA business debt, PPP loans, EIDL loans, or personal guaranties of business debt.** No case located in this research applies a Brunner-style or totality-of-circumstances "undue hardship" test outside the student-loan/educational-benefit context. SBA guaranty debt is not listed in § 523(a)(8), is not an "educational benefit," and courts have no textual hook to import that standard into an SBA guaranty dispute.

**Conclusion on Point 1:** The physician's SBA guaranty debt is dischargeable in his personal Chapter 7 **unless** the SBA/lender can prove one of the § 523(a) exceptions applies — realistically, only § 523(a)(2)(A)/(B) (fraud/false financial statement) or, on unusual facts, § 523(a)(4) (fiduciary fraud, embezzlement, larceny — this would require some fiduciary relationship beyond an ordinary loan guaranty and is very unlikely to apply to a garden-variety SBA guarantor). There is no fraud exception analog for "the debtor had good motives" or "the debtor is a physician." Good motive and occupation are legally irrelevant to whether the debt discharges; they are not a defense a creditor must overcome, and they are not a basis a debtor must invoke, because the debt is dischargeable by default.

## 2. § 707(b) "abuse" analysis — not a live issue on these facts

### 2.1 The means test's income trigger doesn't reach Social Security income

The Chapter 7 "means test" under § 707(b)(2) operates off "current monthly income" (CMI) as defined in 11 U.S.C. § 101(10A). That definition **expressly excludes** Social Security Act benefits from CMI.

For an 82-year-old physician whose income is primarily or entirely Social Security, this means the means test calculation on Form 122A-1/122A-2 will show little or no "current monthly income" subject to the means test in the first place, which forecloses a presumption of abuse arising from income-based calculations. (Tenth Circuit authority has also confirmed Social Security income is excluded from means-test/good-faith analysis in the Chapter 13 context — persuasive by analogy.)

### 2.2 § 707(b)(1)'s "primarily consumer debts" gate

More fundamentally, § 707(b)(1) itself only authorizes dismissal/conversion for abuse "in a case filed by an individual debtor under this chapter **whose debts are primarily consumer debts**." "Consumer debt" is defined in § 101(8) as debt "incurred by an individual primarily for a personal, family, or household purpose." A personal guaranty of a business's PPP/EIDL loan is a **business-purpose debt**, not a consumer debt — it was incurred to support the LLC/medical practice, not for personal, family, or household purposes.

```
Statute: 11 U.S.C. § 707(b)(1)
Text (per secondary-source quotation): "After notice and a hearing, the court, on its own motion or on a motion by the United States trustee, trustee (or bankruptcy administrator, if any), or any party in interest, may dismiss a case filed by an individual debtor under this chapter whose debts are primarily consumer debts... if it finds that the granting of relief would be an abuse of the provisions of this chapter."
Source: Cornell LII, https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/11/707 ; Gensburg Calandriello & Kanter, P.C., "Section 707(b)(1) – Primarily Consumer Debts," https://www.gcklegal.com/section-707b1-primarily-consumer-debts/
Verification Status: VERIFY BEFORE FILING (confirm exact current text via govinfo.gov)
```

If the physician's guaranty debt (plus any other business-related debt) constitutes more than 50% of his total scheduled debt — the majority-view threshold for "primarily consumer debts" — then § 707(b) does not apply to his case at all, regardless of income. This should be confirmed once his full debt schedule is known (i.e., does he have significant separate consumer debt — credit cards, medical bills, a mortgage — that could push the "primarily consumer" balance the other way?). Assuming the SBA guaranty debt dominates his liabilities, § 707(b) abuse dismissal is not a realistic risk, and the physician's motives/occupation are moot to this analysis because the gating question (consumer vs. non-consumer debt) is answered by the debt's *origin*, not the debtor's character.

### 2.3 If § 707(b)(3) "totality of the circumstances" is ever invoked

Even where § 707(b)(1)'s consumer-debt gate is not met, or in the rare case a trustee raises bad faith under a debtor's overall financial situation, courts look to a fact-specific totality-of-circumstances inquiry (financial need, ability to repay, etc.). This is where the physician's COVID-era story becomes *relevant as equitable context* — not as a freestanding doctrine, but as one fact among many bearing on good faith. This is a fallback point, not the primary defense, since § 707(b) is unlikely to be implicated at all on these facts.

## 4. § 523(a)(2)(B) screening checklist for counsel — false financial statement exposure

Before advising the client the SBA guaranty debt is "safely dischargeable," counsel should have the client's loan application file (EIDL/PPP application, any required financial statements, the Loan Agreement/Note, and the personal guaranty document itself) reviewed against the elements of § 523(a)(2)(B). The SBA/lender bears the burden of proving each element by a preponderance of the evidence:

1. **A statement in writing** — the writing need not be a free-standing "financial statement" document; courts have found emails and certifications within a loan application/agreement sufficient.
2. **That is materially false** — a false statement about a specific, identifiable aspect of financial condition (assets, liabilities, income, contingent liabilities/pending litigation, use-of-funds certifications, existing debt).
3. **Respecting the debtor's or an insider's financial condition** — courts (post-*Lamar, Archer & Cofrin, LLP v. Appling*, 584 U.S. 709 (2018), which held a statement about a single asset can qualify) construe this broadly to include statements about specific assets/liabilities, not just formal balance-sheet-style statements.
4. **On which the creditor reasonably relied** — judged by an objective "reasonably cautious lender" standard; SBA/lenders typically submit loan-officer declarations describing reliance (as in *Schoenmann*, above).
5. **Made or published with intent to deceive** — can be shown by circumstantial evidence, including reckless disregard for the truth in some circuits.

**Practical screening questions for counsel to ask the client / review in the file:**
- Did the EIDL/PPP application or loan agreement require disclosure of "contingent liabilities," pending litigation, judgments, or existing debts? Was anything omitted (cf. *Schoenmann*)?
- Did the application or agreement include a certification about the intended *use* of funds (e.g., "working capital," payroll)? Were funds in fact used consistently with that certification, or diverted to something else (cf. *Schoenmann*, *Sarfo*)?
- Were revenue, payroll, or employee-count figures on the application accurate as of the certification date?
- Was the client's or the LLC's litigation, bankruptcy, or criminal-history status (if any) truthfully disclosed, if the application asked (cf. Dr. Bishai matter, referenced in search results, involving alleged nondisclosure of a pending indictment on a PPP application)?

If the file is clean on these points — which appears likely given the facts as described (an existing practice using loan proceeds to keep operating during COVID, not diverting funds to unrelated purposes) — there is no §523(a)(2)(B) exposure, and the guaranty debt should discharge in the ordinary course.

## 5. The physician/COVID narrative's proper (limited but real) role in the memo

Although there is no freestanding "physician COVID hardship" discharge doctrine, counsel should not discard the narrative — it has three legitimate, more modest uses:

1. **§ 707(b)(3) totality-of-circumstances fallback.** If, contrary to the analysis in Section 2 above, a trustee or the U.S. Trustee ever raised a bad-faith/abuse argument, the physician's story (kept a practice open through a public-health emergency, took on debt for a legitimate business purpose, income now limited to Social Security at age 82) is exactly the kind of equitable narrative that totality-of-the-circumstances review credits. It is a defensive fact pattern, not an offensive doctrine — it rebuts a hypothetical bad-faith argument rather than establishing an affirmative discharge right.
2. **Leverage in SBA settlement/Offer-in-Compromise negotiation short of bankruptcy.** The SBA has an administrative offer-in-compromise process for defaulted SBA loans. A sympathetic, well-documented narrative (age, retirement, fixed Social Security income, legitimate COVID-era business purpose, no misuse of funds) is classic settlement leverage in that administrative process, wholly apart from bankruptcy doctrine. This is worth flagging to the client as a parallel or alternative track to a Chapter 7 filing.
3. **Client reassurance.** Dischargeability turns on the absence of fraud, not on the presence of virtue. Counsel can accurately tell the client that his conduct in keeping the practice open was not wrongful, and — separately and more importantly — that its wrongfulness or rightfulness is not what the discharge analysis turns on at all. The debt discharges because it is ordinary dischargeable debt, full stop, not because the client's motives earn him leniency. This framing may be the most practically useful takeaway for client communication, even though it is legally unremarkable.
