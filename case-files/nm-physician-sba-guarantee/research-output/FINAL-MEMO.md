## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY — DIRECT ANSWERS TO THE REFERRING ATTORNEY'S QUESTIONS

### Q1: "Will the doctor also have to file Chapter 7 because he signed personal guarantees?"

**No — not automatically, and not as a legal consequence of the LLC's
bankruptcy.** Two independent doctrines confirm this:

- **11 U.S.C. § 524(e)** provides that discharge of a debt of the debtor (the
  LLC) "does not affect the liability of any other entity" on that debt.
  Guarantors remain on the hook regardless of what happens to the primary
  obligor.
- More fundamentally: **the LLC will not even receive a technical
  "discharge."** Under **11 U.S.C. § 727(a)(1)**, only an *individual*
  debtor gets a Chapter 7 discharge. The LLC's case will be a liquidation —
  assets collected, distributed, case closed — not a discharge. The SBA
  debt simply becomes uncollectible from the LLC because the LLC has
  nothing left, not because it was legally erased.

Either way, the physician's personal guaranty is a separate, independent
contractual obligation that the LLC's bankruptcy does not touch. **His own
bankruptcy filing is entirely his voluntary choice** — a tool to use if and
when the guaranty debt becomes unmanageable, not something the LLC's filing
forces on him. (Full analysis: Part II.)

### Q2: "Will he lose his house and car in New Mexico?"

**Very likely not, if he does end up needing to file — and probably not
even outside of bankruptcy.** New Mexico substantially increased its
exemption amounts in a 2023 statutory overhaul:

| Asset | NM Exemption | Statute |
|---|---|---|
| Homestead (primary residence) | **$150,000** (or $300,000 for a qualifying recent widow/er) | NMSA 1978 § 42-10-9 |
| Motor vehicle | **$10,000** | NMSA 1978 § 42-10-1 |

Assuming his equity in the home and car falls under these caps (a factual
question — see Part VII action items), both assets are fully protected from
the SBA/guaranty creditor, whether collection is attempted inside a
personal Chapter 7 or through an ordinary state-court judgment outside of
bankruptcy (NM's exemption statutes apply to judgment execution generally,
not just in bankruptcy). New Mexico is **not** an opt-out state, so state
exemptions must be affirmatively elected over the (much smaller) federal
exemptions — but state exemptions are clearly the better choice here. (Full
analysis: Part III.)

**One important exception — his Social Security income, not his house, is
the actual point of vulnerability.** See Q4 below.

### Q3: "Does NM have a homestead exemption?"

**Yes** — currently **$150,000** (NMSA 1978 § 42-10-9), up from $60,000
before a July 1, 2023 amendment, with a possible further CPI adjustment
that should be confirmed as of the actual filing date. This is likely more
than sufficient for a typical physician's home equity, especially net of
any remaining mortgage balance.

### Q4: "Is there case law about discharging SBA COVID loans because the debtor is a physician who kept the practice open to help people?"

**No — and counsel should not represent to the client that such a doctrine
exists.** Exhaustive searching across all five research tracks found **no
case, in any court, that recognizes a special leniency rule, an
undue-hardship-style standard, or an equitable exception to dischargeability
because a debtor took out COVID-era SBA debt or is a physician/essential
worker.** This is a clean negative finding, not a research gap:

- "Undue hardship" is a **§ 523(a)(8) student-loan-specific** concept
  (*Brunner* / totality-of-circumstances tests). It has no analog for SBA
  business debt, and no case was found importing it into that context.
- The one closely on-point case found — ***In re Schoenmann*** (9th Cir.
  BAP, unpublished, Mar. 2026) — is an EIDL nondischargeability ruling that
  cuts the **opposite** direction: the debtor's COVID-purpose certification
  ("to alleviate the economic injury caused by the COVID-19 pandemic") was
  used *against* her once she diverted the funds, not credited as
  mitigation.
- DOJ press releases on physician PPP/EIDL fraud prosecutions (e.g., the
  *Sarfo* matter) confirm occupation cuts neither way — physicians get no
  special protection from fraud liability, but by the same token a
  physician who did *not* misrepresent anything gets no special *exposure*
  either.

**The good news underneath this correction:** this client's plain-vanilla
fact pattern — legitimate business loan, used to keep the practice running
during a genuine crisis, now unable to repay because of ordinary financial
hardship — is *exactly* the kind of debt Chapter 7 is designed to discharge
by default. He doesn't need a special doctrine; he needs the *absence* of
fraud, which on the facts as described he appears to have. The physician
narrative still has real (if more modest) value — see Part IV.5. (Full
analysis: Part IV.)

### Q5: What is the single most important strategic point in this research?

**Treasury Offset Program (TOP) exposure against his Social Security
benefit — not asset seizure — is the real risk, and it is the strongest
argument for proactively filing rather than waiting.** Social Security is
broadly protected from ordinary creditors (42 U.S.C. § 407(a)), and his
home/car will likely be fully exempt either way. But an SBA guaranty is a
**federal** debt, and Congress carved out an exception allowing the
government to administratively offset **up to 15% of his monthly Social
Security benefit** (above a floor of roughly $750/month) to collect
delinquent federal non-tax debt — **without ever going to court.** Because
his Social Security is, per the facts, already fully committed to his
mortgage payment, this offset could trigger a mortgage default even though
the house itself would otherwise be fully protected. A bankruptcy discharge
forecloses this exposure permanently; simply waiting does not. (Full
analysis: Part VI.)

## PART I: SBA COVID LOAN PROGRAM MECHANICS — WHICH LOANS ACTUALLY CREATED PERSONAL GUARANTY EXPOSURE

This threshold, fact-specific question should be resolved before any
strategic advice is finalized: **which programs, and at what loan amounts,
did the LLC actually borrow under, and which of those required (and
received) an actual personal guaranty from the physician?**

### PPP (Paycheck Protection Program) — likely NOT a source of personal exposure

PPP loans (CARES Act § 1102; 15 U.S.C. § 636(a)(36)) were designed to be
**non-recourse against individual owners/members**, per the statute's
nonrecourse provision (15 U.S.C. § 636(a)(36)(F)(v)) and SBA's implementing
interim final rule (85 Fed. Reg. 20811, Apr. 15, 2020) — **no personal
guarantee, no collateral required**, so long as (a) the LLC was an eligible
borrower and (b) proceeds were used for authorized purposes (payroll,
covered costs). If the LLC's PPP funds were used properly, PPP debt alone
should not create personal liability, regardless of any certification the
physician signed as the LLC's authorized representative (a certification of
authority to sign for the entity is not the same as a personal guaranty of
repayment). **Action item:** confirm the actual PPP note/application (SBA
Form 2483/2483-SD) did not have a guaranty rider attached — some lenders
reportedly added one despite SBA guidance.

### COVID-19 EIDL (Economic Injury Disaster Loan) — the likely source of exposure

COVID EIDL loans used a relaxed, COVID-specific threshold:

| EIDL amount | Collateral required? | Personal guaranty required? |
|---|---|---|
| Under $25,000 | No | No |
| $25,001 – $200,000 | Yes (UCC blanket lien on business assets) | No |
| **Over $200,000** | Yes | **Yes** — from any individual/entity owning ≥20% of the borrower |

As 100% owner, the physician would fall within the guaranty requirement for
any EIDL loan (or aggregate EIDL exposure — confirm whether EIDL loans are
aggregated) exceeding $200,000. This is consistent with the facts as given
("he personally signed guarantees on at least some of these loans"). **Any
EIDL loan the LLC took at or under $200,000 should NOT have carried a
personal guaranty** — if the loan file shows one anyway, that is worth
flagging as a potential negotiating point, though more relevant to
settlement leverage than to whether bankruptcy is needed.

### SBA Debt Relief Program (CARES Act § 1112) — no new guaranty

Section 1112 simply had SBA make 6 months of principal/interest/fee
payments (later extended) directly to the lender on qualifying *pre-existing*
7(a)/504 loans — it did not create, modify, or require any new guaranty
instrument. If the LLC's debt stack includes a pre-COVID 7(a)/504 loan that
received § 1112 subsidy payments, the physician's exposure on that loan
traces to whatever guaranty he signed at *origination* (pre-COVID), not to
anything created by § 1112.

### The guaranty instrument itself: SBA Form 148 (Unconditional Guarantee)

If a guaranty was required and signed, it is very likely on SBA Form 148 or
a lender's substantially similar form. Key characteristics, all bearing
directly on the strength of the SBA's position and the weakness of any
defense:

- **Independent, unconditional obligation** — legally separate from the
  underlying note; the LLC's bankruptcy does not touch it (this is the
  doctrinal root of the Part II analysis).
- **Broad waiver of defenses** — the guarantor waives, "to the extent
  permitted by law," essentially all traditional suretyship defenses
  (lender's failure to perfect collateral, changes to loan terms, no
  requirement the lender exhaust remedies against the borrower first,
  etc.). Do not expect a defense-on-the-merits strategy to be viable.
- **Federal-law choice-of-law/preemption clause** once SBA is the holder —
  forecloses state-law suretyship defenses.
- **Fee-shifting** — obligates the guarantor to reimburse enforcement costs.

**Action item:** obtain and review the actual executed guaranty
document(s), not just the assumption that a standard Form 148 was used.

### Enforcement landscape (informs realistic risk assessment, not just doctrine)

No reported court decision was found anywhere enforcing a COVID-era
PPP/EIDL personal guaranty against an individual guarantor. This gap is
explained, not just unexplained: an August 2025 SBA Office of Inspector
General report found SBA had **not** been referring delinquent COVID EIDL
debt to DOJ for litigation "regardless of amount," instead routing
collection through Treasury's administrative cross-servicing/offset
process. More recent reporting (2026) indicates enforcement has escalated
sharply this year — hundreds of thousands of pandemic-era loans have
reportedly been referred to Treasury and DOJ for "enhanced debt
collection" following the March 2026 expiration of SBA's temporary
authority to continue servicing defaulted COVID EIDLs. **Practical
upshot:** the government's tool of choice against guarantors like this
client is administrative (Treasury offset, wage garnishment, credit
reporting) rather than court litigation — reinforcing the Part VI TOP
analysis as the operative real-world risk, more than a lawsuit risk.

## PART II: DOES THE LLC'S CHAPTER 7 FORCE THE PHYSICIAN INTO HIS OWN BANKRUPTCY?

**No.** This is the client's central question and the answer is clear on
the law, even though the practical financial pressure it puts him under is
real.

### The controlling statutory framework

> **11 U.S.C. § 524(e):** "Except as provided in subsection (a)(3) of this
> section, discharge of a debt of the debtor does not affect the liability
> of any other entity on, or the property of any other entity for, such
> debt."

This codifies the long-standing rule that a discharge is a personal defense
of the debtor, not a satisfaction of the debt itself. Persuasive authority
applying this rule to guarantors specifically (none binding in the Tenth
Circuit, but consistent and unrebutted nationally):

- *Republic Supply Co. v. Shoaf*, 815 F.2d 1046 (5th Cir. 1987) — confirms,
  by negative implication, that absent an express plan release (only
  possible in a Chapter 11 reorganization with a confirmed plan — not
  applicable to this LLC's Chapter 7 liquidation), § 524(e) leaves guarantor
  liability untouched.
- *Underhill v. Royal*, 769 F.2d 1428 (9th Cir. 1985) — bankruptcy court
  lacks power to discharge or release a non-debtor's liability absent an
  express statutory exception.
- *In re Walker*, 927 F.2d 1138 (10th Cir. 1991) — § 524(e) preserves
  creditors' rights against non-debtor third parties notwithstanding the
  primary debtor's discharge (Tenth Circuit — the circuit that governs
  New Mexico, though this particular case involves a recovery-fund fact
  pattern rather than a pure guaranty, so cite it for the general
  principle rather than as guaranty-specific authority).

### A sharper point: the LLC won't even get a "discharge"

> **11 U.S.C. § 727(a)(1):** "The court shall grant the debtor a discharge,
> unless— (1) the debtor is not an individual."

Only individuals receive a Chapter 7 discharge. A corporation or LLC is
**liquidated only** — assets collected and distributed by the trustee, case
closed — and never receives a discharge at all. Congress deliberately
eliminated corporate/partnership Chapter 7 discharges to prevent
"trafficking in corporate shells." Practically: the SBA's claim against the
LLC doesn't get erased by some official discharge order; it simply becomes
worthless against the LLC because the LLC will have no assets and (soon) no
legal existence left to collect from. The claim against the **guarantor**
is completely unaffected by any of this.

### What actually happens next

The SBA (or its servicer, or Treasury once referred) can pursue the
physician directly — by lawsuit, or increasingly by non-judicial means
(Treasury offset, administrative wage garnishment under 13 C.F.R. § 140.11,
credit-bureau reporting) — without any requirement that he file his own
bankruptcy case first. His options once pursued:

1. **Pay or negotiate a direct settlement.**
2. **SBA Offer in Compromise (OIC)** — SBA Form 1150 (Offer in Compromise)
   with SBA Form 770 (Financial Statement), evaluated under the government-wide
   Federal Claims Collection Standards (31 C.F.R. § 902.2), which directs
   the agency to consider "the applicable exemptions available to the
   debtor under state and Federal law" in assessing collectability — a
   directly favorable factor here, given how much of this client's
   financial picture (Social Security, likely-exempt home/car) is legally
   unreachable. Governing SOP is likely SOP 50 57-series (for the PPP/7(a)
   piece) and SOP 50 52-series (for the EIDL/disaster-loan piece) —
   **confirm current SOP numbering directly with SBA before advising the
   client**, as this area has been revised multiple times.
3. **Suretyship defenses** — largely foreclosed by Form 148's broad waiver
   language (see Part I).
4. **His own voluntary Chapter 7 or Chapter 13** — discussed in Part V.

### Could creditors force him into an *involuntary* bankruptcy? (11 U.S.C. § 303)

Theoretically possible, but very unlikely on these facts. It requires
either (a) three or more petitioning creditors with qualifying
non-contingent, undisputed claims if he has 12+ total creditors, or (b) a
single creditor if he has fewer than 12 total creditors, with aggregate
qualifying claims exceeding the current inflation-adjusted threshold
(reportedly **$21,050** as of April 2025, not the $18,600 figure sometimes
cited — confirm current figure). SBA/Treasury's documented practice favors
administrative collection tools over litigation, let alone the unusual step
of an involuntary petition — this risk should be noted to the client but
not treated as a driving planning factor.

## PART III: NEW MEXICO EXEMPTIONS — PROTECTING THE HOME, CAR, AND INCOME

### Correction to a common assumption: New Mexico is NOT an opt-out state

Under 11 U.S.C. § 522(b)(2)–(3), a New Mexico debtor may choose **either**
the federal § 522(d) exemptions (currently roughly $27,900 homestead per
debtor, plus an "unused homestead" wildcard of roughly $13,950 — confirm
current figures) **or** the New Mexico state exemptions (NMSA 1978 §§
42-10-1 through 42-10-13). Because New Mexico's 2023-enhanced state
exemptions are now dramatically more generous, the client should almost
certainly elect **state exemptions** on Schedule C — but counsel should run
both sets of numbers against his actual equity before formally electing.

### Homestead exemption — NMSA 1978 § 42-10-9

- **Current amount: $150,000** (or **$300,000** if his spouse died within
  the two years preceding the claim and would independently have qualified
  for the exemption).
- Amendment history: $30,000 pre-2007 → $60,000 (2007) → **$150,000**,
  effective July 1, 2023 (2023 N.M. Laws ch. 104 / Senate Bill 216).
- The statute also provides for periodic CPI adjustment (search results
  were inconsistent on exact cadence — possibly already adjusted upward as
  of a July 1, 2025 index date). **Confirm the current, possibly
  higher, figure before finalizing any advice** — any adjustment only helps
  the client.
- "Domicile" is broadly defined (includes mobile homes, trailers, RVs, and
  similar shelters) and the exemption expressly applies "from attachment,
  execution or foreclosure by a judgment creditor and from any proceeding
  of receivers or trustees in insolvency or bankruptcy proceedings" — i.e.,
  it protects the home both inside and outside a bankruptcy filing.
- **Open question:** whether jointly-owned homesteads still allow doubling
  (each joint owner claiming a separate $150,000) under the post-2023 text,
  as they did under the old $60,000 regime. Likely moot if the physician is
  sole title-holder or the only filer — confirm current title/vesting.
- The exemption does **not** defeat taxes, garnishment, or properly
  perfected liens (mortgages, mechanics' liens) — relevant if the SBA ever
  obtained a recorded lien on the residence specifically (see "critical
  factual assumption" below).

### Motor vehicle exemption — NMSA 1978 § 42-10-1

- **Current amount: $10,000** (up from $4,000 pre-July 2023).
- Companion increases in the same 2023 rewrite, useful for other personal
  property: household goods/furnishings $75,000; jewelry $5,000 (beyond
  wedding/engagement rings, unconditionally exempt); tools of the
  trade/professional equipment $15,000 (potentially relevant if the
  physician personally — not the LLC — owns any medical instruments).

### "Wildcard" exemption — NMSA 1978 § 42-10-10 — NOT applicable to this client

This is **not** a general stackable wildcard. By its terms it is available
only to a resident who **does not own a homestead** ($15,000 in lieu of the
homestead exemption). Because this client owns his home, he takes the much
larger $150,000/$300,000 homestead exemption instead — he doesn't need and
can't stack the wildcard on top of it. New Mexico does not appear to have a
separate, freestanding wildcard for homeowners of the type found in some
other states.

### Social Security income — protected two different ways, independent of state law

1. **Anti-attachment (42 U.S.C. § 407(a))**: Social Security benefits are
   not "subject to execution, levy, attachment, garnishment, or other legal
   process, or to the operation of any bankruptcy or insolvency law" — a
   "super-preemption" clause (§ 407(b)) that the Supreme Court has read
   broadly (*Philpott v. Essex County Welfare Board*, 409 U.S. 413 (1973);
   *Bennett v. Arkansas*, 485 U.S. 395 (1988)). This protection applies
   regardless of which state's exemption scheme is used, and is not
   "property of the estate" reachable by a Chapter 7 trustee. **Caution:**
   commingling Social Security funds with other money in a bank account
   can create tracing complications — advise the client to keep Social
   Security deposits segregated if possible.
2. **Excluded from the means test (11 U.S.C. § 101(10A)(B))**: Social
   Security is expressly excluded from "current monthly income," the figure
   that drives Chapter 7 eligibility screening (Part V).

**Important nuance (see Part VI): this general protection has one specific,
important exception for federal debt via Treasury offset — do not tell the
client Social Security is 100% untouchable.**

### Interaction with the SBA guaranty debt (critical factual assumption to verify)

This analysis assumes the SBA guaranty debt is a **general unsecured
claim** against the physician personally — i.e., no mortgage, deed of
trust, judgment lien, or UCC lien against his personal residence or vehicle
securing the guaranty. This is the ordinary structure for EIDL loans over
$200,000 (UCC lien on the *business's* assets, unsecured personal
guaranty) — but **must be confirmed against the actual loan/guaranty
documents and any recorded liens (county real property records; UCC
filings) before finalizing this analysis.** If a lien was in fact recorded
against the home, the analysis changes materially (though a *judicial*
lien impairing the homestead exemption can potentially be avoided under 11
U.S.C. § 522(f), unlike a consensual mortgage lien).

**If the assumption holds:** the trustee cannot reach any home equity or
vehicle equity within the exemption caps, the SBA guaranty debt has no
independent lien to enforce against either asset, and — assuming no
successful § 523(a) dischargeability objection (Part IV) — this would
functionally be a no-asset Chapter 7 as to the SBA claim, discharged
without the creditor recovering anything from the house or car.

### Confirming case law from the District of New Mexico

- *In re McCune*, Case No. 20-12326-j7 (Bankr. D.N.M. Apr. 5, 2024) —
  homestead exemption vs. judicial lien dispute; confirms the D.N.M.
  bankruptcy court's active, current application of § 42-10-9 and the
  availability of § 522(f) lien avoidance against a judicial lien
  impairing the homestead exemption.
- *In re Beach*, Case No. 21-10762-t13 (Bankr. D.N.M., Feb. 2022) —
  applied the pre-2023 $60,000/$120,000 figures (predates the 2023
  amendment) but illustrates the district's methodology for calculating
  how liens and the homestead exemption interact — useful for reasoning,
  not for the current dollar figure.

## PART IV: DISCHARGEABILITY OF THE GUARANTY DEBT

### The governing framework — no special doctrine, ordinary § 523(a) analysis

An individual Chapter 7 debtor discharges all pre-petition debts **except**
those enumerated in 11 U.S.C. § 523(a) — a closed list; courts do not
import equitable exceptions. For an SBA guaranty debt, the only realistic
exposure is:

- **§ 523(a)(2)(A)** — debt obtained by false pretenses/false
  representation/actual fraud.
- **§ 523(a)(2)(B)** — debt obtained via a materially false *written*
  statement respecting financial condition, reasonably relied upon, made
  with intent to deceive (elements confirmed by *Lamar, Archer & Cofrin,
  LLP v. Appling*, 584 U.S. 709 (2018) (binding, U.S. Supreme Court) — a
  statement about even a single asset can qualify).
- **§ 523(a)(4)** — fraud/defalcation in a fiduciary capacity, embezzlement,
  larceny (unlikely to apply absent a fiduciary relationship beyond the
  ordinary guaranty).

**There is no "undue hardship" exception for SBA debt.** That standard
(*Brunner* test / totality-of-circumstances test) is textually limited to
**§ 523(a)(8) educational loans** — no case was found extending it, or
anything like it, to SBA business debt.

### Screening checklist — has counsel confirmed there's no fraud exposure?

Before advising the client the debt is "safely dischargeable," review the
actual EIDL/PPP application, financial statements, loan agreement, and
guaranty document against these questions:

- Did the application require disclosure of contingent liabilities, pending
  litigation, judgments, or existing debts? Was anything omitted? (This is
  precisely what sank the debtor in *Schoenmann*, below.)
- Did the application/agreement certify a specific *use* of funds (e.g.,
  "working capital," payroll)? Were funds used consistently with that
  certification?
- Were revenue, payroll, or employee-count figures accurate as of
  certification?
- Was any pending litigation, bankruptcy, or criminal-history status
  truthfully disclosed if asked?

If the file is clean on these points — which appears likely given the facts
as described (an existing practice using loan proceeds to stay operating
during a genuine public-health emergency, not diverting funds to unrelated
personal purposes) — the guaranty debt should discharge in the ordinary
course.

### Case law: what generates a dischargeability fight, and what doesn't

**No case was found crediting a debtor's status as a physician,
healthcare provider, or essential worker as an equitable factor — in any
COVID-era SBA dischargeability dispute, even in dicta.** This should be
reported to the client directly: it does not exist as a doctrine.

The most factually proximate case found cuts the other way:

> ***In re Schoenmann*** (9th Cir. BAP, unpublished, Mar. 2026; underlying
> Bankr. N.D. Cal.) — the bankruptcy court held the debtor's EIDL debt
> nondischargeable under § 523(a)(2)(A)/(B) because she certified the loan
> would be used "solely for working capital ... to alleviate the economic
> injury caused by the COVID-19 pandemic" but instead diverted proceeds to
> personal litigation costs, and separately because she omitted contingent
> liabilities (undisclosed probate litigation) from required financial
> disclosures. The COVID-purpose certification became the very
> representation she was found to have breached — evidence *against* the
> debtor, not mitigation for her. (Unpublished, persuasive only, and
> out-of-circuit — but the clearest available illustration of what does and
> does not generate exposure.)

DOJ enforcement matters (not bankruptcy dischargeability rulings, but useful
contrast) confirm the same pattern with physician defendants specifically —
e.g., *United States v. Kofi Sarfo and Rose Sarfo* (Nevada) (doctor and
spouse indicted over alleged diversion of $1.3M in EIDL/PPP funds to
personal stock/cryptocurrency purchases) and the *Physician Partners of
America* $24.5M False Claims Act settlement (included a PPP-application
misrepresentation). In every located case, exposure tracks
**misrepresentation and diversion of funds**, never occupation and never
motive. A physician who did not misrepresent anything gets no special
exposure, and — critically for this client — gets no special leniency
doctrine either, because none exists. He doesn't need one: ordinary
dischargeability already covers his fact pattern.

### § 707(b) "abuse" is not a live threat (cross-reference Part V)

Even setting aside § 523(a), a trustee/UST cannot use § 707(b) to block or
convert the case: Social Security produces $0 "current monthly income" by
statutory exclusion, and § 707(b)(1) itself is limited to cases where debts
are "primarily consumer debts" — the SBA guaranty is business debt, likely
taking this case outside § 707(b) entirely regardless of income. Full
analysis in Part V.

### The physician/COVID narrative's real (more modest) role

Although no freestanding doctrine exists, the story is not wasted:

1. **§ 707(b)(3) totality-of-circumstances fallback** — if, contrary to the
   Part V analysis, a trustee ever raised bad faith, this narrative (kept a
   practice open through a public-health emergency, legitimate business
   purpose, now on fixed Social Security income at 82) is exactly what
   totality-of-circumstances review credits. Defensive, not offensive.
2. **Settlement/OIC leverage** — a sympathetic, well-documented narrative
   (age, retirement, fixed income, no misuse of funds) is real leverage in
   direct negotiation with SBA, wholly apart from any bankruptcy doctrine.
3. **Client reassurance** — dischargeability turns on the *absence* of
   fraud, not the *presence* of virtue. Counsel can accurately tell the
   client his conduct wasn't wrongful, and — more importantly — that its
   rightness or wrongness isn't what the discharge analysis turns on at
   all. This may be the most practically useful message for the client,
   even though it's legally unremarkable.

## PART V: CHAPTER 7 ELIGIBILITY, VENUE, AND CHAPTER 13 COMPARISON

### The means test is very likely a non-issue — two independent reasons

1. **Social Security produces $0 "current monthly income."** 11 U.S.C. §
   101(10A)(B) expressly excludes Social Security Act benefits from CMI,
   the figure that drives the § 707(b)(2) means test, regardless of the
   actual dollar amount received.
2. **The SBA guaranty is not "consumer debt," which independently takes
   the case outside § 707(b) altogether.** Section 707(b)(1) only applies
   to cases where debts are "primarily consumer debts." "Consumer debt" is
   defined (§ 101(8)) as debt "incurred by an individual primarily for a
   personal, family, or household purpose" — a guaranty of a business loan
   does not meet that definition; it takes on the business character of the
   debt it backstops. The Tenth Circuit (binding on the District of New
   Mexico) has held "primarily" means more than 50% by dollar amount:

   > ***Stewart v. United States Trustee***, 175 F.3d 796 (10th Cir. 1999)
   > (binding in D.N.M.) — "primarily" for § 707(b) purposes means consumer
   > debt exceeding 50% of the dollar amount of total debt, adopting the
   > Ninth Circuit's approach in *In re Kelly*, 841 F.2d 908 (9th Cir. 1988),
   > and rejecting the Fifth Circuit's additional number-of-claims gloss in
   > *In re Booth*, 858 F.2d 1051 (5th Cir. 1988).

   If the SBA guaranty debt is the dominant liability by dollar amount
   (likely, given typical EIDL practice-level borrowing), § 707(b) does not
   apply to this case at all — no means test, no presumption of abuse.
   **Action item:** run this arithmetic once his full debt schedule
   (consumer and non-consumer, by dollar amount) is known.

Either ground alone defeats a presumption-of-abuse concern; together, this
is about as clean a Chapter 7 eligibility case as exists.

### Venue: District of New Mexico is proper

28 U.S.C. § 1408 supports D.N.M. venue on two independent grounds: ordinary
domicile/residence (180 days), and the pending affiliate case (the LLC's
own Chapter 7, presumably also in D.N.M.). **Keep this analytically
separate** from the *exemption-choice* domicile question (11 U.S.C. §
522(b)(3)(A)'s 730/180-day lookback, discussed in Part III) — different
statutes, different lookback periods, though on these facts (longtime NM
resident) both point to the same result.

### Chapter 7 vs. Chapter 13 — Chapter 7 is the clear fit

Chapter 13 requires "regular income" (11 U.S.C. § 109(e)) and a confirmable
plan funded from disposable income. Two structural problems make it a poor
fit: (1) no surplus income — his Social Security is already fully committed
to the mortgage, leaving nothing to fund a plan; (2) Social Security is
excluded from disposable-income calculations the same way it's excluded
from CMI (persuasive Tenth Circuit authority applies this rule in the
Chapter 13 context). Given his age, fixed income, and the absence of any
non-exempt asset Chapter 7 would otherwise force liquidation of, **Chapter
7 is clearly the right chapter if he files at all.**

### Non-bankruptcy alternatives, briefly

- **SBA Offer in Compromise** — a real, legitimate avenue (Part II), though
  practitioner sources describe SBA as currently slow/uncertain on
  COVID-EIDL-specific compromises specifically (more established for the
  PPP/7(a) piece).
- **"Wait and see" / judgment-proof analysis** — because Social Security
  (§ 407(a)) and likely home/vehicle equity (NM execution exemptions) are
  largely protected from *ordinary* creditor process even outside
  bankruptcy, one could ask whether personal bankruptcy is strictly
  necessary. **This is where Part VI's Treasury offset finding matters most**
  — it is the one meaningful hole in an otherwise strong "judgment proof"
  picture, and the main reason simply waiting is a weaker strategy than a
  discharge.
- **Statute of limitations** — the government generally has six years to
  sue on a contract claim (28 U.S.C. § 2415(a)), but this does *not* limit
  non-judicial tools (Treasury offset, wage garnishment) once a debt is
  referred for cross-servicing — those can continue indefinitely. Do not
  advise the client that simply running out the clock protects him from
  offset.

## PART VI: THE KEY STRATEGIC FINDING — TREASURY OFFSET PROGRAM EXPOSURE ON SOCIAL SECURITY

This deserves its own section because it changes the practical
recommendation and is the least intuitive finding in this research.

Social Security's general anti-attachment protection (42 U.S.C. § 407(a))
is not absolute against the federal government itself. Section 407(b)
allows Congress to override it by *express reference*, and Congress did so
for administrative offset of federal benefits to collect federal non-tax
debt:

> **31 U.S.C. § 3716(c)(3)(A)** authorizes offset of Social Security
> old-age benefits "[n]otwithstanding any other provision of law (including
> sections 207 and 1631(d)(1) of the Social Security Act)," subject to a
> floor: the statute protects roughly **$9,000 per 12-month period** from
> offset.
>
> **31 C.F.R. § 285.4** implements this: the offset from any given monthly
> benefit payment is capped at the **lesser of 15% of the monthly benefit,
> or the amount by which the benefit exceeds $750/month** — i.e., the first
> $750/month is protected, and the balance above that is subject to up to
> a 15% offset.

An SBA guaranty is a federal non-tax debt. Once referred to Treasury (the
SBA's documented, and increasingly used, path — see Part I), it becomes TOP
offset-eligible. **Because the client's Social Security is, per the facts,
already fully committed to his mortgage payment, even a modest offset could
precipitate a mortgage default** — not through a homestead-exemption
failure, but through income-side attrition of the very funds he relies on
to make the payment. This directly speaks to his stated fear of "losing the
house," even though the asset-protection analysis in Part III is otherwise
favorable.

**A bankruptcy discharge forecloses this exposure prospectively and
permanently** — once the guaranty debt is discharged, it is no longer
"legally enforceable" and should no longer be TOP-offset-eligible (subject
to the separate, harder question of any offset *already* effectuated before
filing, which is a setoff-versus-discharge timing issue not fully addressed
in this preliminary pass and should be researched separately if any offset
has already begun).

**This is the strongest affirmative reason, on this record, for the
physician to consider filing his own Chapter 7 proactively** rather than
assuming he's "judgment proof" and doing nothing — the wait-and-see approach
leaves him exposed to a specific, recurring, quantifiable monthly offset
risk that a discharge eliminates.
