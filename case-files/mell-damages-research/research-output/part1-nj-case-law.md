## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

New Jersey courts do not cap compensatory damages in civil sexual assault or child sexual abuse cases. The NJ Punitive Damages Act caps punitive damages at 5× compensatory or $350,000 (whichever is greater), but the **Child Sexual Abuse Act (N.J.S.A. 2A:61B-1) expressly exempts those claims from the cap**. Since 2019, the Child Victims Act (CVA, S477/P.L.2019, c.120) has fueled a wave of large verdicts. Recent NJ jury verdicts in single-plaintiff child sexual abuse cases have ranged from **$750,000 to $30 million**, with two verdicts in the $25–30 million range returned in 2024–2026.

The controlling NJ Supreme Court remittitur standard requires that a damages award "shock the judicial conscience" — a high bar that NJ courts apply deferentially. Comparative verdict methodology (comparing other jury verdicts to assess excessiveness) was **expressly abandoned** by the NJ Supreme Court in *Cuevas v. Wentworth Group* (2016).

## SECTION I: CONTROLLING LEGAL STANDARDS

### A. Damages Available Under the Child Sexual Abuse Act

**N.J.S.A. 2A:61B-1** (Child Sexual Abuse Act / "CSAA") provides that a prevailing plaintiff shall be awarded:
- **Actual damages** = compensatory damages + **punitive damages** + costs + reasonable attorneys' fees
- Compensatory damages may include: pain and suffering, medical expenses, emotional trauma, diminished childhood, diminished enjoyment of life, costs of counseling, lost wages
- Statutory floor: $10,000 or actual damages, whichever is greater

**Key statutory exception to punitive damages cap:** N.J.S.A. 2A:15-5.14 exempts from the 5× / $350,000 punitive damages cap any causes of action brought under N.J.S.A. 2A:61B-1 (the CSAA). [VERIFY BEFORE FILING — confirm current statutory text of 2A:15-5.14 exclusion language]

### B. Punitive Damages Cap (General Rule)

**N.J.S.A. 2A:15-5.14** (Punitive Damages Act):
- Cap: No defendant liable for punitive damages exceeding **5× compensatory damages OR $350,000**, whichever is greater
- Trial judge must ascertain that the award is reasonable in amount and justified in the circumstances, in light of the purpose to punish the defendant and to deter that defendant from repeating such conduct
- Exceptions: LAD claims, CEPA claims, and CSAA (N.J.S.A. 2A:61B-1) claims are exempt from this cap

**Tarr v. Bob Ciasulli's Mack Auto Mall, Inc.**, 194 N.J. 212 (2008) — NJ Supreme Court held that punitive damages may aim only to deter the specific defendant, not general deterrence of others. [VERIFY BEFORE FILING]

### C. NJ Remittitur Standard — "Shock the Judicial Conscience"

The controlling NJ Supreme Court standard for remittitur:

**Cuevas v. Wentworth Group**, 226 N.J. 480 (2016):
- To grant remittitur, the judge must find that the award is **"so grossly excessive that it shocks the judicial conscience"**
- Courts must exercise **great restraint** — "the jury — not the judge — is charged with the responsibility of deciding the merits of a civil claim and the quantum of damages"
- **Comparative verdict methodology is ABANDONED**: "The comparison of supposedly similar verdicts to assess whether a particular damages award is excessive is ultimately a futile exercise that should be abandoned." This expressly overruled the prior approach endorsed in *He v. Miller*.
- A judge should NOT rely on personal knowledge of other verdicts OR comparative verdict surveys when deciding a remittitur motion
- Citation: 226 N.J. 480 (2016) [VERIFY BEFORE FILING — confirm reporter citation]

**Orientale v. Jennings**, 239 N.J. 569 (2019):
- Restructured the procedural requirement for consent: When a trial judge determines that a damages award is either so grossly excessive or grossly inadequate that a new damages trial is justified, the judge may set a remittitur at an amount a reasonable jury would award — but the parties may simply reject it and get a new trial
- **Mutual consent required** for remittitur to stand without a new trial
- Does not involve sexual assault; involves auto accident ($200 verdict found inadequate)
- Citation: 239 N.J. 569, 218 A.3d 806 (2019) [VERIFY BEFORE FILING]

**Mickens v. Misdom**, NJ App. Div. (Jan. 7, 2015) (Published):
- Affirmed that in deciding remittitur, the trial judge's use of personal experience with other verdicts "defies greatly valued attributes of our judicial system, namely, a party's right to discovery and the right to confront and cross-examine information used to adjudicate the dispute"
- Citation: App. Div. Published, Jan. 7, 2015 [VERIFY BEFORE FILING — confirm full citation]

**Standard Summary:**
> "The decision to order a remittitur must spring from an overriding sense of injustice, a shock to the court's conscience, a certain and abiding belief that the award, in light of the facts and the evidence, falls outside the relatively wide range of one that is acceptable and appropriate."

The appellate court additionally defers to the trial judge's **"feel of the case"** regarding intangibles. There is **no cap** on compensatory damages for pain and suffering in NJ civil cases generally.

## SECTION II: NJ SUPREME COURT OPINIONS ON CIVIL SEXUAL ABUSE DAMAGES

### 1. J.S. and M.S. v. R.T.H., 155 N.J. 330, 714 A.2d 924 (1998)

**Court:** New Jersey Supreme Court
**Year:** 1998
**Facts:** Two girls (ages 12 and 15) were sexually abused by their neighbor R.T.H. over more than a year. Neighbor was criminally convicted. Civil suit was brought against the abuser AND his wife, alleging the wife had knowledge and failed to warn or prevent.
**Damages:** Trial court awarded **each child $100,000 compensatory damages and $25,000 punitive damages**, plus pre-judgment interest of $12,439.72 per plaintiff. Total award to both plaintiffs: approximately $274,000 including interest.
**Issue on Appeal:** Whether the abuser's wife owed a duty of care to the victims.
**Holding:** NJ Supreme Court held YES — a spouse with actual knowledge or special reason to know of a spouse's likely sexual abuse of identified persons has a duty to take reasonable steps to prevent or warn of the harm.
**Remittitur:** Not discussed — issue was duty, not damages amount.
**Note on Amounts:** The $100,000/$25,000 amounts are relatively modest by today's standards but reflect the 1998 pre-CVA era and the fact that the defendant (wife) was a private individual.
**Source:** Justia, https://law.justia.com/cases/new-jersey/supreme-court/1998/a-98-97-opn.html; also CourtListener [VERIFY BEFORE FILING]

### 2. A.B. v. Y.Z., 184 N.J. 328 (2005)

**Court:** New Jersey Supreme Court
**Year:** 2005
**Facts:** Parents A.B. brought a civil action on behalf of their minor son H.T. against Y.Z. for sexual abuse, assault and battery, and intentional infliction of emotional distress. Defendant failed to answer; default was entered. The matter proceeded to a proof hearing on damages.

### 4. Simpkins v. South Orange-Maplewood School District / Hornor v. Upper Freehold Regional BOE (consolidated), NJ Supreme Court (March 11, 2026)

**Court:** New Jersey Supreme Court (6-1 decision)
**Year:** 2026
**Facts (Simpkins):** Three plaintiffs (Ormond Simpkins Jr., Frankie Jerome, Brandon Hayes) sued South Orange-Maplewood School District after their teacher Nicole Dufault (language arts / special education) sexually abused them during the 2013-14 school year. Students were ages 14–17. Abuse occurred in Dufault's classroom during school hours AND in her car on school grounds and off campus. Dufault pleaded guilty in 2020 to three counts of aggravated criminal sexual contact.
**Facts (Hornor):** Russell Forde Hornor alleged sexual assault by a school employee (Hutler) at his residence outside school hours.

The CVA "fundamentally altered" the law governing civil claims against public entities. Standard: the school must have given the abuser authority over the student's educational environment that resulted in the abuse, and must have appeared to tacitly approve.
**Remittitur:** Not applicable — case at pleading stage.
**Significance:** Major expansion of school district liability in NJ. Will produce large future verdicts. Vicarious liability even for off-campus, off-hours abuse by school employees.

## SECTION III: NJ APPELLATE DIVISION PUBLISHED OPINIONS — CIVIL SEXUAL ABUSE DAMAGES

### 5. Lockley v. Turner, 344 N.J. Super. 1, 779 A.2d 1092 (App. Div. 2001)

**Court:** NJ Superior Court, Appellate Division (Published)
**Year:** 2001
**Facts:** Robert Lockley, a corrections officer at Mid-State Correctional Facility, was subjected to escalating sexual harassment by a co-worker Ronda Turner beginning in 1990, including vulgar and graphic language about his anatomy and sexual orientation. Jury trial held May 1999.
**Damages:** Jury awarded **$750,000 compensatory damages** and **$3 million punitive damages** against the NJ DOC.
**Appeal Outcome:** Appellate Division found the trial court's jury instructions on punitive damages **fatally flawed** and **reversed the punitive damages verdict**, remanding for further proceedings. Compensatory damages affirmed.
**Supreme Court (2003):** Lockley v. N.J. Dept. of Corrections — NJ Supreme Court **affirmed compensatory damages**, attorneys' fees, and costs; returned case to trial court for reconsideration of punitive damages. Trial court had rejected remittitur motion, finding the awards — "although high and surprising" — did not shock the judicial conscience.
**Remittitur:** Trial court denied remittitur. Not discussed by Supreme Court on merits (case remanded for punitive damages reconsideration on different grounds).
**Note:** This is an employment harassment case under LAD (not a sexual assault tort), but important for the "shock the conscience" standard as applied by an NJ court.
**Source:** Justia, https://law.justia.com/cases/new-jersey/appellate-division-published/2001/a1783-99-opn.html; Supreme Court: https://law.justia.com/cases/new-jersey/supreme-court/2003/a-108-01-opn.html [VERIFY BEFORE FILING]

### 7. C.W. v. Roselle Board of Education, NJ App. Div. (Published, 2023) and App. Div. (2026)

**Court:** NJ Superior Court, Appellate Division (Published)
**Year:** 2023 (first decision); 2026 (second decision)
**Facts:** Plaintiff alleged sexual abuse on two occasions in 2004–2005 by a middle school math teacher. Filed complaint in 2020 under the NJ Child Sex Abuse Act and negligence. Issue: whether plaintiff could claim pain and suffering damages without meeting the Tort Claims Act's $3,600 medical expense threshold.
**Trial Outcome:** Trial court granted summary judgment for the school district — plaintiff could not meet the TCA monetary threshold for pain and suffering, so his claims for emotional distress/PTSD/depression were barred.
**Appellate Division (2023):** Affirmed the summary judgment; held that PTSD and severe depression from sexual abuse constitute "pain and suffering" under the TCA, subject to the monetary threshold requirement.
**Legislative Response:** In March 2025, the NJ Legislature amended the TCA to **eliminate the verbal and monetary thresholds in sexual abuse cases**.
**Appellate Division (2026):** Affirmed the summary judgment again — declined to apply the 2025 amendment retroactively to plaintiff's pending claim.
**Remittitur:** Not applicable — no verdict reached; case ended at summary judgment stage.
**Significance:** Identifies an important barrier to damages recovery that existed for public-entity defendants in NJ sexual abuse cases prior to the 2025 TCA amendment. Post-amendment, this limitation is now eliminated.
**Sources:** NJ Courts, https://www.njcourts.gov/court-opinion/cw-vs-roselle-board-of-education-et-al-l-0153-20-union-county-and-statewide-record-0; 2026 opinion: https://www.njcourts.gov/system/files/court-opinions/2026/a2236-23.pdf [VERIFY BEFORE FILING]

## SECTION VI: NJ SUPREME COURT ON DAMAGES CAPS AND PROPORTIONALITY IN SEXUAL TORT CASES

### Punitive Damages Proportionality

The NJ Punitive Damages Act (N.J.S.A. 2A:15-5.14) requires the trial judge to ascertain that any punitive award is reasonable in amount. NJ courts apply the BMW/Gore federal due process framework for constitutional limits on punitive damages against **private defendants**. For public entities, additional scrutiny applies.

**Tarr v. Bob Ciasulli's Mack Auto Mall, Inc.**, 194 N.J. 212 (2008): Punitive damages must be aimed solely at deterring the specific defendant, not general deterrence of others. [VERIFY BEFORE FILING]

**CSAA Exception:** Cases brought under N.J.S.A. 2A:61B-1 (Child Sexual Abuse Act) are **expressly exempt** from the 5× / $350,000 punitive damages cap. This is a significant feature for cases involving intentional child sexual abuse — the defendant's conduct may warrant very large punitive awards unconstrained by the cap.

### NJ Tort Claims Act — Now Amended for Sexual Abuse

Prior to March 2025, NJ's Tort Claims Act (N.J.S.A. 59:9-2(d)) required a plaintiff to establish at least $3,600 in medical expenses (the "verbal and monetary threshold") to recover pain and suffering damages from a public entity. The 2025 TCA amendment **eliminated this threshold for sexual abuse claims against public entities**. See *C.W. v. Roselle Board of Education* (App. Div. 2023, 2026). This amendment expands the universe of recoverable damages against public schools, DYFS, and state agencies.

### No Cap on Compensatory Damages

New Jersey does not cap compensatory damages for pain and suffering in civil cases. The jury may award any amount reasonably supported by the evidence. Appellate review of compensatory damages applies the "shock the conscience" standard (*Cuevas*, 2016).

## SECTION VII: REMITTITUR — KEY HOLDINGS

**The operative NJ Supreme Court holdings on remittitur most relevant to sexual abuse damages:**

1. **Cuevas v. Wentworth Group**, 226 N.J. 480 (2016): Abandoned comparative verdict methodology. Courts may NOT compare other juries' verdicts to decide if a particular award is excessive. Standard remains "shocks the judicial conscience." [VERIFY BEFORE FILING — confirm reporter citation]

2. **Orientale v. Jennings**, 239 N.J. 569, 218 A.3d 806 (2019): Mutual consent required for remittitur to stand in lieu of new trial. Both parties must agree to the judge's fixed amount; either party may reject and receive a new trial instead. [VERIFY BEFORE FILING]

3. **No NJ Supreme Court published opinion found** directly addressing remittitur of a sexual assault or child sexual abuse compensatory verdict as "grossly excessive." The closest is *Lockley* (2003), where the trial court denied remittitur of a $750,000 LAD award, finding it "although high and surprising, did not shock the judicial conscience," and the Supreme Court did not disturb that finding.

4. **Practical implication for Mell bankruptcy:** Under *Cuevas*, a defendant challenging a large NJ sexual abuse verdict as excessive on remittitur cannot rely on "but other verdicts are lower" arguments — that methodology is expressly barred. The verdict must be shown to shock the conscience based on the specific facts, not cross-verdict comparisons.

## SECTION IX: STRATEGIC OBSERVATIONS FOR MELL BANKRUPTCY

1. **The NJ damages landscape is favorable to large single-plaintiff awards.** Recent CVA verdicts of $25M and $30M against institutional defendants establish that NJ juries treat child sexual abuse as warranting very large compensatory awards. Even against an individual private defendant, the Mell facts (adult preying on a 15-year-old over five months with criminal conviction) present a strong damages case.

2. **No comparative verdict methodology permitted.** Under *Cuevas* (2016), any debtor or trustee argument that B.B.'s civil judgment is "excessive compared to other NJ verdicts" is legally invalid. The court cannot rely on cross-verdict comparisons in remittitur analysis.

3. **The CSAA exempts punitive damages from the 5× cap.** If B.B.'s civil claim was brought under N.J.S.A. 2A:61B-1 (the CSAA), punitive damages in that case are uncapped. This is significant for nondischargeability analysis under § 523(a)(6) — the size of the punitive component reflects the intentional-injury finding.

4. **Default judgment / proof hearing procedures.** If Mell failed to appear in or actively contest the civil case (e.g., given his federal imprisonment), damages may have been established at a proof hearing using the *A.B. v. Y.Z.* (2005) framework. That procedure is well-established in NJ and produces valid, enforceable judgments — but the "actually litigated" prong of collateral estoppel analysis may be weaker for default-entered elements. 5. **The 2025 TCA amendment** has no bearing on Mell (a private individual, not a public entity), but it demonstrates the NJ Legislature's intent to maximize recovery in sexual abuse cases by eliminating procedural barriers.

6. **Published Appellate Division opinion in B.B. v. Mell (2020) is controlling authority** for asset attachment and attorney's fees issues in the underlying civil case — relevant if the bankruptcy estate holds or has transferred assets that were the subject of that attachment writ.
