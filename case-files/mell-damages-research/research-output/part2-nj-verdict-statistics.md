## I. KEY NJ VERDICT STATISTICS FROM GOVERNMENT DOCUMENTS

### A. NJ State Custody Sexual Abuse Cases (Official Budget Data)

The most statistically significant aggregate NJ data comes from the NJ Office of Management and Budget, as reported in April 2026 budget documents and verified by the New Jersey Monitor:

- **Median settlement cost (state-defendant sexual abuse cases): $975,000** as of March 2026
- **Active pending cases:** approximately 350 lawsuits remain against the State of New Jersey
- **Total estimated exposure:** at least **$340 million** for pending state-custody sexual abuse cases
- **2024 aggregate state payouts:** more than **$23 million** to settle over a dozen sexual abuse complaints in a single year
- **2024 total state lawsuit costs:** $177.9 million (including all categories), up **$56 million** from 2023 ($121.9 million for 364 claims)

*Sources: New Jersey Monitor (April 20, 2026); NJ OMB budget documents (March 2026)*

> **Significance:** The $975,000 median is a hard data point from official government records. It reflects negotiated settlements, which typically run below verdict values. Actual trial verdicts in these cases have reached $25 million.

## V. NATIONAL DATA ON SEXUAL ASSAULT CIVIL DAMAGES

### A. Academic Research — Miller, Cohen & Hendrie Study (2017)

Ted R. Miller, Mark A. Cohen, and Delia V. Hendrie, *Noneconomic Damages Due to Physical and Sexual Assault: Estimates from Civil Jury Awards* (SSRN Abstract No. 2903628; also published on OAtext.com):

- **Average noneconomic damages for sexual assault/rape victims:** approximately **$204,576** (2015 dollars, based on 2010-2015 incidence data)
- The study used regression analysis to construct predictive estimates of pain, suffering, and reduced quality of life from jury award data
- The study found "considerably more variation" in jury awards for sexual assault compared to physical assault
- **Methodology note:** These figures represent modeled average noneconomic loss applied to "average" victimizations — actual jury awards in cases with institutional defendants and severe/prolonged child abuse will substantially exceed this baseline [VERIFY BEFORE FILING — confirm exact figures by accessing full study]

### B. BJS Civil Justice Survey Data

The Bureau of Justice Statistics' Civil Justice Survey of State Courts provides background context (most recent comprehensive data from 2005):

- **Overall median tort award (all tort types):** $28,000 (2005)
- Over **14% of plaintiff winners** were awarded damages exceeding $250,000
- **Intentional tort cases** represented approximately 2.7% of tort cases going to jury trial in the 75 largest counties
- **Punitive damages** were awarded in approximately 6% of jury cases

> **Note:** BJS does not separately publish statistics for sexual assault civil cases. The overall median figures mask the substantially higher values seen in sexual tort cases versus typical personal injury claims.

## VI. PUNITIVE DAMAGES IN NJ SEXUAL ABUSE CASES

### A. The Statutory Framework

**General NJ punitive damages cap (N.J.S.A. 2A:15-5.14):**
- Punitive damages are capped at the **greater of:**
  - 5 times the compensatory damages awarded, OR
  - $350,000

**Critical exception — Child Sexual Abuse Act (N.J.S.A. 2A:61B-1):**
- The punitive damages cap under N.J.S.A. 2A:15-5.14 **does not apply** to CSAA claims
- Child sexual abuse claims are specifically listed among the exceptions to the cap
- Result: in child sexual abuse cases, there is effectively **no statutory ceiling** on punitive damages

**Standard for punitive damages:** "Clear and convincing" evidence that defendant acted with actual malice or wanton and willful disregard of plaintiff's rights.

### B. Punitive Damages in Practice — NJ Cases

The documented NJ cases show mixed results on punitive damages:

- **Delbarton School (2025):** $5M compensatory; **punitive damages DENIED** despite abbot admitting he destroyed the victim's complaint letter; jury found plaintiff failed to prove "malice or wanton disregard" by clear and convincing evidence
- **Newark BOE ($30M verdict):** Total verdict — compensatory/punitive breakdown not confirmed in public record [VERIFY BEFORE FILING]
- **DYFS ($25M verdict):** Total verdict — breakdown not confirmed [VERIFY BEFORE FILING]
- **General observation:** NJ juries have denied punitive damages even where egregious institutional conduct is documented; the clear-and-convincing standard is a meaningful barrier

### C. Compensatory/Punitive Breakdown in National Sexual Abuse Cases

National data suggests punitive damages awards are less common than popularly assumed:
- Historical data: median punitive award **over all cases** was $0 (i.e., punitive damages were more often denied than awarded)
- When awarded, punitive damages in institutional sexual abuse cases have ranged from token amounts to multiples of compensatory damages
- Notable comparison: In the NH state custody case (2024), a jury awarded $20M punitive + $18M compensatory (roughly 1:1 ratio)

## VII. FACTORS THAT DRIVE VERDICT SIZE IN NJ SEXUAL TORT CASES

Based on the documented cases, the following factors correlate with higher NJ verdicts and settlements:

1. **Institutional defendant vs. individual defendant:** Institutional defendants (school boards, state agencies, religious orders) carry substantially higher verdict potential than individual perpetrators
2. **Severity and duration of abuse:** Prolonged abuse (e.g., the 3-foster-home, 4-perpetrator case resulting in $25M) commands higher damages than single incidents
3. **Age of victim at time of abuse:** Child victims generally receive higher awards than adult victims
4. **Nature of cover-up or institutional failure:** Document destruction (Delbarton), failure to supervise after prior complaints (Newark BOE), repeated DYFS failures across multiple placements (DYFS case) — all drove higher damages
5. **Post-abuse psychological sequelae:** PTSD, depression, diminished life enjoyment, failed relationships, and lost earning capacity all documented in high-value cases
6. **No NJ damages cap on noneconomic damages:** NJ does not cap pain and suffering in sexual abuse cases

## VIII. NJ LEGAL FRAMEWORK DEVELOPMENTS AFFECTING VERDICT TRENDS

### A. Child Victims Act (2019)

- Enacted December 1, 2019 (N.J.S.A. 59:2-1.3)
- **Retroactively revived** time-barred claims; created 2-year revival window
- Extended SOL to age 55 or 7 years after victim discovers injury-abuse connection
- Eliminated prior governmental immunity for sexual abuse claims against public entities
- Result: flood of new litigation; the $25M DYFS verdict was the "first CVA trial against the State of New Jersey"

### B. Simpkins v. South Orange-Maplewood School District (NJ Supreme Court, March 2026)

- NJ Supreme Court held that the CVA eliminates immunity that public entities previously enjoyed under the Tort Claims Act
- Schools may be held **vicariously liable** for off-campus teacher sexual abuse if the teacher used school-conferred authority over the student's educational environment
- Approximately 350 additional cases likely affected [VERIFY BEFORE FILING]
- This ruling is expected to increase both the volume of NJ sexual abuse cases going to trial and the institutional defendant exposure

## X. SUMMARY FINDINGS FOR LITIGATION USE

### What the Data Establishes

1. **NJ jury verdicts in institutional child sexual abuse cases range from approximately $331,000 to $30 million**, with documented verdicts at $25M and $30M representing cases with multiple victims, prolonged abuse, and severe institutional failures.

2. **The official NJ government median for sexual abuse settlements (state-defendant cases) is $975,000**, per NJ OMB budget documents as of March 2026. This represents negotiated settlements, not trial outcomes.

3. **Trial verdicts substantially exceed settlement medians** — the $8M settlement reached "days before trial" in 2026 and the $6.75M settlement reached the day before a scheduled trial both suggest defendants assess trial exposure in the multi-million dollar range even where settlements cluster around $1M.

4. **NJ child sexual abuse cases have no cap on punitive damages** (N.J.S.A. 2A:15-5.14 exception for N.J.S.A. 2A:61B-1 claims), but punitive damages are not automatic — the Delbarton jury declined them.

5. **The CVA (2019) and Simpkins (2026) dramatically expanded institutional liability exposure** in NJ, creating a more plaintiff-favorable environment than existed at the time of the Mell case's underlying facts.

6. **The Diocese of Camden aggregate** (~$267.5 million across 300-330 claimants) suggests per-victim values averaging $500K-$800K+ in clergy abuse cases — but individual case values in clergy abuse litigation have ranged from $10,000 to $513,000 at the settlement level and reached $5M at trial.

### Cautions

- All verdict statistics are drawn from publicly reported cases; lower-value verdicts and defense verdicts may be underreported
- The $30M Newark verdict and $25M DYFS verdict are outliers in frequency but reflect achievable outcomes in the most egregious cases
- No aggregated NJ-specific median/average verdict data for sexual assault cases was found in publicly accessible form without subscription access to NJ Jury Verdict Review & Analysis
- The $204,576 average noneconomic damages figure from the Miller/Cohen study is a national baseline for "average" victimizations — cases involving institutional defendants, child victims, and prolonged abuse substantially exceed this
