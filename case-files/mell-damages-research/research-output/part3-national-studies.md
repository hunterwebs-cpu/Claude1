## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Five core findings emerge from national research on civil sexual assault damages:

1. **The median civil sexual assault verdict/settlement nationally is approximately $1.25 million** — driven upward by large institutional cases. Single-plaintiff cases against individual (non-institutional) defendants are significantly lower.

2. **Awards exceeding $10 million are statistical outliers** in single-plaintiff cases involving individual perpetrators. Such awards appear primarily in: (a) cases with institutional co-defendants (churches, schools, foster care); (b) cases involving multiple incidents over years of abuse; (c) cases with particularly aggravated facts or celebrity defendants; or (d) punitive-heavy nuclear verdicts that are typically reduced on remittitur or appeal.

3. **Child sexual abuse cases routinely command higher verdicts than adult victim cases.** The research consistently identifies youth, vulnerability, and duration of abuse as the primary upward multipliers. The Mell case involves a victim who was 14-15 at the time of the conduct — positioning it between the adult and child-abuse ranges.

4. **Punitive damages are infrequent nationally** (awarded in approximately 5% of all civil cases with plaintiff wins; somewhat higher but not ubiquitous in sexual abuse cases). In New Jersey, punitive damages in sexual abuse cases brought under N.J.S.A. 2A:61B-1 (Child Sexual Abuse Act) are **uncapped** by statute. For adult sexual assault claims not brought under 2A:61B-1, the standard cap of $350,000 or 5x compensatory damages applies.

5. **The empirical literature confirms wide variance and unpredictability** in sexual assault jury awards — more so than in any other tort category studied. This variance cuts both ways in the bankruptcy context.

## SECTION I: KEY ACADEMIC LITERATURE

### A. Miller, Cohen & Hendrie (2017) — The Primary Empirical Study

**Citation:** Ted R. Miller, Mark A. Cohen & Delia V. Hendrie, *Noneconomic Damages Due to Physical and Sexual Assault: Estimates from Civil Jury Awards*, Forensic Science & Criminology, vol. 2(1): 1-10 (2017) [SSRN Abstract No. 2903628; also published at OAtext.com]

**Methodology:** The researchers purchased and manually coded a dataset from **1,467 jury awards and settlements** involving physical or sexual assault (excluding wrongful death) from **1980 to 1991**, obtained from Jury Verdict Research (JVR), now a Thomson Reuters product. Approximately **361 cases** were classified as sexual assault; the remaining **1,106 cases** were physical (non-sexual) assault.

**Key Findings:**

- The researchers estimated that **average non-economic losses for sexual assault/rape** (in 1990 dollars) were substantial but highly variable across cases.
- When updated to **2015 dollars**, the average non-economic damages for sexual assault were estimated at approximately **$204,576** in the base calculation — though wide variance existed.
- **Sexual assault awards showed considerably MORE variation** than physical assault awards. The researchers "were able to explain 45%-50% of the variation in the natural log of jury awards for physical assault" but found "considerably more variation in the case of sexual assault, indicating that sexual assault awards are less predictable based on the measured factors."
- Past and future medical and wage losses were reported in approximately **60% of the assault cases**.
- Data were converted to 1990 dollars using medical price adjusters.

**Limitations acknowledged by researchers:** JVR data are reported voluntarily, which limits their representativeness and raises concerns about selection bias. JVR data largely exclude defense verdicts and settlements. The dataset predates modern awareness of long-term trauma and is therefore likely to understate contemporary verdicts.

**Relevance to Mell:** This is the most rigorous academic dataset on civil jury awards for sexual assault. Even accounting for inflation to 2026 dollars, the modeled non-economic damages for a single-plaintiff sexual assault case — against an individual, non-institutional defendant — remain substantially below the $10 million range. The study found that sexual assault verdicts are the least predictable of all assault categories, meaning any claimed "typical" $10 million figure has no statistical foundation.

### B. Bublick (2006) — Tort Suits by Rape and Sexual Assault Victims

**Citation:** Ellen M. Bublick, *Tort Suits Filed by Rape and Sexual Assault Victims in Civil Courts: Lessons for Courts, Classrooms and Constituencies*, 56 SMU L. Rev. 1779 (2003) [SSRN Abstract No. 883671; full text at SMU Law Review]

**Methodology:** Bublick examined **hundreds of recent appellate court decisions** in tort cases filed by rape and sexual assault victims over a thirty-year period, comparing state supreme court decisions in sexual assault cases in the early 1970s versus the early 2000s.

**Key Findings:**

- "Over this 30-year time frame, reported state supreme court decisions in sexual assault cases rose by more than 1,000 percent."
- The cases from 2000-2004 were "markedly different" from earlier periods, with many cases focused on **institutional responsibility** — business owners, employers, third parties — rather than direct perpetrators.
- "At the appellate level, the vast majority of tort claims filed by sexual assault victims involve **at least some claims against a third-party defendant**."
- The study documents both the growth in civil sexual assault litigation and the continuing legal obstacles for victims in what Bublick characterizes as "this disjointed and developing area of law."

**Relevance to Mell:** The Bublick study's finding that high-appellate-profile cases overwhelmingly involve **institutional/third-party defendants** corroborates the inference that large verdicts in civil sexual assault cases typically involve institutional misconduct or cover-up — not single individual perpetrators acting alone. B.B. v. Mell involves an individual perpetrator (Mell) without a third-party institutional co-defendant.

### C. Sharkey (2006) — Dissecting Damages: Sexual Harassment Awards

**Citation:** Catherine M. Sharkey, *Dissecting Damages: An Empirical Exploration of Sexual Harassment Awards*, 3 J. Empirical Legal Stud. 1 (2006) [SSRN Abstract No. 852264]

**Methodology:** Examined **232 cases** in which plaintiffs won some positive amount of compensatory damages in state and federal, trial and appellate court decisions from **1982 to 2004** in sexual harassment cases.

**Key Findings:**

- Identified a "consistent, and statistically significant, positive relationship between punitive and compensatory damages" in cases where punitive damages were awarded.
- The critical predictive factors were "damages limitations" — including whether the **1991 Civil Rights Act cap** applied and whether plaintiffs appended state civil rights and tort claims.
- Confirmed that variables pertaining to "the nature and severity of harassment" were NOT the primary predictors of damages amount — systemic legal and procedural factors mattered more.
- The study involved workplace sexual harassment (Title VII context), not rape/assault — damages there are generally lower than for physical sexual assault.

**Relevance to Mell:** Even in employment sexual harassment cases (a less severe category than rape), the predictors of high awards were institutional and procedural, not merely severity of conduct. This reinforces that large damages in sexual misconduct cases require institutional accountability factors not present in a single-perpetrator/single-victim civil case.

### D. Lytton (2023) — Tort Claims for the Coverup of Child Sexual Abuse

**Citation:** Timothy D. Lytton, *Tort Claims for the Coverup of Child Sexual Abuse: Private Litigation, Corporate Accountability, and Institutional Reform*, 72 DePaul L. Rev. (2023) [SSRN Abstract No. 4457413]

**Key Findings:**

- Lytton analyzes how tort claims against the **Catholic Church, Boy Scouts of America, and USA Gymnastics** drove major accountability and reform.
- Over **13,000 children and adolescents** were abused by Catholic priests since 1950.
- The largest verdicts and settlements in child sexual abuse civil litigation arose from institutional defendants, not individual perpetrators.
- These cases succeeded in "bringing the scandal to light, focusing attention on the need for institutional reform, and spurring Church leaders and public officials into action."

**Relevance to Mell:** The largest financial outcomes in the national child sexual abuse civil litigation data are institutional cases — dioceses, the BSA, national gymnastics programs. These are categorically different from a civil case against an individual perpetrator without an institutional co-defendant. Citing these outlier institutional verdicts as the "norm" for individual-defendant cases would be analytically unsound.

## SECTION II: BUREAU OF JUSTICE STATISTICS (BJS) DATA

### A. BJS Civil Justice Survey — Tort Bench and Jury Trials, 2005

**Citation:** U.S. Dep't of Justice, Bureau of Justice Statistics, *Tort Bench and Jury Trials in State Courts, 2005* (NCJ 223851, Oct. 2008)

**Key Statistics (National):**

- In 2005, **over 14,000 plaintiffs received monetary damages** in civil trials nationwide.
- **Less than five percent** of winning plaintiffs received damages exceeding **$1 million**.
- The **median final damage award** across all civil bench and jury trials was **$28,000**.
- For **tort cases specifically**, the median award was **$24,000**.
- The median final award for plaintiff winners in **motor vehicle accident cases** was **$15,000**.
- **Plaintiffs won 52% of tort trials** (jury) and **68% of bench trials** in 2005.
- Intentional tort cases (including assault and battery) were tracked as a separate category from negligence torts; assault/battery cases historically carry higher median awards than automobile cases but lower than medical malpractice.

**Key BJS Finding on $1 Million Verdicts:**

"In 2005, less than five percent of plaintiffs who won in civil bench and jury trials received damages exceeding $1 million." (BJS Press Release, NCJ 223851, 2008)

**Relevance to Mell:** Even in 2005, over 95% of civil trial plaintiff-winners received less than $1 million. While sexual assault cases may be above the median, the BJS data establishes the baseline: million-dollar verdicts are atypical even in a winning tort case, and ten-million-dollar verdicts are statistical outliers.

### B. BJS Punitive Damage Awards in State Courts, 2005

**Citation:** U.S. Dep't of Justice, Bureau of Justice Statistics, *Punitive Damage Awards in State Courts, 2005* (2009)

**Key Statistics (National):**

- Litigants sought punitive damages in **12% of all estimated 25,000 civil trials** in 2005.
- Punitive damages were **actually awarded** in approximately **5% of civil cases with plaintiff wins** — approximately 700 out of 14,000 cases.
- The **median punitive damage award** was **$64,000**.
- **26% of punitive awards** exceeded compensatory awards by a ratio of **4 to 1 or greater**.
- An earlier (1995) DOJ study found juries awarded punitive damages in **6% of all successful suits**, with **approximately half of punitive damage awards at $50,000 or less**.

**Case Type Breakdown on Punitive Damages:**
The BJS report discusses punitive damages by category including: intentional tort, automobile accident, medical malpractice, product liability, and employment discrimination. Intentional torts (which include assault and battery) had higher punitive damage rates than negligence torts.

**Relevance to Mell:** Punitive damages are an exceptional rather than routine feature of civil tort verdicts nationally. Even in intentional tort cases — the category that would encompass sexual assault — punitive damages are not awarded in the majority of cases. The median punitive award where awarded at all was $64,000.

## SECTION III: NEW JERSEY-SPECIFIC DATA

### A. New Jersey Punitive Damages Framework for Sexual Abuse

**Statutory Text — N.J.S.A. 2A:15-5.14 (General Cap):**
"No defendant shall be liable for punitive damages in any action in an amount in excess of five times the liability of that defendant for compensatory damages or $350,000, whichever is greater."

**Exception for Child Sexual Abuse — N.J.S.A. 2A:61B-1:**
The punitive damages cap under N.J.S.A. 2A:15-5.14 does **NOT apply** to causes of action brought under the Child Sexual Abuse Act (C.2A:61B-1). Under 2A:61B-1, the statutory minimum recovery is $10,000 plus reasonable attorney's fees, or actual damages (compensatory + punitive), whichever is greater.

**P.L. 2019, c.120 (S477) — Extension to Adult Survivors:**
Effective December 1, 2019, New Jersey extended N.J.S.A. 2A:61B-1 to cover victims 18 years of age or older who suffered sexual assault. This law also:
- Extended the statute of limitations to 7 years from discovery
- Created a two-year look-back window for previously time-barred claims
- Made public entities liable for sexual assault by their employees/agents

**Standard for Punitive Damages in NJ (N.J.S.A. 2A:15-5.12):**
Punitive damages may be awarded "only if the plaintiff proves, by clear and convincing evidence, that the harm suffered was the result of the defendant's acts or omissions, and such acts or omissions were actuated by actual malice or accompanied by a wanton and willful disregard of persons who foreseeably might be harmed by those acts or omissions."

**Relevance to Mell:** B.B.'s civil claim against Mell could invoke N.J.S.A. 2A:61B-1 (as amended in 2019 to cover adult victims 18+) to seek uncapped punitive damages. Mell's conduct underlying the civil case — grooming a 14-year-old from approximately 2015, continuing until discovered in 2018 — would likely have been prosecuted under the child victim provisions. | Adult sexual assault settlements (general) | Not stated | **$30,000–$50,000 range** | Non-institutional, lower-severity end |

**Critical Methodological Note:** The $9.2 million mean is driven by a small number of **catastrophic outlier cases** (BSA settlements, institutional cover-up cases, celebrity defendants) that inflate the average far above what a typical case yields. The **median of $1.25 million** is the more informative central tendency — and even this figure includes institutional cases that systematically push outcomes higher.

### B. Child vs. Adult Victim Comparison

The research consistently identifies the following ordering (lowest to highest damages):

1. **Adult victim, minor assault, single incident** — lowest (often $30K–$200K)
2. **Adult victim, rape/penetration, individual defendant** — mid-range ($200K–$2M typical range)
3. **Child victim (CSA), individual defendant** — higher ($450K–$3M typical range)
4. **Child victim, institutional defendant** — highest ($1M–$50M+ range)
5. **Child victim, institutional cover-up, multiple victims** — outlier range ($10M–$100M+)

**Reasoning:** Younger victims receive higher compensation because: (a) developmental harm occurs during formative years; (b) the abuse typically spans longer periods; (c) long-term psychological and relational consequences are more severe; (d) courts and juries view the breach of trust as more egregious.

**Application to Mell:** B.B. was 14 at the time Mell began grooming her — clearly a minor child victim. This places the case in the child victim category (factors 3-4 above), which commands higher damages than adult victim cases. However, the defendant here is an individual (Mell), without a co-institutional defendant, which means the institutional multipliers (factors 4-5) are absent.

### C. Punitive Damages in Sexual Abuse Cases — National Pattern

**National BJS Data (baseline):**
- Sought in 12% of civil trials
- Awarded in ~5% of cases with plaintiff wins
- Median punitive award: $64,000

**Sexual Abuse Context (elevated rate, but still minority):**
- Sexual abuse cases, by nature of intentional conduct, have higher punitive damage rates than negligence torts
- Punitive damages are more commonly sought and more commonly awarded where there is evidence of a pattern of abuse, concealment, or predatory targeting
- Even where awarded, large punitive amounts (multiples of compensatory) are subject to judicial reduction on due process grounds (BMW v. Gore, 517 U.S. 559 (1996); State Farm v. Campbell, 538 U.S. 408 (2003))

**Delbarton School (NJ, 2025) — Instructive NJ Data Point:**
Despite a first-of-its-kind NJ clergy abuse trial and a sympathetic plaintiff who was abused by a monk decades ago, the jury **declined to award any punitive damages** to the plaintiff, even after a bifurcated punitive phase. The $5M verdict was entirely compensatory. This is the most recent NJ jury data on sexual abuse punitive damages.

## SECTION V: THE $10 MILLION THRESHOLD — STATISTICAL CONTEXT

### A. When $10 Million+ Verdicts Occur in Sexual Abuse Cases

Based on the research, verdicts at or above $10 million in single-plaintiff sexual assault civil cases occur primarily in the following narrow circumstances:

1. **Institutional co-defendant with resources** — A school, church, corporation, or government entity with deep pockets and a history of concealment (e.g., Catholic Church cases, Boy Scouts, foster care systems)

2. **Serial perpetrator with multiple victims** — The aggregated harm establishes a pattern that supports large punitive awards

3. **High-profile celebrity or wealthy individual defendant** — E.g., the Cassie Ventura/Diddy settlement ($20M); Bill Cosby civil verdict ($59.25M)

4. **Extreme aggravation or violence** — Physical injuries, sex trafficking, kidnapping, or repeated acts over long periods

5. **Nuclear verdict dynamics** — Jury sympathy in high-profile cases producing verdicts that are subsequently remitted (e.g., the Los Angeles $900M verdict reduced to $90M on motion)

**Illustrative Cases Near/At $10M Against Individual Non-Celebrity Defendants:**

- Wisconsin (June 2025): $4.1M verdict ($3.1M compensatory + $1M punitive) against individual Milwaukee restaurateur with criminal conviction; adult victim
- New York CVA (2022): $5M total ($4M compensatory + $1M punitive) against individual uncle; child victim; appellate-affirmed
- California tennis coach case (2022): $10M verdict — individual coach at school (institutional context)
- New Hampshire (Michael Gilpatrick, 2025): $10M from state government for institutional abuse in youth detention

**Pattern:** Where $10M verdicts appear against individual defendants (without institutional co-defendants), they are: (a) reported as notable outliers by the legal press; (b) frequently subject to remittitur or appeal; or (c) against individuals with deep pockets (celebrities, executives). They are explicitly characterized in legal reporting as exceptions, not the norm.

### B. The Mell-Specific Context

The Mell civil case involves:
- Single plaintiff (B.B.)
- Individual defendant (Mell), serving concurrent federal/state sentences
- Mell's available assets are limited (wage attachment from Gulfstream entities was litigated in the 2020 interlocutory appeal)
- No institutional co-defendant (based on case caption and Appellate Division opinions)
- Abuse of a 14-year-old (child victim — more serious category)
- Criminal conviction of defendant (supports punitive element, but Mell lacks resources for large punitive recovery)
- Single victim (not part of a larger pattern exposed through litigation)

The absence of an institutional co-defendant with resources, and Mell's pending bankruptcy, are critical variables that distinguish this from the large-verdict outlier cases.

## SECTION VI: RELEVANCE TO BANKRUPTCY PROCEEDINGS

### Why These Studies Matter in Bankruptcy

In the bankruptcy context, the creditor (B.B., through Nagel) will likely assert that her civil claim is worth a substantial amount to justify (a) dismissal of the bankruptcy as a "bad faith" filing to avoid the debt, and (b) that the debt is nondischargeable under § 523(a)(6). Both arguments depend on the **magnitude and nature of the claimed debt**.

**Key bankruptcy implications from this damages research:**

1. **The civil judgment amount is not necessarily the debt amount for bankruptcy purposes.** If the trial court awarded a judgment that included speculative future therapy costs or punitive damages that exceed constitutional limits, the proper debt amount for § 523(a)(6) analysis is the compensatory portion reflecting actual harm.

2. **Punitive damages in NJ may be uncapped** under N.J.S.A. 2A:61B-1 (if B.B.'s claim was brought under the CSAA), but the due process limits of *BMW v. Gore* and *State Farm v. Campbell* still apply as a federal constitutional ceiling.

3. **The national data supports that a $10 million verdict against an individual non-institutional perpetrator is atypical**, which may be relevant to: (a) any judicial evaluation of whether the civil judgment reflects fair market value of the claim; (b) any discussion of settlement leverage versus actual trial risk; and (c) rebutting any suggestion that Mell filed bankruptcy solely to evade a "certainty" of a multi-million verdict.

4. **The Delbarton School data point** (NJ's most recent jury verdict in a sexual abuse trial) is directly relevant: even in the most sympathetic NJ sexual abuse facts (historical clergy abuse, institutional concealment), the jury awarded $5M compensatory and **zero punitive**. This sets a realistic NJ jury benchmark.
