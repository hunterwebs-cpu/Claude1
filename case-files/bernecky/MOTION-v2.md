# DEFENDANT'S MOTION TO MODIFY CONDITIONS OF SUPERVISED RELEASE
## PURSUANT TO 18 U.S.C. § 3583(e)(2) AND FED. R. CRIM. P. 32.1(c)

Defendant Jeffrey Bernecky, proceeding *pro se*, respectfully moves this Court, pursuant to 18 U.S.C. § 3583(e)(2) and Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 32.1(c), to modify the conditions of supervised release imposed by the Judgment entered June 12, 2018 (Dkt. 28). In support of this motion, Mr. Bernecky states as follows.


## PRELIMINARY STATEMENT

The commencement of supervised release marks a critical juncture for any defendant. Having paid his debt through imprisonment, he must now reintegrate into his community and prove himself a productive, law-abiding member of it. The conditions that will govern that reintegration, however, are almost always fixed years earlier, at a sentencing hearing where the room's attention is on one question only — how much time the defendant will spend in prison — and rarely on how a computer-monitoring clause will read once that defendant is actually applying for jobs, or how much the technology behind it will have changed by the time he gets the chance to find out. The years that typically separate sentencing from the start of supervision, combined with how quickly technology moves during them, produce exactly the kind of anomaly this motion addresses.

Mr. Bernecky's case shows what that anomaly looks like in practice. He was sentenced in 2018. His ten-year term of supervision begins on or about July 27, 2026. In the interval, an employer that offered him permanent, full-time work read Special Condition (a)'s text on its own and grew concerned that a monitoring provision written for Mr. Bernecky might extend to the company's own systems — a possibility no one at sentencing was ever asked to consider. Probation has since given two different informal answers to that concern, neither in writing and neither consistent with the other, and the employer has told Mr. Bernecky not to report to work until the question is resolved. He has been out of work for roughly two weeks, at the exact moment he is completing his transition out of Bureau of Prisons custody and has just taken on a one-year apartment lease.

Because of this and a number of other changed circumstances, Mr. Bernecky respectfully requests that the Court modify or clarify a number of the conditions imposed in 2018, so that he is able to succeed at the reintegration supervised release is meant to produce, and so that he is not forced to unknowingly sacrifice constitutionally protected rights — including the privilege against self-incrimination that Special Condition (g)'s polygraph and voice-stress requirements will otherwise place at risk, since that condition as written offers him no counsel and no advance ruling on what the privilege actually covers. Mr. Bernecky seeks modification or clarification of ten conditions in total. As the discussion below shows, the relief requested as to each is justified.

Section 3583(e)(2) exists precisely so that a defendant does not have to wait and find out — the Advisory Committee's own reason for the rule is to let a person "obtain resolution of a dispute over an ambiguous term or the meaning of a condition without first having to violate it." Mr. Bernecky asks the Court to use that authority now, at the moment the Sentencing Commission's own November 2025 amendment identifies as the right one: the start of supervision. *See* U.S.S.G. § 5D1.4(a) (2025). This is not a challenge to the 2018 sentence and does not ask the Court to reopen anything the sentencing judge actually decided. It asks for ten corrections, taken up one condition at a time in the order the Judgment lists them, so that when Mr. Bernecky's term of supervision begins, he begins it knowing what each condition actually requires.


## JURISDICTION AND STANDARD OF REVIEW

Section 3583(e)(2) authorizes the Court to "modify, reduce, or enlarge the conditions of supervised release, at any time prior to the expiration or termination of the term of supervised release, pursuant to the provisions of the Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure relating to the modification of probation." 18 U.S.C. § 3583(e)(2). That cross-reference makes Rule 32.1(c) the governing vehicle here.

The authority is general and forward-looking; a defendant need not show changed circumstances before invoking it. *United States v. Lussier*, 104 F.3d 32, 36 (2d Cir. 1997) (the Advisory Committee's Notes explain that "the sentencing court must be able to respond to changes in the [defendant's] circumstances as well as new ideas and methods of rehabilitation"). *Lussier* also marks the outer edge of that authority: § 3583(e)(2) reaches how a condition is scoped and applied going forward, not whether it was lawful when the sentencing court first imposed it — that question belongs to direct appeal or 28 U.S.C. § 2255. *Id.* at 33. Every request in this motion falls on the forward-looking side of that line: each asks the Court to clarify or adjust what a condition means and how it will operate, not to declare the 2018 Judgment invalid.

No waiting period applies. The statute permits modification "at any time," and the Sentencing Commission's most recent guidance affirmatively invites courts to reassess existing conditions "in consultation with the probation officer after the defendant's release from imprisonment" — Mr. Bernecky's exact circumstance right now. U.S.S.G. § 5D1.4(a) (2025). There is no reason to leave any of the following questions for after supervision starts when the record already supports resolving them today.


## STATEMENT OF FACTS

On June 12, 2018, the Honorable David G. Larimer sentenced Mr. Bernecky to 120 months' imprisonment and ten years of supervised release. Mr. Bernecky pleaded guilty quickly, to possessing child pornography involving prepubescent minors, and that sentence was imposed concurrently with the resolution of a supervised-release violation from an earlier federal conviction, at docket 6:11-CR-06045-001, for possessing and distributing child pornography. Mr. Bernecky is a recidivist on this record, and this motion does not ask the Court to look past that fact. What the record also shows, and what this motion does rely on, is that Mr. Bernecky has been cooperative and honest with supervision authorities, including his current probation officer, throughout the period since. He is scheduled to complete his prison term and begin his ten-year term of supervision on or about July 27, 2026.

Much has changed in the years since 2018. Mr. Bernecky's sister had children while he was incarcerated, so that the nieces and nephews he would see today at an ordinary family gathering — his mother's house at Christmas, for instance — did not exist at the time of sentencing and could not have been the subject of any finding then. Mr. Bernecky also believes the $500 restitution obligation set out in the Judgment has already been paid in full, which, once confirmed, leaves nothing for Special Condition (i) to secure.

The most urgent change concerns his employment, and the sequence of events matters. While housed at the Rochester Residential Reentry Center, Mr. Bernecky obtained a position with a contact lens manufacturer through a temporary staffing agency. After roughly ninety to one hundred twenty days in that role — the point at which the company's own practice allows a temporary worker to be considered for direct hire — the manufacturer invited him to apply for permanent, full-time employment. In that application, Mr. Bernecky was fully forthcoming about his conviction and his coming supervision. The company came back with questions about what his conditions would require of it, centered on whether Special Condition (a) would require the company to consent to government surveillance of its own computer systems simply because Mr. Bernecky was on its payroll. Mr. Bernecky gave the company the condition's exact language, and, when that did not resolve the question, the entire Judgment.

Having reviewed it, the company told Mr. Bernecky it needed assurance from someone — Probation or the Court — that its systems would not automatically be subject to surveillance or search because he worked there. Mr. Bernecky told the company's human resources office that his actual job duties did not appear to involve computer use at all; HR explained that every employee is issued an account regardless of duties, but offered, on its own, to disable public internet access on that account if doing so would resolve Probation's concern. In the meantime, the company told Mr. Bernecky not to report to work until the question was resolved. He has not worked in approximately two weeks, at the exact moment he is completing his transition out of Bureau of Prisons custody and is responsible for a one-year apartment lease he has already signed, begun furnishing, and is currently permitted to occupy only on three-day passes.

Once the company told him it needed assurances, Mr. Bernecky called his probation officer directly to try to get the question resolved quickly. On that call, the probation officer told him it should be fine "so long as they can monitor you," and said Probation would follow up directly with human resources; Mr. Bernecky gave Probation his full, unrestricted consent to discuss the matter candidly with the company. Two weeks later, Mr. Bernecky learned that Probation had in fact contacted human resources — and had told the company something different from what Mr. Bernecky himself had been told: that there would be no problem so long as there was no internet access. Neither statement is a clarification of record, and the two are not even consistent with each other — one ties approval to monitoring capability, the other to the absence of internet access entirely — which is itself evidence that Probation has no settled, considered position on what this condition requires of an employer, only an improvised one that shifts depending on who is asking.

Nor is the exposure limited to this one employer or this one moment. The same silence in the condition's text, and the same shifting, capability-contingent answers Probation has already given twice, will resurface with any future employer, for as long as Mr. Bernecky is separately required, under Standard Condition 7, to work full time.


## ARGUMENT

### I. Special Condition (a) — The Computer and Internet Monitoring Program

Once Mr. Bernecky's term of supervision begins, Special Condition (a) will require him to participate in CIMP as a condition of using any "internet capable device," will authorize Probation to install monitoring software on any device "owned or operated by the defendant," and will require any monitoring to "avoid, as much as possible, reading any privileged information." Left as written, this single condition raises four separate problems.

**A. No finding under U.S.S.G. § 5F1.5 exists anywhere in the record, and without one, the condition cannot be read, once supervision begins, to reach an employer's own systems.** An occupational restriction requires the sentencing court to find "a reasonably direct relationship . . . between the defendant's occupation, business, or profession and the conduct relevant to the offense of conviction," together with a finding that nothing less restrictive would work. U.S.S.G. § 5F1.5(a); *see United States v. Peterson*, 248 F.3d 79, 85-86 (2d Cir. 2001) (per curiam). Nothing available in this case — not the Judgment itself, not any Statement of Reasons this motion's preparation was able to locate, not any filing by either party — shows that finding was ever requested, let alone made. That gap is not cosmetic. The § 5F1.5 finding is the thing that makes an occupational restriction lawful; without it, there is nothing to enforce as one. A sentencing court that was never asked to weigh, and never did weigh, whether Mr. Bernecky's occupation bears the required relationship to his offense cannot have authorized this condition to operate as an occupational restriction, because it never performed the analysis that authorization depends on.

Because the finding was never requested and never made, the common-sense reading is the simplest one: the condition never applied to employment at all. Told that an employer had questions about Special Condition (a), Probation's answer should have been exactly that — it does not apply to you. Instead, Probation gave two different answers, and both of them were the opposite of that: an affirmative restriction. Asked directly by Mr. Bernecky, the probation officer said the arrangement should be fine "so long as they can monitor you." Two weeks later, contacted separately by the employer's human resources office, Probation said instead that there would be no problem so long as there was no internet access. Each of those statements tells an employer that some condition attaches to it — monitoring capability in one version, internet access in the other. The correct answer, given the absence of any § 5F1.5 finding, is that no condition attaches to an employer at all. Probation has now shown twice that it will impose some version of an affirmative restriction rather than say that plainly. Mr. Bernecky asks the Court to say it instead: Special Condition (a) does not apply to his employment in any respect, regardless of what any employer's systems can or cannot do.

The Second Circuit's own recent decisions confirm the stakes of getting this wrong. Even a bare employer-*notification* clause, far milder than a monitoring-capability requirement, has been treated as an occupational restriction demanding this exact showing. *See United States v. Love*, No. 24-1706-cr, 2025 WL 1077176 (2d Cir. Apr. 8, 2025); *United States v. Jenkins*, 854 F.3d 181 (2d Cir. 2017); *United States v. Ruff*, No. 18-3247-cr (2d Cir. Nov. 14, 2019) (vacating outright); *United States v. Lombardi*, No. 17-2352-cr (2d Cir. Mar. 22, 2018) (remanding); *United States v. Piper*, No. 19-1058-cr (2d Cir. Mar. 30, 2020) (remanding). Mr. Bernecky's condition imposes no notification requirement at all — he volunteered one anyway. It has already produced something considerably heavier than notification: a live capability dispute with a real employer, months before supervision even begins.

*United States v. Brown*, 173 F.4th 45 (2d Cir. 2026), does not change this analysis. Brown argued that his condition operated as a *compelled* notification requirement; Mr. Bernecky makes no comparable claim, since his own disclosure was voluntary and already accepted by the employer. Brown's condition also included its own "as permitted by the employer" consent mechanism, which this condition has none of, and the Brown panel decided the occupational-restriction question on plain-error review — a standard built for correcting a lower court's mistake on appeal, which has nothing to do with a motion asking this Court to exercise its own § 3583(e)(2) discretion for the first time. The mismatch is sharper still on these facts: the job requires no public internet access, the employer has already offered a disabled-internet internal account, and CIMP's purpose — controlling internet-facilitated risk — has no application to a job like this one.

None of this depends on the formalities of how conditions get created. It depends on the more basic rule that only the sentencing court may impose a special condition of supervised release. *United States v. Matta*, 777 F.3d 116, 122 (2d Cir. 2015). Where a probation officer's administration of a condition would reach further than what the court actually ordered, the Second Circuit has been direct about it: a requirement that would "contradict, rather than execute" the sentence is one "the Probation Office was plainly without power to impose." *United States v. Browder*, 866 F.3d 504, 514 (2d Cir. 2017). *United States v. Kunz*, 68 F.4th 748, 766-67 (2d Cir. 2023), applies the identical principle to this same CIMP language.

Leaving this to be worked out informally, one anxious employer at a time, does not fix anything; a written order does. The Tenth Circuit confronted the identical confusion over materially the same condition language in *United States v. Mike*, 632 F.3d 686, 696-97 (10th Cir. 2011), where even the government conceded the condition "cannot be read fairly" to reach an employer's computers — and the court did not leave that ambiguity standing. It remanded with instructions to clarify the condition, directing that if the district court actually meant to reach an employer's devices, it would need to make the § 5F1.5 findings first. *Id.*; *see also United States v. Matteson*, 327 F. App'x 791 (10th Cir. 2009). That is the relief requested here: a declaration that, absent any § 5F1.5 finding in this record, Special Condition (a) will not be read, once supervision begins, to reach an employer's own systems.

Mr. Bernecky brings this claim in his own right, not the employer's. The threat to his own employment, and the risk of a violation finding built on an unwritten and shifting understanding of his own condition, are injuries to him — the sort of interest a defendant may raise directly. *Bond v. United States*, 564 U.S. 211, 220 (2011). Should the Court view it differently, ordinary third-party standing reaches the same outcome, given how directly his compliance depends on the employer's cooperation and how impractical it would be to expect the employer to litigate its own interests inside someone else's criminal case. *See Powers v. Ohio*, 499 U.S. 400 (1991); *Kowalski v. Tesmer*, 543 U.S. 125 (2004); *Singleton v. Wulff*, 428 U.S. 106 (1976); *Warth v. Seldin*, 422 U.S. 490 (1975).

Two related conditions share the same underlying flaw and should be clarified alongside this one. Standard Condition 5 lets Probation approve or reject where Mr. Bernecky lives; if applied as an unguided veto rather than simple verification, it presents the identical delegation problem. Standard Condition 6 lets Probation visit him anywhere and seize anything it sees in plain view; at a workplace he neither owns nor controls, he has no authority to consent to a search of property that belongs to someone else. Neither condition should be read, once supervision begins, to reach property outside Mr. Bernecky's own ownership or control.

**B. Left unqualified, the monitoring method Probation would use cannot coexist with the privilege protection the condition already promises.** The condition itself states that monitoring "shall be designed to avoid, as much as possible, reading any privileged information or any private material that is not illegal." *Kunz* treated this exact clause as a real, enforceable limit — language that "constrains the supervisors in their surveillance . . . by requiring them not to abuse their power and access," not a formality. 68 F.4th at 766-67. Logging every keystroke would capture privileged attorney-client communications along with everything else, before anyone has sorted privileged material from anything else. A monitoring method that captures everything cannot be one that avoids reading privileged material "as much as possible" — it avoids nothing.

Precedent involving this same category of offense supports narrowing the method, not the promise. *United States v. Sofsky*, 287 F.3d 122, 126-27 (2d Cir. 2002), approved "a more focused restriction, limited to pornography sites and images," as sufficient in an image-based case. *United States v. Lifshitz*, 369 F.3d 173, 192 (2d Cir. 2004), compared unfiltered, content-blind monitoring to "searching [a defendant's] diary" rather than "the highly targeted diagnosis accomplished by drug testing," and on remand specifically endorsed keyword- or phrase-triggered monitoring in place of "recording all varieties of computer-related activity." *Id.* at 193 n.11. Mr. Bernecky's offense concerns images, not written communications; nothing about it justifies capturing every word he types once he is on supervision.

The Court should modify Special Condition (a) so that CIMP, once implemented, uses a method that does not indiscriminately sweep in privileged or non-image material — domain review, file-type filtering, image-hash scanning, or the keyword-triggered approach *Lifshitz* itself endorsed — rather than full keystroke capture.

**C. "Internet capable device" should be read, once the condition takes effect, to mean a device capable of handling images — not any device that happens to reach the internet for an unrelated purpose.** The Second Circuit has already told this district how to read this exact boilerplate without absurd consequences. Facing the identical CIMP language, it refused a literal reading of the condition's encryption-related terms, holding it "would be absurd to interpret" Probation as requiring pre-approval every time a supervisee visits an ordinary "https" site or sends an encrypted message. *Kunz*, 68 F.4th at 764-65. The court described its own method plainly: "a sensible reading of the restrictions neutralizes the most troubling of those concerns." *Id.* at 754-55. That is not an unusual interpretive tool — plain condition language is not read to compel "the impossible," *id.* at 763 (citing *United States v. Johnson*, 446 F.3d 272, 281 (2d Cir. 2006)) — and the same resistance to absurd literal readings runs through general statutory construction. *See Public Citizen v. U.S. Dep't of Justice*, 491 U.S. 440, 454-55 (1989); *Griffin v. Oceanic Contractors, Inc.*, 458 U.S. 564, 571 (1982); *Green v. Bock Laundry Mach. Co.*, 490 U.S. 504, 509 (1989).

The absurdity of the broadest reading is not hard to find. Ordinary homes today run on devices that connect to the internet for a purpose that has nothing to do with images: a refrigerator that reorders groceries, a video doorbell that pings a phone, a fitness band that uploads a nightly heart rate, a CPAP machine relaying a sleep-apnea patient's breathing to a physician. Every one of these is, taken literally, an "internet capable device." None has a browser, an app store, or any way to locate, store, or display a picture. No one would seriously argue a refrigerator belongs under CIMP because it has a Wi-Fi chip. The word "operates" fares no better read literally — the thing a person most naturally operates is not a phone but a car, and an ordinary car today, equipped with OnStar, FordPass Connect, or comparable telematics, is exactly as "internet capable" as a refrigerator. *See State v. Henderson*, 907 N.W.2d 623 (Minn. 2018) (defining "operate" a motor vehicle as "any act that causes a motor vehicle to function or controls the functioning of the motor vehicle"). Either the government accepts that a refrigerator, a doorbell, and a car would all fall under CIMP surveillance, or it accepts what *Kunz* already holds: the boundary that actually matters is what a device can do with an image, not whether it can reach the internet.

That boundary already sits inside the condition's own text, just applied inconsistently. The opening sentence limits itself to "any internet capable device"; the monitoring-authorization clause that follows drops the qualifier and reaches anything "owned or operated by the defendant." Reading the condition to mean, throughout, what its own first sentence says is not an outside addition — it enforces the document as written. Other circuits already draw this line. *E.g.*, *United States v. Ullman*, No. 14-1414, 2015 WL 3559221 (10th Cir. June 8, 2015) ("devices without Internet access, such as gaming systems, are not restricted"). None of this touches which devices belong to an employer or any other third party — that question is answered independently in Part I.A, above, based on the absence of any § 5F1.5 finding at all.

A simpler alternative exists for devices Mr. Bernecky does not own: bar him from using them, rather than authorize monitoring or inspection of whoever does own them. That protects the government's interest without imposing anything on a person not before this Court.

**D. As written, the fee obligation has no floor, no schedule, and no ability-to-pay check.** Special Condition (a) will require Mr. Bernecky to "pay the cost of monitoring services" without ever specifying an amount, a method for setting one, or any accommodation for inability to pay. Judicial Conference guidance warns against exactly this kind of open-ended delegation and favors "a court-approved sliding scale . . . responsive to changes in a defendant's financial circumstances" — nothing like that appears here. Before nonpayment of a financial condition may support revocation, the law requires an inquiry into willfulness and good-faith effort. *Bearden v. Georgia*, 461 U.S. 660 (1983). And should Mr. Bernecky ever become Medicaid-eligible for a covered service, an open-ended payment obligation risks conflicting with the federal rule against billing a Medicaid beneficiary beyond the plan's own cost-sharing limits. 42 C.F.R. § 447.15. The identical defect will recur in Special Conditions (b), (g), and (h), addressed in Parts II, VI, and VII below; rather than repeating the same argument four times, this motion requests the same fix each time the problem appears — an actual ability-to-pay mechanism.

**E. Requested relief.** The Court should order: (i) a declaration that, absent a § 5F1.5 finding in the record, Special Condition (a) does not apply to Mr. Bernecky's employment in any respect, and imposes no burden on any employer, regardless of what that employer's systems can or cannot do; (ii) a modification of the monitoring method to exclude indiscriminate keystroke capture; (iii) a clarification that "internet capable device" means a device capable of accessing, storing, or displaying images, together with a prohibition on using another person's device rather than a mandate to monitor it; (iv) a clarification that Standard Conditions 5 and 6 do not reach property Mr. Bernecky does not own, lease, or control; and (v) a court-approved, ability-to-pay-adjusted fee schedule.


### II. Special Condition (b) — Sex-Offense-Specific Treatment

Once supervision begins, Special Condition (b) will require Mr. Bernecky to "follow the rules and regulations" of a sex-offense-specific treatment program with no court-imposed limit on what those rules may say, and will require him to contribute to the cost of the program.

**A. A private treatment provider should not be able to write rules that exceed what the sentencing court itself ordered.** Only the court may restrict a defendant's liberty as a condition of supervision; that authority cannot pass to a private provider by default. *Matta*, 777 F.3d at 122. *Browder* involved exactly this scenario: a treatment provider's own agreement barred contact with *any* minor, including the defendant's own children, even though the court's sentence had specifically excluded his children from that restriction. The Second Circuit struck the provider's rule down as something "the Probation Office was plainly without power to impose." 866 F.3d at 514. Mr. Bernecky faces the identical structure in advance: a program category the court will order, whose actual content a provider alone will set. The Court should make clear now that a provider rule exceeding what this Court itself ordered cannot support a future violation finding.

One further point belongs here as context rather than as an independent legal claim. Cognitive-behavioral therapy is legitimate treatment. Feeding a provider continuous CIMP surveillance data to reshape a specific pattern of thought is a different undertaking, worth naming even though it is not the basis for relief. The government "can[not] prescribe what shall be orthodox . . . in matters of opinion." *West Virginia State Bd. of Educ. v. Barnette*, 319 U.S. 624, 642 (1943). Mr. Bernecky does not ask the Court to revisit *Stanley v. Georgia*, 394 U.S. 557 (1969), or the reasons *Osborne v. Ohio*, 495 U.S. 103 (1990), declined to extend it to child pornography, given the real harm to real children. His point is narrower: no court has ever authorized turning surveillance into this particular treatment tool, and that missing authorization — not any claim about the content of his thoughts — is what Part II.A already addresses.

**B. Fee obligation.** The same defect described in Part I.D, above, applies here, and the same fix is requested: an ability-to-pay mechanism consistent with *Bearden*.


### III. Special Condition (c) — Contact with Minors and Loitering

Special Condition (c) will forbid Mr. Bernecky from "deliberate contact with any child under 18 years of age, excluding his biological or adopted children." He has neither, so the condition's only carve-out has never applied to him and never will.

**A. Enforced as written, this condition will not merely limit contact with one relative — it will remove Mr. Bernecky from ordinary family life altogether.** Once he is on supervision, he will not be able to attend a family gathering without first knowing, with certainty, that no minor relative will be present — and the condition gives him no way to find that out safely in advance. If he stops by his mother's house on Christmas and his sister's children are already there, he will be in violation the moment he does not immediately leave, not because anyone has found him a danger to those children, but because the condition draws no line between an unplanned family visit and whatever the sentencing court actually meant to prevent. The same would be true if grandchildren arrive unannounced. Nothing in the record addresses whether Mr. Bernecky poses a danger to any specific relative, or whether mere presence at a family event — as distinct from some other kind of contact — poses any risk at all.

The right to maintain relationships with extended family, not just one's own children, is constitutionally protected, and this condition's illusory carve-out sits directly on top of that right. *See Moore v. City of East Cleveland*, 431 U.S. 494 (1977) (protecting the choice to live among extended family and demanding "careful scrutiny" of government intrusion on it). The Second Circuit requires an individualized basis before restricting that right this broadly. *United States v. Myers*, 426 F.3d 117, 129-30 (2d Cir. 2005) (vacating a contact-with-own-child condition for the identical absence of an individualized record, and requiring heightened scrutiny wherever a condition touches familial association). *United States v. Bleau*, 930 F.3d 35, 42-43 (2d Cir. 2019), likewise required a court to explain the necessity of a minors-contact condition absent an obvious basis in the record, and vacated one where the underlying offense involved no actual contact with minors. Mr. Bernecky is not *Bleau*'s defendant — this is at least his second child-pornography-related conviction, and this motion does not pretend otherwise. What it asks for is a structure that accounts for that record honestly once supervision begins: contact with minor relatives permitted in the presence of a knowledgeable adult already aware of his conviction — his mother, father, or sister — so that an ordinary family gathering will not require advance clearance, or a sudden exit, every time a child happens to be there. Other circuits already use exactly this kind of carve-out, reading minors-contact conditions "to exclude incidental encounters." *United States v. DaSilva*, 844 F.3d 8 (1st Cir. 2016).

His sister's children having been born during his incarceration is, on its own, a genuine change in circumstance under *Lussier*: the relatives whose presence at family gatherings will create this problem did not exist, and could not have been the subject of any finding, when he was sentenced in 2018.

**B. The condition should also be clarified, before supervision begins, to impose no restriction on Mr. Bernecky's occupation.** A seventeen-year-old coworker, or a minor customer encountered in the course of his job duties, should not trigger "deliberate contact" requiring advance permission or an abrupt exit — for the same reason given in Part I.A: no § 5F1.5 finding supports reading any condition in this Judgment as an occupational restriction.


### IV. Special Condition (d) — Financial Monitoring

Special Condition (d) will give Probation open access to Mr. Bernecky's personal and business financial records to monitor "compliance with not buying or subscribing to online services that provide child pornography." No one has ever alleged that Mr. Bernecky purchased child pornography — his conviction is for possession. A condition aimed at detecting a purchase has nothing to detect in a record with no purchase in it. The fix is to require a reasonable-suspicion trigger before financial records may be accessed, matching the structure the Judgment already uses for computer monitoring itself, which activates "as triggered by impermissible/suspicious activity" rather than running on an open-ended basis.


### V. Special Condition (e) — Sex Offender Registration and Presentence Report Disclosure

Special Condition (e) will authorize disclosure of Mr. Bernecky's presentence report to the New York State Board of Examiners of Sex Offenders, and further disclosure "to the county court and to the parties involved in the determination of the defendant's final classification level." A presentence report may be disclosed to a third party only on a "compelling" and "particularized" showing made at the time disclosure is actually sought — not through a blanket authorization entered years earlier, with no chance for the defendant to be heard once the moment actually arrives. *See United States v. Charmer Indus., Inc.*, 711 F.2d 1164 (2d Cir. 1983). A standing waiver of that case-specific showing is difficult to reconcile with a rule that places the burden squarely on whoever requests disclosure.

The registration language in the same condition adds nothing: Mandatory Condition 6 already requires registration under 34 U.S.C. § 20901 *et seq.* The Court should strike the advance PSR authorization and the duplicate registration language now, without foreclosing a properly noticed, case-specific disclosure request if one is ever actually made.


### VI. Special Condition (g) — Polygraph and Computerized Voice Stress Analyzer Testing

Special Condition (g) will require Mr. Bernecky to submit to polygraph and CVSA examinations and answer whatever he is asked, reserving only a later opportunity to argue his answers cannot be used against him. Every other condition addressed in this motion concerns a practical harm that has not yet occurred. This one concerns a constitutional harm that has not yet occurred, and the difference matters.

**A. Answering first and arguing later will not solve the actual problem: Mr. Bernecky will not answer any polygraph or CVSA question, on any topic, without a lawyer present to advise him as he answers.** That is his position now, in advance, not a negotiating opener once an exam is scheduled. The reason is straightforward: deciding whether a specific question calls for a privileged answer is a legal judgment, not an instinct, and nothing about an examination room will equip anyone to make that judgment correctly under pressure. *United States v. Johnson*, 446 F.3d 272 (2d Cir. 2006), upheld an after-the-fact challenge structure — but only against the narrow immunity theory Johnson raised on his own facts, and it never addressed either point raised here. *Minnesota v. Murphy* supplies the governing rule: a statement is compelled whenever the state, "either expressly or by implication, asserts that invocation of the privilege would lead to revocation." 465 U.S. 420, 435 (1984). A condition that will require Mr. Bernecky to "answer the questions posed" before any privilege determination is made does exactly that. *United States v. Von Behren*, 822 F.3d 1139, 1150-51 (10th Cir. 2016), applied *Murphy* to a nearly identical sex-offense polygraph and held that threatening program termination and revocation for refusing an incriminating question is unconstitutional compulsion. The privilege covers more than a direct confession — it reaches anything that supplies "a link in the chain of evidence" usable to prosecute. *Hoffman v. United States*, 341 U.S. 479, 486 (1951). Because this is at least Mr. Bernecky's second child-pornography-related conviction, any future compelled disclosure carries real stakes — a fact that favors the protections requested here, not one that undercuts them. And nothing in his history justifies subjecting him to this scrutiny to begin with: he has never once been found dishonest in treatment or supervision, and a condition premised on dishonesty that has never actually occurred cannot satisfy § 3583(d)'s reasonably-related standard no matter how the privilege question comes out.

**B. Requested protections.** Until each of the following is in place, Mr. Bernecky will be entitled to decline any polygraph or similar examination question: (i) counsel present before, during, and after the examination; (ii) enough time, per question, for counsel to determine whether invocation is warranted, since that determination is legal rather than instinctive; (iii) examiner credentials sufficient to withstand a *Daubert* challenge, with CVSA reliability addressed separately; and (iv) no disclosure of anything the examination produces without leave of court.

No federal court appears to have granted a request quite like this one, and that should be said plainly rather than dressed up as settled law. It fits most naturally as a discretionary safeguard the Court may craft under 18 U.S.C. § 3583(d) — not as an argument that an exam room is custodial in the Miranda sense, a framing that would invite comparison to authority already rejecting a counsel-presence claim on custodial grounds. *See J.B. v. New Jersey State Parole Bd.*, 229 N.J. 21 (2017). It is enough that the privilege question is a legal one, and that federal supervision policy already treats it as too delicate for a probation officer to resolve alone, directing that uncertain invocations go to the court instead. Guide to Judiciary Policy, Vol. 8E, § 190.45. If the Court will not order counsel's presence throughout, Mr. Bernecky asks, in descending order, for consultation access at any point during the exam — the same accommodation a grand jury witness receives to step out and consult counsel, *see United States v. Mandujano*, 425 U.S. 564 (1976) — or, failing that, advance written review of the question list by counsel, with disputed questions referred to the Court rather than decided by the examiner.

**C. This request continues the same case rather than starting a new one, and that distinction should not wait for the government to raise it.** The Sixth Amendment right to counsel attaches offense by offense, with a separate critical-stage analysis to follow. *Rothgery v. Gillespie County*, 554 U.S. 191 (2008); *see Texas v. Cobb*, 532 U.S. 162 (2001); *McNeil v. Wisconsin*, 501 U.S. 171 (1991); *Kirby v. Illinois*, 406 U.S. 682 (1972). That rule forecloses only extending the right to a brand-new, uncharged offense; it says nothing about whether the right persists through ongoing supervision in the *same* case where it already attached at indictment, and supervision continues that same conviction rather than starting a new one. The harder line of authority is *Pennsylvania v. Finley*, 481 U.S. 551 (1987), together with *Gagnon v. Scarpelli*, 411 U.S. 778 (1973), and *Morrissey v. Brewer*, 408 U.S. 471 (1972) — treating post-conviction proceedings as due process matters rather than Sixth Amendment ones, and this motion confronts that line directly instead of leaving it for reply. *Estelle v. Smith*, 451 U.S. 454 (1981), is the closer analogy: a compelled psychiatric exam counted as a critical stage precisely because it was tied to a sentencing decision still pending. *Finley*'s concern — that no Sixth Amendment right outlasts "exhaustion of the appellate process" — never arose in *Estelle*, because that proceeding, unlike a closed conviction, was still open. Mr. Bernecky's supervision is open in the same sense: the sentencing court's jurisdiction over it continues, which is the entire premise on which this motion can be brought at all.

**D. Counsel should be appointed now to assist with these examinations once they occur, and there is no softer version of this request.** 18 U.S.C. § 3006A(a)(1)(E) already requires appointed counsel for a financially eligible person who "faces modification . . . of a condition . . . of a term of supervised release" — a description that fits exactly, since resolving this condition's procedures is what this Part asks the Court to do. The interests of justice point the same direction from another angle: appointing counsel to help someone choose, in the moment, between answering under compulsion and risking revocation for silence is not a courtesy for paperwork — it is what actually protecting the right requires. The choice is this, stated plainly rather than softened: either the Court appoints counsel to assist with these examinations as described in Part VI.B, or Mr. Bernecky will invoke the Fifth Amendment categorically as to every such examination once supervision begins, rather than attempt to make real-time privilege calls alone.

**E. Fee obligation.** The same defect described in Part I.D, above, applies here, and the same fix is requested.


### VII. Special Condition (h) — Mental Health Treatment and Alcohol Abstinence

Once supervision begins, Special Condition (h) will require participation in mental health treatment and, "while in treatment or taking psychotropic medication," complete abstinence from alcohol, along with a contribution toward the cost of the program.

**A. Alcohol abstinence should follow an actual physician's determination, not apply automatically regardless of one.** Mr. Bernecky has no objection to treatment itself. His objection is to a blanket abstinence rule that will apply whether or not any physician has ever said a specific medication he takes interacts with alcohol. This Circuit has already vacated a comparable blanket restriction for lacking the individualized nexus § 3583(d) demands. *United States v. Betts*, 886 F.3d 198 (2d Cir. 2018) (striking a total alcohol ban as "not reasonably related to any of the factors outlined in Section 5D1.3(b)" where nothing in the record tied the offense, any violation, or any history to alcohol). It has since applied the same rule to a nearly identical "abstain from alcohol during treatment" clause, rejecting the government's position that the clause could simply be reimposed without any individualized look. *See United States v. Rodriguez*, No. 22-1820-cr (2d Cir. Dec. 7, 2023) (summary order).

The clause's own wording makes this worse: it triggers on "treatment **or** medication," meaning talk therapy alone — with no medication at all — is enough to require abstinence, even though no interaction risk could exist without a medication in the picture. Even within the medication branch, treating "psychotropic medication" as one uniform risk category ignores that alcohol-interaction risk differs sharply across drug classes. This precise clause, word for word, is this district's standard template for mental-health-treatment conditions, adopted after this district's own remand in *Browder* — not a finding made specifically about Mr. Bernecky.

The fix: the alcohol-abstinence requirement should apply only while, and only because, a treating physician has determined alcohol is contraindicated for a specific medication or treatment Mr. Bernecky is actually receiving.

**B. Fee obligation.** The same defect described in Part I.D, above, applies here, and the same fix is requested.


### VIII. Special Condition (i) — General Financial Disclosure and Asset Restraint

Special Condition (i) will give Probation broad access to Mr. Bernecky's finances and control over his assets, tied by its own terms to "the collection of any unpaid fine or restitution." Mr. Bernecky believes the $500 restitution has already been paid in full. If the record confirms that, the condition has nothing left to collect, and *Lussier* confirms no separate showing of changed circumstances is needed to vacate a condition whose entire stated purpose has already been fulfilled. 104 F.3d at 36.


### IX. Standard Condition 8 — No Communication with Felons

Standard Condition 8 will forbid Mr. Bernecky from "communicat[ing] or interact[ing]" with anyone he knows has a felony conviction, absent advance permission. That reaches further than it may first appear: by some estimates, roughly five percent of American adults have a felony conviction, meaning a blanket communication ban with no exception for incidental contact or after-the-fact discovery of someone's record is likely to be triggered by ordinary life, not by any deliberate association with crime. The condition bans *communication*, not merely association — the two words appear separately and mean different things, and "communicate" covers a single momentary exchange in a way "associate" does not — and it will sweep in conduct plainly worth protecting: asking a public figure with a felony record for an autograph, attending a rally for felon voting rights, or trading pleasantries with a coworker or neighbor whose record becomes known only later. It will also conflict with Special Condition (b)'s own requirement that Mr. Bernecky attend group treatment with other convicted people, and with the ordinary reality that sex-offender housing tends to sit near other people with felony records — a proximity the government's own registration and residency rules helped create.

The general vagueness principle applies directly: "[a] condition is too vague if it requires a reasonable person to speculate as to its meaning." *United States v. Reeves*, 591 F.3d 77, 80-81 (2d Cir. 2010) (also recognizing intimate personal association as a protected interest, even though *Reeves* itself concerned a different disclosure requirement). No case squarely limits a "communicate or interact" felon condition to sustained relationships, and this motion says so rather than overstating what exists; the argument rests on first principles and the condition's own text. The fix is to narrow the condition to cohabitation with, or harboring of, a known felon, or, at minimum, to add exceptions for incidental contact and after-the-fact discovery consistent with ordinary civic and working life.


### X. Standard Condition 12 — Risk Notification

The version of this condition Probation has described in its pre-release materials predates the Second Circuit's own correction of it and the district's standing order implementing that correction. *See United States v. Boles*, 914 F.3d 95 (2d Cir. 2019) (holding the standard risk-notification condition vague and ordering its scope clarified). Mr. Bernecky asks the Court to apply the existing WDNY standing order to him, exactly as it already applies to everyone else in this district. This motion does not challenge the standing order's mechanism on its face.[^1]
