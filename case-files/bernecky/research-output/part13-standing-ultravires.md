## BOTTOM LINE (read this first)

This is **not** a third-party standing problem, and it should not be pled as one. Bernecky's ultra vires claim is properly characterized as an assertion of **his own** direct, personal injury — that Probation is exceeding the scope of authority § 3603 and his own Judgment give it over **his own** supervision, causing **him** concrete harm (lost/suspended employment, exposure to a violation finding for non-compliance with a demand that has no textual or statutory basis, and an ongoing, unauthorized restraint on his liberty). Under the black-letter rule of *Warth v. Seldin*, third-party standing doctrine is triggered only when a litigant "rest[s] his claim to relief on the legal rights or interests of third parties" — and Bernecky's claim does not do that. It rests on the scope of authority the Judgment and § 3603 give Probation over *him*. The employer's involvement is incidental to the mechanism of the overreach, not the legal basis of the claim.

The strongest authority is **structural, not a single square holding**, because no case was located that says this in so many words in the supervised-release context. The strongest single piece of authority is **Bond v. United States**, 564 U.S. 211 (2011), which holds that an individual defendant has standing to raise a structural/scope-of-authority argument (there, federalism/Tenth Amendment) that nominally protects someone else's interest (there, the States'), because the defendant "seeks to vindicate *her own* constitutional interests" in not being subjected to government action in excess of its authority — she is not required to show the States' own standing. That is Bernecky's argument transposed: he seeks to vindicate his own interest in not being subjected to Probation action in excess of *its* delegated authority, even though the mechanism of the overreach runs through a demand on a third party (the employer) whose own Fourth Amendment interest is not what Bernecky is litigating.

The second-strongest support is a **pattern-based argument**: the existing delegation cases (Matta, Kunz, Carlineo, Peterson, Browder) and, more directly on point, the Second Circuit's entire employer-notification/occupational-restriction line (**Jenkins, Lombardi, Ruff, Piper, Salazar, Gorychka, Brown**) all adjudicate a defendant's challenge to a condition whose practical operation runs through an employer, without any court engaging in third-party-standing analysis. This is honest circumstantial support (courts simply proceed straight to the merits), not an explicit holding that third-party standing doctrine doesn't apply — no case was found saying so in those words. That gap should be disclosed, not papered over.

As a backstop, if a court insists on applying the *Kowalski v. Tesmer*/*Powers v. Ohio* "close relationship + hindrance" test, Bernecky satisfies it: an ongoing, shared-interest employment relationship (close relationship) and an employer with no practical vehicle to intervene in someone else's criminal supervision proceeding and no independent incentive to litigate a dispute that costs it nothing to simply avoid by declining to hire (hindrance, easily "genuine" per *Kowalski*'s standard, which requires only genuine, not insurmountable, hindrance).

**Confidence: high** that this is not, in substance, a third-party standing problem, and that framing it as an own-injury/scope-of-authority claim is correct and defensible. **Moderate** confidence that a court would agree without pushback — because the fact pattern (relief that in practical effect controls what the employer must show) blurs the line, and no case was found squarely rejecting a standing objection on materially identical facts. The recommended drafting strategy (Part 6, below) is built to manage that residual risk.

## PART 1 — Correct Characterization of the Claim: Own Injury, Not Third-Party Rights

### The doctrinal on/off switch: what triggers third-party standing analysis at all

*Warth v. Seldin*, 422 U.S. 490 (1975) **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — reconstructed from Justia/Cornell LII/Constitution Annotated summaries]**, states the general rule: a plaintiff "generally must assert his own legal rights and interests, and cannot rest his claim to relief on the legal rights or interests of third parties." This is the operative test — and it is a test about **what the claim rests on**, not about who else happens to be affected by the dispute's resolution. The rule is a **prudential** limitation (not required by Article III itself), aimed at situations where the litigant is functionally standing in for someone else's cause of action.

Applying this: Bernecky's claim, correctly framed, does **not** rest on the employer's legal rights (e.g., "the employer's Fourth Amendment right against unreasonable search of its business systems has been violated"). It rests on **Bernecky's own** legal position that:

1. His Judgment's CIMP condition contains no textual authorization for Probation to impose *any* requirement on an employer (confirmed directly against the verbatim condition text — the word "employ-" does not appear in Special Condition (a) at all; see client-directives.md).
2. 18 U.S.C. § 3603 defines and limits a probation officer's duties by reference to "the conditions specified by the sentencing court" as to "a probationer or a person on supervised release, who is under his supervision" — i.e., Bernecky (see Part 3, below). There is no statutory hook for imposing requirements on non-supervisees.
3. Probation's "like kind and degree" demand on the employer is, as a result, **ultra vires as to Bernecky's own case** — an exercise of supervisory power the Judgment and the statute do not give it — and it is causing **Bernecky**, personally, concrete injury: a suspended/lost job, continued financial precarity, and exposure to being found in violation of supervised release (once it begins) for "non-compliance" with a demand that was never a lawful condition of his release in the first place.

None of that requires proving the employer's Fourth Amendment rights were violated. Bernecky does not need the employer's rights to be violated for his own claim to succeed — his claim succeeds or fails entirely on the scope of *Probation's* authority over *him*, which is a classic ultra vires/scope-of-delegated-authority question the defendant asks the sentencing court (which retains § 3583(e)(2) jurisdiction over his own case) to resolve. This is precisely the distinction *Warth* draws: the employer's separate Fourth Amendment interest is *relevant context* (it explains why Probation reached for this particular unauthorized demand and why the demand has real-world bite), but it is not the *legal basis* of the relief requested. The requested relief — a declaration/clarifying order that the condition does not authorize this demand and that Probation may not administer it this way — runs entirely between Bernecky and the government/Probation, and would fully redress Bernecky's injury without any adjudication of the employer's own rights. (This mirrors the "practical framing" already identified in Part 4 of part1-cimp-occupational-delegation.md — that memo's instinct on this point was correct; this track supplies the standing-doctrine confirmation for it.)

### Bond v. United States — the closest analytical fit located

**Citation:** *Bond v. United States*, 564 U.S. 211 (2011).
**Verification status:** **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — reconstructed from Justia, Cornell LII, Pacific Legal Foundation commentary, and other secondary sources; not independently read in full.]**

**Facts/posture (as reconstructed):** Carol Anne Bond was federally prosecuted under a chemical-weapons statute for a personal, non-terrorism-related act (poisoning a romantic rival). She argued the statute, as applied, exceeded Congress's enumerated powers and intruded on the States' reserved police power under the Tenth Amendment. The government argued she lacked standing to raise a Tenth Amendment claim because that Amendment protects the *States'* sovereign interests, not hers personally — i.e., the government tried to recast her structural claim as an assertion of a third party's (the States') rights.

**Holding (as reconstructed, corroborated across multiple sources):** The Supreme Court unanimously rejected that recharacterization. The Court held that "an individual, in a proper case, can assert injury from governmental action taken in excess of the authority that federalism defines," and that in making that argument, Bond "seeks to vindicate her own constitutional interests." The Court explained that federalism exists not merely to protect States as such, but "to protect individual liberties by diffusing government power," and that the Tenth Amendment therefore "secures the freedom of the individual" as well as the States. Because Bond herself was prosecuted — a concrete, personal injury — she did not need to establish the States' own standing to raise the argument that the statute exceeded the government's delegated authority.

**Why this is the strongest available authority for Bernecky's threshold problem:** Bond confirms a general principle applicable well beyond federalism: **a person subjected to government action has standing, in her own right, to argue that the government exceeded the scope of authority delegated to it — even where the sphere of authority being exceeded is conventionally understood to belong to, or protect, someone else** (there, the States; here, the employer's own Fourth Amendment interest in its business systems). The touchstone is not whose interest the "scope of authority" rule is conventionally said to protect — it is whether the person raising the argument has suffered her own concrete injury from the action that exceeded that scope. Bernecky has: a real, suspended job offer, ongoing financial harm, and exposure to a violation finding. Bond's framework directly supports treating this as Bernecky's own claim.

**Necessary candor about the limits of the analogy:** Bond is a federalism/enumerated-powers case, not a supervised-release/probation-officer-authority case, and no case was found applying Bond's reasoning specifically to an agency's or probation officer's scope-of-delegated-authority in the supervision context. It should be cited **by analogy**, for its general principle about standing to raise scope-of-authority arguments that formally implicate someone else's sphere of protection, not as directly on-point supervised-release authority.

## PART 2 — The Delegation-Doctrine and Employer-Notification Cases: A Pattern of Merits-Only Adjudication, No Standing Analysis

### 2(a) The delegation cases already in the file

*Matta* (777 F.3d 116), *Kunz* (68 F.4th 748), *Carlineo* (No. 20-1020), *Peterson* (248 F.3d 79), and *Browder* (866 F.3d 504) were re-searched specifically for any discussion of standing. **None of the available secondary-source summaries mention any standing analysis, objection, or discussion in any of these five cases.** Each proceeds directly to the merits of whether the delegation/condition was lawful:

- **Matta** — the court held delegating the inpatient/outpatient treatment choice to Probation was "tantamount to allowing [Probation] to decide the nature or extent of the defendant's punishment," and vacated. No standing discussion in any source reviewed.
- **Kunz** — affirmed a delegation because an "objective, rule-based program" (the ICMP) constrained Probation's discretion. No standing discussion located.
- **Carlineo** — vacated a program-participation condition as unconstitutionally vague and an impermissible delegation because the named program ("Partners in Restorative Initiatives") left "too many blanks" for Probation to fill in. No standing discussion located.
- **Peterson** — held that employer-notification decisions could not be left to the probation officer's "unfettered discretion," and that if the sentencing court wanted mandatory notification for some employment but not others, it had to specify guidelines itself. **This case is directly on point for the standing question because it necessarily involves a third party (the employer to be notified), yet the Second Circuit resolved it purely as a delegation-and-discretion problem belonging to the defendant, with no third-party-standing gloss of any kind** in any source reviewed.
- **Browder** — held that a treatment provider's ("Mid-Erie") rule exceeding the court's own sentence (requiring permission before *any* contact with minors, including Browder's own children, when the court's sentence excluded his own children from that restriction) could not be enforced: "Because such a requirement... would contradict, rather than execute, Browder's sentence, the Probation Office was plainly without power to impose it." Again, no standing discussion — the court treated this as a straightforward question of whether Probation/the provider exceeded the scope of what the court itself ordered as to Browder.

**Honest limitation:** this is **circumstantial, not explicit, support**. None of these opinions contains a sentence saying "this is not a third-party standing problem" or "the defendant need not satisfy *Kowalski*'s test." The most that can honestly be said is: **courts in this exact doctrinal family — delegation-of-authority challenges that necessarily implicate a third party's role (a treatment provider, an employer) — routinely resolve the defendant's challenge on the merits without ever treating it as requiring third-party standing analysis.** That pattern is itself evidence of how courts intuitively categorize these claims (as belonging to the defendant), even though it falls short of "ironclad authority" in the form of an explicit holding.

### 2(b) The stronger, more direct pattern: the employer-notification/occupational-restriction line

This research surfaced a directly on-point line of Second Circuit authority not in the original CIMP memo: **United States v. Jenkins**, 854 F.3d 181 (2d Cir. 2017); **United States v. Lombardi** (2d Cir. 2018); **United States v. Ruff** (2d Cir. 2019); **United States v. Piper** (2d Cir. 2020); **United States v. Salazar** (2d Cir. 2025); **United States v. Gorychka**, 2024 WL 3964287 (2d Cir. Dec. 28, 2024); and **United States v. Brown**, No. 24-1227 (2d Cir. Apr. 21, 2026) (already flagged as adverse authority in Part 1's memo). **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — all reconstructed from WebSearch snippets, not independently read; Jenkins citation in particular should be double-checked, as one snippet suggested 854 F.3d 181 while the original client-directives reference is to different Jenkins/Ruff line cases by name only.]**

**The doctrinal point that matters here:** this entire line holds that an **employer-notification condition** — i.e., a condition whose practical effect requires a defendant to disclose his conviction to, or otherwise involve, his employer — **is itself treated as an "occupational restriction" subject to U.S.S.G. § 5F1.5**, reviewable and vacatable **as the defendant's own claim**. Per one secondary source, the Second Circuit has "broadly stated that '[w]e have treated ... employer notification conditions as occupational restrictions'" (quoting *Gorychka*). Every one of these cases necessarily involves a third party (the employer) as the mechanism through which the condition operates — an employer must be told something, or must consent to something, or must accommodate something — and **not one of them was litigated, or needed to be litigated, as a third-party standing question**. They were litigated, exclusively, as questions about whether the *defendant's* own occupational liberty was impermissibly restricted without the required § 5F1.5 findings.

This is a stronger and more direct analog to Bernecky's situation than the delegation cases generally, because it is the **same condition-type** (computer-monitoring/employer-facing supervision requirements) and the **same practical structure** (a condition's real-world operation running through the employer) as Bernecky's CIMP dispute. If an employer-notification requirement's effect on the employer relationship is routinely treated as the defendant's own occupational-restriction claim (no standing barrier), then Probation's "like kind and degree" monitoring-capability demand — a materially more burdensome ask on the employer than mere notification — should be at least as available to Bernecky as his own claim, for the same reason: **the injury it inflicts (job loss, restricted occupational liberty) is Bernecky's own, regardless of the fact that the demand is nominally addressed to the employer.**

**Recommended integration:** this line should be cited in the standing section as the closest and most direct real-world demonstration that Second Circuit courts do not treat "the condition operates through a third party" as triggering third-party-standing analysis — while candidly noting (as with the delegation cases) that none of these opinions contains an explicit standing holding; the support is structural/pattern-based.

## PART 3 — Statutory Analysis: § 3603 and § 3583(e)(2) Confirm the Scope-of-Authority Question Runs to Bernecky, Not the Employer

### 18 U.S.C. § 3603 — Duties of probation officers

**[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — reconstructed from Cornell LII/govinfo/House Office of Law Revision Counsel search snippets, cross-corroborated across sources.]**

The statute's first three enumerated duties are:

> (1) instruct **a probationer or a person on supervised release, who is under his supervision**, as to the conditions specified by the sentencing court, and provide him with a written statement clearly setting forth all such conditions;
> (2) **keep informed, to the degree required by the conditions specified by the sentencing court**, as to the conduct and condition of a probationer or a person on supervised release, who is under his supervision, and report his conduct and condition to the sentencing court;
> (3) use all suitable methods, **not inconsistent with the conditions specified by the court**, to aid a probationer or a person on supervised release who is under his supervision...

Three textual features matter for the standing analysis:

1. **The duty-defining phrase is "the conditions specified by the sentencing court."** Section 3603 does not grant probation officers freestanding, general supervisory power — it authorizes them to act only "to the degree required by the conditions specified by the sentencing court." A probation officer's authority is thus, by the statute's own terms, **derivative of and bounded by the Judgment**, not an independent font of power the officer can invoke to impose whatever conditions she judges useful. Probation's "like kind and degree" demand on Bernecky's employer is not tied to any condition "specified by the sentencing court" — the CIMP condition says nothing about employers — so it falls outside § 3603's own textual grant of authority on its face.
2. **The duty runs to "a probationer or a person on supervised release, who is under his supervision"** — i.e., to Bernecky, specifically and exclusively. The statute nowhere authorizes the probation officer to impose independent requirements on persons who are *not* "under his supervision." The employer is not, and never will be, "a person on supervised release... under [Probation's] supervision." Whatever authority § 3603 confers, it is authority *over Bernecky's supervision*, which confirms that a challenge to whether Probation exceeded that authority is necessarily and exclusively a challenge about the scope of power being exercised over **Bernecky's own supervision** — not a dispute about the employer's separate legal position.
3. This textual structure supports treating Probation's employer-facing demand as *ultra vires as an act of supervising Bernecks* — the demand is unauthorized supervision-of-Bernecky conduct dressed up as a requirement addressed to someone else, and the question "did Probation exceed § 3603 authority in supervising Bernecky" is quintessentially Bernecky's own question to raise.

### 18 U.S.C. § 3583(e)(2) — Modification of conditions

**[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — reconstructed from Cornell LII/FindLaw/govinfo snippets.]**

Section 3583(e)(2) authorizes the sentencing court, after considering enumerated § 3553(a) factors, to "modify, reduce, or enlarge **the conditions of supervised release**, at any time prior to the expiration or termination of the term of supervised release." The statute frames the entire modification power around "the conditions of supervised release" applicable to the defendant — again, a power that exists to define and adjust the scope of restriction on **the defendant's own liberty**, exercised in **the defendant's own criminal case**, on **the defendant's own motion** (§ 3583(e)(2) modification motions are brought by the defendant or the government in the underlying criminal docket, not as a separate civil action requiring an independent plaintiff with an independent cause of action against a non-party). This procedural posture itself supports the own-injury characterization: Bernecky is not initiating a lawsuit against, or on behalf of, his employer; he is asking the sentencing court, in his own ongoing criminal case, to clarify and correct the scope of a condition that governs him. That is definitionally a claim about his own case.

**Practical upshot for drafting:** the statutory analysis supports the framing already recommended in Part 4 of part1-cimp-occupational-delegation.md — request a declaration/clarifying order running between Bernecky and the government (that the CIMP condition does not authorize an employer-facing monitoring-capability-parity demand, and that Probation lacks § 3603 authority to impose one), not an adjudication of the employer's own Fourth Amendment rights. The statutory text supports that this is exactly the kind of relief § 3583(e)(2) is designed to provide, and that it is Bernecky's own case to bring.

## PART 4 — Third-Party Standing Backstop: If a Court Frames This Differently

If, notwithstanding Part 1–3, a court (or the government) recharacterizes Bernecky's claim as asserting the employer's own Fourth Amendment rights, the following backstop analysis should be ready.

### The controlling test

*Powers v. Ohio*, 499 U.S. 400 (1991) **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — reconstructed from Justia/Cornell LII/Quimbee, cross-corroborated]**, synthesizes the modern three-part test for third-party standing:
1. The litigant must have suffered an **injury-in-fact**, giving a "sufficiently concrete interest" in the dispute's outcome;
2. The litigant must have a **close relation** to the third party; and
3. There must be **some hindrance** to the third party's own ability to protect its interests.

*Kowalski v. Tesmer*, 543 U.S. 125 (2004) **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — reconstructed from Justia/Cornell LII/Casemine, cross-corroborated]**, applied this test to deny standing to attorneys who had "no relationship at all" with the indigent defendants whose rights they sought to assert, and who had shown no hindrance since the indigents had "open avenues" (a direct appeal-of-right procedure) to raise the claim themselves. Kowalski clarifies that the hindrance requirement is satisfied by a **"genuine," not "insurmountable,"** obstacle — a meaningfully lower bar than requiring the third party be literally unable to sue.

*Singleton v. Wulff*, 428 U.S. 106 (1976) **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — reconstructed from Justia/Cornell LII/Casebriefs]**, is the foundational application: physicians were permitted to assert their Medicaid patients' abortion-funding rights because (a) the physician-patient relationship is close/confidential, and (b) patients face a genuine obstacle — the "desire to protect privacy" deters some from suing, and claims risk mootness given the time-sensitivity of the underlying medical procedure.

*Craig v. Boren*, 429 U.S. 190 (1976) **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — reconstructed from Justia/Cornell LII]**, is the reverse configuration worth noting for completeness: a beer vendor was permitted to assert her underage male customers' equal protection rights because the statute's legal duties were "addressed directly to vendors such as her," inflicting a direct injury-in-fact on her (economic harm/loss of license), which was independently sufficient, and the customer relationship/obstacle showing followed from there.

### Application to Bernecky/employer, if required

- **Injury-in-fact:** easily satisfied independently — Bernecky's own job loss and exposure to a violation finding are concrete and particularized to him, exactly as *Craig v. Boren*'s vendor had her own independent economic injury from a rule nominally about someone else's rights.
- **Close relationship:** an actual, ongoing (if currently suspended) employment relationship, entered into with full mutual knowledge of Bernecky's conviction and supervision status, and a shared, concrete interest in resolving the dispute so the job can proceed — arguably closer and more concrete than the abstract attorney/prospective-client relationship rejected in *Kowalski*, and comparable to the physician/patient relationship credited in *Singleton*.
- **Hindrance:** genuine and easily shown. The employer (a) is not a party to Bernecky's criminal case and has no independent procedural vehicle to intervene in a § 3583(e)(2) proceeding in someone else's criminal docket; (b) has no live case or controversy of its own to litigate — no one has sued the employer or attempted to compel it to do anything through a proceeding to which it is a party — so there is no proceeding in which it even could raise this; and (c) has every practical incentive to simply decline to hire Bernecky rather than incur the cost, distraction, and continuing entanglement with federal criminal supervision of litigating a Fourth Amendment position on his behalf. This is, if anything, a more acute hindrance than *Kowalski*'s indigent defendants (who at least had a direct-appeal avenue in their own case) or *Singleton*'s patients (who could theoretically sue, subject to mootness risk) — the employer here has **no proceeding of its own at all** in which to raise the issue.

**Conclusion on the backstop:** if a court insists this is a third-party standing question, Bernecky/the employer relationship satisfies *Powers*/*Kowalski*'s test comfortably. But this should remain a backstop, not the primary theory, because conceding the third-party-standing frame invites exactly the recharacterization risk the client is worried about, and (per Part 1) is not the more accurate characterization of the claim in the first place.

## PART 5 — Closer Analogous Authority Beyond the Supervision Context

### Bond v. United States (see Part 1) — the single closest fit, addressed above.

### Kerry v. Din / Trump v. Hawaii — standing based on one's own concrete interest, notwithstanding that the challenged government action formally targets a third party

**[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — reconstructed from Constitution Annotated (Congress.gov)/Cornell LII secondary sources; not independently read.]**

These consular-nonreviewability cases present a structurally similar standing configuration: a U.S. citizen challenges government action that is formally and legally directed at someone else (a foreign-national relative denied a visa/entry), on the theory that the citizen has her own concrete injury (inability to reunite with a family member) flowing from that third-party-directed action. Per Constitution Annotated's summary of *Trump v. Hawaii*, "a person's interest in being united with his relatives is sufficiently concrete and particularized to form the basis of an Article III injury in fact" for the citizen's *own* standing — even though the formal legal action (visa denial/entry restriction) runs against the foreign national, not the citizen. *Kerry v. Din* is in tension on the separate question of whether that interest rises to a *cognizable liberty interest* under the Due Process Clause (Justice Scalia's plurality said no), but even under *Din* the citizen's Article III standing to bring the claim in the first place was not the barrier — the case was decided on the merits/scope of the substantive right, not on a threshold third-party-standing dismissal.

**Application to Bernecky, with appropriate caution:** this line supports the general proposition — outside the supervision context entirely — that courts routinely permit an individual to sue over government action that is formally addressed to a third party, where the individual's own injury is concrete and particularized, without treating this as raising a third-party-standing problem at all. It is offered as **persuasive, general standing-doctrine support**, not as supervision-specific authority, and the *Din* plurality's separate (and unfavorable to the citizen) merits holding on the underlying due-process question should not be confused with, or allowed to undercut, the standing point — the two are analytically distinct, and only the standing point is being borrowed here.

### Administrative law generally — a genuine complication to flag honestly, and why it does not apply here

General administrative-law standing doctrine (as reflected in Article III causation/redressability doctrine and the APA's "zone of interests" test) makes clear that **when a plaintiff is not the direct object of a challenged government action, but rather the action is directed at a regulated third party whose response injures the plaintiff, standing is "ordinarily substantially more difficult to establish"** — because causation/redressability then depend on the independent, unpredictable choices of that regulated third party. This doctrine, if it applied, would cut *against* easy standing here, since Probation's demand is nominally directed at the employer, and Bernecky's injury (no job) flows through the employer's own response to that demand.

**Why this doctrine does not control Bernecky's situation, and should be affirmatively distinguished in the motion:**
1. **This is not APA review of independent agency action; it is a § 3583(e)(2) motion in Bernecky's own criminal case.** The "zone of interests"/third-party-causation doctrine developed to police standing for outside plaintiffs seeking judicial review of agency rules under the APA — a posture that presupposes the plaintiff needs an independent cause of action and litigable interest to get into court at all. Bernecky already has an unquestioned right to be in front of the sentencing court on his own case; § 3583(e)(2) is the vehicle, and no independent "cause of action" or "zone of interests" showing is required for a defendant to ask his own sentencing court to interpret and correct the conditions of his own supervision.
2. **Causation and redressability are not actually attenuated here the way the doctrine worries about.** In the classic problematic case (e.g., a plaintiff who is injured only because a regulated business might independently decide to raise prices in response to a regulation), the third party's response is genuinely independent and unpredictable. Here, by contrast, the causal chain is direct and undisputed on the current record: Probation made the demand; the job was suspended *because of* the demand; a declaration that the demand exceeds Probation's authority removes the demand and directly redresses the injury. There is no speculative intervening third-party choice to worry about — the employer's own position (willing to hire, willing to accommodate an internal-only account, objecting only to the extra-textual monitoring-parity demand) is already a matter of record, not conjecture.
3. **This doctrine, properly understood, is best used affirmatively by Bernecky's own briefing** — to preemptively distinguish it and explain why the concerns that animate it (speculative causation, plaintiffs bootstrapping into court via someone else's regulatory dispute) are absent here, rather than waiting for the government to invoke it.

## PART 6 — Bottom-Line Assessment and Recommended Framing for the Motion

### How strong is Bernecky's standing to raise this claim?

**Strong on the merits of the characterization question, moderate on the risk of successful mischaracterization by an adversary or a skeptical court.**

- The *Warth v. Seldin* on/off-switch test is squarely in Bernecky's favor: his claim rests on his own legal position under his own Judgment and § 3603, not on the employer's Fourth Amendment rights.
- *Bond v. United States* supplies real, on-point (if doctrinally distant) Supreme Court authority for the general proposition that a person subjected to government action has standing to argue the government exceeded its delegated authority, even where the sphere of authority conventionally belongs to or protects someone else.
- The statutory text of § 3603 and § 3583(e)(2) independently confirms that the scope-of-authority question is inherently about Bernecky's own supervision.
- The delegation cases and (more directly) the employer-notification/occupational-restriction line (Jenkins/Lombardi/Ruff/Piper/Salazar/Gorychka/Brown) provide a consistent real-world pattern of courts adjudicating exactly this type of claim — a condition operating through a third party — as the defendant's own claim, without any third-party-standing gate.
- The backstop test (*Powers*/*Kowalski*) is independently satisfied if a court insists on applying it anyway.

**What keeps this from being "ironclad" in the sense of an explicit, squarely-on-point holding:** no case was located — in the supervised-release context or, despite a dedicated search, in general administrative law — that says in so many words "a supervisee has standing to challenge a probation officer's ultra vires demand on a non-party even though the demand's mechanism runs through the non-party's cooperation, and this is not a third-party-standing problem." That specific sentence does not appear to exist yet in reported case law. The client's instinct is correct, and well-supported by structural authority and consistent pattern, but this remains, candidly, a **first-impression application** of settled principles to a fact pattern (an ultra vires demand on a non-party employer as the mechanism of harm to the supervisee) that no court appears to have addressed in precisely these terms.

### Recommended framing for the motion (to minimize standing risk)

1. **Lead with Bernecky's own injury, not the employer's rights.** State the claim as: "Probation has exceeded the scope of authority conferred on it by this Court's Judgment and by 18 U.S.C. § 3603 in the course of supervising Mr. Bernecky, causing him — not the employer — direct and ongoing harm." Do not lead with, or frame the requested relief as, a vindication of the employer's Fourth Amendment interests.
2. **Request relief that runs only between Bernecky and the government.** A declaratory/clarifying order that (a) the CIMP condition contains no employer-facing component, (b) Probation's "like kind and degree" demand has no basis in the condition or in § 3603, and (c) Bernecky may not be found in violation for the employer's failure or refusal to satisfy an unauthorized demand. Do not ask the court to adjudicate, or purport to bind, the employer's own rights — this both avoids the standing trap and tracks the relief already recommended in Part 4 of part1-cimp-occupational-delegation.md and the proposed modification language in client-directives.md.
3. **Cite Bond affirmatively, in the standing/threshold section of the brief, before reaching the merits** — to frame the issue for the court up front as a scope-of-authority-over-me question, not an employer's-rights question, before the government has a chance to suggest otherwise.
4. **Cite the Jenkins/Lombardi/Ruff/Piper/Salazar/Gorychka/Brown line as the closest real-world demonstration** that this Circuit treats employer-mediated supervision conditions as the defendant's own occupational-liberty claim — this is likely to be the most persuasive point to a Second Circuit district judge, since it is circuit precedent in materially the same doctrinal family (computer-monitoring/employer-facing supervision conditions), not a distant analogy.
5. **Preemptively distinguish the administrative-law "regulated third party" causation doctrine** in a footnote or short paragraph, rather than waiting for the government to raise it, along the lines of Part 5 above — this shows the court defense counsel has already considered and rejected the doctrine's application, which is more persuasive than reactive distinguishing in a reply brief.
6. **Keep the *Powers*/*Kowalski* backstop analysis drafted and ready** (Part 4) but do not lead with it — introducing the third-party-standing framework at all, even to satisfy it, risks inviting the court to adopt that frame. Hold it in reserve for a reply brief or oral argument if the government raises the objection.
