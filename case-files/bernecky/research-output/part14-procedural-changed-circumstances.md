## PART 1 â€” The Current Standard for Â§ 3583(e)(2) Modification: *Lussier* Confirmed and Extended

### 1(a). *Lussier*'s exact holding and pincite (primary source read in full)

**United States v. Lussier, 104 F.3d 32 (2d Cir. 1997)** (Newman, C.J.). Full opinion read directly from the Drive folder (`US v. Lussier, 104 F. 3d 32 - Court of Appeals, 2nd Circuit 1997 - Google Scholar.pdf`, Drive ID `1NILvkfqbV72yTy5ww_Uj4JUCrJLh6rTr`).

**The actual holding is narrower â€” and more double-edged â€” than a bare "changed circumstances not required" headnote suggests.** *Lussier*'s core holding is that **Â§ 3583(e)(2) does not reach the *legality* of a condition of supervised release at all**, regardless of whether the defendant preserved the point:

> "We agree with the District Court that it lacked authority to entertain Lussier's challenge to the legality of the restitution order under subsection 3583(e)(2)." 104 F.3d at 33.

> "The plain language of subsection 3583(e)(2) indicates that the illegality of a condition of supervised release is not a proper ground for modification under this provision... Conspicuously absent from the list of relevant considerations is the legality of the condition... Subsection 3583(e)(2) on its face authorizes the court to modify conditions of supervised release only when general punishment goals would be better served by a modification. **It does not authorize the court to assess the lawfulness of a condition of release.** Other procedures, such as a direct appeal under 18 U.S.C. Â§ 3742 or a collateral attack under 28 U.S.C. Â§ 2255, are available to challenge the legality of a condition of supervised release, as long as the requirements of those procedures have been met." 104 F.3d at 34â€“35 (emphasis added).

Separately, and this is the proposition the `part3-fees-financial.md` memo correctly extracted, *Lussier* describes Â§ 3583(e)(2) as a **general, ongoing, punishment-policy tool** that does **not** require a threshold showing of changed circumstances:

> "Section 3583(e) provides the district court with retained authority to revoke, discharge, or modify terms and conditions of supervised release following its initial imposition of a supervised release term **in order to account for new or unforeseen circumstances. Occasionally**, changed circumstances â€” for instance, exceptionally good behavior by the defendant or a downward turn in the defendant's ability to pay a fine or restitution imposed as conditions of release â€” will render a previously imposed term or condition of release either too harsh or inappropriately tailored to serve the general punishment goals of section 3553(a)." 104 F.3d at 35â€“36 (emphasis added).

Read carefully, "occasionally" signals that changed circumstances is an **illustrative, typical scenario**, not an element the movant must plead and prove. Nothing in Â§ 3583(e)(2)'s text â€” which requires only that the court "consider" the enumerated Â§ 3553(a) factors â€” imposes a changed-circumstances gate. **My assessment, stated candidly: *Lussier*'s language is not perfectly clean on this point.** In distinguishing Lussier's own claim, the court wrote: "Lussier's Hughey-based challenge, endeavoring to dispute the legality of the restitution order, **does not involve changed circumstances or affect in any way general punishment aims** such as deterrence, rehabilitation, and proportionality." 104 F.3d at 36. That sentence pairs "changed circumstances" and "general punishment aims" almost as alternative sufficient grounds â€” which, if anything, is even more favorable to Bernecky than a flat "changed circumstances is irrelevant" rule, since it confirms a movant can proceed on general punishment-policy grounds **alone**, with no change in circumstances at all.

**Bottom line on 1(a): changed circumstances is a factor that may support a modification, but it is not a required element, and the operative gate that *does* exist is different and more dangerous â€” the exclusion of legality/validity challenges from Â§ 3583(e)(2)'s scope entirely.** See Part 3 below; this is the single most important nuance for Bernecky's motion.

## PART 2 â€” Plain Error Is Not the Applicable Standard (One Paragraph, Per Scope Instruction)

This is a district-court motion under Â§ 3583(e)(2), not a direct appeal, so the plain-error standard of Fed. R. Crim. P. 52(b) and **United States v. Olano, 507 U.S. 725, 731â€“32 (1993)** (plain error requires (1) an error, (2) that is plain, (3) that affects substantial rights, and, if so, (4) that "seriously affect[s] the fairness, integrity, or public reputation of judicial proceedings" before an appellate court will exercise discretion to correct it) â€” has no application here, regardless of whether Bernecky objected to any condition in 2018. Every plain-error discussion located in this research (*Olano*; *United States v. Dupes*, 513 F.3d 338, 343 (2d Cir. 2008); *United States v. Betts*, 886 F.3d 198 (2d Cir. 2018); *United States v. Lombardi*, No. 17-356-cr (2d Cir. Mar. 22, 2018); *United States v. Piper*, No. 18-3654-cr (2d Cir. Mar. 30, 2020); *United States v. Leone*, No. 19-1670 (2d Cir. May 20, 2020); *United States v. Gorychka*, 2024 WL 3964287 (2d Cir. Aug. 28, 2024); *United States v. Burnash*, Nos. 23-8024/8025-cr (2d Cir. Dec. 20, 2024)) arises exclusively on **direct appeal**, where Rule 52(b) governs *appellate* review of unpreserved claims. A Â§ 3583(e)(2) motion is not an appeal at all; it is the district court's exercise, in the first instance, of its own retained statutory authority. Nothing in Rule 52(b), *Olano*, or any of the above cases purports to import an appellate preservation standard into a district court's original consideration of a modification motion. This point is included only to close the loop per the scope instructions and is not developed further.

## PART 3 â€” Waiver/Forfeiture of Unobjected-to Conditions and the "Void Ab Initio" Counter-Theory (Most Important Part)

### 3(a). Does Second Circuit case law impose a forfeiture bar on *later Â§ 3583(e)(2) motions* (as opposed to direct appeals)?

**No such case was located, and there is good reason to think none exists.** Every single forfeiture/plain-error discussion this research turned up â€” in *Olano*, *Dupes*, *Betts*, *Lombardi*, *Piper*, *Leone*, *Gorychka*, and *Burnash* â€” arises in the **direct-appeal** posture and is keyed explicitly to Rule 52(b)'s appellate-preservation framework. None of these cases hold, or even suggest, that a defendant's failure to object at the original sentencing forecloses a **later, independent Â§ 3583(e)(2) motion** filed in the district court. That is unsurprising: Rule 52(b) plain-error review exists to police what an *appellate court* may correct when an issue was not raised *below*; a Â§ 3583(e)2) motion is not an appeal, and there is no "below" to have preserved anything *from* â€” the district court is simply being asked, in the first instance, to exercise a freestanding statutory power it always retained. The statute's own language reinforces this: Â§ 3583(e)(2) allows modification "at any time prior to the expiration or termination of the term of supervised release," with no textual precondition that the defendant must have objected at the time of the original sentence.

**Confirmation from *Lussier* itself:** critically, when the Second Circuit rejected Lussier's Hughey-based challenge, it did **not** do so on a forfeiture/waiver theory (even though Lussier likewise had failed to raise this specific argument on his own direct appeal, see 104 F.3d at 33â€“34, discussing his prior, unsuccessful direct appeal in *United States v. Lussier*, 71 F.3d 456 (2d Cir. 1995)). The court could easily have said "you forfeited this by not raising it on direct appeal." **It did not.** Instead, it held that Â§ 3583(e)(2) is categorically the wrong *vehicle* for a legality challenge, preserved or not. This confirms that forfeiture-at-sentencing is simply not the doctrinal frame Second Circuit courts use to police Â§ 3583(e)(2) motions â€” the frame is subject-matter scope (see 3(b) below), not preservation.

### 3(b). Is there a "void ab initio" exception, and does it fit here?

**Short answer: not as an independent doctrine developed for this context â€” and, more importantly, Bernecky does not need one.** I found no Second Circuit (or persuasive out-of-circuit) case articulating a "void ab initio" exception to forfeiture specifically for supervised-release conditions imposed without the required statutory/Guidelines findings (e.g., a missing U.S.S.G. Â§ 5F1.5 occupational-restriction finding). What I found instead, reading *Lussier* closely, is a **structural point that does the same work without needing a void-ab-initio label**: because forfeiture/waiver is a direct-appeal doctrine that has no application to an original Â§ 3583(e)(2) motion in the first place (Part 3(a)), Bernecky does not need to establish that the conditions were "void" to escape a forfeiture argument â€” there is no forfeiture doctrine operating on this motion to escape from. The stronger, better-supported response to a government forfeiture argument is simply: **"Rule 52(b) plain-error review is an appellate doctrine; it does not govern a district court's threshold consideration of a Â§ 3583(e)(2) motion, and no case holds otherwise."**

Where a "void ab initio"-style argument becomes actively **risky** is if it is presented as a claim that the *original 2018 judgment was illegal or invalid* â€” because that is precisely the category of claim *Lussier* holds Â§ 3583(e)(2) **cannot** reach. 104 F.3d at 34â€“35 ("[i]t does not authorize the court to assess the lawfulness of a condition of release"). If Bernecky's brief argues "Special Condition (a) [CIMP] was void ab initio because no Â§ 5F1.5 finding was ever made," a government or court reading *Lussier* literally could respond: "then your remedy was direct appeal or Â§ 2255, not Â§ 3583(e)(2)" â€” and *Lussier* is squarely on their side for that specific argument.

### 3(c). General void-judgment/void-order doctrine (Rule 60(b)(4)) as a persuasive analogy â€” imported into this context?

**Not imported, on this record.** The general civil doctrine is real and well-established: under Fed. R. Civ. P. 60(b)(4), a judgment is void, and therefore subject to vacatur "at any time" without the ordinary reasonable-time/excusable-neglect balancing that governs other Rule 60(b) grounds, only in the "rare instance" where the rendering court lacked jurisdiction (subject-matter or personal) or where enforcement would be inconsistent with due process â€” see **United Student Aid Funds, Inc. v. Espinosa, 559 U.S. 260, 270â€“71 (2010)** (a judgment is not void merely because it is erroneous; voidness requires a jurisdictional or fundamental due-process defect). I located no Second Circuit or other authority importing this civil-judgment doctrine into the supervised-release-conditions space, and I think there is a structural reason none exists: **the criminal system already has its own dedicated mechanism for correcting an illegally imposed sentence or condition** â€” direct appeal under 18 U.S.C. Â§ 3742, or collateral attack under 28 U.S.C. Â§ 2255 â€” which is exactly what *Lussier* held is the *proper* (and exclusive) route for a legality challenge, as opposed to Â§ 3583(e)(2). 104 F.3d at 35, 36â€“37 (extensively discussing how the Sentencing Reform Act's "streamlined scheme of sentencing review" would be "disrupted" by treating Â§ 3583(e)(2) as a freestanding illegal-sentence-correction tool). Courts have had no occasion to borrow the civil void-judgment doctrine because Congress already built a criminal-law analog â€” it is simply not the one Bernecky can most easily use today (see 3(d)).

### 3(d). Candid bottom line, and which of Bernecky's specific arguments are most vulnerable vs. most protected

**The void-ab-initio theory is not well supported as an independent basis for relief under Â§ 3583(e)(2), and leaning on it risks handing the government a *Lussier* argument for free.** The good news is that Bernecky does not need it: because forfeiture/plain-error is a direct-appeal doctrine with no purchase over an original Â§ 3583(e)(2) motion (3(a)), and because *Lussier* (properly read) imposes **no changed-circumstances threshold** on ordinary, forward-looking, punishment-policy-based modification requests (Part 1), the strongest and safest posture for **every one** of Bernecky's arguments is to frame the request as an ordinary, present-tense request that the court now conduct the individualized, forward-looking reassessment that Â§ 3583(e)(2), *Lussier*, and (as of November 1, 2025) U.S.S.G. Â§ 5D1.4(a) all authorize â€” **not** as a claim that the 2018 judgment was void, illegal, or invalid when entered. That framing sidesteps the entire forfeiture/void-ab-initio debate, because it does not matter, under this framing, whether the original condition was lawfully imposed â€” the court is simply being asked to decide, now, whether the condition remains appropriate, which every case and Guideline discussed above confirms is always available "at any time prior to the expiration of the term."

That said, being candid, the specific arguments differ in how *easily* they can be reframed this way, because some are inherently pleaded as legality claims:

**Most vulnerable to a "this is really a legality challenge barred by *Lussier* from Â§ 3583(e)(2)" objection, if pleaded in their most natural form:**
- **Treatment-provider delegation (Special Condition (b)):** the non-delegation theory (only a court, not a private provider, may impose binding conditions) is, at bottom, a claim that the condition as administered *exceeds statutory authority* â€” a legality argument. *Browder*, 866 F.3d at 514 (provider's rule "would contradict, rather than execute" the sentence, so "the Probation Office was plainly without power to impose it") was itself decided on **direct appeal**, not a modification motion.
- **CIMP/occupational-restriction (Special Condition (a)):** "no Â§ 5F1.5 finding was ever made" is naturally pleaded as a claim that the restriction was never validly authorized â€” again a legality frame.
- **PSR disclosure/*Charmer* (Special Condition (e)):** "this disclosure outright violates *Charmer Industries*" is explicitly a legality/validity claim about the disclosure authorization.
- **Polygraph (Special Condition (g)):** the Fifth Amendment framework (Track 2/client-pushback material) is a constitutional-validity argument about the condition's structure.

**Most protected â€” cleanly available as ordinary forward-looking "appropriateness" reassessment, no legality theory required:**
- **Fees/ability-to-pay (cross-cutting):** this is exactly *Lussier*'s own fact pattern for the "changed circumstances *may* support modification" dictum (a "downward turn in the defendant's ability to pay," 104 F.3d at 35â€“36) and is the paradigm case for present-tense appropriateness review, not a legality claim about 2018.
- **Family contact/minors (Special Condition (c)):** easily pleaded as "given the current record â€” no dangerousness finding, no biological children â€” is this restriction still appropriately tailored," not "was this void in 2018."
- **Risk-notification/*Boles* (Standard Condition 12):** *Traficante* itself confirms this is how the Second Circuit and W.D.N.Y. have *already* handled this exact condition â€” as a prospective clarification/modification, not a retroactive validity attack. 966 F.3d at 104â€“07.

**Recommendation for drafting:** every argument â€” including the four "most vulnerable" ones â€” should be **led** with the forward-looking appropriateness framing ("the court should now exercise its retained Â§ 3583(e)(2)/Â§5D1.4(a) authority to reassess this condition in light of [current facts/current law/the commencement of supervision]"), with the legality-flavored material (missing Â§ 5F1.5 findings, *Charmer*, delegation doctrine, Fifth Amendment structure) presented as **evidence that the condition is not currently appropriately tailored**, rather than as an independent claim that the 2018 judgment was void. This gets Bernecky the same substantive mileage without inviting a *Lussier* scope objection.

## PART 4 â€” Does the USSC's 2025 Individualized-Assessment Directive Abrogate "Legality Is Not a Factor"?

### 6(b). Rule 32.1's procedural framework â€” notice, hearing, counsel
