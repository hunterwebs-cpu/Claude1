## TASK 2 — The "Silence Means No Occupational Restriction" Doctrinal Package

### 2(a). "Oral Pronouncement Controls" — the actual rule, and an honest assessment of fit

**The rule, as consistently confirmed across independent sources (Justia, CaseMine, Lexology, FindLaw):**

- **United States v. Marquez, 506 F.2d 620 (2d Cir. 1974).** This is the foundational Second Circuit statement of the rule: *"It is the oral sentence which constitutes the judgment of the court, and which is authority for the execution of the court's sentence"*; *"the written judgment is nothing more than mere evidence of the sentence imposed orally in court by the judge."* The rationale is expressly tied to the defendant's **constitutional right to be present at sentencing**: *"where an unambiguous oral sentence conflicts with the written judgment, the constitutional right of a defendant to be present at sentencing dictates that the oral pronouncement of sentence must control."* Marquez itself struck a written-judgment addition (continued commitment until a fine was paid) that had not been mentioned at the oral sentencing.

- **United States v. Truscello, 168 F.3d 61 (2d Cir. 1999).** Applied the rule but found no conflict on its facts: the written judgment's listing of standard/mandatory supervised-release conditions not separately stated aloud did not conflict with the oral sentence, because such conditions were **"implicit in the very nature of supervised release"** and the written judgment merely "provided an explanation of what that supervised release entailed" rather than adding anything new. Truscello also generally restated the governing rule: *"where there is a direct conflict between an unambiguous oral pronouncement of sentence and the written judgment and commitment... the oral pronouncement... must control."*

- **United States v. Rosario, 386 F.3d 166 (2d Cir. 2004).** Applied the rule to strike a written-judgment addition made in the defendant's absence — the written judgment had narrowed a father-son contact exception by adding a New York Family Court approval requirement never mentioned orally. The government **conceded** the addition was improper and had to be deleted; the court remanded rather than simply deleting the provision, on the theory that if the sentencing court still wanted the additional restriction, it could reimpose it at a hearing with the defendant present. This confirms the doctrine's core concern is procedural — the defendant's presence and opportunity to be heard on any restriction of his liberty — not merely which of two documents wins a tie.

**Critical, very recent development that changes the picture — United States v. Maiorana, No. 22-1115 (2d Cir. Aug. 28, 2025) (en banc).** This is a major finding not previously in the case file and should be flagged prominently to the drafting team. Sitting en banc, the Second Circuit **overruled Truscello** and held that **all non-mandatory conditions of supervised release — including "standard" conditions under § 5D1.3(c), not just "special" conditions — must be orally pronounced (or expressly adopted/incorporated by reference to a specific written source made available to the defendant, such as the PSR, the Guidelines, or a court-adopted notice) at the sentencing hearing itself, in the defendant's presence, to be valid.** On the facts, the sentencing court had orally imposed only certain special conditions; the written judgment then added thirteen discretionary standard conditions, plus a treatment-cost obligation, never mentioned orally. The en banc court vacated those thirteen conditions and the cost provision and remanded. The opinion frames the holding as flowing from the defendant's **right to be present at sentencing** — entailing "notice and a contemporaneous opportunity to object to every non-mandatory condition of supervised release" — and notes that, in recent years, **nine circuits** have reached the same "must be orally pronounced" rule Maiorana now adopts for the Second Circuit. Maiorana postdates Bernecky's June 2018 sentencing by more than seven years and is binding, en banc, published authority — squarely within the "post-2018 legal development = changed circumstances" framework already established as the controlling drafting strategy (client-directives.md, "CONTROLLING STRATEGIC DIRECTIVE," item 1).

**Honest assessment of fit for Bernecky's silence argument — this is the crux of the assigned task, and the client is right that it is not a perfect fit, but Maiorana changes the calculus meaningfully:**

The classic oral-pronouncement trilogy (Marquez/Truscello/Rosario) is, as the client anticipated, **not a perfect fit** on its own terms: every one of those cases resolves a *conflict* between two sources — something was said (or omitted) orally, and the written judgment then said something *different* or *additional*. Bernecky's situation is not a conflict between two sources; it is **total silence in both** — no oral statement about employment, and no written statement about employment, anywhere in the CIMP condition's text (confirmed against the source PDF directly, per client-directives.md). A rule built to referee disagreements between two conflicting documents does not, by its own terms, obviously reach a case where neither document says anything at all. If pressed hard, a court could distinguish the trilogy on exactly this ground: "there is no conflict here to resolve, so this line of cases doesn't apply."

**But Maiorana meaningfully strengthens the analogy, and arguably converts it from an analogy into a freestanding argument.** Maiorana's holding is not merely about resolving conflicts — it is an affirmative rule that **a non-mandatory condition does not come into legal existence at all unless it was orally pronounced (or expressly incorporated by reference) at sentencing.** That is precisely the proposition Bernecky needs: if an occupational/employment restriction was never mentioned orally at the 2018 sentencing (a fact that should be confirmed against the sentencing transcript — **[VERIFY: pull the transcript and confirm no oral reference to employment, employer notification, or occupational restriction appears anywhere in the CIMP-condition colloquy]**), then under Maiorana's own rule, no such restriction could have validly come into existence as a condition of Bernecky's supervision, full stop — not merely "it's ambiguous," but "it does not exist as a condition." This is a stronger, more direct fit than the older conflict-resolution cases, because Maiorana's rationale (contemporaneous notice and opportunity to be heard on any liberty restriction) supports the broader principle the client is reaching for: **what the court never said, in the defendant's presence, imposes nothing.**

**A candid strategic caution, tying directly back to the Track 14/*Lussier* problem already flagged in this file:** if this argument is pleaded as "the occupational restriction was never validly imposed under Maiorana, therefore it is void," that is once again a legality/validity claim about the 2018 judgment — precisely the category *Lussier* holds is outside § 3583(e)(2)'s scope (Part 14, Part 3(d), "Most vulnerable" list already includes the CIMP/occupational theory for exactly this reason). **The client's own "KEY REFRAME" is the correct answer to this exact tension**: do not argue "Maiorana makes this condition void." Argue instead that **Maiorana's rationale independently corroborates what the record's silence already shows** — because the Second Circuit (as of Maiorana) treats oral pronouncement as the sole legitimate source of a supervised-release restriction's existence, the total absence of any oral or written reference to employment is even more clearly indicative that the sentencing court never intended, and never created, an occupational restriction — supporting a request that the court now clarify the condition's scope consistent with what the record has always shown, not a request to declare the 2018 judgment invalid. Framed this way, Maiorana is used as corroborating context for a forward-looking clarification (safe under *Lussier*), not as the operative legal basis for a void-ab-initio ruling (unsafe under *Lussier*).

**Recommended treatment in the motion:** cite Marquez/Truscello/Rosario briefly for the general principle and its due-process rationale, candidly flag (rather than paper over) that those specific cases are conflict-resolution cases and Bernecky's is a total-silence case, and then lead with Maiorana as the more directly supportive, and far more recent, authority — while keeping the ultimate request framed as clarification, per the controlling strategic directive, not as a claim that the 2018 judgment was void.

### 2(b). Expressio Unius Est Exclusio Alterius — the canon, its real scope, and the Echazabal citation-integrity check

**The canon, stated correctly:** expressio unius est exclusio alterius — "the expression of one thing implies the exclusion of others" (Scalia & Garner, *Reading Law*, the "negative-implication canon") — permits an inference that an omitted item was deliberately excluded, **but only where the expressed items form a genuine "associated group or series"** such that the omission looks like a considered choice rather than an oversight, and **only where the list is not itself framed as illustrative/non-exclusive** (e.g., a list introduced by "including," "such as," or "may include" defeats the inference, because an illustrative list is not purporting to be exhaustive in the first place).

**Chevron U.S.A. Inc. v. Echazabal, 536 U.S. 73 (2002) — verified independently, and the client's flag was well-founded, though not quite in the way suspected.** This is **not a hallucinated or fabricated citation** — the case is real, and it genuinely does discuss expressio unius, at the pages cited (80–81). **But the AI-generated Drive document's use of it is backwards, and this must be corrected before it is relied on in the motion.** Echazabal is a case where the Supreme Court **rejected** the plaintiff's expressio unius argument, not one that applied or endorsed it. The relevant facts: the ADA lists "a threat to the health or safety of other individuals in the workplace" as an example of a permissible job qualification standard; Echazabal argued that specifying threats-to-others impliedly excluded threats-to-oneself (i.e., an EEOC regulation letting employers screen out applicants who pose a risk to their *own* health was invalid because Congress's enumeration of threats-to-others "excluded" self-regarding threats). The Court held the canon **did not apply**, for two independently sufficient reasons stated verbatim in the opinion:

> "The first strike against the expression-exclusion rule here is in the statute, which includes the threat-to-others provision as an example of legitimate qualifications that are 'job-related and consistent with business necessity.' ... And the expansive 'may include' phrase points directly away from the sort of exclusive specifications that Echazabal claims." 536 U.S. at 80–81.

The Court also emphasized that the canon requires a genuine "series" of associated terms, which the single threat-to-others example did not supply. **Citing Echazabal as blanket support for "the omission of employment from the Judgment means employment was excluded" is therefore precisely backwards — it is a case demonstrating the canon's *failure* to apply to an illustrative, non-exhaustive list, not its successful application.** If Echazabal is used in the motion at all, it must be used correctly: as the source of the *doctrinal test* for when the canon does and does not apply (genuine closed series vs. illustrative "may include" list) — and then the motion must affirmatively show that Bernecky's Judgment is on the *right* side of that line (see below), rather than citing Echazabal as if its own facts supported the inference.

**A materially stronger, more current, and directly on-point authority the prior AI-generated research missed entirely: Esteras v. United States, 606 U.S. ___, 145 S. Ct. 2027 (2025) (decided June 20, 2025).** This is the single best piece of authority located for the silence/omission argument, for three reasons: it is current (postdates the 2018 sentencing and postdates *Lussier*), it is Supreme Court authority, and — critically — **it applies expressio unius directly to 18 U.S.C. § 3583(e), the very same statutory subsection under which Bernecky's motion is brought.** Justice Barrett, writing for a 7-2 majority, held that because Congress listed most of the § 3553(a) sentencing factors as considerations a court "shall" weigh under § 3583(e) but **conspicuously omitted § 3553(a)(2)(A)** (the retribution/"just punishment" factor) from that list, a district court **may not** consider retribution when deciding whether to revoke supervised release under § 3583(e) — even though retribution is an ordinary sentencing factor everywhere else in the Guidelines scheme. The Court reasoned that Congress's **selective enumeration of eight of the ten ordinary § 3553(a) factors, while pointedly leaving two out, raises a strong inference that the omission was deliberate**, not accidental.

**This is directly useful to Bernecky in two independent ways:**

1. **It confirms the canon is alive and controlling within § 3583's own architecture, right now, in 2025–2026** — meaning a court asked to apply the same interpretive logic to the *conditions* enumerated in Bernecky's Judgment (as opposed to the *factors* enumerated in the statute) is not being asked to import some foreign or antiquated interpretive tool; it is being asked to apply the exact same reasoning the Supreme Court itself just used, one section over, in the same statutory scheme, within the last year.
2. **It dovetails with — and gives fresh, current force to — a move the Second Circuit itself already made in *Lussier* in 1997**, without ever labeling it as expressio unius: recall from Part 14/15 that *Lussier* held illegality is not a proper § 3583(e)(2) ground because "**[c]onspicuously absent from the list of relevant considerations is the legality of the condition**" — 104 F.3d at 34–35. That is expressio unius reasoning in substance, applied to the very same statutory list *Esteras* later formalized the same technique on. Citing *Esteras* alongside *Lussier*'s own "conspicuously absent" language shows the Second Circuit and the Supreme Court have both already used exactly this interpretive move on this exact statute — reinforcing, rather than reaching outside of, this case's own governing authority.

**Honest limitation, stated candidly:** *Esteras* applies the canon to a **statute** (Congress's enumerated list of factors in § 3583(e)), not to a **sentencing court's own judgment** (the list of conditions in Bernecky's Judgment). No case was located — in this search or in the prior research passes — applying expressio unius specifically to a set of judicially enumerated supervised-release conditions to infer that an unlisted restriction (like an occupational bar) was deliberately not imposed. **This appears to be a novel extension, not a settled application**, and should be presented to the court as such: a reasonable, doctrinally grounded analogical extension of a canon the Supreme Court has just applied to this exact statute, not as if some prior court has already blessed this precise move. That said, the analogical fit is genuinely strong on the facts, because Bernecky's Judgment is structured exactly like the kind of list *Esteras* found controlling and exactly *unlike* the kind of list *Echazabal* found the canon inapplicable to: the Judgment does not use "including," "such as," or "may include" anywhere in its conditions section; it sets out a **specific, closed, itemized list** — Special Conditions (a) through (j), each independently numbered and fully drafted, covering CIMP, polygraph/CVSA, sex-offense treatment, contact with minors, financial monitoring, sex-offender registration, mental health treatment, general financial disclosure, and CP possession — with **zero catch-all or illustrative language inviting additional unstated restrictions.** That is precisely the "genuine associated series, closed rather than illustrative" structure *Esteras* (and, by negative example, *Echazabal*) says the canon requires. **This is the strongest textual point in the entire package: the Judgment itself supplies the "series," and employment/occupation is not in it, in a document containing no signal that the list was ever meant to be open-ended.**

**One further, minor supporting citation, worth a brief footnote but not a leading role:** *United States v. Chemical Foundation, Inc.*, 272 U.S. 1, 14–15 (1926) — the foundational "presumption of regularity" case (public officials, including judges, are presumed to have properly discharged their duties absent clear contrary evidence) — was independently confirmed as accurately cited for that proposition. It supplies a complementary, non-expressio-unius argument already gestured at in the AI-drafted "Jeff3 family contact and work" Google Doc: the court should presume the 2018 sentencing judge acted in accordance with U.S.S.G. § 5F1.5's two-part findings requirement, and therefore presume he did **not** silently impose an occupational restriction he never made the required findings to support. This is a serviceable reinforcing point but should not be foregrounded — it proves less directly than the Judgment's own textual structure does.

**Recommendation:** Lead the "silence" argument for Special Condition (a) with (1) the Judgment's own closed-list textual structure (no catch-all language, fully itemized (a)-(j)), (2) *Esteras*'s fresh, on-point application of expressio unius to this very statute, and (3) *Lussier*'s own 1997 "conspicuously absent" language as a Second Circuit precursor to the same reasoning. Use *Echazabal* only if corrected to state its actual holding (the canon failed there because of "may include" language) and immediately distinguished (Bernecky's Judgment has no such illustrative language). Use *Chemical Foundation*'s presumption-of-regularity point as a brief, secondary reinforcement.

### 2(c). Conditions Never Actually Imposed Cannot Be Enforced by Probation — the strongest, most direct thread

This is, as the assignment anticipated, the most directly useful of the three threads, and it is already substantially developed in this case file — this section consolidates it, confirms it against the delegation-doctrine authority already gathered, and adds Maiorana as a significant reinforcement.

**The existing authority, already verified in this file, states the proposition in close to these exact terms:**

- **United States v. Browder, 866 F.3d 504, 514 (2d Cir. 2017)** — already quoted verbatim in client-directives.md and treated as leading authority for Special Condition (b): *"Because such a requirement... would contradict, rather than execute, Browder's sentence, the Probation Office was plainly without power to impose it, as Browder correctly argued."* This is close to a direct statement of the exact proposition the assignment asks about: a probation-administered rule that goes beyond what the court itself ordered is not merely disfavored — the Probation Office is "plainly without power" to impose it at all.
- **United States v. Matta, 777 F.3d 116, 122 (2d Cir. 2015)** — "a district court may not delegate to the Probation Department decisionmaking authority which would make a defendant's liberty itself contingent on a probation officer's exercise of discretion" — and, as independently confirmed via the Leone summary order's own framing (Part 15 research, item 1, quoting Leone quoting Matta): **"the power to impose special conditions of supervised release is vested exclusively in the district court, see 18 U.S.C. § 3583."**
- **United States v. Kunz, 68 F.4th 748, 767 (2d Cir. 2023)** — extends Matta to technology conditions: "any special condition granting Probation discretion to decide whether or not to restrict a supervisee to a single internet-connected device would constitute an impermissible delegation" (already quoted in Part 14).

**Taken together, these three cases already say, in substance, exactly what the assignment is looking for: authority over special conditions belongs exclusively to the district court; Probation may administer the "ministerial details" of a condition the court actually imposed, but it may not create, enlarge, or enforce a restriction the court did not impose — and where it tries to, the attempted restriction is not merely questionable, it is something Probation was "plainly without power" to impose (Browder) in the first place.** This maps directly onto Bernecky's employer/CIMP fact pattern: the PO's demand that the employer prove "like kind and degree" monitoring-capability parity has no textual basis anywhere in the CIMP condition (which never mentions employers, employment, or third-party systems at all, per client-directives.md) — so under Browder/Matta/Kunz, this is not a case of Probation reasonably interpreting an ambiguous condition; it is Probation attempting to enforce a restriction that does not exist in the court's own order, which these cases say it has no power to do.

**Maiorana (discussed fully in 2(a)) reinforces this thread as well, from a different angle.** Browder/Matta/Kunz establish that Probation cannot enforce a restriction the *court's own conditions* do not support once those conditions are properly construed. Maiorana adds a threshold, chronologically prior point: a non-mandatory restriction does not even come into legal existence as "a condition the court imposed" unless it was orally pronounced (or expressly incorporated) at sentencing in the first place. Read together, the two lines of authority reinforce each other: **Maiorana establishes that an unstated occupational restriction was never validly created; Browder/Matta/Kunz establish that even if a condition's text is read expansively by Probation, Probation still has no power to enforce anything beyond what the court actually and validly ordered.** Either doctrinal path arrives at the same place: Probation's "like kind and degree" employer-capability demand has no source of authority in this record.

**Assessment of strength:** this is, candidly, **the strongest and most directly on-point thread of the three assigned tasks.** Unlike the oral-pronouncement trilogy (which requires bridging a conflict-resolution framework to a total-silence fact pattern) and unlike expressio unius (which requires an admittedly novel extension from statutory text to a judicial judgment), Browder's holding is **already, on its own terms, about a treatment/monitoring condition being administered by Probation beyond what the court's own order supports** — the closest factual analog in the entire research file, as the client's own correction already recognized (client-directives.md, "CORRECTION — Browder belongs here"). The only refinement this task adds is: (1) explicitly generalizing Browder's holding beyond Special Condition (b) (treatment-provider rules) to Special Condition (a) (CIMP/employer demands), since the underlying principle — Probation cannot enforce what the court did not order — is condition-agnostic; and (2) adding Maiorana as a fresh, independent, and very recent reinforcement of the same "no court order, no valid restriction" logic from the oral-pronouncement angle.

**Recommendation:** lead the CIMP/employer section's "no textual basis" argument with Browder's "plainly without power to impose it" language (already the client's preferred authority for Special Condition (b) — extend it explicitly to Special Condition (a) here), backed by Matta's "vested exclusively in the district court" framing and Kunz's extension to technology/device conditions, and cross-reference Maiorana as independent, reinforcing (not load-bearing) support for the proposition that an unstated restriction has no valid existence in the first place.
