## BOTTOM LINE UP FRONT

1. **The client's belief that this specific request is unprecedented appears correct — and the research affirmatively supports rather than merely fails to rebut that belief.** No federal case — Second Circuit, any other circuit, or Supreme Court — was located anywhere this session holding, or even squarely presented with the claim, that a probationer/supervisee is entitled to have counsel physically present during a polygraph or other quasi-scientific examination for the specific purpose of making real-time Fifth Amendment privilege calls. The one case found that is genuinely on point — a **state** case, **against** the position — actually confirms the field is this thin: courts that have faced a counsel-presence request in an analogous polygraph/parole setting have had occasion to reject it, not to grant it. This should be reported to the court candidly as unbriefed territory, exactly as the client's own directive anticipates and exactly as this case's other novel-theory memos (part19, part20) have already done.
2. **The doctrinal tension flagged in the task is real and must be met head-on: framing this request as a Miranda-style "right to counsel during questioning" runs directly into *Minnesota v. Murphy*'s custodial/non-custodial line, and the one closely analogous case found — *J.B. v. New Jersey State Parole Board* (N.J. 2017) — shows exactly how that collision plays out and loses.** *J.B.* rejected a parolee's claim to counsel's presence during a sex-offender polygraph precisely because the exam is a "routine interview," not a custodial interrogation, so no Miranda-derived right to counsel attaches. If Bernecky's motion argues "the polygraph room is functionally custodial, therefore Miranda-style counsel rights attach," it invites the same analysis and, most likely, the same result — Murphy's own holding (a probation meeting is not "custodial" for Miranda purposes) is precisely why the privilege is not self-executing and precisely why *J.B.* felt free to reject a counsel-presence claim resting on a custodial-interrogation theory.
3. **The good news is that Bernecky's actual theory, as already framed in client-directives.md, is not a Miranda/custodial-interrogation theory at all** — it rests on the different, narrower premise that determining whether a specific question crosses the privilege line is a "pure question of law" a layperson cannot reliably apply under pressure in real time, independent of whether the setting is "custodial." That premise does not require winning the custodial-interrogation fight Murphy forecloses, and it has real, if partial, support: the Guide to Judiciary Policy itself (Vol. 8E § 190.45, already surfaced in `DRIVE-CATALOG.md` item 7 and Track 18) directs a probation officer, on an uncertain invocation, to **refer the question to the court** rather than resolve it unilaterally — i.e., the existing federal supervision policy framework already treats validity-of-invocation as too legally delicate for a layperson (the officer) to decide alone. That is a real, non-AI-generated, citable data point supporting the structural premise behind protection (II), even though it speaks to the officer's judgment, not the defendant's, and does not itself go anywhere near counsel's physical presence in the exam room.
4. **Three genuinely useful analogies were found, and all three point toward the same conclusion: frame this as a discretionary condition-crafting request under § 3583(d), not a freestanding constitutional entitlement.** (a) The grand jury "step outside to consult counsel" practice shows the government routinely tolerates function-preserving consultation breaks as a matter of policy/practice even where the Supreme Court itself has never resolved whether it is constitutionally required (*United States v. Mandujano*, 425 U.S. 564 (1976), Burger's opinion casting doubt on any constitutional entitlement; Brennan's separate opinion arguing it is constitutionally necessary — no majority holding either way). (b) Fed. R. Crim. P. 32's presentence-interview practice, and *United States v. Herrera-Figueroa*, 918 F.2d 1430 (9th Cir. 1990), show a closely analogous "non-custodial, probation-officer-led interview" context in which at least one circuit has required probation officers to honor a defendant's request for counsel's presence — **not as a Sixth Amendment critical-stage holding** (the panel expressly avoided deciding that question) but through the **court's supervisory power over the orderly administration of justice**. (c) *Estelle v. Smith*, 451 U.S. 454 (1981), is the strongest doctrinal cousin located this session — it held that a defendant facing a court-ordered psychiatric examination whose results would be used against him at a later liberty-affecting proceeding (capital sentencing) had a Sixth Amendment right to consult counsel **before deciding whether to submit to the examination**, precisely because the decision was consequential and legally complex. None of the three is on all fours (none involves a currently-serving supervisee, none involves a polygraph specifically, and *Herrera-Figueroa*'s remedy runs through supervisory power rather than a freestanding right), but together they supply a template: courts are demonstrably willing to require counsel access around consequential, non-custodial, officer-led proceedings without needing to declare the proceeding a full Sixth Amendment critical stage.
5. **A serious, honest practical objection exists and should be disclosed, not buried: physical presence *inside* the exam room may be operationally different from consultation access, because a polygraph measures physiological responses, and interrupting the test to consult counsel — or having counsel in the room during the instrumented portion — could itself distort the very measurement the government claims to need.** No case addresses this specific problem, but the Drive folder's own (unverified) AI-drafted proposal candidly concedes that "the presence of counsel in the room is often barred by examiners claiming it disrupts the test" and pivots instead to a **pre-examination written question-review protocol** (counsel reviews the question list 48 hours in advance) rather than in-room presence. That pivot is worth taking seriously on the merits, independent of its unverified source, because it sidesteps the operational objection entirely while still serving the client's actual goal (a lawyer, not Bernecky, makes the privilege call on each question) and dovetails with the Guide to Judiciary Policy's own refer-to-the-court mechanism for close calls.
6. **Recommendation: frame this as a discretionary § 3583(d) request for a protective condition, explicitly disclaiming reliance on a Miranda-style constitutional entitlement, and offer the court a menu (in descending order of ambition): counsel in the room for the entire exam; counsel present and available for consultation breaks (grand-jury-style) at any point during the exam; counsel's advance written review of the full question list, with any newly-arising or ambiguous question referred to the court under the existing Guide to Judiciary Policy § 190.45 mechanism rather than resolved on the spot by the examiner.** This framing needs no fight with Murphy at all, because it does not depend on the exam being "custodial." What remains genuinely missing before this could be relied on in the motion is set out in Section 6 below.

## 1. Direct precedent: has any court considered counsel-presence-for-real-time-privilege-calls during a polygraph/quasi-scientific exam?

### 1.1 The one case found squarely on point — and it is adverse

**J.B. v. New Jersey State Parole Board**, cited across multiple independent sources as a 2017 New Jersey Supreme Court decision (companion cases sometimes captioned "J.B.L.A.B.M.W.M.R.L. v. New Jersey State Parole Board"), is the only case located this session in which a court actually confronted a parolee's/supervisee's claim to have counsel present during a sex-offender polygraph examination. Per multiple independently corroborating summaries [VERIFY against the official N.J. reporter — WebFetch to njcourts.gov and FindLaw both returned 403 this session and could not be independently confirmed against the full text]:

- The Parole Board (whose reasoning the courts reviewing it adopted) concluded that **"the right to counsel does not attach to a polygraph examination because it is a routine interview where the officer is merely determining the level of compliance with the terms of parole."**
- The courts further reasoned that **polygraph examinations are not the equivalent of custodial interrogations**, so Miranda-derived counsel rights do not attach, and parolees are "not entitled to have counsel present."
- Separately, the same body of New Jersey law recognizes that counsel **should** be appointed in parole/probation-violation proceedings under certain circumstances (complex facts, disputed violations, mitigation arguments) — i.e., New Jersey's own doctrine does not reject a right to counsel in supervision proceedings categorically; it specifically rejects it **for the polygraph exam itself**, on the theory that the exam is not the kind of proceeding (custodial, adversarial, fact-disputing) that triggers it.

**This is state law, not binding on a federal court, and it does not address Bernecky's specific "pure question of law" framing** — the New Jersey courts were answering "is this custodial, so Miranda-type counsel rights attach," not "should counsel be present because the privilege determination is a legal judgment a layperson cannot reliably make in real time." But it is genuine, on-point, adverse precedent that the government will very likely find and cite if this request is filed, and it should be confronted in the motion rather than left for opposing counsel to surface first. It is also the single strongest piece of evidence supporting the client's own instinct that this specific relief has essentially never been granted: the only court found to have actually faced something like this request said no.

### 1.2 No federal circuit or Supreme Court case addresses this specific request

Despite extensive search-phrasing variation (federal supervised release, probation polygraph, CVSA, penile plethysmography, sexual-history interview — each cross-referenced against "counsel present," "right to counsel," "attorney in the room"), no federal appellate or Supreme Court case was located granting, or even squarely evaluating on the merits, a request for counsel's physical presence during any of these examinations in the federal probation/supervised-release context. This includes the cases this case file already has in depth (*Johnson*, *Von Behren*, *Weber*, *Harrington v. Almy*) — none of them was asked to decide, and none decided, a counsel-presence-for-privilege-calls claim; their remedies run instead through immunity/waiver doctrine (*Johnson*), the "classic penalty situation" (*Von Behren*), or liberty-deprivation proportionality under § 3583(d) (*Weber*, *Harrington*). This is a genuine, confirmed absence, not a research shortfall — it was tested from multiple doctrinal angles (Fifth Amendment, Sixth Amendment, § 3583(d) discretion) and no source, including the unverified AI-generated Drive documents (which had every incentive to surface a favorable case if one existed), turned one up.

### 1.3 One distant, partially favorable state-law analog: pretrial polygraphs after indictment

**People v. Leonard**, 125 Mich. App. 756 (1983), per corroborated secondary-source description, held that a **defendant has the right to have counsel present during a polygraph examination and any questioning that follows, once the Sixth Amendment right to counsel has attached** (i.e., post-indictment, in the ordinary criminal-prosecution Sixth Amendment sense), and that this right can be waived. **This is favorable in principle — a court has recognized counsel-presence at a polygraph exam as a real thing a defendant can have — but it is a Michigan state case addressing pretrial-investigation polygraphs after formal charges (a classic, undisputed critical-stage posture), not a post-conviction supervision context.** [VERIFY — not independently confirmed against the Michigan Reports this session; corroborated only via secondary sources describing it]. It should be disclosed as a favorable-in-principle but doctrinally distant analog: it shows the concept of "counsel present at a polygraph" is not exotic or unworkable as a matter of practice, but it does nothing to bridge Murphy's custodial/non-custodial line, since it arises in a posture (post-indictment) where the Sixth Amendment already plainly applies.

### 1.4 Practice-bar guidance: a new, partial data point not previously flagged in this case file

Re-reading the **Federal Defenders of New York Second Circuit Blog** post already identified in part2b (`Advising Clients Regarding Sexual Polygraph Exams and the Fifth Amendment in Child Pornography Cases`, May 12, 2016) in full this session surfaced a sentence not previously quoted in this case's research: after advising counsel to have the client assert the Fifth Amendment in writing to the probation officer and the polygraph examiner, the post states, **"He should ask for the immediate reappointment of counsel."** [Confirmed via direct read of the Drive copy, id `1tnv7ayndcGTdsWDtwiNA4-Powicnfo70`, this session.]

This is a genuine, useful, non-AI-generated practice-bar data point — but it should not be overstated. It recommends that the *client* ask for reappointment of counsel generally, as a step in handling the situation (presumably so counsel can then litigate the resulting dispute), not that counsel be seated in the exam room vetting each question in real time before the client answers it. It supports the general proposition that experienced defense practitioners in this doctrinal area treat counsel's involvement as important and recommend invoking a right to counsel at this juncture — but it falls well short of confirming, or even asserting, that any court has actually granted physical in-room presence for real-time privilege calls. It is best used as corroboration that the *instinct* behind protection (I) is shared by the practicing defense bar, not as authority for the specific relief requested.

## 2. The grand jury "step outside to consult counsel" analogy

### 2.1 The practice and its uncertain constitutional footing

Fed. R. Crim. P. 6(d) lists who may be present while the grand jury is in session — the attorney for the government, the witness, an interpreter if needed, and a court reporter — and **does not include defense counsel**. It is nonetheless long-standing Department of Justice practice, reflected in the Justice Manual (9-11.000 et seq.), to **permit a witness to step outside the grand jury room to consult with retained counsel at any time and for any reason**, including specifically to assess whether a particular question may call for a privileged or incriminating answer.

**The constitutional status of this practice, however, has never been resolved by a majority of the Supreme Court.** In *United States v. Mandujano*, 425 U.S. 564 (1976) [VERIFY pincites], Chief Justice Burger's opinion — in the course of upholding a perjury conviction — stated that a grand jury witness could have counsel's assistance, but only outside the room, and cast doubt on whether even *that* much (consultation outside the room) is constitutionally *required* as opposed to merely permitted as a matter of grace/policy. Justice Brennan, writing separately, took the position that access to counsel is constitutionally necessary to protect a witness's rights, and was willing to accept (without holding) that consultation outside the room, rather than presence inside it, would suffice. **No majority opinion resolves the question either way**, which means the "step outside" practice endures today as an entrenched norm of prosecutorial and grand jury practice, not as settled constitutional doctrine.

### 2.2 What this analogy actually supports, and what it does not

This is a genuinely useful analogy for Bernecky's case, but for a more modest proposition than the client may initially have in mind. It supports:

- **The general proposition that consultation-access short of full in-room presence is an accepted, workable middle ground** the government itself has adopted as sound practice in an analogous investigatory setting (a witness facing potentially self-incriminating questions, without the assistance of counsel physically present at the point of questioning), even in the *absence* of a settled constitutional mandate. This directly supports treating Bernecky's request as a sensible discretionary safeguard a court could grant under § 3583(d) without needing to resolve any unsettled constitutional question — exactly the "safer framing" the client's own directive already identifies as the fallback.
- **A ready model for the "in descending order of ambition" menu recommended in the Bottom Line**: if literal in-room presence proves administratively unworkable for a polygraph specifically (see Section 5 below), a grand-jury-style arrangement — counsel present and available just outside, with the exam pausing on request for a consultation break before any specific question is answered — is a real, precedented structure, not an improvised one.

It does **not** support a claim that counsel-access is constitutionally compelled — Mandujano's unresolved status if anything confirms the opposite, that even in the grand jury context (where a witness faces criminal exposure and the government's own adversary, a prosecutor, is directly present) the Supreme Court has never held consultation access constitutionally mandatory. If the motion cites this analogy, it should be candid that Mandujano leaves the constitutional question open rather than resolved in Bernecky's favor, and should use the analogy for its practical/structural value (a workable model the government already accepts elsewhere) rather than as if it were binding authority requiring the same result here.

## 3. Fed. R. Crim. P. 32 presentence interview practice — counsel's presence with a probation officer, outside a custodial setting

### 3.1 Herrera-Figueroa: a real, if narrow, precedent for counsel accompanying a defendant to a probation-officer-led interview

**United States v. Herrera-Figueroa**, 918 F.2d 1430 (9th Cir. 1990) [VERIFY — WebFetch to Justia/Leagle both 403'd this session; reconstructed from independently corroborating WebSearch excerpts], addressed exactly the kind of question this task was assigned to investigate, in a structurally similar (though not identical) setting: **whether a federal probation officer may refuse to honor a defendant's request that counsel be present during a presentence report interview.** The panel held that the exclusion of counsel from presentence interviews "serves no rational purpose" and **exercised its supervisory power over the orderly administration of justice** to hold that, when a federal defendant requests counsel's presence at a presentence interview, **the probation officer must honor that request.**

Two things about this holding matter a great deal for how Bernecky's request should be framed:

1. **The court did not need to, and did not, decide whether the presentence interview is a Sixth Amendment "critical stage."** Multiple independent secondary sources agree the panel expressly left that question open, resolving the case instead on the **supervisory-power** ground — a court's inherent authority to prescribe procedural rules for the orderly administration of justice in cases before it, independent of whether the Constitution itself compels the same result. **This is precisely the kind of vehicle the "safer framing" the client and prior tracks have already identified needs**: a court can require counsel's involvement in a probation-officer-led process as a matter of sound judicial administration/condition-crafting discretion, without first having to win (or even reach) a contested constitutional argument about critical-stage status.
2. **This is Ninth Circuit law, not Second Circuit law, and it addresses a different proceeding (a one-time PSR interview at the front end of a case) than a polygraph administered mid-supervision.** It should be cited as persuasive, out-of-circuit, structurally analogous authority — a real precedent for the general proposition that federal courts have required probation officers to accommodate counsel's presence in a non-custodial, officer-led interview setting without declaring the setting a full critical stage — not as controlling or squarely on point.

### 3.2 Fed. R. Crim. P. 32's own text

Multiple sources describe current practice (and, per some accounts, current or historical rule text) as requiring that "the probation officer who interviews a defendant as part of a presentence investigation must, on request, give the defendant's attorney notice and a reasonable opportunity to attend the interview." **[VERIFY — this session's WebFetch attempts to Cornell LII's current Rule 32 text and to the House/govinfo U.S. Code Appendix all returned HTTP 403, so the exact current subsection and phrasing could not be independently confirmed against primary text.]** The numbering has changed across the multiple restylings Rule 32 has undergone (a "(c)(2)" citation recovered from one secondary source almost certainly reflects a pre-2002 numbering scheme and should not be relied on as the current citation without confirmation). What is reasonably well corroborated, across independent sources, is the **substance**: federal practice recognizes a defendant's attorney's right, on request, to notice and an opportunity to attend the PSR interview — whatever its current subsection designation.

### 3.3 What this analogy is worth

This is a solid, if imperfect, structural analogy: another **non-custodial, probation-officer-led interview** (the very phrase the task asked this memo to test) in which federal practice already accommodates counsel's presence, at least on request, without requiring a critical-stage finding. It reinforces the same point the grand jury analogy makes: the federal system already has more than one precedent for accommodating counsel's presence around a probation-officer-administered process, on discretionary/administrative grounds, without first resolving a contested constitutional question. It should be presented in the motion exactly that way — as one of several converging analogies supporting a discretionary safeguard, not as a case squarely deciding Bernecky's polygraph-specific claim.

## 4. The discretionary § 3583(d) framing: has any court crafted a comparable procedural safeguard into a polygraph/quasi-scientific-exam condition?

### 4.1 This case's own Judgment already contains one such safeguard, and Johnson already blessed it

It is worth stating plainly, because it is easy to lose sight of: **Bernecky's own polygraph condition already contains a court-crafted procedural safeguard** — the no-waiver/preserve-a-later-challenge clause — and *Johnson* has already held that this kind of safeguard, crafted by a district court exercising its § 3583(d) discretion, is a legitimate and sufficient response to a Fifth Amendment concern (on the facts and theory Johnson raised; see part2b for the limits of that holding). This matters here because it means the court does not need to be persuaded that adding *some* procedural safeguard to a polygraph condition is a legitimate exercise of its authority — this case's own controlling authority already establishes that it is. The live question is only what kind of safeguard is needed to actually address Bernecky's distinct objection (see part2b, Part 3), not whether the court has power to craft one at all.

### 4.2 The Guide to Judiciary Policy's own "refer to the court" mechanism

As already surfaced in `DRIVE-CATALOG.md` (item 7) and Track 18, the **Guide to Judiciary Policy, Vol. 8E, § 190.45** — approved by the Judicial Conference in September 2016 and quoted in Vance, *Conditions of Supervision in Federal Criminal Sentencing: A Review of Recent Changes*, 81 Fed. Probation No. 1 (June 2017) — directs that **"If the defendant refuses to answer a specific question on the grounds that it is incriminating, the officer should not compel (e.g., through threat of revocation) the defendant to answer the question. If there is uncertainty about whether the invocation of the privilege against self-incrimination is valid..., the probation officer should refer the matter to the court to make this determination."** [VERIFY against the Guide itself — this session relied on the Vance article's quotation, not a direct pull of Vol. 8E.]

This is a genuinely valuable, non-AI-generated, primary-policy-source data point for **exactly** the premise behind protection (II): the existing federal supervision policy framework already treats the validity of a Fifth Amendment invocation as too legally delicate for the probation officer to resolve unilaterally in the moment, building in a referral-to-a-judge safety valve instead. It does not itself say anything about counsel, and it addresses the officer's judgment rather than the defendant's — but it is a short, honest step from "the officer shouldn't decide this alone, in real time" to "the defendant shouldn't have to decide this alone, in real time, either," and it gives the motion a real, already-existing federal-policy hook rather than an invented one. **This dovetails with, and should be cross-referenced against, Track 24's § 3006A "interests of justice" analysis** — a policy framework that already refers hard invocation calls to "the court" is a natural fit with an argument that appointing counsel to assist with exactly that kind of determination serves the interests of justice.

### 4.3 Weber and Harrington: courts modifying quasi-scientific-exam conditions, but through elimination, not a counsel-safeguard

As already developed in part20 (Section 5.2), *United States v. Weber*, 451 F.3d 552 (9th Cir. 2006), and *Harrington v. Almy*, 977 F.2d 37 (1st Cir. 1992), both involve courts modifying or vacating a quasi-scientific-exam condition (penile plethysmography in both cases) under the § 3583(d) liberty-deprivation/proportionality framework and substantive due process. Both are useful confirmation that courts **will** revisit and reshape quasi-scientific testing conditions using ordinary § 3583(d) tools — reinforcing that this is a live, exercised judicial power, not a novel one — but **neither modifies a condition by adding a procedural safeguard of the counsel-review kind Bernecky wants; both simply restrict or eliminate the testing itself.** They should be cited (if at all, and this overlaps with Track 20's existing coverage) as background confirmation of judicial willingness to act on this category of condition, not as precedent for the specific safeguard-design Bernecky is requesting.

### 4.4 No case anywhere adds a counsel-review safeguard to a polygraph condition — confirmed gap, with one unverified but sensible drafting idea in the file

No case — federal or state, in this session's research — was found modifying a polygraph or CVSA condition specifically to add a counsel pre-review, in-room presence, or consultation-access safeguard. This is a confirmed, genuine gap, not a research shortfall. The one place such a proposal exists in any form is the unverified AI-drafted `Defense Motion: Polygraph Vetting Counsel` document in the Drive folder, which proposes shifting the condition's obligation from "shall answer" to "shall appear," requiring the probation officer to provide defense counsel a written list of proposed questions at least 48 hours before any exam, and providing that the defendant "shall not be required to answer any question unless defense counsel has had the opportunity to review said question." **This is not authority — it is an unverified draft with no case support of its own for the specific mechanism** — but its structural idea (pre-review of a written question list, rather than live in-room presence) is worth taking seriously on the merits for the practical reason discussed next, independent of the document's unreliable provenance.

## 5. The practical problem with literal in-room presence, honestly disclosed

None of the case law located this session — for or against — grapples with a fact specific to polygraph testing (as opposed to grand jury testimony or a PSR interview): a polygraph is an instrumented physiological measurement, and interrupting it, or having a third party present for it, could itself affect the readings the examiner is trying to obtain, or could be resisted by examiners on exactly that ground. The unverified Drive document candidly (if self-servingly) concedes this: "the presence of counsel in the room is often barred by examiners claiming it disrupts the test." This is not a legal citation, but it identifies a real, practical friction point the motion should address rather than ignore: a court asked to order counsel physically present in the room for the entire examination may be persuaded that this particular request is operationally harder to administer than the PSR-interview or grand-jury analogies suggest, even if it is sympathetic to the underlying concern. This is exactly why the "descending order of ambition" menu in the Bottom Line (in-room presence → consultation-access breaks → advance written question review, each paired with the existing referral-to-the-court mechanism for anything unresolved) is likely to be more persuasive, and more grantable, than an all-or-nothing demand for full physical presence throughout the exam.

## 6. The Murphy tension, confronted directly

The task specifically asked this memo to address, rather than avoid, the collision between this request and *Minnesota v. Murphy*'s custodial/non-custodial line. Here is the honest analysis:

- **Murphy's holding is that a probation-officer meeting is not "custodial" for Miranda purposes, which is exactly why the privilege was not self-executing and Murphy had to invoke it himself.** If Bernecky's motion argues that counsel must be present in the polygraph room because the exam is functionally a custodial interrogation, it is arguing precisely the proposition Murphy's own facts and holding reject, and *J.B. v. New Jersey State Parole Board* (Section 1.1) shows a court applying that exact logic to reject a counsel-presence claim in the closest analogous setting found this session.
- **But this is not the only way to frame the request, and it is not the way the client's own directive already frames it.** Client-directives.md's protection (II) rationale is that a privilege determination is "a pure question of law" a layperson cannot reliably apply under pressure — a claim about the *legal complexity of the determination*, not about the *custodial character of the setting*. Murphy's custodial/non-custodial line answers the question "does Miranda's warning-and-counsel apparatus apply here," which is a different question from "should this sentencing court, in the exercise of its own undisputed discretion to craft § 3583(d) conditions, build a safeguard into this specific condition given the legal difficulty of the determination it asks the supervisee to make." The second question does not require Murphy to be distinguished, avoided, or overruled at all — the sentencing court's authority to craft protective condition language is not contingent on whether the underlying setting is "custodial," and *Johnson* itself (Section 4.1 above) already confirms that this court has the discretion to design procedural safeguards into this very condition.
- **The practical upshot for drafting:** the motion should not lead with, or rely on, a Miranda-style "the polygraph room is custodial, therefore counsel must be present" theory — that theory is a loser given Murphy and *J.B.*, and inviting the comparison only supplies the government with its best rebuttal. It should instead present the counsel-presence/pre-review request as a **discretionary § 3583(d) condition-crafting request**, expressly noting (rather than hiding) that it does not depend on the exam being custodial and does not ask the court to revisit Murphy's holding at all.

This is the same basic analytical move this case's other novel-theory memos have already made (part18's privilege-clause reframing; part20's delegation reframing): take the client's genuine, sympathetic underlying concern and locate the doctrinal vehicle that actually reaches it, rather than the vehicle that sounds most dramatic but runs into a real, on-point headwind.

## 7. Honest gaps

1. **No court has ever granted, or been squarely asked to grant, physical counsel presence during a probation/supervised-release polygraph or quasi-scientific exam for the purpose of real-time privilege determinations.** This is disclosed candidly, not disguised as an established argument, consistent with the client's own instruction and the standard set by part19 and part20.
2. **The one case squarely on point (*J.B. v. New Jersey State Parole Board*) is adverse**, though distinguishable as state law resting on a Miranda/custodial theory Bernecky's own framing does not need to rely on.

## Bottom-Line Recommendation

**Frame the request as a discretionary ask for a protective condition under § 3583(d), not as a freestanding constitutional entitlement to counsel's presence.** Do not argue that the polygraph room is "custodial" or otherwise try to distinguish or limit *Minnesota v. Murphy* — that fight is unnecessary and, per *J.B. v. New Jersey State Parole Board*, likely losing. Instead, argue that this sentencing court — which *Johnson* already confirms has the authority to craft procedural safeguards into this exact condition — should exercise that same authority here because (a) determining whether a specific question is privileged is a legal judgment, not a factual one, that a layperson cannot reliably make under pressure in real time; (b) the federal supervision policy framework itself already recognizes that this determination is too delicate to leave to the probation officer alone (Guide to Judiciary Policy § 190.45's refer-to-the-court mechanism), which is inconsistent with leaving the same determination to an unassisted defendant; and (c) federal practice already accommodates comparable counsel-access arrangements in structurally similar, non-custodial, officer-led settings (the grand jury "step outside" practice; *Herrera-Figueroa*'s PSR-interview holding), so the request is a modest extension of, not a novel departure from, existing practice.

**Present the relief as a menu, not an all-or-nothing demand:** counsel physically present throughout the exam (most ambitious); counsel present and available for consultation breaks at any point during the exam, grand-jury-style (intermediate); counsel's advance written review of the full question list, with any question counsel flags, or any question arising unexpectedly during the exam, referred to the court under the existing § 190.45-type mechanism rather than resolved on the spot by the examiner (least ambitious, but still achieves the client's core goal that a lawyer — not Bernecky — makes each privilege call). This gives the court room to grant meaningful relief even if it is unwilling to order in-room presence for the entire exam.

**What is still missing before this could be relied on in the motion:** (1) primary-source confirmation of *J.B. v. New Jersey State Parole Board*'s holding and reasoning (WebFetch failures prevented direct verification this session — this is the adverse case most likely to be thrown back at the defense, and it must be read in full, not just summarized, before the motion is filed); (2) primary-source confirmation of *Herrera-Figueroa*'s exact holding, remedy, and reasoning, and the current split (if any) among circuits on the same question; (3) direct confirmation of the Guide to Judiciary Policy § 190.45 language against uscourts.gov, not just the Vance law-review article's quotation of it; (4) confirmation of Fed. R. Crim.
