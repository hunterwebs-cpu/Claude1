## Why Browder is the closest factual match

*United States v. Browder*, 866 F.3d 504 (2d Cir. 2017), is not just a delegation case in the
abstract — it is a case about a **treatment provider's own rules exceeding a court's order**,
which is precisely Bernecky's Special Condition (b) concern (a not-yet-selected sex-offense
treatment provider's unwritten "rules and regulations" automatically becoming enforceable,
revocation-backed terms of supervision). Browder had refused to sign Mid-Erie's "Computer/
Internet Monitoring Program Participant Agreement," whose own "General Conditions" (fifteen of
them, expressly labeled "conditions" within the agreement) required him to obtain permission from
both the Probation Office **and** "the treatment team" before any contact with anyone under 17 —
including his own children — even though the district court's actual sentencing condition
expressly *excluded* Browder's own children from the no-minors-contact restriction. The Second
Circuit held:

> "[T]he treatment agreement would have subjected him to a more punitive condition governing
> contact with minors than did the condition to which Browder was actually sentenced by the
> District Court," and Browder "was therefore well within his rights to object to that term of
> the agreement."
>
> "**The key question is not whether permission, if sought, would be granted; it is whether
> Browder can be required, by the Probation Office or its designee, to ask in the first place.
> Because such a requirement as to Browder's children would contradict, rather than execute,
> Browder's sentence, the Probation Office was plainly without power to impose it, as Browder
> correctly argued.**"

866 F.3d at 513–14. This is grounded in *Matta*, quoted in Browder's own footnote 20: "The power
to impose special conditions of supervised release... is vested exclusively in the district
court," and while a court may delegate "minor details" (e.g., "the selection of a therapy
provider or treatment schedule"), it "may not delegate to the Probation Department
decisionmaking authority which would make a defendant's liberty itself contingent on a probation
officer's exercise of discretion." 866 F.3d at 509–10 n.20.

**This is a stronger, more concrete match than Matta's own facts** (a choice between inpatient
and outpatient treatment) or Carlineo's (a wholly undefined, unnamed program). Browder involves
the exact mechanism Bernecky's condition creates: a private provider's own written rules,
adopted after sentencing, that could exceed what the court itself authorized — with the Second
Circuit holding the supervisee cannot be compelled to comply with, or found in violation for
refusing, the excess.

## Corrected argument section (replaces part7's Special Condition (b) draft)

*[DRAFT — ready for insertion, following the ultra vires/delegation framework already developed
in part1 and part7]*

Special Condition (b) requires Mr. Bernecky to "participate in a sex offense-specific treatment
program and follow the rules and regulations of that program," while "[t]he probation officer
will supervise the details of [his] participation in the program, including the selection of a
provider and schedule." Sex-offense-specific treatment is, unlike the program at issue in
*United States v. Carlineo*, 998 F.3d 533 (2d Cir. 2021), a well-established, Guidelines-
recognized category of supervision programming, and selecting a specific, licensed provider
within that category is the kind of "detail[] of implementation" this Court's delegation cases
permit Probation to handle. *See* United States v. Kunz, 68 F.4th 748, 767 (2d Cir. 2023);
U.S.S.G. § 5D1.3(d). Mr. Bernecky does not challenge that piece of the condition.

What he challenges is the separate clause making his compliance with supervision automatically
contingent on "the rules and regulations" that provider unilaterally adopts — rules that do not
exist in writing anywhere at the time of sentencing, are set entirely by a private entity the
court has never reviewed, and can change at that provider's own discretion during treatment, with
no mechanism for the court, or Mr. Bernecky, to review or contest their content before they become
enforceable, revocation-backed terms of his supervision. This Court has already confronted, and
resolved, precisely this problem. In *United States v. Browder*, 866 F.3d 504 (2d Cir. 2017), a
treatment provider's own participant agreement imposed a contact-with-minors restriction more
restrictive than the one the sentencing court had actually ordered — the provider's rule reached
Browder's own children, whom the court's own condition had expressly excluded. This Court held
that the defendant "was... well within his rights to object" to that provider-imposed expansion,
because "[t]he key question is not whether permission, if sought, would be granted; it is whether
[the defendant] can be required, by the Probation Office or its designee, to ask in the first
place," and that "[b]ecause such a requirement... would contradict, rather than execute, [the]
sentence, the Probation Office was plainly without power to impose it." *Id.* at 514.

Special Condition (b) creates the identical risk in advance, categorically, rather than after the
fact: it commits Mr. Bernecky, sight unseen, to comply with whatever rules a not-yet-selected
private provider adopts — rules that, as in Browder, may readily exceed the scope of anything this
Court itself has ordered. Browder confirms that when they do, the provider's rule is
unenforceable and the Probation Office (or, by extension, the provider) is "plainly without power
to impose it." This is fully consistent with *Matta*'s rule that "the district court, not the
Probation Office, retains exclusive authority to set conditions of supervised release," and that a
court may not "delegate decision-making authority which would make a defendant's liberty
contingent on a probation officer's exercise of open-ended discretion over a program no one knows
much about." *Matta*, 777 F.3d at 122–23, *as applied in Browder*, 866 F.3d at 509–10 n.20, and
*as restated in Carlineo*, 998 F.3d at 537–38 ("the Probation Office may supervise and execute a
sentence but may not fashion a sentence's terms").

Here, the delegation runs one level further than either Browder's or Matta's own facts. In
Browder, a specific, written provider rule had already been adopted, and this Court could compare
it directly against the court's own order to see that it exceeded it. Special Condition (b)
presents that same risk *prospectively and categorically*, before any provider has even been
selected — Mr. Bernecky is bound in advance to "follow the rules and regulations" of an as-yet-
unknown program, with no opportunity to know, in advance or even during treatment, whether a
given provider rule executes this Court's sentence or, as in Browder, contradicts and exceeds it.

**Requested relief:** an order clarifying that Mr. Bernecky cannot be found in violation of
supervised release for declining to comply with a specific treatment-provider rule that exceeds
the scope of what this Court itself has ordered — consistent with Browder's holding that such a
provider-imposed excess is unenforceable — and that any provider rule intended to carry the force
of a condition of supervision must first be presented to and approved by the Court, consistent
with Matta's requirement that liberty-defining terms be fixed by the district court rather than
delegated to another's (or another's designee's) open-ended discretion.

## Note on the alcohol clause (Special Condition (h))

Per part9's independent research (already completed and correctly scoped), Browder does **not**
carry the physician-gating argument for the alcohol-abstinence clause — that argument rests on
*Betts*, 886 F.3d 198 (2d Cir. 2018), and the textual overbreadth of the clause's disjunctive
"or taking psychotropic medication" language. Browder's relevance to Special Condition (h) is
limited to the narrower, secondary points part9 already identified: (1) a defensive/prospective
protection if a selected provider later tries to expand the alcohol rule beyond the condition's
own textual limits, and (2) the factual point that the clause is a district-wide template
inherited from Browder's own remand order, not a Bernecky-specific finding. Both of those minor
uses remain valid and should stay in the Special Condition (h) section — they are simply
secondary there, whereas Browder is central/leading authority for Special Condition (b).
