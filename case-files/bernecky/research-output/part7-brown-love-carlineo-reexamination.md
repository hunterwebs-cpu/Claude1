## TASK 1 — Is Brown's § 5F1.5 occupational-restriction holding a categorical, fact-independent rule, or narrower/thinner than that?

### 1.1 The full text of the occupational-restriction passage, verbatim

Brown's opinion has two discrete sub-holdings under separate headings: **Part III.A, "The Electronic-Monitoring Condition Is Not Overbroad"** (the general liberty-deprivation/narrow-tailoring holding, which *does* rely on Brown's individual aggravating history), and **Part III.B, "The Electronic-Monitoring Condition Is Not an Occupational Restriction"** (the passage at issue here). Part III.B, in full:

> "Brown also asserts that the component of the special condition restricting his 'use of any devices in the course of employment' is 'tantamount to an occupational restriction' since it 'effectively requires him to notify any prospective employers of his fraud conviction.' Brown Br. at 14, 17. Because Brown did not object to this condition at sentencing, we once again review for plain error. Dupes, 513 F.3d at 343.
>
> "Section 5F1.5(a) of the Sentencing Guidelines provides that the trial court may impose a special condition 'prohibiting the defendant from engaging in a specified occupation, business, or profession, or limiting the terms on which the defendant may do so' only if (1) 'a reasonably direct relationship existed between the defendant's occupation, business, or profession and the conduct relevant to the offense of conviction'; and (2) 'imposition of such a restriction is reasonably necessary to protect the public because there is reason to believe that, absent such restriction, the defendant will continue to engage in unlawful conduct similar to that for which the defendant was convicted.' U.S.S.G. § 5F1.5(a). We have also long recognized that occupational restrictions 'may not be imposed to any greater extent than reasonably necessary to' protect the public. United States v. Doe, 79 F.3d 1309, 1321 (2d Cir. 1996).
>
> "Here, the challenged monitoring condition is not an occupational restriction. The condition does not bar Brown from a specific occupation, business, or profession, see U.S.S.G. § 5F1.5(a), restrict him from using a computer or computer equipment such that he would be functionally barred from occupations requiring such use, see United States v. Peterson, 248 F.3d 79, 83 (2d Cir. 2001), or compel Brown to notify his employers of the nature of his conviction. And even if the condition functionally required Brown to notify future employers about the nature of his conviction, such a requirement would not transform the challenged monitoring condition into an occupational restriction because the condition does not target 'a specified occupation, business, or profession.' U.S.S.G. § 5F1.5(a) (emphasis added).
>
> "Brown also relies on our summary order in United States v. Gorychka, No. 23-6457, 2024 WL 3964287, at *2 (2d Cir. Aug. 28, 2024), which broadly stated that '[w]e have treated ... employer notification conditions as 'occupational restrictions,'' citing Peterson, 248 F.3d at 85-86. But Peterson, the only case cited by Gorychka for this proposition, considered a special condition that required a supervisee to notify particular employers, identified by a probation officer, of his conviction. 248 F.3d at 85-86. It did not consider a general electronic-monitoring condition that encompassed electronics used in the course of any employment. And since Peterson, we have upheld employer-notification requirements without subjecting them to the special requirements for occupational restrictions. See United States v. MacMillen, 544 F.3d 71, 77-78 (2d Cir. 2008). We thus decline to follow Gorychka here.
>
> "Given that the challenged computer-monitoring condition neither restricts Brown from 'specified occupations' nor functionally bars him from jobs requiring the use of a computer, we find that this special condition is not an occupational restriction as contemplated by section 5F1.5. Accordingly, we conclude that the district court did not plainly err in imposing this special condition of supervised release."

173 F.4th at 50-51 (pagination per the slip opinion's internal numbering, pages 11-14 of 14).

### 1.2 Length/depth — correcting the prior memo's characterization

**The prior memo's "one-line fragment" framing (part6 § 1.4, quoting only "does not target 'a specified occupation, business, or profession'") understates what is actually there, and the attorney is right to have pushed on this.** Part III.B is **five paragraphs and roughly seventeen sentences**, has its own heading separate from the overbreadth holding, recites the full two-part § 5F1.5(a) test, cites *Doe* for the least-restrictive-alternative gloss, applies the test's threshold/definitional element to the condition's text, and then affirmatively distinguishes two lines of contrary-seeming authority (*Gorychka*'s reliance on *Peterson*, and *MacMillen*). This is not a bare, unreasoned conclusion. **It is a genuine, self-contained sub-holding, not dicta and not a throwaway line.**

**At the same time, the attorney's underlying instinct — that something is thin here — is also correct, just for a more precise reason than "it's short."** The passage is thin not because it lacks paragraphs, but because **every one of those paragraphs stops at the threshold/definitional question and never reaches the two-part § 5F1.5(a) nexus-and-necessity test it recites.** The panel's own logic is: "this is not an occupational restriction *at all*, so we never have to ask whether a reasonably direct relationship existed between Brown's occupation and his offense, or whether restricting it is reasonably necessary." The two-part test is quoted in full in paragraph two and then never applied — because the panel resolves the case one step earlier, on the definitional question of what counts as "a specified occupation, business, or profession" under § 5F1.5(a). That is a real form of thinness (a single-premise, three-times-repeated textual point — "does not target a specified occupation" — carrying the entire holding), even though it is not a *short* discussion.

### 1.3 Does the passage reference Brown's individual facts?

**No — confirmed by a complete re-read.** Not one sentence in Part III.B mentions Brown's fraud scheme, his prior aliases, his history of lying to Probation, his prior state supervision, or any other individualized fact about his record. Every individualized aggravator the opinion relies on anywhere ("Brown's repeated use of electronic devices to engage in unlawful conduct, his continuation of unlawful conduct while on probation for a previous state offense, and his history of lying to Probation," 173 F.4th at 49) appears exclusively in **Part III.A**, the separate overbreadth/liberty-deprivation holding. This confirms — and strengthens — the prior memo's Part 1.3 finding on this specific point: the individualized-record aggravators are simply not available as a distinguishing tool against the occupational-restriction holding, because the occupational-restriction holding never used them in the first place.

### 1.4 Categorization: (a), (b), or (c)?

The honest answer is **primarily (a), with real elements of (c) — not (b).**

- It is **(a), a categorical rule independent of the defendant's individual record**, in the specific sense that matters most for Bernecky: the rule the panel actually announced — a condition regulating device use generically "in the course of employment," without naming a specific job, industry, or occupation, does not trigger § 5F1.5 at all, "even if" it functionally compels employer notification — is a pure construction of the guideline's text ("specified occupation, business, or profession") that would apply with equal force to *any* defendant with a similarly-worded condition, regardless of criminal history. Nothing in the reasoning is keyed to fraud, to Brown's aliases, or to anything else that is unique to him.
- It also has real characteristics of **(c), a brief, single-premise treatment vulnerable to narrow reading**, because the panel's analysis rises and falls on one textual proposition, repeated rather than developed, and because it resolves the appeal at the threshold/definitional stage without ever engaging the substantive two-part nexus-and-necessity inquiry the Guideline actually sets out. A later panel — or this Court, on a materially different condition-text — is not required to extend that single textual premise to a condition that does not share the feature the panel relied on (see Part 2, below).
- It is **not (b)** in the sense of being "textual in form but still implicitly bounded by the case's context" — there is no context-boundedness here at all; the panel did not hedge, qualify, or tie its reasoning to Brown's fraud scheme or record in any way. If anything, that is what makes the rule genuinely portable to other defendants' conditions — which cuts against Bernecky on the pure textual point (see part6 § 1.4's own candid acknowledgment of this) but is precisely why the theory should not be pled as a frontal, condition-text-based § 5F1.5 challenge modeled on Brown's own condition. It should instead be pled on the ground developed in Part 2 below: Bernecky's condition does not share the textual feature ("in the course of employment... as permitted by the employer") that Brown's panel was construing, and the theories Brown never had before it (ultra vires/delegation as to Probation's extra-textual demand) remain fully intact.

### 1.5 The precedential-weight question — answered directly

**The general rule controls here, and nothing about this opinion's posture is different.** *Brown* is a signed, three-judge panel opinion (Sullivan, J., joined by Wesley and Park, JJ.), published in full at 173 F.4th 45. No portion of it — including Part III.B — carries any different precedential designation than any other portion; there is no "unpublished" or "non-precedential" label anywhere in the opinion, and Part III.B was squarely presented, squarely argued (Brown Br. at 14, 17, quoted in the opinion itself), and squarely necessary to the disposition (the panel had to resolve the occupational-restriction argument to affirm in full). **This is textbook holding, not dicta, and it is binding in its entirety.** The general rule the attorney asked about — that a published Second Circuit opinion binds in full regardless of the brevity of any one portion's discussion, with brevity affecting only how narrowly that portion should be read and distinguished, not whether it technically binds — applies here without qualification. If anything, **the subsequent history reinforces rather than undercuts this**: Brown's own appellate counsel sought rehearing en banc specifically on this holding (Reh'g Pet. at 9-14, framing it as one of "two precedential rulings" in conflict with circuit precedent), and the petition was **denied** (per part6's independently-verified docket research, Docket #54, denied June 24, 2026). The en banc court had the opportunity to revisit or narrow this exact holding and did not. That makes the holding's *formal* bindingness, if anything, more secure — even though, as explained above, its *substantive reach* remains genuinely narrow because of what it does and does not say.

**Bottom line on Task 1: the prior memo's substantive conclusion (this is a portable, condition-text-based rule that forecloses a frontal, Brown-styled § 5F1.5 argument) was correct and is confirmed by the full-text read. But the prior memo's characterization of the passage as a "one-line fragment" was an understatement that gave the attorney's skepticism false purchase on the wrong ground — the real vulnerability is not brevity, it is that the rule rests on a single textual premise about Brown's specific condition wording and never reaches the substantive two-part test. The attorney is right that this is a "thin" holding in a meaningful sense; the attorney is not correct that it carries reduced precedential weight as a formal matter, or that it was not substantively addressed at all.**

## TASK 2 — The "as permitted by the employer" clause: what does it actually do?

### 2.1 The clause's precise text and where it comes from

The condition (Appendix at A.151-53, quoted verbatim in Brown's rehearing petition at 6, and consistent with the client-directives.md rendering) reads:

> "Use of any devices in the course of employment will be subject to monitoring restrictions as permitted by the employer."

(The panel's own paraphrase — "subject any electronic devices that Brown could use 'in the course of employment' to 'monitoring restrictions as permitted by the employer,'" 173 F.4th at 46 — is consistent but not a verbatim quotation of the Appendix language.)

### 2.2 Textual/legal-effect analysis

Grammatically, "as permitted by the employer" modifies "monitoring restrictions" — it defines the *scope and source* of the restriction, not Brown's own conduct. The clause does not say the employer "shall" or "must" permit monitoring; it says the monitoring/restriction that will occur is bounded by whatever the employer chooses to permit. **This functions as a built-in accommodation to the employer's own prerogative, embedded in the condition's own operative text.** If an employer simply declines to permit any monitoring of its systems, the condition's own language supplies no independent authority for Probation (or Brown) to override that refusal — the condition does not purport to compel the employer to do anything; it only authorizes monitoring "as permitted." Read for what it says (rather than for what it might be argued to functionally require), the clause is **employer-deferential, not employer-coercive**: it is Brown's *use* that is restricted, and the *content* of that restriction is set by the employer's own choice, not by Probation's demand.

### 2.3 Was this analyzed anywhere in the briefing or the opinion? (An honest, load-bearing negative finding)

**No — and this is worth stating plainly because it means the client's/attorney's point is genuinely novel, not merely underused.** A full read of every available document in the Brown record turns up **no discussion anywhere of what happens if an employer refuses to permit monitoring**:

- **Brown's opening brief (Point V, pp. 28-31)** treats "as permitted by the employer" solely as the textual hook establishing that disclosure is *functionally required*: "The Court should also vacate the special condition requiring Mr. Brown to permit the Probation Office to install software to allow for the monitoring of any devices he might use 'in the course of employment as permitted by the employer.' S.40; A.153. The monitoring of a defendant's work computers would likely function as a de facto employer notification condition..." Brown Appellant Br. at 28. It never asks what happens if the employer says no.
- **Brown's rehearing petition** makes the identical move, slightly more explicitly: "Brown also argued that the condition requiring that any computer he uses in the course of employment be 'subject to monitoring as permitted by the employer' exceeded the court's authority. **Because this condition requires him to notify an employer of his status (since its 'permi[ssion]' is required for monitoring**) it constituted an occupational restriction..." Reh'g Pet. at 7 (emphasis added). Again, the clause is used only to infer a notification obligation — never to ask about the employer-refusal scenario.
- **The government's appellee brief and both of its Rule 28(j) letters** do not address the clause's employer-refusal mechanics at all; they defend the condition entirely on the individualized-aggravator ground (Brown's fraud/dishonesty history).
- **The panel opinion** does not discuss the clause's operation upon an employer's refusal anywhere in Part III.B (or elsewhere).

**This is an unlitigated point, not a weakly-litigated one.** No party or panel member appears to have asked the question the client is asking. That is a genuine gap this office can fill independently — the point does not need to be "underused" language salvaged from the record; it is fresh analysis of text nobody else construed this way.

### 2.4 The distinguishing argument this supports for Bernecky

Brown's clause, read on its own terms, is the *most* employer-protective structure that has appeared anywhere in this line of cases: it does not compel the employer to do anything, does not impose any burden or standard on the employer, and expressly subordinates the scope of monitoring to whatever the employer will allow. Bernecky's CIMP condition, by contrast, contains **no employer language at all** (confirmed against the Judgment text, client-directives.md) — and yet Probation is reported to be **affirmatively demanding that the employer independently prove "like kind and degree" monitoring capability, including keystroke-level logging, as a precondition to Bernecky's employment proceeding.** That is not merely a demand with no textual basis (already established in part1/part6's ultra vires analysis); it is, structurally, the **inverse** of even the most aggressive textual precedent in this area. Brown's condition, at its most demanding, lets the *employer* decide what it will permit. Probation's posture here puts the burden the other way: the *employer* must satisfy Probation's own unarticulated standard before Bernecky's employment can even proceed. If a condition that merely defers to the employer's own choice was never even alleged (by the government, or found by the court) to impose any legal obligation on a non-party employer, then an ad hoc demand that goes further than that — requiring the employer to affirmatively meet a monitoring-capability standard neither the Judgment nor any court order specifies — cannot be justified by pointing to Brown, which never blessed, and never had occasion to consider, anything like it.

**Bottom line on Task 2: the attorney is correct, and the point is stronger than "underused" — it is untouched by any party or the panel in the entire Brown record. This should be built out as an affirmative distinguishing/comparative point in the motion (see Part 5 below), not treated as a minor addition.**

## TASK 3 — The Love comparison: was Brown's counsel's Rule 28(j) letter weak, and what is the strongest independent Love-Bernecky comparison?

### 3.1 The Rule 28(j) letter's actual content, verbatim

Filed April 14, 2025, four days after argument:

> "In Love, the Court, on plain error review, vacated the following special condition of supervised release: 'If [Love's] employment requires the use of a computer, [he] may use a computer in connection with the employment approved by the probation officer, at [his] place of employment, provided he notif[ies] [his] employer of: (1) the nature of [his] conviction; and (2) the fact that [his] conviction was facilitated by the use of his computer.' Slip. Op. at 9.
>
> "The Court held that the above condition amounted to an unlawful occupational restriction because there was 'nothing in the record to indicate that Love used a work computer to commit his crime, nor is there any evidence that his crime was connected to his occupation.' Slip. Op. at 10. This holding relied upon the prior decisions in United States v. Jenkins, 854 F.3d 181, 195 (2d Cir. 2017) and United States v. Ruff, 759 F. App'x 6, 8 (2d Cir. 2019) (summary order). The Court in Love vacated the employer-notification condition and remanded for further proceedings. Slip. Op. at 10, 11.
>
> "Here, as in Love (and Jenkins and Ruff), the record does not indicate that Brown used an employer's computer or other electronic device to commit his crime, or that his crime was connected to his occupation (as a maintenance worker, from 2016 until his arrest in this case). See PSR at 16, ¶ 77. Accordingly, the employer-notification condition in this case should also be vacated..."

### 3.2 Independent assessment: is it accurate, and is it weak?

**On pure factual accuracy: the letter is correct, not "wrong."** Every factual assertion it makes about Love checks out against the actual summary order: Love's Special Condition 13 is quoted accurately (verbatim match against the order's own text); the "nothing in the record..." holding-language is an accurate, verbatim quotation; the reliance on Jenkins and Ruff is accurate; and the parallel drawn to Brown (no record connection between his employment as a maintenance worker and his fraud offense) is also accurate — confirmed independently by the rehearing petition's own citation, PSR ¶ 77: "The job had nothing to do with his crime." **So the attorney's characterization that counsel "got the comparison wrong" is not correct if "wrong" means factually inaccurate.**

**There is one small, genuine accuracy error: the reporter citation for Ruff is transposed.** The letter cites "United States v. Ruff, 759 F. App'x 6, 8 (2d Cir. 2019)." The correct citation — confirmed independently against both the Love order itself ("United States v. Ruff, 795 F. App'x 6, 8 (2d Cir. 2019)") and Brown's own rehearing petition ("United States v. Ruff, 795 F. App'x 6 (2d Cir. Nov. 14, 2019)") — is **795 F. App'x 6**, not 759. This is a minor transposition typo, consistent with the letter having been drafted and filed within the Rule 28(j) letter's compressed timeframe (four days post-argument, and Rule 28(j) itself caps such letters at 350 words), but it is a real, confirmable citation error.

**Where the letter is genuinely weak — and this is the attorney's better point, even though "wrong" is not quite the right word for it — is a strategic omission, not a factual one.** Love's Special Condition 13 and Brown's condition are **not textually identical in a respect that turned out to matter to the panel.** Love's condition affirmatively *compelled* two things: (1) mandatory employer notification of "the nature of [his] conviction" and "the fact that [his] conviction was facilitated by the use of [his] computer," and (2) advance *Probation-Office approval* of the specific employment itself ("may use a computer in connection with the employment **approved by the probation officer**"). Brown's clause does neither of those things on its face — it never uses the word "notify," and it never requires PO approval of Brown's employment. It merely says monitoring will occur "as permitted by the employer" (see Task 2). The panel's own opinion seized on precisely this kind of distinction to reject Brown's argument: "The condition does not bar Brown from a specific occupation... **or compel Brown to notify his employers of the nature of his conviction**." 173 F.4th at 51 (emphasis added). Fields's 28(j) letter treated Love's and Brown's conditions as interchangeably strong analogs ("as in Love... the employer-notification condition in this case should also be vacated") without ever anticipating or rebutting the argument that Brown's clause is textually weaker as an "occupational restriction" candidate than Love's explicit notify-and-approve clause was. **That is a real gap: the letter did not foreclose, or even address, the precise textual distinction the panel ultimately relied on.** Whether a 350-word Rule 28(j) letter could ever have carried that additional argument is a fair question — but as filed, the letter did not do that work.

**Net assessment: agree in part.** The letter is factually accurate (net of one citation typo) and not "wrong" about Love's facts or holding. But it is analytically thin in exactly the way the attorney sensed: it presents Love as a straightforward, on-point parallel without engaging the one textual difference between the two conditions that the panel ultimately used to distinguish employer-notification conditions generally from Brown's specific clause. The client's instinct not to let the motion's own Love argument rest on "the panel was told and stayed silent" is well-founded for an additional, independent reason developed below: **the panel's silence, on this record, proves nothing about Love's continuing validity — because the panel never once mentions Love anywhere in its opinion.** (Compare part6 § 1.1/2.2's characterization that the panel "acknowledged this conflict" as to the whole Love/Salazar/Gorychka/Piper/Ruff/Lombardi line — that overstates it. A full re-read of the opinion confirms the panel **expressly named and declined to follow only *Gorychka***, 173 F.4th at 51 ("We thus decline to follow Gorychka here"). *Love* is not cited, quoted, distinguished, or mentioned by name anywhere in the opinion. Only Brown's *own rehearing petition* — not the panel — characterizes Love as part of an acknowledged conflict, and that petition was denied without an opinion, so the asserted conflict was never actually adjudicated by anyone. This is a meaningful correction to preserve: Love has not been rejected, sub silentio or otherwise, by any body with authority to reject it. It remains live, unaddressed, persuasive Second Circuit authority.)

### 3.3 An independent, from-scratch Love-Bernecky comparison

Built without reference to the panel's silence or to counsel's letter:

1. **Same offense family, decided on point.** Love pleaded guilty to receipt of child pornography, 18 U.S.C. § 2252A(a)(2)(A) — the same offense family as Bernecky's, not the fraud/identity-theft family that distinguishes Brown. Love applied U.S.S.G. § 5F1.5(a)'s two-part test directly to an employer/computer-use condition and vacated it because "there is nothing in the record to indicate that Love used a work computer to commit his crime, nor is there any evidence that his crime was connected to his occupation." This is not an analogical stretch from a different kind of case; it is the identical statutory question, decided for a defendant convicted of the identical statute Bernecky was convicted under (or its possession-offense sibling).

2. **Offense severity is not the axis the vacatur turned on — an affirmative point, not a defensive one.** The district court in Love found the defendant possessed "over 300 images of child pornography," including images "portray[ing] sadistic conduct and other depictions of violence or sexual abuse," and that he "distributed at least some of the child pornography he possessed." None of that mattered to the § 5F1.5 analysis — the panel vacated the condition anyway, because aggravation of the *underlying CP offense* does not supply the *employment nexus* the Guideline separately requires. This means Bernecky does not need to distinguish his own record favorably from Love's on offense severity to prevail on this theory (he can, since Love's conduct was considerably more aggravated on this record) — the point is that severity is simply irrelevant to the nexus question, which is a narrower and more winnable showing than "my case is less bad than Love's."

3. **Bernecky's position is, if anything, stronger than Love's — an a fortiori argument that does not depend on the panel's treatment of Brown at all.** Love's Special Condition 13 was an explicit, court-ordered, individually-tailored condition, with a stated (if ultimately inadequate) judicial rationale on the record ("[d]ue to advancing technology, ... it is unlikely that upon the defendant's release, he will encounter employment which does not somehow allow him access to a computer"). Even *that* — an actual court order, with an actual articulated (if insufficient) reason — could not survive § 5F1.5(a) without individualized nexus findings, and the panel vacated it. Bernecky's situation has **no comparable process at all**: no court ever ordered anything employer-related in his CIMP condition, and no reason of any kind — sufficient or insufficient — has ever been articulated on any record for Probation's "like kind and degree" employer-monitoring-capability demand. If a reasoned, court-ordered condition targeting the defendant's own employment failed for want of individualized findings, an unreasoned, court-unordered administrative demand necessarily fails too, and does not require reliance on anything Brown said or didn't say about Love to reach that conclusion.

4. **Honest qualifications, stated candidly (unchanged from part6's assessment, and correctly so):** Love is a non-precedential summary order under 2d Cir. Local Rule 32.1.1 and should be cited as persuasive, not controlling, authority; it was decided on plain-error review with a government concession, a different procedural posture from Bernecky's pre-supervision § 3583(e)(2) modification motion; and Love's own condition, unlike Bernecky's, did contain explicit employer/computer language (though of a materially different, more restrictive kind than Brown's — see Task 2). None of these qualifications undercuts points 1-3 above.

**Bottom line on Task 3: the attorney is right that the letter should not be leaned on as if the panel's silence proves anything — because the panel's silence as to Love specifically is total, not a considered rejection, and because the letter itself did not anticipate the panel's actual distinguishing rationale. But the attorney's characterization that the letter is factually "wrong" is not accurate — it is correct on the facts, just strategically incomplete. The independent comparison built above (same offense family; severity-irrelevance; the a fortiori point that Bernecky's process-free demand is weaker than Love's process-full one) stands entirely on its own and should lead the motion's Love discussion.**

## TASK 4 — Does Carlineo still fit Special Condition (b), and what should replace/refine it?

### 4.1 Carlineo's actual vice, verbatim

> "The condition prescribed by the district court is vague because it would leave a reasonable person guessing as to what the requirements of the Program might be. During sentencing, the district court orally instructed Carlineo to participate in the program and noted that participation could include a sentencing circle and a listening circle... However, exactly what this might entail is not clear. No guidance was given as to what specific activities the sentencing circle and or the listening circle would involve. And none was given as to the required frequency of the circle sessions or as to what 'successful participation' might look like." 998 F.3d at 536-37.
>
> "The Guidelines authorize the imposition of special conditions such as substance abuse and mental health treatment. See U.S.S.G. § 5D1.3(d). However, these programs must be 'approved by the United States Probation Office.' U.S.S.G. § 5D1.3(d)(4); U.S.S.G. § 5D1.3(d)(5). **In contrast, the Program has not been approved. It has not been evaluated by the Probation Office, and there are no apparent licensing, certification, or objective standards that govern and define the Program.** The lack of prior approval and the cursory description of the Program in the Partners' letter together render the challenged special condition unduly vague." Id. at 537 (emphasis added).
>
> "We also conclude that the special condition results in the delegation of too much authority to the Probation Office. We have been clear that the district court, not the Probation Office, retains exclusive authority to set conditions of supervised release. In United States v. Matta, we held that the district court erred when it imposed a special condition of supervised release that authorized the Probation Office to choose between an in-patient substance abuse treatment program and outpatient treatment program. 777 F.3d 116, 123 (2d Cir. 2015)... To be sure, a district court may delegate to the Probation Office decision-making authority over details of supervised release such as a treatment schedule or duration. But it may not delegate decision-making authority which would make a defendant's liberty contingent on a probation officer's exercise of open-ended discretion over a program no one knows much about. See Matta, 777 F.3d at 122. **In other words, the Probation Office may supervise and execute a sentence but may not fashion a sentence's terms.**" Id. at 537-38 (emphasis added).

### 4.2 Is the client's distinction correct? Yes — confirmed, and it is a significant weakness in Carlineo's fit as currently deployed.

Both of Carlineo's independent holdings — vagueness and delegation — are expressly grounded in the fact that **the program category itself** ("Partners in Restorative Initiatives") was never evaluated, licensed, certified, or governed by any objective standard, and was not even a recognized *type* of program the court or Probation had any institutional familiarity with: "Prior to imposing this condition, it does not appear that either the district court or the Probation Office had any particular familiarity with Partners." Id. at 536. The opinion goes out of its way to contrast this with the Guidelines-recognized categories (substance abuse, mental health treatment) that **are** expressly authorized by § 5D1.3(d) precisely *because* they come with an approval mechanism and (implicitly) an established, licensed field behind them.

Bernecky's Special Condition (b) — "The defendant must participate in a sex offense-specific treatment program and follow the rules and regulations of that program" — names exactly the kind of Guidelines-recognized program category Carlineo contrasts itself against, not the kind of undefined ad hoc program Carlineo condemns. Sex-offense-specific treatment is an established field with licensing/certification standards (e.g., state-certified sex-offender treatment providers, often ATSA-informed), and the Guidelines themselves authorize a standard condition requiring "participat[ion] in a program **approved by the United States Probation Office** for the treatment and monitoring of sex offenders" (U.S.S.G. § 5D1.3(d), sex-offense treatment subsection — numbered (d)(5) in some Manual editions and (d)(7) in others depending on the version in effect; **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING against the Guidelines Manual edition applicable to Bernecky's original sentencing and any amendments since — direct ussc.gov retrieval failed in this session with the same session-wide tool error noted in part1; the substance of the provision, cross-confirmed across multiple secondary sources and consistent with the language Carlineo itself quotes for the analogous substance-abuse/mental-health subsections, is not in doubt, but the exact current subsection number should be pinned down before citing it]**). A court applying Carlineo to Bernecky's condition would have a ready, textually clean basis to distinguish it: *"Unlike Partners in Restorative Initiatives, sex-offense-specific treatment is a Guidelines-recognized category with an established professional field and a PO-approval mechanism built into the Guidelines themselves — Carlineo does not apply."*

**The client is correct: Carlineo, deployed as the primary authority for Special Condition (b) exactly as part1 currently frames it, is vulnerable to being "wildly distinguished" on this basis, and the argument needs to be refined before filing.**

### 4.3 The refinement: relocate the theory from the program-category layer to the provider-rule layer, with Matta (not Carlineo) as the lead authority

The vice in Bernecky's condition is not that "sex offense-specific treatment" is undefined — it is well-defined as a category. The vice is the separate clause: **"and follow the rules and regulations of that program"** — combined with **"[t]he probation officer will supervise the details of the defendant's participation in the program, including the selection of a provider and schedule."** Unpacked, this clause structure does three things Matta's rule speaks to directly, even though Matta involved a different kind of choice (inpatient versus outpatient) than Carlineo's (an undefined program):

1. It delegates *provider selection* to Probation. That much is consistent with, and arguably compelled by, the Guidelines' own model condition (§ 5D1.3(d)'s "approved by the United States Probation Office" language) and with Matta/Kunz's "minor details of implementation" carve-out — this piece is not independently objectionable, and should be conceded as such rather than contested, to keep the argument focused on what is actually vulnerable.
2. It then automatically incorporates, as binding, revocation-enforceable terms of supervision, **whatever rules that not-yet-selected private provider unilaterally adopts** — rules that are unknown at sentencing, unwritten anywhere in the Judgment, set by a private entity the court has never reviewed, and changeable at that entity's own discretion during the course of treatment, with no mechanism for court review of the content of those rules at any point.
3. Nothing in the Guidelines' own recommended sex-offense-treatment condition text (so far as this research could confirm) requires or contemplates that a provider's own internally-adopted rules become independently enforceable conditions of supervised release in this way — the Guidelines' own model authorizes PO approval of *the provider*, not incorporation of *the provider's rulebook* as a term of the sentence. Bernecky's Judgment condition goes further than the Guidelines' own template on this specific point.

This is precisely the "significant liberty interest" delegation Matta condemns, just one layer further removed than Matta's own facts. Matta held that letting Probation choose between inpatient and outpatient treatment "is tantamount to allowing him to decide the nature or extent of the defendant's punishment" because that choice determines how much liberty is actually restricted. 777 F.3d at 123 (as characterized in Carlineo, 998 F.3d at 537). Here, the choice of *what conduct will and will not violate supervision* is not even left to Probation's own discretion — it is left to a **private treatment provider's** discretion, with Probation merely "supervis[ing]" that provider's exercise of it. If Matta forbids the court from letting *Probation* fix the "nature or extent" of a liberty restriction by choosing between two known treatment modalities, it follows a fortiori that the court cannot let an *unselected private provider* fix that same liberty restriction by writing its own rules after the fact, unreviewed, and changeable at will. Carlineo's own gloss on Matta supplies the exact language for this: **"the Probation Office may supervise and execute a sentence but may not fashion a sentence's terms."** 998 F.3d at 538. Here, it is not even Probation "fashioning" the terms — it is a private, non-governmental provider doing so, with even less judicial or institutional accountability than the Probation Office itself would have.

**This reframing has three advantages over the current Carlineo-led approach:**
- It survives the category-is-defined distinction entirely, because it does not depend on the program category being undefined — it depends only on the *provider's rules* being undefined, unwritten, and unreviewable, which is true regardless of how well-established "sex offense-specific treatment" is as a field.
- It leads with **Matta** (777 F.3d 116) as the primary rule of decision — a holding about the court/Probation division of authority that does not turn on any program-definition question at all — and uses **Carlineo only for its restatement and application of Matta's rule** ("may supervise and execute... may not fashion a sentence's terms"), while candidly conceding that Carlineo's own *vagueness* holding (Part I of that opinion) does not map onto Bernecky's condition, since the category here is not vague in the way Partners in Restorative Initiatives was.
- It does not require identifying a wholly different case: no better-fitting candidate authority for this specific delegation problem appears anywhere else in client-directives.md or part1-cimp-occupational-delegation.md (which already gathered Matta, Kunz, and Keleher as the operative delegation-doctrine family, and independently flagged, at Part 3(c), the exact "fill in too many blanks" tension this task asks about — see part1 at "confirm whether the panel drew any distinction between a wholly undefined program... and Bernecky's condition, which at least names a category of program... even though the specific provider and that provider's specific rules are undefined"). The correct fix is not a new case; it is redeploying the existing authority around the provider-rule layer instead of the program-category layer.

**Bottom line on Task 4: the attorney's pushback is correct and identifies a real, filing-relevant weakness in how part1 currently frames the argument. The fix is not to abandon Carlineo, but to demote it from lead authority to a supporting citation for its Matta restatement, while making Matta itself — applied to the provider's unilateral, unwritten, unreviewable rulemaking rather than to the program category's definition — the primary authority for Special Condition (b).**

### [REVISED DRAFT] Brown Is Adverse Authority That Must Be — and Can Be — Distinguished on Its Own Terms; the "As Permitted By the Employer" Clause Confirms the Distinction; Love Independently Confirms Mr. Bernecky's Position

The government may point to *United States v. Brown*, 173 F.4th 45 (2d Cir. 2026), a published, precedential Second Circuit decision rejecting a § 5F1.5 challenge to a computer-monitoring condition reaching a defendant's workplace devices. Counsel addresses it directly, candidly, and in full — including the possibility that portions of it may be read as more categorical than others — because it is binding authority and the defense has an obligation of candor to the tribunal.

Properly read, Brown's occupational-restriction holding (Part III.B of the opinion, 173 F.4th at 50-51) is a genuine, reasoned holding — not a throwaway line — but it rests entirely on the construction of a single textual feature: whether a condition referencing "employment" generically, without naming a specific job or industry, "target[s] 'a specified occupation, business, or profession'" under U.S.S.G. § 5F1.5(a). Id. at 51. The panel never applied § 5F1.5(a)'s substantive two-part nexus-and-necessity test to Brown's individual record in this portion of its opinion — it resolved the appeal one step earlier, on the definitional question, and its reasoning does not reference Brown's criminal history, his fraud scheme, or any other individualized fact anywhere in this specific passage. That combination — a fully reasoned, fully precedential holding that is nonetheless narrowly premised on a single textual point — means Brown must be engaged with seriously, but confines what it actually decided.

**First, Brown's own condition — unlike Mr. Bernecky's — contains express employment language, and that language is itself instructive.** The clause Brown challenged provided that "[u]se of any devices in the course of employment will be subject to monitoring restrictions **as permitted by the employer**." This is not merely employer-referencing text; on its own terms, it makes the scope of any monitoring contingent on what the employer is willing to allow — an express accommodation to the employer's own prerogative, built into the condition itself. No party and no member of the panel ever addressed what happens if an employer declines to permit monitoring, because the question never arose on Brown's record; but the clause's text answers it: there is no independent authority in the condition for Probation to monitor an employer's systems beyond what that employer permits. Mr. Bernecky's CIMP condition contains **no comparable language of any kind** — the words "employ," "employer," and "employment" appear nowhere in it. There is no textual accommodation to an employer's own choice for Probation to observe, and, more importantly, no textual basis at all for what Probation has instead done here: demand that Mr. Bernecky's prospective employer affirmatively prove "like kind and degree" monitoring capability, including keystroke-level logging, as a precondition to his employment. If even Brown's condition — which the government has never argued imposes any obligation on the employer, and which by its own terms simply defers to whatever the employer will permit — falls short of authorizing a demand on the employer, then Probation's freestanding demand here, which goes further than anything Brown's clause contemplated by requiring the employer to satisfy an affirmative, unstated standard, cannot be justified by appeal to Brown. Brown resolved whether a condition's own text, deferring entirely to the employer's choice, is an occupational restriction; it did not resolve, and had no occasion to resolve, whether Probation may unilaterally impose a new, more demanding requirement with no textual basis at all. That is the ultra vires and delegation question already developed above, and Brown does not speak to it.

**Second, Brown's occupational-restriction holding is a construction of Brown's specific condition text, and Mr. Bernecky's condition does not share the feature that construction depended on.** The panel's rule — that a condition regulating employment-related device use generically, without naming a specific job or industry, does not trigger § 5F1.5 "even if" it functionally requires employer notification — is a textual rule about conditions that at least *mention* employment. Mr. Bernecky's CIMP condition does not mention employment at all; the entire employer dimension of this dispute was invented by the probation officer in an administrative conversation, with no textual hook in the Judgment whatsoever. Brown's holding, read for exactly what it says, simply has no condition text here to apply itself to.

**Third, Brown was a fraud case whose separate overbreadth holding (Part III.A, not Part III.B) rested on a documented history of device-based reoffending and dishonesty to Probation that Mr. Bernecky's record does not share.** Brown pleaded guilty to fourteen counts of making false, fictitious, or fraudulent claims and one count of theft of government funds, arising from a multi-year scheme in which he impersonated an attorney to steal incarcerated victims' identities and filed fraudulent tax returns in their names. 173 F.4th at 47-48. The panel's separate conclusion that the monitoring condition did not impose a greater deprivation of liberty than reasonably necessary rested explicitly on "Brown's repeated use of electronic devices to engage in unlawful conduct, his continuation of unlawful conduct while on probation for a previous state offense, and his history of lying to Probation" — a record showing Brown used at least six aliases over multiple decades and gave Probation and the sentencing court "conflicting accounts" of his own identity. Id. at 49. Mr. Bernecky's offense is possession of child pornography, a fundamentally different offense, and his supervision history contains none of these aggravators: one violation, coextensive with the instant offense itself, and no history whatsoever — on this specific record — of any violation connected to employment, a workplace, or a work-related device.

**Fourth, and affirmatively: United States v. Love, No. 24-1706-cr, 2025 WL 1077176 (2d Cir. Apr. 8, 2025), decided under the identical statutory test and involving the identical offense family as Mr. Bernecky's, confirms that his position is correct — independent of anything Brown's own appellate counsel argued or the panel did not address.** In Love, the Second Circuit vacated a special condition requiring a child-pornography defendant to obtain probation-officer approval and notify his employer before using a computer at work, holding that "there is nothing in the record to indicate that Love used a work computer to commit his crime, nor is there any evidence that his crime was connected to his occupation." 2025 WL 1077176, at *5. That holding is squarely on point: like Love, Mr. Bernecky has never used any employer's device to commit any offense, and there is no record connection whatsoever between his CP-possession offense and any occupation, including the specific job (at a contact lens manufacturer) now at issue. The district court in Love found that the defendant possessed "over 300 images of child pornography," including images depicting "sadistic conduct," and had distributed some of it — a considerably more aggravated record than anything reflected in Mr. Bernecky's file — yet the panel vacated the employer-notification-and-approval condition anyway, because the severity of the underlying offense does not supply the separate employment nexus § 5F1.5(a) requires. If a defendant with that record could not sustain an explicit, court-ordered employment-approval-and-notification condition — a condition considerably more restrictive on its face than anything appearing in Mr. Bernecky's Judgment, since it affirmatively compelled notification and required advance Probation-Office approval of the specific job — for want of an offense-to-occupation nexus, then Probation's freestanding, court-unauthorized demand here, which lacks even the facial judicial process Love's condition had, cannot stand either. Love is a summary order and does not bind this Court, but it resolves the identical statutory question this Court must resolve here, for a defendant convicted of the same offense as Mr. Bernecky.

**In sum:** Brown does not foreclose relief here. Its occupational-restriction holding is a genuinely reasoned, fully precedential construction of a specific textual feature — generic, non-job-specific employment language — that Mr. Bernecky's silent condition does not share, and its "as permitted by the employer" clause, on its own terms, gives the employer more protection than Probation has extended to Mr. Bernecky's prospective employer here. Brown's separate overbreadth holding rests on an individualized record of aggravating facts Mr. Bernecky's record does not share. And Love — resolving the identical § 5F1.5 question for the identical offense — confirms that where there is no record connection between the offense and the defendant's occupation, an employment-conditioning restriction cannot stand without the individualized findings that were never made here. The relief Mr. Bernecky seeks is not a ruling that Brown was wrongly decided; it is a ruling that Brown's narrow, textually-bounded holding does not reach a condition that never mentions employment at all, and that Probation's ad hoc administration of that silent condition — going further than even Brown's or Love's own conditions ever purported to go — requires the same result Love reached.

### [NEW DRAFT] Special Condition (b) — The Delegation of Undefined, Unreviewable Provider Rules to a Private Treatment Provider Is What Matta Forbids, Regardless of Whether "Sex Offense-Specific Treatment" Is a Recognized Program Category

Special Condition (b) requires Mr. Bernecky to "participate in a sex offense-specific treatment program **and follow the rules and regulations of that program**," while "[t]he probation officer will supervise the details of [his] participation in the program, including the selection of a provider and schedule." Sex-offense-specific treatment is, unlike the program at issue in United States v. Carlineo, 998 F.3d 533 (2d Cir. 2021), a well-established, Guidelines-recognized category of supervision programming, and the selection of a specific, licensed provider within that category is the kind of "detail[] of implementation" that this Court's delegation cases permit Probation to handle. See United States v. Kunz, 68 F.4th 748, 767 (2d Cir. 2023); U.S.S.G. § 5D1.3(d) (authorizing participation in a program "approved by the United States Probation Office"). Mr. Bernecky does not challenge that piece of the condition.

What he challenges is the separate clause making his compliance with supervision automatically contingent on "the rules and regulations" that provider unilaterally adopts — rules that do not exist in writing anywhere at the time of sentencing, are set entirely by a private entity the court has never reviewed, and can change at that provider's own discretion at any time during treatment, with no mechanism for the court, or Mr. Bernecky, to review or contest their content before they become enforceable, revocation-backed terms of his supervision.

This Court has been clear that "the district court, not the Probation Office, retains exclusive authority to set conditions of supervised release," and that while a court may delegate "decision-making authority over details of supervised release such as a treatment schedule or duration," it "may not delegate decision-making authority which would make a defendant's liberty contingent on a probation officer's exercise of open-ended discretion over a program no one knows much about." United States v. Matta, 777 F.3d 116, 122-23 (2d Cir. 2015), as restated in Carlineo, 998 F.3d at 537-38. Carlineo's own gloss on this rule is precise: **"the Probation Office may supervise and execute a sentence but may not fashion a sentence's terms."** Id. at 538.

Here, the delegation runs one level further than Matta's own facts. Matta forbade letting the Probation Office decide between two known treatment modalities (inpatient or outpatient) because that choice determines "the nature or extent of the defendant's punishment." Special Condition (b) does not even leave that choice with Probation — it leaves the definition of what will and will not violate Mr. Bernecky's supervision to a **private treatment provider**, selected by Probation, whose own unwritten and unreviewed rules automatically become binding conditions the moment that provider adopts them. Probation's role, on the face of the condition, is only to "supervise the details of [his] participation" — precisely the "supervise and execute" function Carlineo says is permissible — while the actual content of what Mr. Bernecky must do to avoid a violation is "fashioned," in the first instance, by an entity even further removed from judicial oversight than the Probation Office itself. If Matta forbids the district court from letting Probation make that kind of liberty-defining choice, it follows a fortiori that the court cannot let an as-yet-unselected private provider make it instead, unreviewed and subject to change at will.

Requested relief: an order clarifying that Mr. Bernecky cannot be found in violation of supervised release for declining to comply with a specific treatment-provider rule that exceeds the scope of what this Court itself has ordered, and that any provider rule intended to carry the force of a condition of supervision must first be presented to and approved by the Court, consistent with Matta's requirement that liberty-defining terms be fixed by the district court rather than delegated to another's open-ended discretion.
