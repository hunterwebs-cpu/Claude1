## PART 1 — VERIFICATION: *United States v. Brown*, No. 24-1227 (later published at 173 F.4th 45 (2d Cir. Apr. 21, 2026))

### 1.1 Procedural posture and current status — confirmed

- Panel: Wesley, Sullivan (author), Park, JJ. Argued April 10, 2025; decided April 21, 2026.
- **Brown sought rehearing en banc on June 3, 2026, specifically on the two grounds most relevant here** (the occupational-restriction holding and the suspicionless-monitoring holding). **The petition was denied on June 24, 2026** (Docket #54, "ORDER, denying petition for panel rehearing, or, in the alternative, for rehearing en banc"). **Brown is therefore binding, precedential Second Circuit authority, now published at 173 F.4th 45, and it must be addressed candidly — it cannot be dismissed as unsettled, non-final, or likely to be overturned.**
- Brown's own rehearing petition documents, and the panel's opinion itself acknowledges, that the occupational-restriction holding conflicts with a line of prior Second Circuit authority (*Doe*, *Peterson*, *Jenkins I*, and — critically for Bernecky — the unpublished *Love*, *Salazar*, *Gorychka*, *Piper*, *Ruff*, and *Lombardi* decisions). The panel expressly acknowledged this conflict and "decline[d] to follow" *Gorychka*. 173 F.4th at 51. En banc review to resolve that acknowledged conflict was requested and refused. This means: (a) Brown is good law and controls unless and until the Circuit revisits it; but (b) the tension with the *Love*/*Jenkins*/*Ruff*/*Gorychka* line is not this office's invention — it is an admitted, live fault line in Second Circuit law, which is legitimate to argue as a reason the *Brown* rule should be read narrowly and confined to its own facts and condition-text, and as room to argue Bernecky's case falls on the *Love*/*Jenkins*/*Ruff* side of that fault line rather than *Brown*'s.

### 1.2 Attorney's Point 1 — Does Brown's own condition expressly invoke occupational/employment requirements?

**CONFIRMED, with the exact quote.** The condition Brown challenged was a single, multi-clause special condition. The clause specifically at issue reads, verbatim, across the opinion, both briefs, and the PSR-quoted text (all sources are word-for-word consistent):

> **"The use of any Device(s) in the course of employment will be subject to monitoring or restriction as permitted by the employer."**
> (Opinion, 173 F.4th at 46 (paraphrasing as "subject any electronic devices that Brown could use 'in the course of employment' to 'monitoring restrictions as permitted by the employer'"); Appellant Br. at 13 (verbatim, quoting A.153); Appellee Br. at 7 (verbatim, quoting PSR at 30).)

This is unambiguous, express employment/employer text — "in the course of employment," "as permitted by the employer" — appearing in the condition's own operative language, ordered by the district judge. Brown's own brief argued this clause "effectively requires him to notify any prospective employers of his fraud conviction" and is "tantamount to an occupational restriction." Brown Br. at 14, 17.

**By contrast, Bernecky's CIMP condition (Special Condition (a)) contains no employment/employer language anywhere** — confirmed directly against the Judgment text in client-directives.md: *"Confirmed: the word 'employ'/'employer'/'employment' does NOT appear anywhere in this condition's text."* The entire employer dimension of Bernecky's dispute — the PO's "like kind and degree" monitoring-capability demand directed at his prospective employer — has no textual counterpart in the Judgment at all. It was invented in an administrative conversation, not ordered by any judge.

**This is a real, verified, and important distinction — but it cuts in an unexpected direction that must be framed carefully (see Part 1.4 below).** Because Brown's panel held that even an *explicit*, court-ordered "employment"/"employer" clause is not an occupational restriction (because it does not name a "specified occupation, business, or profession"), a naive reading might suggest Bernecky's *even less* occupation-specific, employer-silent condition is *even further* from being an occupational restriction under Brown's own categorical test. **The correct response is not to fight Brown's textual holding on its own terms, but to point out it answers a different question than the one Bernecky's case presents** — see Part 4 below.

### 1.3 Attorney's Point 2 — Is Brown a fraud/fictitious-identity case involving lying to probation?

**CONFIRMED, with exact quotes.** Brown pleaded guilty to fourteen counts of making false, fictitious, or fraudulent claims (18 U.S.C. § 287) and one count of theft of government funds (18 U.S.C. § 641) — a Stolen Identity Refund Fraud ("SIRF") scheme in which Brown (and confederates) stole personally identifiable information from at least nine incarcerated victims (in some instances by mailing letters "posing as an attorney" to solicit their PII), filed 14 fraudulent tax returns in the victims' names, and obtained $136,672 in IRS refunds. Opinion at 4-5; Appellee Br. at 2-4. This is a **fraud/identity-theft offense**, categorically different in character from Bernecky's CP-possession offense — it is a deception-based crime by its nature, not a possession offense.

On the "lying to Probation" / multiple-identities point, the opinion states directly (footnote 2, reproduced in the body of the panel's reasoning at 173 F.4th at 49):

> **"Brown's repeated use of electronic devices to engage in unlawful conduct, his continuation of unlawful conduct while on probation for a previous state offense, and his history of lying to Probation, we conclude that the computer-monitoring condition is not an unreasonable or excessive deprivation of Brown's liberty."**

The underlying record for this (from the PSR, quoted in the government's appellee brief) shows Brown "assumed several identities with contradictory accounts of his personal and family data," used at least six different names/aliases over multiple decades (Mark Richard Brown, Richard/"Ritchie" McDermott, Akeem Rakeem, Rakeem Akeem, Hugh Neil, Tylon C. Williams), obtained government-issued identification in multiple names (including two of his own victims' names), and gave the sentencing court and Probation "conflicting accounts of his background" such that it was, and reportedly still is, "unclear ... who the defendant is or where he is from." Appellee Br. at 8-9.

**Important precision the attorney's team should preserve:** this "lying to Probation" aggravator is about Brown's **identity/biographical dishonesty** (assumed aliases, false personal history, unclear true identity) discovered largely through the PSR interview and cross-referenced against DMV and documentary records — it is not, on this record, a discrete finding that Brown lied about a *specific supervision violation* or about *work-related device use* during an active term of supervision. It should be characterized precisely as "a documented, multi-decade pattern of assumed identities and biographical dishonesty toward Probation and the court," not embellished as something more specific than the record shows.

**One further, important nuance the prior research pass did not catch and that materially affects how this point should be used:** the "history of lying to Probation" / "repeated use of electronic devices" aggravators appear in **Part III.A of the opinion — the general overbreadth / "no greater deprivation of liberty than reasonably necessary" holding** (173 F.4th at 49-50). They do **not** appear in **Part III.B — the separate occupational-restriction holding** (173 F.4th at 50-51), which instead rests entirely on a categorical textual construction of § 5F1.5(a)'s "specified occupation, business, or profession" language, independent of anything specific to Brown's individual history. This means: **Brown's individualized aggravating record is the correct tool for distinguishing the general/overbreadth CIMP challenge (Bernecky's Lifshitz-based narrow-tailoring theory), but it is not, by itself, what distinguishes the occupational-restriction holding — that holding needs a different, textual/structural distinguishing argument.** See Part 4.

### 1.4 The categorical rule Brown actually adopted for the occupational-restriction question

Brown's occupational-restriction holding is narrower and more mechanical than a fact-bound "these aggravators justify it" ruling. The panel's operative reasoning:

> "[E]ven if the condition functionally required Brown to notify future employers about the nature of his conviction, such a requirement would not transform the challenged monitoring condition into an occupational restriction **because the condition does not target 'a specified occupation, business, or profession.'**" 173 F.4th at 51 (quoting U.S.S.G. § 5F1.5(a); emphasis in original).

The panel distinguished *Peterson*, 248 F.3d 79 (2d Cir. 2001) — Brown's principal authority — on the ground that Peterson involved notifying "particular employers ... identified by a probation officer," 173 F.4th at 51, and it expressly declined to follow *Gorychka*, 2024 WL 3964287 (2d Cir. Aug. 28, 2024) (summary order), which had applied Peterson to a materially identical employer-approval-of-monitoring clause. **Brown's own rehearing petition disputes the panel's characterization of Peterson** (arguing Peterson in fact applied to "potential employers or educational programs" generally, not particular ones identified in advance, Reh'g Pet. at 12-13) — a factual dispute about Peterson's own holding that the en banc court declined to resolve.

**Net effect for Bernecky:** Brown stands for the proposition that a **generic** computer-monitoring condition that happens to extend to devices used "in the course of employment," without naming any specific job, industry, or occupation, is not an occupational restriction under § 5F1.5 — full stop, regardless of the defendant's individual history. That rule, read at face value, would apply with at least equal force to a condition (like Bernecky's) that doesn't even mention employment. **This means the direct, frontal argument "CIMP reaching employer devices = per se occupational restriction requiring § 5F1.5 findings" is foreclosed by binding authority and should not be the lead theory.** The theories that survive Brown intact — and that should now lead — are (1) the ultra vires/delegation theory (the PO's extra-textual demand, not the condition itself, is the problem — a question Brown's panel never had before it and did not decide), and (2) the as-applied, concrete-harm framing (Bernecky is not asking the court to find the condition itself unlawful in the abstract, but to declare that Probation may not unilaterally invent and impose an unauthorized new requirement on a non-party). See Part 4.

## PART 2 — VERIFICATION: *United States v. Love*, No. 24-1706-cr, 2025 WL 1077176 (2d Cir. Apr. 8, 2025) (summary order)

### 2.1 Facts, condition text, and holding — confirmed by full-text read

Thomas Love pleaded guilty to receipt of child pornography, 18 U.S.C. § 2252A(a)(2)(A) — **a CP case, the same offense family as Bernecky's**, not a fraud/identity-theft case. Sentenced to 121 months' imprisonment (30 months below the 151-181 month Guidelines range) and a 20-year term of supervised release. The district court found Love possessed "over 300 images of child pornography," some depicting "sadistic conduct," and had distributed some of it.

Love challenged two special conditions. The one relevant here, **Special Condition 13**, reads verbatim:

> **"If [Love's] employment requires the use of a computer, [he] may use a computer in connection with the employment approved by the probation officer, at [his] place of employment, provided he notif[ies] [his] employer of: (1) the nature of [his] conviction; and (2) the fact that [his] conviction was facilitated by the use of the computer."**

The district court's entire stated justification: "[S]pecial condition 13 is necessary in order to protect the public from further crimes of the defendant. Due to advancing technology, ... it is unlikely that upon the defendant's release, he will encounter employment which does not somehow allow him access to a computer."

**Holding (with the government's concession of error):**

> "[T]he [district] court did not ... explain what, if any, connection existed between Love's employment and his offense of conviction." ... "[T]here is nothing in the record to indicate that Love used a work computer to commit his crime, **nor is there any evidence that his crime was connected to his occupation.**"

The panel applied U.S.S.G. § 5F1.5(a)'s two-part test directly, relied on *United States v. Jenkins*, 854 F.3d 181, 195 (2d Cir. 2017) (vacating "the same condition" because "the relationship between the restrictions on [the defendant's] employment and [his] offense and circumstances is not readily apparent"), and *United States v. Ruff*, 795 F. App'x 6, 8 (2d Cir. 2019) (summary order) (vacating an employer-notification provision where the defendant "had only ever held employment in the food services industry" and there was "no evidence that he had used a workplace computer to access child pornography"). **The panel VACATED Special Condition 13 and remanded** for the district court to either drop the condition or make the individualized findings § 5F1.5(a) requires.

(Love also succeeded in vacating a separate one-internet-device limitation, Special Condition 12, on a different theory — under *United States v. Kunz*, 68 F.4th 748, 767 (2d Cir. 2023), a severe restriction on the number of devices requires "particularized justification," and the government itself conceded that improved monitoring technology undercut the need for a one-device cap. That holding is a secondary, useful cross-reference for Bernecky's per-device-cost/IoT theory (client-directives.md, Special Condition (a) theories 4-6), but is not this memo's focus.)

### 2.2 Love's place in the Brown litigation — an important, documented connection

*Love* is not merely a parallel authority found independently — **it was affirmatively brought to the Brown panel's attention** in real time. Brown's own appellate counsel (Darrell Fields) filed a Rule 28(j) letter on April 14, 2025 — four days after argument — specifically citing Love and arguing:

> "[A]s in Love (and Jenkins and Ruff), the record does not indicate that Brown used an employer's computer or other electronic device to commit his crime, or that his crime was connected to his occupation (as a maintenance worker, from 2016 until his arrest in this case). ... Accordingly, the employer-notification condition in this case should also be vacated."

**The Brown panel's opinion, issued more than a year later, does not cite, discuss, or distinguish Love anywhere in its text** (confirmed by a full read of the published opinion). The panel instead rested its occupational-restriction holding on the purely textual "specified occupation" reasoning described in Part 1.4, above, and separately declined to follow Gorychka (Love's close cousin) without addressing Love by name. Brown's rehearing petition specifically flagged this unaddressed conflict (citing Love, 2025 WL 1077176, at *4-5, in its list of "well-settled" contrary authority, Reh'g Pet. at 11-12) and rehearing was denied without opinion.

**What this means for Bernecky's brief:** Love should be presented candidly as (a) directly on point on its facts (a CP case; an employer/computer-use condition with no offense-employment nexus; vacated under the exact § 5F1.5 test Bernecky needs); (b) part of a documented, multi-case line (Jenkins I, Ruff, Gorychka, Salazar, Love, Piper, Lombardi) that the Brown panel acknowledged conflicts with its own ruling and declined to reconcile; and (c) not silently overridden by Brown — Brown's holding is narrower (a categorical "no specified occupation" construction) than a holding that would require rejecting Love's reasoning on facts like Bernecky's, where, unlike Brown, there is no condition text purporting to authorize the employer-facing restriction at all.

### 2.3 Independent assessment: how strong is the Love parallel to Bernecky, really?

**Strong, on the core legal question — but not a perfect factual match, and its precedential weight is limited. Both should be stated candidly.**

**Points of genuine strength:**
1. **Same offense family.** Love is a CP receipt case; Bernecky is a CP possession case. Neither is a fraud/deception case. This makes Love a closer factual analog to Bernecky than Brown is on the "what kind of defendant is this" axis.
2. **Squarely on the legal question.** Love applies the exact § 5F1.5(a) two-part test Bernecky needs, to an employer/computer-use restriction, and vacates it for lack of nexus — using language ("nothing in the record to indicate that [defendant] used a work computer to commit his crime, nor is there any evidence that his crime was connected to his occupation") that maps almost word-for-word onto Bernecky's situation: Bernecky did not use any employer's device to commit his offense, and there is no record connection between his CP-possession offense and any occupation, including the contact-lens-manufacturing job now at issue.
3. **Severity of the underlying offense did not save the condition.** Love's CP conduct (300+ images, sadistic material, some distribution) was considerably more aggravated than anything in Bernecky's file appears to be, yet the panel still vacated the condition because aggravation of the *underlying offense* does not supply the required *nexus to employment* — those are different questions. This is an affirmatively useful point: Bernecky does not need to show his record is spotless to prevail on this theory; he needs to show (and can show) there is no employment/work-device nexus, which is a narrower and more winnable showing.

**Points requiring honest qualification:**
1. **Love is a non-precedential summary order.** Under 2d Cir. Local Rule 32.1.1, it may be cited but carries no precedential weight, and it is now in tension with a *published, precedential* decision (Brown) that declined to follow its reasoning's closest cousin (Gorychka). It should be cited as persuasive authority within a documented, acknowledged split — not represented as controlling law.
2. **Love's own condition, like Brown's, contained explicit employment/employer language and an explicit PO-approval gate** ("may use a computer in connection with the employment approved by the probation officer ... provided he notif[ies] [his] employer"). Bernecky's CIMP condition has no such language at all. This is a genuine factual difference from Love (not just from Brown) — and it needs to be turned into an affirmative, not a defensive, point: if even an explicit, court-ordered, employment-specific approval-and-notification clause required (under Love/Jenkins/Ruff) individualized nexus findings that were absent, then Probation's decision to manufacture an equivalent (indeed, more demanding — "like kind and degree" keystroke-parity) requirement with no court order and no condition text behind it at all is at least as unauthorized, and arguably more so, because it lacks even the facial judicial imprimatur Love's and Brown's clauses had.
3. **Different procedural posture.** Love was decided on direct appeal under plain-error review, with the government conceding error. Bernecky's is a pre-supervision § 3583(e)(2) modification motion, not an appeal — a different vehicle. This doesn't weaken Love's substantive holding but means it should be cited for its articulation and application of the § 5F1.5 standard, not for anything about the plain-error standard itself.

**Bottom line:** the parallel is real and useful, but it should be pled as "part of an acknowledged, unresolved split that this court should follow because it is the better-reasoned line, and because Bernecky's facts are further outside Brown's categorical rule than Love's own were" — not as an uncontested, slam-dunk match.

## PART 3 — Corrected Factual Framing: Bernecky's Own Supervision History

This corrects and sharpens the record as directed by the attorney, for direct use in the motion's fact section and in distinguishing Brown's aggravators.

**Precise framing for the motion:**

> Mr. Bernecky's supervision record contains a single violation of supervised release, and it is not evidence of an independent pattern of noncompliance. Mr. Bernecky was on a term of supervised release stemming from a 2012 conviction when he committed the instant offense in 2018. Because the commission of a new federal offense while on an existing term of supervised release is, automatically and as a matter of law, itself a violation of that existing term, the "violation" in Mr. Bernecky's history is coextensive with — the same conduct as — the instant offense now being addressed in this proceeding. It is not a separate, independent instance of misconduct during supervision, and it does not reflect any pattern of repeated violations, evasion of supervisory authority, or deception directed at Probation.
>
> Critically, and in sharp contrast to *Brown* — where the defendant's aggravating record consisted specifically of repeated device-based reoffending, continuation of unlawful conduct while on probation, and a documented, multi-decade history of assumed identities and biographical dishonesty toward Probation — **Mr. Bernecky has never, at any time, been found to have violated any condition of supervision in connection with his employment, a workplace, or any work-related device.** That is the specific axis on which both Brown's aggravating findings and the present CIMP-employer dispute turn, and it is, without qualification, clean in Mr. Bernecky's record.

This framing (1) does not overstate Bernecky's record as spotless (correctly disclosing the single, coextensive SR violation), (2) precisely explains why that single violation is not independently probative of anything beyond the instant offense itself, and (3) draws the sharp, honest contrast with Brown's *specific* aggravator category (device/employment-adjacent deception during supervision) rather than a vague "he has no history" claim that could be challenged as incomplete.

### [DRAFT] Brown Is Adverse Authority That Must Be — and Can Be — Distinguished; Love Confirms Mr. Bernecky's Position

The government may point to *United States v. Brown*, 173 F.4th 45 (2d Cir. 2026), a recent, published Second Circuit decision rejecting a § 5F1.5 challenge to a computer-monitoring condition that reached a defendant's workplace devices. Counsel addresses it directly rather than await its citation in opposition, because it is binding authority and the defense has an obligation of candor to the tribunal. Properly understood, however, Brown does not control this case, for three independent reasons — and a second, equally recent Second Circuit decision, *United States v. Love*, No. 24-1706-cr, 2025 WL 1077176 (2d Cir. Apr. 8, 2025), applying the identical statutory test to materially closer facts, confirms that Mr. Bernecky's position is correct.

**First, Brown's own condition — unlike Mr. Bernecky's — expressly invoked employment and required employer involvement.** The condition Brown challenged provided, in the district court's own words, that "[t]he use of any Device(s) in the course of employment will be subject to monitoring or restriction as permitted by the employer." 173 F.4th at 46. The Second Circuit's holding that this clause was not an "occupational restriction" rested entirely on the observation that, notwithstanding its express employment language, the clause did not "target 'a specified occupation, business, or profession'" — i.e., it applied to any employer, not a named job or industry. Id. at 51. Mr. Bernecky's CIMP condition contains no comparable language at all. The words "employ," "employer," and "employment" do not appear anywhere in the condition the sentencing court imposed. There is, in short, no court-ordered text here for Brown's "not a specified occupation" holding to even apply to. The entire employer dimension of this dispute — the demand that Mr. Bernecky's prospective employer independently demonstrate "like kind and degree" monitoring capability before his employment can proceed — was invented by the probation officer, not ordered by any judge, and has no textual anchor in the Judgment whatsoever. Brown resolved a challenge to a court-ordered condition's own text; it did not resolve, and had no occasion to resolve, whether Probation may unilaterally manufacture a new employer-facing requirement with no textual basis at all. That is an ultra vires and delegation question (addressed above), not a § 5F1.5 question, and Brown does not speak to it.

**Second, Brown was a fraud case built on a documented history of device-based reoffending and dishonesty to Probation; Mr. Bernecky's case, and his record, are of a fundamentally different character.** Brown pleaded guilty to fourteen counts of making false, fictitious, or fraudulent claims and one count of theft of government funds, arising from a multi-year "Stolen Identity Refund Fraud" scheme in which he stole personal information from incarcerated victims — in some instances by impersonating an attorney — and filed fraudulent tax returns in their names. 173 F.4th at 47-48. The panel's conclusion that the monitoring condition "does not impose a greater deprivation of liberty than reasonably necessary" rested explicitly on "Brown's repeated use of electronic devices to engage in unlawful conduct, his continuation of unlawful conduct while on probation for a previous state offense, and his history of lying to Probation" — a record showing Brown had used at least six different names and aliases over multiple decades and gave Probation and the sentencing court "conflicting accounts" of his own identity and background. Id. at 49. Mr. Bernecky's offense is possession of child pornography — a fundamentally different, non-deception-based offense — and, as set forth above, his supervision history contains no comparable pattern. He has one, coextensive violation (the instant offense itself, which necessarily also violated an existing 2012 SR term), no history of repeated device-based reoffending, and — critically, on the precise axis both Brown's aggravators and this dispute turn on — no history of any violation connected to employment, a workplace, or a work-related device. Brown's fact-specific rejection of the overbreadth/liberty-deprivation challenge simply does not transfer to a record this different.

**Third, and affirmatively, *United States v. Love* confirms that the § 5F1.5 test — applied to a defendant convicted of the same offense as Mr. Bernecky, with no employment nexus to his crime — requires vacatur of exactly this kind of condition.** In Love, the Second Circuit vacated a special condition requiring a child-pornography defendant to obtain probation-officer approval and notify his employer before using a computer at work, holding that "there is nothing in the record to indicate that Love used a work computer to commit his crime, nor is there any evidence that his crime was connected to his occupation." 2025 WL 1077176, at *5. That holding applied U.S.S.G. § 5F1.5(a)'s two-part test directly, and relied on the same line of authority — United States v. Jenkins, 854 F.3d 181, 195 (2d Cir. 2017), and United States v. Ruff, 795 F. App'x 6, 8 (2d Cir. 2019) — that the Brown panel, in a footnote-free silence Brown's own counsel unsuccessfully sought en banc review to correct, never addressed. Love is a summary order and does not bind this Court, but it is squarely on point: like Love, Mr. Bernecky has never used any employer's device to commit any offense, and there is no record connection whatsoever between his CP-possession offense and any occupation — including the specific job (at a contact lens manufacturer) now at issue, where Mr. Bernecky told the employer, and the employer accepted, that he would not need to use the employer's shared systems at all. If a defendant with a considerably more aggravated CP record than anything reflected in Mr. Bernecky's file could not sustain an explicit, court-ordered employment-approval-and-notification condition for want of an offense-to-occupation nexus, then Probation's freestanding, court-unauthorized demand here — which lacks even the facial judicial authorization Love's condition had — cannot stand either.

**In sum:** Brown does not foreclose relief here. It resolved a different question (whether an express, court-ordered, employer-referencing clause is itself an occupational restriction when it does not name a specific job or industry) on a different record (a fraud defendant with a documented history of device-based reoffending and identity dishonesty toward Probation). Mr. Bernecky's condition contains no employment language for that holding to reach; his record contains none of Brown's aggravators; and Love — applying the same statutory test the government must satisfy — confirms that where, as here, there is no record connection between the offense and the defendant's occupation or any workplace device, an employment-conditioning restriction cannot stand without the individualized § 5F1.5(a) findings that were never made in this case, on the face of the Judgment or otherwise. The relief Mr. Bernecky seeks is not a ruling that Brown was wrongly decided; it is a ruling that Brown's narrow, textual holding does not reach a condition that never mentions employment at all, and that Probation's ad hoc administration of that silent condition — precisely the situation Love, Jenkins, and Ruff addressed and refused to permit — requires the same result here.
