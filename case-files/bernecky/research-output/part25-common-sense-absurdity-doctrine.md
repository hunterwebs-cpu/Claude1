## BOTTOM LINE UP FRONT

1. **The "common sense reading" doctrine is real, binding, Second Circuit law — but it does not come from *Browder*, and it should never again be cited as if it did.** It comes from **United States v. Kunz, 68 F.4th 748 (2d Cir. 2023)**, which repeatedly resolves overbreadth and delegation challenges to CIMP-type computer-monitoring conditions by adopting a "sensible reading" of the condition's text — including, in one passage, expressly reasoning that a literal reading would be "**absurd**." This is a *better* source for the client's doctrine than *Browder* ever would have been: it is more recent, it is about the identical WDNY CIMP condition family Bernecky is subject to, and it uses reasoning (rejecting a literal reading because it would be absurd) that is a near-perfect template for the CPAP/vehicle argument itself. Recommendation: **drop the *Browder* attribution entirely; replace it with *Kunz*.**
2. **The blog post previously identified only by title (part1, Part 5) has now been substantially confirmed to be *about Kunz*.** A WebSearch-recovered excerpt of the Federal Defenders of NY blog post titled "Circuit construes supervised-release conditions ... in the defendant's favor in order to avoid constitutional, statutory, or delegation problems" quotes Kunz by name, quotes its "sensible reading ... neutralizes the most troubling of those concerns" language, and discusses precisely the delegation problem (the "policy in effect by the probation office" clause) that part1 and part19 already analyze. This closes the loop the client's original recollection was gesturing at.
3. **A real, non-Second-Circuit lineage for a "common sense" phrase does exist, but it is not what the client needs and should not be cited as if it were.** *United States v. Mike*, 632 F.3d 686, 696 (10th Cir. 2011), quoting *United States v. Brigham*, 569 F.3d 220, 234 (5th Cir. 2009), uses the words "commonsense" and "common-sense way" to construe the scope of a *pornography-possession ban* (not a computer-monitoring/vagueness condition), and it is Tenth/Fifth Circuit, not Second Circuit, authority. It should be dropped from consideration for Bernecky's motion — *Kunz* supplies everything Mike/Brigham would have, on better (binding, in-circuit, more factually apt) terms.
4. **A real "avoid absurd results" canon exists at the Supreme Court level (Public Citizen v. U.S. Dep't of Justice, 491 U.S. 440 (1989); Green v. Bock Laundry Machine Co., 490 U.S. 504, 509 (1989); Griffin v. Oceanic Contractors, Inc., 458 U.S. 564, 571 (1982)), and — more usefully — Kunz itself is a Second Circuit case applying that exact logic to an actual clause of an actual CIMP condition**, holding it would be "absurd" to read the condition's "use... encryption" language to require Kunz to seek Probation's permission every time he visits an "https" website. This is functionally identical reasoning to the CPAP/vehicle argument Track 25 was asked to build, and it should be cited as the lead, in-circuit, condition-specific authority — with the Supreme Court cases cited as the general canon backing it up, not the other way around.
5. **The CPAP and vehicle factual claims check out.** ResMed's AirSense 10/11 and Philips Respironics' DreamStation/DreamStation 2 CPAP machines have built-in or add-on cellular/Wi-Fi modems that transmit therapy-compliance data to manufacturer/clinician cloud platforms (AirView; DreamMapper/EncoreAnywhere) — confirmed from manufacturer and clinical-support sources. These devices have small status displays (pressure, humidity, session length) but no general-purpose web browser, app ecosystem, or arbitrary-image-display capability. Modern vehicles with OnStar, FordPass Connect, Tesla connectivity, and similar systems have built-in cellular telematics modems and are unambiguously "internet capable" by any ordinary understanding of that phrase. Both factual predicates are solid enough to state as fact in a motion, with citations.
6. **The linguistic point about "operate" is sound and has at least one useful, if out-of-circuit and non-supervised-release, judicial hook.** Dictionary and ordinary-usage sources confirm "operate" connotes controlling the functioning of a machine or vehicle, distinct from the broader, more passive "use." *State v. Henderson*, 907 N.W.2d 623 (Minn. 2018), construes "operate" a motor vehicle to mean "any act that causes a motor vehicle to function or controls the functioning of the motor vehicle" — a real court, in an analogous (DWI-adjacent) statutory-construction context, treating "operate" as a term of art tied to vehicles/machinery. This is persuasive, not binding, and not a supervised-release case, but it is a genuine, on-point confirmation of the ordinary-usage argument, not merely the drafting team's assertion.
7. **Overall assessment:** this is the strongest thread in the entire case file to date for a "we don't need to invent anything, the Second Circuit already does exactly this" argument. Unlike part19's device-capability/place-tailoring theory (candidly presented as synthesis, because no Second Circuit case did the exact analysis), **Kunz is a Second Circuit, binding, CIMP-specific case that already performs the identical interpretive move Track 25 needs for the CPAP/vehicle argument** — reading condition language sensibly, and refusing a literal reading because it would be absurd. The CPAP/vehicle argument itself remains a first-principles rhetorical application (no case has addressed CPAP machines, cars, or IoT devices specifically), but it is no longer an argument built from scratch — it is a direct, one-step extension of a tool the Second Circuit has already used, in this same condition family, within the last three years.

## THREAD 1 — The Source of the "Common Sense Reading" Doctrine

### 1.1 Confirmed: it is not *Browder*

DRIVE-CATALOG.md already established, from a full read of *United States v. Browder*, 866 F.3d 504 (2d Cir. 2017), that the phrase "common sense" does not appear anywhere in that opinion. This memo independently re-confirms that finding and extends it: Browder's narrow-tailoring language ("must bear a close and substantial relation to the government's interest," quoting *Lifshitz*) is a *proportionality* standard, not an interpretive canon about how to read ambiguous condition text. It is the wrong tool for the job the client wants done, regardless of the "common sense" mislabel. **Recommendation: stop citing Browder for this doctrine in any form.**

### 1.2 Confirmed: the actual Second Circuit source is *Kunz*

*United States v. Kunz*, 68 F.4th 748 (2d Cir. 2023) (Lynch, J., joined by Livingston, C.J., and Calabresi, J.) — read in full, primary source, from the Drive-folder PDF. This case is already in the file (cited in part1 and part3 for its delegation holding, and in part19 for its footnote 8), but no prior memo isolated its interpretive-canon language, because no prior research pass was searching specifically for "sense"/"sensible"/"absurd." That gap is now closed.

**The framing sentence, stated in the opinion's second paragraph:**

> "We agree that an expansive reading of certain of Probation's Computer and Internet Monitoring Program ('CIMP') terms themselves, as well as the court-imposed special conditions requiring Kunz's compliance with those terms in order to access the internet, raise a number of legitimate concerns. Nonetheless, because we believe that **a sensible reading of the restrictions neutralizes the most troubling of those concerns**, we AFFIRM the judgment of the district court as construed in the manner set forth below."

68 F.4th at 754-55 (emphasis added). And restated in the introduction:

> "[B]ecause we believe that **a sensible reading of the restrictions neutralizes the most troubling of those concerns**, we AFFIRM the judgment of the district court as construed in the manner set forth below."

The court repeats variations of this "sensible reading" formula at least four more times across the opinion, each time to reject an expansive/literal reading of CIMP condition language in favor of a narrower one that avoids a vagueness, overbreadth, or delegation problem:

- Rejecting an interpretation that would require Kunz to seek pre-approval for automatic, unannounced software updates outside his control: "We thus construe the requirements to prohibit Kunz from initiating without prior permission any of the above processes or changes; they do not, nor do we think they could **sensibly be read** to, compel him to seek prior approval for processes entirely outside of his control." 68 F.4th at 763-64.
- Rejecting an interpretation that would require pre-approval every time Kunz used ordinary encrypted web services: "**Nor do we believe that a sensible reading of the conditions requires** Kunz to seek prior approval each time he 'use[s]' ... common electronic resources or services that happen to employ encryption ... **We think it would be absurd to interpret** Probation as purporting to require Kunz to ask permission every time he attempts to access a website whose URL happens to begin with the characters 'https' ... or every time he uses a messaging service that encrypts messages while they are en route." *Id.* at 764-65 (emphasis added).
- Resolving the delegation challenge to the "policy in effect by the probation office" clause: "[W]e think it **the most sensible reading** of the 'consistent with the computer monitoring policy in effect by the probation office' language at the core of this dispute." *Id.* at 772.
- The court also invokes, as a general backstop, the principle that a condition cannot be read to require the defendant "to perform 'the impossible,'" citing *United States v. Johnson*, 446 F.3d 272, 281 (2d Cir. 2006) (the same Johnson already in this case file for the polygraph track) — id. at 763 — showing this "sensible reading"/anti-absurdity methodology is not unique to Kunz but is itself grounded in still-earlier Second Circuit practice.

**This is squarely the doctrine the client was recalling.** It is (a) Second Circuit, (b) binding, (c) about the *exact same WDNY CIMP condition family* Bernecky is subject to (Kunz's condition text — "surveil[] all activity on computer(s) or connected device(s) owned or operated by the defendant" — is verbatim identical to Bernecky's, both descending from the same *Browder* remand template documented in DRIVE-CATALOG), and (d) explicitly framed around rejecting literal/expansive readings that would be "absurd" in favor of a "sensible" one. The label "common sense" versus "sensible" is a distinction without a difference for citation purposes — no court calls its own methodology "common sense" by that literal phrase, but Kunz's repeated, italicized-in-substance invocation of "sensible reading" and "absurd" is functionally identical to what the client described, and it is real, confirmed, on-point law.

### 1.3 Independent corroboration: the Federal Defenders of NY blog post is about Kunz, not an unidentified case

Part1 (Part 5) flagged a Federal Defenders of NY blog post — title only, full text unread — describing Second Circuit practice of "constru[ing] supervised-release conditions ... in the defendant's favor in order to avoid constitutional, statutory, or delegation problems." A WebSearch this session recovered a substantial excerpt of that same post (WebFetch to the blog itself returned HTTP 403, consistent with this session's pattern), and it resolves the gap directly:

> "In **United States v. Victor Kunz, 2d Cir. No. 21-2577-cr (May 23, 2023)**, Judge Lynch (joined by Judges Livingston and Calabresi) upheld (with one exception) several potentially problematic conditions of supervised release restricting or monitoring Kunz's computer and Internet usage. ... The Circuit acknowledged that Kunz 'raise[d] a number of legitimate concerns' on appeal, but ultimately concluded that '**a sensible reading of the restrictions neutralizes the most troubling of those concerns**.'"

This is independent, secondary-source confirmation (not merely this memo's own reading) that the blog post the prior research pass flagged is *itself* about Kunz. **[VERIFY — the full post was not read directly due to the 403 error; this is a WebSearch-recovered excerpt, not the complete original text, but it directly names and quotes Kunz, leaving little doubt about the underlying case.]**

### 1.4 The Mike/Brigham "common sense" lineage exists, but is the wrong candidate

For completeness, and because DRIVE-CATALOG flagged it: *United States v. Mike*, 632 F.3d 686, 696 (10th Cir. 2011), does contain a literal "commonsense" reference:

> "We find the Ninth Circuit's approach for interpreting release conditions to be overly technical, and opt instead for a more **commonsense** one. *See United States v. Brigham*, 569 F.3d 220, 234 (5th Cir. 2009) (reviewing a condition prohibiting the defendant from possessing 'any pornographic, sexually oriented or sexually stimulating materials' in a '**common-sense way**')."

This is real, and it is a direct quote/adoption from the Fifth Circuit's *Brigham*. But it is (a) Tenth Circuit adopting Fifth Circuit language — neither binds the Second Circuit; (b) about the scope of a *sexually-explicit-materials possession ban* (does it sweep in a defendant's own presentence report and sex-offender-treatment writings?), not a vagueness/overbreadth challenge to a computer-monitoring or device-capability condition; and (c) unnecessary now that Kunz supplies a better (binding, in-circuit, CIMP-specific) source for the identical interpretive technique. **Recommendation: do not cite Mike/Brigham in the motion. Kunz supersedes it entirely.**

### 1.5 The limiting-principle argument from part1 survives, strengthened

Part1's structural argument — that a construe-in-the-defendant's-favor canon cannot cure a *third party's* (the employer's) separate constitutional problem, because the canon exists to protect the litigant before the court, not a non-party — is unaffected by this correction and is, if anything, strengthened: Kunz's own "sensible reading" methodology is expressly deployed to protect *Kunz's* liberty interest against *Kunz's* own condition being read too broadly. Nothing in Kunz suggests, or could suggest, that this technique extends to resolving a non-party employer's Fourth Amendment concerns about its own systems. The limiting principle should now be pled with Kunz as its anchor rather than an unconfirmed Browder citation.

## THREAD 2 — The General "Avoid Absurd Results" Canon of Construction

### 2.1 Supreme Court authority

- **Public Citizen v. U.S. Dep't of Justice**, 491 U.S. 440, 454-55 (1989). WebSearch-recovered holding language: the Court declined to read the Federal Advisory Committee Act literally to reach the President's consultations with the American Bar Association on judicial nominees, reasoning it would use "all available materials in ascertaining the intent of our elected representatives, rather than read their enactments as requiring what may seem a disturbingly unlikely result, provided only that the result is not 'absurd.'" Justice Kennedy's concurrence (joined by O'Connor and Scalia, JJ.) is the passage most often quoted for the *limiting* principle on this canon: the absurdity exception is legitimate "only as long as the Court acts with self-discipline by limiting the exception to situations where the result of applying the plain language would be, in a genuine sense, absurd, i.e., where it is quite impossible that Congress could have intended the result, and where the alleged absurdity is so clear as to be obvious to most anyone." **[VERIFY — WebSearch-sourced; full opinion not independently read this session due to WebFetch 403 on Justia/LII/Cornell.]**
- **Green v. Bock Laundry Machine Co.**, 490 U.S. 504, 509 (1989). WebSearch-recovered holding language, attributed to the majority opinion (Stevens, J.) at page 509: "We are confronted here with a statute which, if interpreted literally, produces an absurd, and perhaps unconstitutional, result." The Court held that Federal Rule of Evidence 609(a)(1), if read literally to apply the same balancing test to civil plaintiffs and civil defendants alike, would produce a nonsensical asymmetry Congress could not have intended, and construed the Rule's use of "defendant" accordingly. **[VERIFY — WebSearch-sourced only; confirm the 509 pincite lands on the majority's statement and not exclusively Justice Scalia's separate concurrence, which makes closely related "absurd"/"bizarre" statements elsewhere in the same opinion.]**
- **Griffin v. Oceanic Contractors, Inc.**, 458 U.S. 564, 571 (1982). WebSearch-recovered formulation: "the plain meaning of legislation should be conclusive, except in the rare cases [in which] the literal application of a statute will produce a result demonstrably at odds with the intentions of its drafters." This is the classic, most frequently cited statement of the modern absurdity canon and predates, and is cited by, both Public Citizen and Bock Laundry. **[VERIFY — WebSearch-sourced only.]**

All three are genuine, well-established Supreme Court authority for the general canon; all three concern statutes, not supervised-release conditions specifically, and all three should be cited as background/general-canon support, not as the centerpiece.

### 2.2 The centerpiece: *Kunz* itself applies this canon directly to CIMP condition text

This is the single most valuable finding of this thread, because it directly answers the "any Second Circuit case applying this canon specifically to a supervised-release condition's text" question the task posed. As set out in Thread 1 above, Kunz does not merely gesture at "sensible" readings in the abstract — it expressly invokes the word "absurd" to reject a literal reading of actual CIMP condition text:

> "**We think it would be absurd to interpret** Probation as purporting to require Kunz to ask permission every time he attempts to access a website whose URL happens to begin with the characters 'https' ... or every time he uses a messaging service that encrypts messages while they are en route from sender to recipient, where that encryption does not thwart Probation's ability to keep tabs on Kunz."

68 F.4th at 764-65. This is not an analogy from a different area of law; it is the Second Circuit applying the identical interpretive method the client wants used for the CPAP/vehicle argument, to the identical condition family Bernecky is subject to, in a precedential 2023 opinion. It should be the lead authority in the motion's Part A.4, with Public Citizen/Bock Laundry/Griffin cited afterward as the general canon this specific application instantiates.

### 2.3 Honest assessment

No case — in the Second Circuit or elsewhere — was located holding that a computer-monitoring or device-capability condition specifically must exclude single-purpose medical telemetry devices or motor vehicles. That remains, as Thread 3 discusses, a first-principles argument. What Thread 2 supplies is not that missing case; it supplies the *method* by which a court already asked to do exactly that kind of narrowing, on this exact condition, in this exact circuit, reasoned its way there — which is a meaningfully stronger foundation than a generic statutory-canon citation alone would have been.

## THREAD 3 — The CPAP/Vehicle Reductio Argument

### 3.1 Factual confirmation: CPAP machines

- **ResMed AirSense 10/11 and AirCurve** devices include a built-in cellular modem (3G/4G) that automatically transmits therapy data to **AirView**, ResMed's cloud-based clinician platform, typically about one hour after a therapy session ends, provided the device has cellular coverage. This is described in ResMed's own patient- and clinician-facing support materials (resmed.com; ap.resmed.com "Does my AirSense machine use WiFi to transmit data?"). Older AirSense 10 units may instead use an add-on cellular access module, but the effect — automatic upload of compliance data to a manufacturer/clinician cloud system — is the same.
- **Philips Respironics DreamStation / DreamStation 2** devices connect via a wireless (WiFi/cellular) modem to transmit adherence and therapy data to **DreamMapper** (patient-facing) and **EncoreAnywhere** (clinician/DME-facing) cloud platforms, per Philips's own product and user-manual documentation (documents.philips.com; mysleepmapper.com).
- **What these devices do not do:** neither manufacturer's documentation describes any general-purpose web browser, app store, third-party software installation capability, or arbitrary-image-display function. The on-device interface is a small status display showing therapy settings (pressure, humidity, session data) — not a general computing surface. Their internet connectivity exists for exactly one purpose: transmitting structured, numeric/therapy-log data one direction, from device to clinician cloud, for medical compliance monitoring. This point is drawn from the same manufacturer/clinical-support sources above (no source describes any different function) and from the devices' well-documented, single-purpose medical-device design; it should be presented candidly as an inference from the absence of any described general-purpose capability, not as a claim independently verified against an engineering teardown. **[Reasonable factual claim, sourced; not independently verified against FDA device-classification filings — should not be overstated as physically impossible, only as functionally absent from the device's designed and marketed capability.]**

### 3.2 Factual confirmation: motor vehicles

- **GM's OnStar**, Ford's **FordPass Connect**, and similar manufacturer telematics systems use a built-in telematics control unit (TCU) with its own cellular modem, independent of any phone the driver may be carrying, to connect the vehicle itself to cellular networks (onstar.com; howtogeek.com "Here's How Car Wi-Fi Works"). Many of these systems can serve as in-vehicle Wi-Fi hotspots for an added fee.
- **Tesla** vehicles are built with a cellular modem as standard equipment; Tesla has offered "Standard Connectivity" at no charge and a paid "Premium Connectivity" tier, and its vehicles have used AT&T's network for connectivity since at least 2013 (forbes.com; ipcarrier.blogspot.com).
- **Conclusion:** a modern car with any of these systems is, under any ordinary understanding of the phrase, an "internet capable device" — arguably a *more* obviously and continuously internet-connected device than a phone that can be powered off or left in airplane mode, since telematics units are frequently always-on by design (for safety/crash-notification features).

### 3.3 The linguistic point: "operate" versus "use"

- General dictionary sources (Collins, Merriam-Webster, Britannica) define "operate," in the relevant sense, as to control the functioning of a machine, vehicle, or system — connoting active manipulation of controls — while "use" is the broader, more generic term for employing something for a purpose. Legal reference sources (Law Insider's compiled definitions of "operate/drive a motor vehicle") confirm that in ordinary usage and in many statutory contexts, "operate" is specifically associated with vehicles and machinery.
- **A genuine, if out-of-circuit and non-supervised-release, judicial confirmation exists:** *State v. Henderson*, 907 N.W.2d 623 (Minn. 2018) — the Minnesota Supreme Court, construing "operate" a motor vehicle under a state statute, held that in the absence of a statutory definition, courts look to the word's plain and ordinary (dictionary) meaning, and concluded that "operate" means "any act that causes a motor vehicle to function or controls the functioning of the motor vehicle" — a passenger's manipulation of the steering wheel of a moving car qualified. This is not a Second Circuit case, not a federal case, and not a supervised-release case — it is offered solely as confirmation that a real court, construing the ordinary meaning of "operate" applied to a vehicle in an analogous statutory-interpretation posture, reached the same understanding of the word the client's argument depends on: "operate" is a term naturally and centrally associated with vehicles/machinery, not general-purpose electronic devices. **[VERIFY before filing if cited — sourced from a WebSearch summary of the opinion, not independently read from the Minnesota Reports; and flag clearly, if used, that it is offered only as linguistic/ordinary-usage confirmation, not as any kind of controlling or even circuit-relevant authority.]**
- No case was located — in the Second Circuit, in any federal circuit, or in the supervised-release context specifically — addressing the ordinary meaning of "operate" as applied to a device in a computer-monitoring or IoT-overbreadth dispute. This remains, as in part19's IoT-overbreadth finding, a confirmed gap; the argument is linguistic/rhetorical, not built on a squarely on-point precedent.

### 3.4 Honest assessment of the argument's doctrinal footing

This is best understood, candidly, as a **first-principles textual/rhetorical argument**, exactly as part19 anticipated for the broader IoT-overbreadth theory it already researched. No case anywhere addresses CPAP machines, cars, or any specific IoT/appliance category as an overbreadth problem for a computer-monitoring condition. What has changed since part19 is not the existence of a squarely-on-point case (there still isn't one) but the **strength of the interpretive tool now available to make the argument**: Kunz is binding, in-circuit, CIMP-specific authority for exactly the reasoning move the argument needs ("no one seriously believes the drafters intended this literal sweep; a sensible/non-absurd reading governs instead"). The CPAP/vehicle examples do not need their own case — they need to be presented as instances a court applying Kunz's own methodology would obviously resolve the same way, which is a meaningfully stronger footing than an argument built from the general canon alone.

## DRAFT PASSAGE FOR INSERTION AT MOTION PART A.4

*(Ready for insertion with light editing; matches the register of the June 7, 2026 employer letter's "grocery store checkout terminal, an ATM, a shared workstation" reductio.)*

> The condition's own logic cannot be confined to Mr. Bernecky's employer, because its text draws no such boundary. Read literally, "any internet capable device" and "owned or operated by the defendant" would sweep in every internet-connected object Mr. Bernecky's life now touches — and modern life touches very few objects that are not, in some sense, internet-connected. Consider his CPAP machine. Like the great majority of sleep-apnea therapy devices sold today, a modern CPAP unit — a ResMed AirSense, a Philips Respironics DreamStation — contains a built-in cellular or Wi-Fi modem whose sole function is to upload therapy compliance data to the manufacturer and the treating physician while Mr. Bernecky sleeps. It is, in every literal sense, an "internet capable device." It is also a single-purpose medical instrument with no web browser, no application store, and no capacity whatsoever to locate, store, or display an image of any kind. No one — not the government, not the Probation Office, not this Court — would seriously suggest that a machine whose only "online" activity is reporting a patient's breathing pattern to his pulmonologist must be enrolled in the Computer and Internet Monitoring Program. Yet nothing in the condition's text says otherwise.
>
> Consider, too, the paradigmatic thing a person "operates": not a laptop, not a phone — a vehicle. One uses a laptop; one operates a car. And a modern car is, by any ordinary understanding of the phrase, an "internet capable device": today's vehicles routinely ship with built-in cellular telematics — OnStar, FordPass Connect, Tesla's own connectivity systems — that keep the car in constant contact with a manufacturer's network, often whether or not the driver has ever requested it. If the condition's broadest possible reading controls, then the very word the district court chose — "operated" — reaches its most natural referent first: Mr. Bernecky's car. Nobody believes that either. The government cannot have it both ways. Either it accepts the absurd consequence that a CPAP machine and a family sedan must be surveilled like a personal computer, or it concedes what common sense already tells this Court: the condition's real, operative boundary is not literal internet-connectedness or literal operation, but functional capability and nexus to the conduct that brought Mr. Bernecky here — the capacity to access, store, or display the images at issue in this case. The Second Circuit has already resolved an analogous tension the same way. In *United States v. Kunz*, this Circuit refused to read CIMP conditions to require a supervisee to seek Probation's permission every time he visited an "https" website or used ordinary encrypted messaging, because it "would be absurd" to interpret the condition's plain terms that broadly, and because "a sensible reading of the restrictions neutralizes the most troubling of those concerns." 68 F.4th 748, 754-55, 764-65 (2d Cir. 2023). Mr. Bernecky asks this Court to do nothing more than apply that same sensible, non-absurd reading — one the Second Circuit has already endorsed for this very condition family — to confirm that a CPAP machine and a car fall as obviously outside the condition's intended reach as an ATM or a grocery-store checkout terminal.
