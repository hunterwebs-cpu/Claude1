## 1. Bottom line (read this first)

- **Betts exists, is correctly cited, and is squarely on point.** *United States v. Betts*, 886 F.3d 198 (2d Cir. 2018), is a published, precedential Second Circuit opinion that (a) holds a sentencing court must make an "individualized assessment" and state its reasons before imposing a special condition, with plain-error consequences for failure, and (b) applies that rule to **vacate a district court's blanket ban on all alcohol consumption** specifically because neither the underlying offense nor the violation conduct involved alcohol and no evidence of alcohol abuse was in the record. The prior AI-generated memo's characterization of Betts checks out on independent verification.
- **Browder does not, on its own holding, establish a physician-gating rule.** Its "program rule" holding runs the *opposite direction* from what would help most directly: Browder holds that a treatment provider's rule cannot validly expand *beyond* what the court itself ordered. It does not hold that the court's own order must be individualized on medical/physician grounds — that principle comes from Betts, not Browder. Browder is still useful, but for a narrower, more defensive purpose than "central precedent" status would suggest, and the attorney directing this motion should recalibrate expectations accordingly (see Section 5).
- **The strongest, most concrete argument is not "physician-gating is affirmatively required by case law" (no case says that) — it is the more modest, better-supported argument that the categorical, undifferentiated form of Bernecky's alcohol clause is vulnerable under Betts/§5D1.3(b) individualized-assessment doctrine**, reinforced by a textual point in Bernecky's own condition (the disjunctive "treatment **or** psychotropic medication" — discussed in Section 6) that exposes the clause to sweeping in situations (talk-therapy alone, non-interacting medications) with no plausible alcohol-related rationale at all.
- **Physician-gating as an affirmative substitute rule is not supported by any case law found.** No court decision was located that holds, or even discusses, a condition contingent on an actual treating physician's contraindication finding as opposed to a categorical judicial rule. This part of the argument must be built by analogy (existing AO/probation practice already defers medication management to the prescribing physician) rather than by direct authority. This is a real gap, and the memo says so candidly in Section 3 and Section 8.
- **Overall assessment: moderate-to-strong for narrowing/vacatur-and-remand relief under Betts; weak-to-moderate for the specific physician-gating remedy the client wants as an affirmative rule.** See Section 8 for the full candid assessment.

## 3. Case-by-case analysis

### 3.1 United States v. Betts, 886 F.3d 198 (2d Cir. 2018)

**Citation:** 886 F.3d 198 (2d Cir. 2018); relevant pincites 886 F.3d at 201–202 (individualized-assessment/self-evident-in-the-record standard) and the alcohol-specific discussion in the same range.
**Binding status:** Binding, published Second Circuit precedent (confirmed as published/precedential — not a summary order — via multiple independent citing sources describing it as such and citing it as binding authority in later published Second Circuit decisions).
**Confidence level:** High. Confirmed across FindLaw's case summary, multiple independent secondary discussions (a U.S. Courts primer reference, a Second Circuit case-law update slideshow, and Federal Defenders of New York blog citations to *Betts* as binding precedent in unrelated later cases), all converging on the same citation, defendant name, procedural history, and holding without material contradiction.

**Procedural posture:** Defendant Jeffrey Betts's underlying conviction was for conspiracy to commit bank fraud, 18 U.S.C. § 1349. He was originally sentenced to 24 months' imprisonment plus 5 years' supervised release; supervision began August 2, 2013. He committed multiple violations of supervised release — two arrests for driving without a license and failure to make restitution payments (10% of gross monthly income, as required). On December 7, 2016, the district court sentenced him on the violations to 10 months' imprisonment and a new 4-year term of supervised release, which included (among other conditions) a **total ban on all alcohol consumption** and a substance-abuse-testing requirement.

**Holding (verbatim, as triangulated across sources):**
- General rule: "A district court is required to make an individualized assessment when determining whether to impose a special condition of supervised release and to state on the record the reason for imposing it[;] the failure to do so is plain error." In the absence of such an explanation, the appellate court may uphold the condition only if the district court's reasoning is **"self-evident in the record."**
- Applying that rule to the alcohol condition specifically: the court held that "the district court's imposition of a total ban of the consumption of alcohol was not reasonably related to the nature and circumstances of defendant's offense." Reasoning, as reconstructed consistently across sources: **neither Betts's underlying offense (bank fraud) nor any of the conduct underlying his violations (driving without a license, failure to pay restitution) involved alcohol in any way; the district court was not presented with any evidence that Betts had ever seriously abused alcohol; and the district court gave no reason for the condition beyond an expressed general displeasure with Betts's performance on supervision.** On that record, the alcohol condition was **not reasonably related to any of the § 5D1.3(b) factors** and the court **vacated it**, remanding for the district court to determine, on an individualized basis, whether any alcohol-related condition was actually necessary.
- **By contrast, the drug-testing condition was affirmed** — described as an appropriate "zero tolerance" condition requiring periodic testing for use of controlled substances without a prescription. This is an important internal contrast within the same opinion: the court did not treat all substance-related conditions identically; it distinguished a **blanket abstinence/consumption ban** (vacated, no nexus) from a **testing regime tied to already-unlawful conduct** (affirmed).
- The additional 4-year term of supervised release itself was held not substantively unreasonable (unrelated to the alcohol issue).

**Application to Bernecky:** This is the single most useful case available. Bernecky's situation is materially similar to (indeed, arguably stronger than) Betts's: there is no alcohol-related offense conduct, no record of alcohol abuse, and (so far as the currently-available record shows) no stated individualized reason for the alcohol clause — the SOR that might supply such a reason has not been located. Betts supports an argument that, at minimum, the categorical alcohol-abstinence clause requires an on-the-record, individualized justification tied to Bernecky's own history/characteristics or the offense, and that absent such a justification (and absent it being "self-evident" from an undeveloped record), the clause is vulnerable to vacatur or must be narrowed on remand-equivalent relief under § 3583(e)(2).

**Important limiting point for candor:** Betts does not itself require or endorse "physician-gating" as the remedy. Its remedy was **vacatur and remand for the district court to reconsider on an adequate record** — not substitution of a physician's future clinical judgment for the court's own. A court following Betts could just as easily hold a resentencing-type hearing, find an individualized basis (e.g., citing Bernecky's own SSRI/psychotropic prescription history or any documented substance-interaction risk), and reimpose the identical categorical clause with reasons stated. Betts closes the "no reasons at all" gap; it does not, by itself, force the physician-contingent structure the client wants.

**Citation:** No. 22-1820-cr (2d Cir. Dec. 7, 2023) (summary order); panel reported as Kearse, Calabresi, and Nathan, JJ.
**Binding status:** Non-precedential summary order (Second Circuit Local Rule 32.1.1) — **may be cited for its persuasive reasoning but has no binding/precedential effect**, and per that same local rule must be cited as a summary order if used in any filing.

**Reported holding:** The Second Circuit vacated two drug- and alcohol-related special conditions on plain-error review. As orally pronounced, the condition required a drug-treatment *evaluation*, with outpatient treatment only "if deemed necessary" — an evaluation-gated, conditional structure. The written judgment, however, imposed an *unconditional* drug-treatment requirement (dropping the evaluation gate), which the court vacated as inconsistent with the oral pronouncement (oral pronouncement controls). **Separately, the district court had imposed a condition that the defendant abstain from alcohol "during" the treatment** — structurally very close to Bernecky's "while in treatment" language — **and the Circuit rejected the government's argument that this alcohol provision could simply be reimposed, on the ground that the district court had not made an "individualized assessment" of the need for that condition.**

**Application to Bernecky:** This is the most structurally similar case found — an alcohol-abstinence clause keyed to "during treatment," vacated for lack of individualized assessment under the Betts standard. It directly supports the argument that a "while in treatment... abstain from alcohol" formulation, standing alone, is not self-justifying — the Second Circuit has already required more than that phrase alone to support the restriction. As a summary order, its citation weight is persuasive only, but it shows **Betts's rule being actively applied to conditions with the same textual triggering structure Bernecky's condition uses.**

### 3.6 U.S.S.G. § 5D1.3(b) and (d) — Guidelines framework (verified via the text quoted directly inside the Browder opinion, and cross-checked against U.S. Courts guidance pages)

**§ 5D1.3(b)** (quoted verbatim as reproduced in *Browder*, 866 F.3d at 510 n.24, itself quoting the Guideline): a court "may impose other conditions of supervised release to the extent that such conditions (1) are reasonably related to (A) the nature and circumstances of the offense and the history and characteristics of the defendant; (B) the need for the sentence imposed to afford adequate deterrence to criminal conduct; (C) the need to protect the public from further crimes of the defendant; and (D) the need to provide the defendant with needed educational or vocational training, medical care, or other correctional treatment in the most effective manner; and (2) involve no greater deprivation of liberty than is reasonably necessary for the purposes set forth above and are consistent with any pertinent policy statements issued by the Sentencing Commission."

**§ 5D1.3(d) commentary** (substance-abuse-specific condition, as consistently reported across U.S. Courts guidance summaries): where "the court has reason to believe that the defendant is an abuser of narcotics, other controlled substances, or alcohol," a condition requiring participation in an approved substance-abuse program (potentially including testing) is warranted. **This is itself an evidentiary-predicate ("reason to believe... abuser") structure, not an automatic, status-based trigger.** The Guidelines' own drafting choice — gating the substance-abuse-program condition on a factual "reason to believe" finding — supports the broader argument that a *categorical* abstinence rule untethered to any such finding sits uneasily with the Guidelines' own structure, even though the commentary is phrased in terms of a factual/behavioral predicate (evidence of abuse) rather than a *medical/physician* predicate (a treating physician's contraindication finding). This is an important nuance: **§ 5D1.3(d)'s gate is evidentiary/behavioral, not medical** — it is the closest Guidelines analog available, but it is not the same thing as the physician-gating structure the client wants, and the motion should not overstate the fit.

## 4. § 3583(d) standards applied to alcohol conditions — synthesis

18 U.S.C. § 3583(d) cross-references, "by means of intricate cross-referencing" (as the Second Circuit itself put it in *Browder*, 866 F.3d at 510 n.24, citing *United States v. Malenya*, 736 F.3d 554, 559 (D.C. Cir. 2013)), essentially the same two-part reasonably-related / no-greater-deprivation-than-necessary test as § 5D1.3(b). Betts, Rodriguez, and Siegel all apply this same two-part test specifically to alcohol conditions and all found deficiencies — either no nexus (Betts, Rodriguez) or impermissible vagueness (Siegel). No case was found holding that a categorical alcohol-abstinence condition survives this test **without** some individualized nexus finding (whether to the offense, to a documented abuse history, or to a specific, on-the-record medical/treatment rationale). That absence-of-contrary-authority is itself useful: the research did not turn up a single Second Circuit (or persuasive out-of-circuit) case affirmatively upholding a truly nexus-free, blanket alcohol-abstinence clause against a properly preserved challenge.

## 5. DEDICATED SECTION — The Browder Analysis (Task 5)

This section directly analyzes both Browder opinions — *United States v. Browder*, 866 F.3d 504 (2d Cir. 2017), and the W.D.N.Y. remand decision, *United States v. Browder*, No. 10-CR-263 (W.D.N.Y. Mar. 14, 2018) (Vilardo, J.) — which were read in full from the Drive folder, as specifically requested.

### 5.1 What Browder actually held

Browder was convicted of possessing child pornography and, after release, was found to have violated two special conditions: (1) the CIMP/computer-monitoring condition, and (2) a mental-health/sex-offender-treatment condition. The Second Circuit's disposition was **split**:

- **Computer monitoring violation: AFFIRMED.** The court held the monitoring, "as construed for purposes of this appeal and under our deferential review, is reasonable," 866 F.3d at 505 — reasonably related to the offense (possession/sharing of 462+ images) and "reasonably necessary" under the *Lifshitz* narrow-tailoring standard, particularly because the third-party monitor (Remote.com) reported only "contraband" hits to the Probation Office and did not surface irrelevant material. 866 F.3d at 511–512. The court nonetheless directed the district court to "review the computer monitoring condition ... and re-state the terms ... with greater specificity," 866 F.3d at 513, because the original 2011 condition had improperly deferred its own content to a later, undefined Probation Office policy.

- **Treatment-condition violation: REVERSED.** Browder had refused to sign Mid-Erie's "Computer/Internet Monitoring Program Participant Agreement" and treatment-provider contract because that contract's own "General Conditions" (fifteen of them, expressly labeled "conditions" within the agreement, 866 F.3d at 513 n.38) required him to obtain permission from **both** the Probation Office **and** "the treatment team" before any contact with anyone under 17 — even his own daughters — whereas the district court's *actual* sentencing condition expressly *excluded* Browder's own children from the no-minors-contact restriction. The Second Circuit held: **"the treatment agreement would have subjected him to a more punitive condition governing contact with minors than did the condition to which Browder was actually sentenced by the District Court,"** 866 F.3d at 513, and that Browder "was therefore well within his rights to object to that term of the agreement." Critically:

> **"The key question is not whether permission, if sought, would be granted; it is whether Browder can be required, by the Probation Office or its designee, to ask in the first place. Because such a requirement as to Browder's children would contradict, rather than execute, Browder's sentence, the Probation Office was plainly without power to impose it, as Browder correctly argued."** 866 F.3d at 514.

This is grounded in the delegation principle articulated in *United States v. Matta*, 777 F.3d 116, 122 (2d Cir. 2015), quoted in Browder's own footnote 20: **"The power to impose special conditions of supervised release ... is vested exclusively in the district court,"** and while "a district court may delegate to a probation officer decisionmaking authority over certain minor details of supervised release — for example, the selection of a therapy provider or treatment schedule," it "may not delegate to the Probation Department decisionmaking authority which would make a defendant's liberty itself contingent on a probation officer's exercise of discretion." 866 F.3d at 509–510 n.20.

On remand, Judge Vilardo's March 14, 2018 order implemented this directly: **"Browder does not need permission to have contact with his own children, and he need not sign any treatment agreement requiring him to obtain such permission. Therefore, while the condition that Browder participate in mental health treatment remains, such treatment may not be contingent on his signing any agreement that forces him to obtain permission to have contact with his own children."** The remand order also, notably, **re-states Bernecky's own eventual condition template almost verbatim** — the W.D.N.Y. remand order's re-imposed conditions include, word for word, the same mental-health-treatment condition language later used in Bernecky's 2018 Judgment, including: *"The defendant is to participate in a mental health treatment program, including a mental health evaluation and any treatment recommended. The probation officer will supervise the details of any testing and treatment, including the selection of a provider and schedule. If inpatient treatment is recommended, however, it must be approved by the Court unless the defendant consents. The defendant is not to leave such treatment until completion or as ordered by the Court. **While in treatment or taking psychotropic medication, the defendant shall abstain from the use of alcohol.** The defendant is required to contribute to the cost of services rendered."* This is not coincidence — this is evidently the W.D.N.Y. standard-form mental-health-treatment condition, and Bernecky's Judgment (entered three months after this remand order, June 12, 2018) appears to simply adopt the district's own template as revised in Browder's remand. That means Bernecky's own condition (h) is, quite literally, the language the W.D.N.Y. produced in direct response to the Second Circuit's Browder mandate — a fact the motion should probably note explicitly, since it shows the alcohol clause was not the product of any Bernecky-specific individualized finding at all, but was carried over from a district-wide template.

### 5.2 Does Browder say anything about the scope of a legitimate "program rule" versus a categorical restriction requiring an individualized/medical predicate?

**Directly: no, not in those terms.** Browder's holding is about **delegation and conflict**, not about **individualized/medical predicates for the court's own substantive conditions**. The doctrinal test Browder actually applies is: does the program/provider rule **execute** the court's actual order, or does it **contradict/exceed** it? A rule that merely fills in **administrative details** the court's own condition already commits to the provider/PO (e.g., "the probation officer will supervise the details ... including the selection of a provider and schedule" — language that appears in both Browder's remand template and in Bernecky's own condition (h)) is a legitimate, enforceable "program rule." A rule that **adds a new substantive restriction the court itself never ordered** (Mid-Erie's expansion of the no-minors-contact rule to include Browder's own children) is not enforceable as a violation trigger, because "the Probation Office was plainly without power to impose it."

This is a **materially different question** from whether the court's *own*, judicially-imposed condition (not a provider's supplemental rule) must itself rest on an individualized/medical predicate. That latter question is answered by Betts and § 5D1.3(b)'s individualized-assessment doctrine, not by Browder.

**Where Browder's reasoning does bear on Bernecky's argument (two distinct, more modest uses):**

1. **Defensive/prospective use.** If a treatment provider selected under Bernecky's condition (h) — exercising the "selection of a provider and schedule" delegation the condition already permits — were to adopt its own house rule extending the alcohol restriction beyond the condition's own textual limits (e.g., a provider rule demanding permanent abstinence, or abstinence even when Bernecky is neither actively in a treatment session nor taking any medication that day), Browder's contradicts-rather-than-executes framework would support refusing to treat noncompliance with that *provider-added* expansion as a violation, because the provider "was plainly without power to impose it" beyond what the court itself ordered. This is a real, usable argument, but it is **prospective and defensive** — it protects against future overreach in *implementation*, it does not attack the *court's own textual scope* of the condition as written.

2. **General institutional-competence point.** Browder's insistence that "the power to impose special conditions of supervised release ... is vested exclusively in the district court" (quoting Matta) reinforces, at a high level of generality, that restrictions on a supervisee's substantive liberty must trace back to an actual judicial determination rather than administrative or programmatic default. This supports the general spirit of "a court, not an administrative actor, must make the call" — but note that in Bernecky's case, it *is* the court (via its Judgment) that imposed the categorical alcohol rule, not the Probation Office or a provider. So Browder's institutional-competence point, if anything, cuts in the *opposite* direction from where the client wants to take it: Browder polices unauthorized delegation *away* from the court, whereas Bernecky's ask is to have the court delegate the abstinence determination *to* a physician. Browder does not address whether that kind of delegation (to a treating physician, on a discrete clinical question) is permissible; it addresses delegation to Probation/providers over supervision *administration*, which is a different category of decision-maker and a different kind of question.

### 5.3 Does Browder's deference to program requirements cut for or against the physician-gating argument?

**Mixed, and on balance more against a "physician-gating is compelled by Browder" framing than for it — but genuinely useful as a narrower, defensive fallback.**

- **Cuts against:** Browder affirmed the computer-monitoring condition under highly deferential review, crediting the Probation Office's *administrative implementation* choices (use of a third-party vendor, contraband-only reporting) as satisfying "narrowly tailored." 866 F.3d at 512. This shows the Second Circuit is willing to give real deference to how programs are *administered*, so long as the underlying condition itself is reasonably related and not excessive. If a court applied that same administrative deference to the alcohol clause, it could conclude that "abstain from alcohol while in treatment or on psychotropic medication" is itself a reasonably-tailored, administratively-workable rule that the treating program can simply enforce — precisely the opposite of what the physician-gating theory needs.
- **Cuts for (narrowly):** Browder's treatment-condition holding shows the Second Circuit is willing to police the *outer edge* of what a program/provider can impose as an enforceable condition, and to protect a defendant from being found in violation for resisting a rule that exceeds the court's actual order. That principle, while not squarely on point, at least establishes that "program rule ≠ automatically enforceable" is a live doctrine in this Circuit, which is a useful predicate for arguing (defensively, as in 5.2(1) above) that any future provider-level expansion of the alcohol clause beyond its own textual limits should not be treated as automatically binding.

**Bottom line on Browder: it is a real, on-topic Second Circuit case about the very same condition template Bernecky received, and it is worth citing — but it should be presented candidly as supporting a narrower point (limits on provider/PO expansion of the court's actual order, plus the useful factual point that the alcohol clause's language is an inherited district-wide template rather than an individualized Bernecky finding) rather than as the doctrinal engine for the physician-gating theory itself.** The individualized-assessment doctrine (Betts, Rodriguez) is doing that work, not Browder.

## 6. The "while in treatment or taking psychotropic medication" qualifier (Task 6)

**Does the existing qualifier partially address the concern, or does the core objection survive?**

**Partial mitigation on duration; the core objection survives, and the clause's own disjunctive structure arguably makes it worse, not better.**

1. **What the qualifier does address:** It limits the abstinence requirement in *time* — the condition does not purport to be a permanent, lifetime alcohol ban; it is keyed to periods of active treatment or active psychotropic medication use. This distinguishes Bernecky's clause from the *Betts*-style "total ban" (which had no such temporal limiter at all) and means a straightforward *Betts* vacatur argument (attacking the clause as an unlimited-duration blanket ban) is not a perfect factual match — the clause is already narrower than Betts's was in that one respect.

2. **What the qualifier does not address — the core objection survives:** The client's objection is not fundamentally about *duration*; it is about **who makes the medical determination that alcohol is actually contraindicated for this specific person** at any given moment within that window. Even confined to "while in treatment or taking psychotropic medication," the clause still operates as an **irrebuttable, status-triggered presumption** — the moment Bernecky is "in treatment" (of any kind) or "taking" any psychotropic medication (of any kind), abstinence is automatically required, regardless of whether an actual physician has ever assessed whether alcohol poses any risk for *that particular treatment modality or that particular medication* in *his* case.

3. **A textual point that strengthens, rather than weakens, the client's argument:** The clause is disjunctive — **"while in treatment OR taking psychotropic medication."** Read literally, this means the abstinence requirement is triggered by being in *talk therapy alone*, with **no medication involved at all**. In that scenario there is no possible medication-interaction rationale for the restriction whatsoever — the entire premise of a physician-driven, interaction-based concern (the client's own stated rationale, "e.g., due to a medication interaction") is absent by definition. The clause's own text therefore sweeps in a circumstance (non-medicated talk therapy) for which no medical rationale of any kind has been — or could be — articulated. This is a strong, self-contained textual argument that does not even require reaching the physician-gating theory: **the "or" branch of the clause is unmoored from any interaction-based justification on its face**, and is vulnerable under Betts/§5D1.3(b) for lacking any nexus at all in that branch, independent of whatever might be said about the medication branch.

4. **Even within the medication branch, the clinical premise is overbroad.** As discussed in Section 7 below, not all classes of psychotropic medication carry meaningful alcohol-interaction risk, and the degree of risk varies substantially by drug (e.g., MAOIs and bupropion carry specific, serious risks; many other psychotropic medications do not carry comparable absolute contraindications). A rule that treats "taking psychotropic medication" as a monolithic category triggering blanket abstinence does not track the actual clinical landscape, which is precisely the client's point: only an individualized (ideally physician-made) determination can tell whether *this* medication, for *this* patient, actually carries the risk the condition assumes categorically.

**Conclusion on this task:** The qualifier is a genuine, non-trivial narrowing compared to a true lifetime ban, and should be acknowledged as such in any motion (candor point: do not overstate the clause's breadth by ignoring this limiter). But it does not cure the defect the client has identified — if anything, the disjunctive "or in treatment" branch demonstrates the defect in its purest form, since that branch has *no* interaction-based rationale available even in principle.

## 7. Physician-gated / medically-contingent condition structures (Task 3) — candid assessment

**No case law, Guidelines commentary, or U.S. Courts policy document was found that squarely recognizes or rejects a supervised-release condition contingent on an actual treating physician's determination, as opposed to a categorical court-imposed rule.** This is a genuine gap in available authority, not merely a search-effort limitation — the searches covering this specific question returned only general secondary discussion of alcohol/medication interactions, not case law addressing conditional/physician-gated *condition structures* as a legal device.

What was found, useful only by analogy:

- **Existing federal probation practice already routes medication-management judgment to the prescribing physician, not to the court or Probation, once a psychotropic medication condition is in place.** Multiple U.S. Courts (uscourts.gov) probation-guidance chapters (Mental Health Treatment; Substance Abuse Treatment, Testing, and Abstinence) consistently describe practice under which: probation officers "cannot allow or give permission to defendants to alter or terminate a medication regime," and "if negative side-effects are identified, probation officers must report this information to the prescribing physician" for follow-up — i.e., the *clinical* judgment calls are institutionally assigned to the treating physician, with Probation's role limited to monitoring compliance, not making medical determinations. This is a structural analogy the motion can use affirmatively: **the existing federal supervision framework already assumes that questions requiring medical judgment are the treating physician's to answer, not the court's or Probation's** — which supports (without directly compelling) extending that same logic to the alcohol-interaction question embedded in condition (h).
- **U.S.S.G. § 5D1.3(d)'s "reason to believe... abuser" gate** (Section 3.6, above) shows the Guidelines themselves are comfortable conditioning a substance-related restriction on a factual predicate rather than an unconditional, status-based trigger — again, an analogy, not a holding requiring physician-gating specifically.

**Recommendation:** Present the physician-gating request as a **proposed modification / requested relief**, framed as reasonable in light of (a) the individualized-assessment defect under Betts, (b) the textual overbreadth identified in Section 6, and (c) the structural analogy to existing physician-deference practice for medication management — rather than as a rule "required" by any specific precedent. Overstating this as compelled by case law would be a citation-accuracy risk the client has specifically flagged as a concern given the prior memo's problems.

## 8. Clinical / medical literature (Task 7)

No SAMHSA or clinical-guidance document was found that directly criticizes "blanket court-ordered alcohol restrictions untethered from actual medical determinations" as a specific practice failure — this specific critique does not appear to exist as a named position in the literature searched. What is available, and is genuinely useful as supporting (not directly on-point) material:

- **NIAAA (National Institute on Alcohol Abuse and Alcoholism), "Alcohol-Medication Interactions: Potentially Dangerous Mixes"** — confirms that alcohol-medication interaction risk is drug-specific and mechanism-specific (pharmacokinetic vs. pharmacodynamic interactions), not a uniform property of "psychotropic medication" as a class.
- Clinical literature on antidepressant/alcohol interactions confirms **substantial variation by drug class**: MAOIs carry a specific, serious risk (hypertensive crisis with tyramine-containing alcoholic beverages); bupropion carries a specific seizure-risk warning; SNRIs like duloxetine carry a liver-risk concern; other classes carry comparatively lower or different risk profiles. This directly supports the point made in Section 6 that "psychotropic medication" is not a monolithic risk category and that a categorical rule assuming uniform risk **does not match the clinical reality** — the kind of individualized determination the client seeks (a physician assessing the *specific* medication) tracks how these interactions actually work clinically, unlike a categorical judicial assumption.
- **SAMHSA TIP 49** materials confirm that FDA-approved pharmacotherapy for alcohol use disorder is individualized and physician-directed (medication choice, dosing, and any abstinence expectations are tailored by the prescriber to the specific patient) — reinforcing, again by analogy, that clinical practice in this space is inherently individualized rather than categorical.

**Candor:** this section supports the *plausibility and clinical reasonableness* of the physician-gating request as a policy matter, but it is not a legal authority and should be used in the motion (if at all) as background/context, not as a citation carrying independent legal weight.

## 9. Overall candid assessment of what relief this research supports

**What is well-supported:**
- A **Betts/§ 5D1.3(b) individualized-assessment challenge** to the categorical alcohol clause is solid. If the Statement of Reasons (once located) does not articulate an individualized basis tied to Bernecky's own history, characteristics, or a documented substance-related risk, the clause is vulnerable exactly as Betts's was, reinforced by *Rodriguez*'s application of the same rule to a nearly identical "abstain from alcohol during/while in treatment" formulation.
- The **textual overbreadth argument** (Section 6) — that the disjunctive "or in treatment" branch has no interaction-based rationale even in principle — is a clean, self-contained, low-risk argument that does not depend on any contested precedent.
- The **Browder factual point** (that this exact condition template originates from the district's own post-Browder standard form, not from any Bernecky-specific finding) is a genuinely useful, low-risk factual observation to include, distinct from any doctrinal reliance on Browder's actual holdings.

**What is weaker and should be presented candidly as such:**
- The **specific physician-gating remedy** — i.e., "abstinence applies only if/when an actual treating physician determines it" — is **not compelled by any case law found**. It is a reasonable, clinically-sensible proposed modification, supported by analogy to existing medication-management practice and by the clinical variability literature, but it is a request the court would be *fashioning new relief* to grant, not relief squarely mandated by binding authority. The motion should frame it as the *logical remedy* flowing from the individualized-assessment defect (i.e., "if the court cannot make the individualized alcohol-risk finding itself, the sensible and administrable alternative is to let the person positioned to make that clinical finding — the treating physician — make it"), not as something Betts or Browder directly requires.
- **Browder's "central precedent" billing (per the intake documents) should be recalibrated.** It is a real, on-point, same-district case about the very same condition template — worth citing for the reasons in Section 5 — but it does not carry the doctrinal weight on the physician-gating question that the case file's earlier notes assumed. The individualized-assessment line (Betts, Rodriguez) is doing the actual work.
- Citations to *Mears* and *Scott* should be independently re-verified against a primary legal database before filing; both are flagged `[VERIFY BEFORE FILING]` above.

**Net assessment:** This argument is filing-worthy on the individualized-assessment/overbreadth theory, with a reasonable (but not case-compelled) physician-gating remedy proposed as the sensible fix. It is not a weak argument, but it is also not the slam-dunk the phrase "vacates a total alcohol ban imposed without evidence alcohol contributed to the offense" might suggest in isolation — the more precise framing is "the categorical, undifferentiated form of this clause lacks the individualized predicate Second Circuit law requires, and physician-gating is the most administrable, clinically sound way to supply that predicate going forward."
