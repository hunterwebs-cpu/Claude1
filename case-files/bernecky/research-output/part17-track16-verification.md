## 1. United States v. Maiorana — THE MOST IMPORTANT ITEM

**Case Name/Citation (confirmed exact):** United States v. Maiorana, **153 F.4th 306** (2d Cir. 2025) (en banc), **No. 22-1115-cr**, August Term 2024, submitted en banc June 25, 2025, **decided August 28, 2025**. On appeal from E.D.N.Y. (Frederic Block, J.), No. 1:20CR00519.

**Binding Status:** Binding, published, en banc Second Circuit authority — the highest form of intra-circuit precedent short of Supreme Court review. Majority opinion by **Merriam, J.**, joined by **Livingston, C.J., and Leval, Parker, Lohier, Sullivan, Bianco, Park, Nardini, Lee, Robinson, Pérez, Nathan, and Kahn, JJ.** (13 judges joining the author = 14 of 15). **Menashi, J., dissented** (joined by no one — a solo dissent). So the vote was 14-1, not merely a bare majority — this is about as strong an en banc mandate as the circuit produces.

**Holding (verbatim, with pincites):**

> "We hold that a sentencing court intending to impose non-mandatory conditions of supervised release, including the 'standard' conditions described in § 5D1.3(c), must notify the defendant during the sentencing proceeding; if the conditions are not pronounced, they may not later be added to the written judgment. A sentencing court need not read the full text of every condition on the record. But it must, at the very least, as part of the pronouncement of the sentence in the presence of the defendant during the sentencing proceeding, expressly adopt or specifically incorporate by reference particular conditions that have been set forth in writing and made available to the defendant in the PSR, the Guidelines, or a notice adopted by the court." 153 F.4th at 314.

And, expressly: **"Proceeding en banc, we now overrule Truscello."** 153 F.4th at 308 (opinion summary). The court explicitly frames the new rule as grounded in "the distinction between mandatory and discretionary conditions as denoted in 18 U.S.C. § 3583(d)... Mandatory conditions may be imposed without prior notice or pronouncement. Discretionary conditions may not." Id. at 313.

**Facts (confirmed):** Maiorana pled guilty to felon-in-possession, 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(1). Neither party's sentencing memorandum discussed conditions of supervised release. The PSR recommended only seven "special" conditions (including a mental-health-treatment condition). At the May 18, 2022 sentencing, the district court orally imposed those seven special conditions (modifying the mental-health condition to remove the cost-contribution piece) and stated only: *"There will be general conditions of supervised release as well, which will be part and parcel of the judgment"* — giving **no indication of what those "general conditions" would be**, and making **no mention whatsoever, oral or otherwise, of either mandatory or "standard" discretionary conditions**. Id. at 309. The **written judgment**, entered five days later, added: (i) the seven special conditions as discussed, but **with the mental-health cost-contribution requirement restored** (directly contradicting the oral pronouncement — both parties agreed this had to be struck); (ii) **thirteen additional discretionary "standard" conditions** substantially mirroring § 5D1.3(c), never mentioned at all at sentencing; and (iii) four mandatory conditions under § 3583(d) (uncontested). Id.

**Confirms-or-corrects part16:** **Confirms in full**, with one citation correction (reporter cite now available: 153 F.4th 306; docket number is "22-1115-cr" with the "-cr" suffix). Part16's characterization of the holding, facts, "nine circuits" consensus point, and the overruling of Truscello are all accurate. One clarifying addition beyond what part16 captured: the court's remedy on remand is a **choice for the district court** — either (a) hold a new hearing, notify the defendant of the thirteen conditions (by full recitation or express incorporation by reference to the PSR/Guidelines/a court notice), and reimpose them, or (b) simply strike them without a new hearing. Id. at 315. This remedial structure is itself useful to Bernecky's framing: it shows Maiorana treats an unpronounced condition as **simply absent from the judgment**, correctable either by lawful reimposition going forward or by deletion — not as a claim that the original sentencing was void or illegal. That maps directly onto the client's "clarification, not illegality" KEY REFRAME.

**Application to Bernecky's facts:** Confirmed as the single strongest new authority for the CIMP/occupational "silence" argument. Maiorana's rule — that a non-mandatory condition "may not later be added to the written judgment" unless pronounced or expressly incorporated by reference at sentencing — squarely supports the inference that if an occupational/employer-facing restriction was never mentioned at Bernecky's 2018 sentencing (and the CIMP condition's text itself contains no such restriction, as independently confirmed against the source PDF per client-directives.md), no such restriction has ever had valid legal existence as "a condition the court imposed." The dissent (Menashi, J.) is worth flagging for completeness: he would have kept Truscello, arguing the majority's rule is a "formulaic" burden that does nothing but force "utterance of specific words," and that an oral reference to "supervised release" should be understood to include default/standard conditions as a matter of common understanding. This dissent is useful only defensively — counsel should be ready for a government argument that Maiorana's oral-pronouncement logic is narrower than it looks, but the majority opinion (14 judges) leaves no room for that argument on its own terms, since it is squarely about non-mandatory conditions in general, not merely "standard" conditions as a technical Guidelines label.

## 2. Esteras v. United States

**Case Name/Citation (confirmed exact):** Esteras v. United States, **145 S. Ct. 2031** (2025), **No. 23-7483**. Argued February 25, 2025; **decided June 20, 2025**. On writ of certiorari to the Sixth Circuit. (Consolidated with companion petitioners Timothy Michael Jaimez and Toriano A. Leaks, Jr., all captioned in the same opinion.)

**Binding Status:** Binding U.S. Supreme Court authority, resolving an acknowledged circuit split.

**Holding (verbatim, with pincites):**

> "A district court considering whether to revoke a defendant's term of supervised release may not consider § 3553(a)(2)(A), which covers retribution vis-à-vis the defendant's underlying criminal offense." 145 S. Ct. at 2034 (syllabus); see also id. at 2037 ("Congress's decision to enumerate most of the sentencing factors while omitting § 3553(a)(2)(A) raises a strong inference that courts may not consider that factor when deciding whether to revoke a term of supervised release.").

The Court's operative language on the omission (part16 called this "conspicuous" — confirmed verbatim):

> "But a court may do so only 'after considering' an enumerated list of sentencing factors: those 'set forth in section 3553(a)(1), (a)(2)(B), (a)(2)(C), (a)(2)(D), (a)(4), (a)(5), (a)(6), and (a)(7).' § 3583(e). **Conspicuously missing from this list is § 3553(a)(2)(A)**, which directs a district court to consider 'the need for the sentence imposed' 'to reflect the seriousness of the offense, to promote respect for the law, and to provide just punishment for the offense.'" 145 S. Ct. at 2036-37.

And the expressio unius reasoning, stated explicitly and citing Echazabal (important cross-link, see Item 6 below):

> "That conclusion follows from the well-established canon of statutory interpretation—'expressio unius est exclusio alterius'—which means that expressing one item of an associated group excludes another item not mentioned. See Chevron U. S. A. Inc. v. Echazabal, 536 U.S. 73, 80 ... While Congress elsewhere set forth ten factors that must generally inform a district court's sentencing decisions, it provided in § 3583(e)—the provision governing the revocation of supervised release—that courts must consider only eight of those ten factors. The natural implication is that Congress did not intend courts to consider the other two factors, including § 3553(a)(2)(A)." 145 S. Ct. at 2034 (syllabus ¶ (b)).

**Vote/composition (confirmed):** Barrett, J., delivered the opinion of the Court, joined by Roberts, C.J., and Thomas, Kagan, and Kavanaugh, JJ., and joined by Sotomayor and Jackson, JJ., as to all but Part II-B — i.e., **7 Justices** in the majority as to the core holding, with Sotomayor and Jackson each filing separate opinions concurring in part/in the judgment on Part II-B. Alito, J., dissented, joined by Gorsuch, J. This confirms part16's characterization as "7-2."

**Facts (confirmed):** Esteras pled guilty in 2018 to conspiring to distribute heroin; sentenced to 12 months plus 6 years supervised release. Three years into supervision, he was arrested for domestic violence/menacing (charges later dismissed at the victim's request). At the revocation hearing, the district court found a violation, called Esteras "no stranger to law violations," found his original sentence had been "rather lenient," and revoked supervised release, imposing an above-guidelines 24-month reimprisonment term explicitly to "promote respect for the law" — the language of § 3553(a)(2)(A). Esteras's counsel objected on the § 3553(a)(2)(A) point (preserving it for appeal even though Sixth Circuit precedent then permitted the consideration). The Sixth Circuit affirmed; the Supreme Court reversed.

**Confirms-or-corrects part16:** **Confirms in full**, with the citation correction (145 S. Ct. 2031, not 2027 — 2027 was apparently the syllabus's internal page-range start point in some secondary source, not the case's own first page). One noteworthy addition beyond part16's characterization: **Esteras itself cites Chevron v. Echazabal, 536 U.S. at 80, for the general statement of the expressio unius canon** — not for Echazabal's specific holding rejecting the canon on its facts. This means Echazabal is doctrinally "good law" precisely as the source of the canon's general articulation (which is how the part16/part17 recommended treatment already uses it), and Esteras's own citation practice validates using Echazabal that way. Also newly confirmed: Esteras relies on and extends Tapia v. United States, 564 U.S. 319 (2011), and Concepcion v. United States, 597 U.S. 481 (2022) — both of which the Court says already read the omission of § 3553(a)(2)(A) from the imposition-of-supervised-release provision (§ 3583(c)) the same way; Esteras simply extends that established reading to revocation under § 3583(e). This is a *third* Supreme Court precedent (beyond Esteras itself) applying the identical negative-inference logic to this exact statutory scheme, which further strengthens the "this interpretive move is not novel, just not yet applied to a judgment's conditions" framing in part16.

**Application to Bernecky's facts:** Confirmed as excellent, fresh, on-point authority for the expressio unius argument regarding Special Condition (a)'s silence on employment. As part16 already recognized, the direct holding concerns statutory factors, not judicial conditions — so the extension to Bernecky's Judgment remains, honestly, a novel analogical move, not a decided question. But Esteras is now confirmed, word-for-word, to supply the strongest possible statement of the "conspicuous omission from an itemized list raises a strong negative inference" principle, decided a year before this motion and applying directly to 18 U.S.C. § 3583 itself.

## 3. United States v. Marquez

**Citation (confirmed exact):** United States v. Marquez, **506 F.2d 620** (2d Cir. 1974). Decided November 4, 1974. Before Oakes, C.J., and Frankel and Kelleher, District Judges (sitting by designation); opinion by Kelleher, D.J.

**Binding Status:** Binding, published Second Circuit panel authority — the foundational case in this circuit's oral-pronouncement line (later cited and built upon in Truscello, Rosario, and Maiorana itself).

**Holding (verbatim, with pincite):** Adopting Judge Moore's concurring dictum in Sobell v. United States, 407 F.2d 180, 184 (2d Cir. 1969), as "the law of this Circuit":

> "It is the oral sentence which constitutes the judgment of the court, and which is authority for the execution of the court's sentence. The written commitment is 'mere evidence of such authority.'" 506 F.2d at 622 (quoting Sobell, 407 F.2d at 184).

And, adopting the Ninth Circuit's formulation:

> "In cases where there is a direct conflict between an unambiguous oral pronouncement of sentence and the written judgment and commitment... the oral pronouncement, as correctly reported, must control. The only sentence that is legally cognizable is the actual oral pronouncement in the presence of the defendant." 506 F.2d at 622 (quoting United States v. Munoz-Dela Rose, 495 F.2d 253, 256 (9th Cir. 1974)).

**Facts (confirmed):** Marquez was orally sentenced to five years plus a $10,000 fine and costs of prosecution — with **no mention of any commitment condition**. The written judgment, entered the same day, added that Marquez would "stand committed until the fine is paid" — i.e., converted a simple fine into a committed fine (contingent imprisonment for nonpayment), a materially more burdensome sentence never stated orally. The panel reversed the denial of Marquez's Rule 36 motion and ordered the written judgment conformed to the oral sentence.

**Confirms-or-corrects part16:** **Fully confirms.** Part16's quotes and characterization are accurate and verbatim.

**Application to Bernecky's facts:** Confirmed as good general background/rationale authority for the oral-pronouncement-controls principle, though — as part16 candidly notes — a conflict-resolution case rather than a total-silence case, so its use should be limited to establishing the doctrine's constitutional (Rule 43/right-to-be-present) rationale before pivoting to Maiorana as the more directly applicable authority.

## 4. United States v. Truscello

**Citation (confirmed exact):** United States v. Truscello, **168 F.3d 61** (2d Cir. 1999). Decided January 22, 1999. (Note: the named appellant in the caption is actually "Steven Crea" — Truscello was a co-defendant in the same multi-defendant caption, but the opinion is universally cited as "Truscello" because Dominick Truscello's name appears first in the case caption; the operative appellant whose motion was at issue was Crea.) Before Kearse, Circuit Judge, and Casey and Pollack, District Judges (sitting by designation); opinion by Casey, D.J.

**Binding Status:** Was binding Second Circuit panel authority; **now expressly overruled in relevant part by Maiorana**, 153 F.4th at 308 ("Proceeding en banc, we now overrule Truscello"), and id. at 313-14 (rejecting Truscello's "implicit in the very nature of supervised release" and "basic administrative requirements" rationales for allowing standard conditions to be added post-hoc without oral pronouncement).

**Holding (verbatim, with pincite) — as it stood before Maiorana:**

> "Implicit in the very nature of supervised release is that certain conditions are necessary to effect its purpose. Crea's appeal must be denied simply because there is no conflict between the oral sentence imposed by Judge Johnson and the written J & C reflecting that oral sentence." 168 F.3d at 62.

The court reasoned that the written judgment's addition of fourteen "standard conditions" — never orally itemized — did not conflict with, but merely clarified, an oral sentence that generally referenced "supervised release," because such conditions were "basic administrative requirement[s] essential to the functioning of the supervised release system." Id. at 63-64 (quoting United States v. Smith, 982 F.2d 757, 764 (2d Cir. 1992)).

**Facts (confirmed):** Crea was orally sentenced to nine months' imprisonment, three years' supervised release, restitution, and a special assessment — with **no oral articulation of any standard conditions**. The written J&C then listed fourteen "standard conditions." Crea's motion to conform the J&C to the oral sentence was denied by the district court and affirmed by the Second Circuit.

**Confirms-or-corrects part16:** **Confirms the pre-Maiorana holding exactly as part16 described it**, and **confirms precisely how Maiorana overruled it**: Maiorana quotes and rejects both of Truscello's core rationales verbatim ("[i]mplicit in the very nature of supervised release [because they] are necessary to effect its purpose," 168 F.3d at 62; "basic administrative requirements essential to the functioning of the supervised release system," id. at 63) as insufficiently protective of the constitutional right to presence. 153 F.4th at 313. Maiorana further notes that Truscello's own reasoning is "difficult to reconcile" with the fact that the very Guidelines label these conditions "recommended," not necessary. Id. Truscello remains citable **only** for background on how the rule used to work and how completely Maiorana rejected it — never as current law on the pronouncement question.

**Application to Bernecky's facts:** Confirmed as useful exclusively for contrast — showing how far Maiorana moved the law, and why an occupational restriction that was never orally mentioned (unlike Truscello's routine "standard conditions," which Maiorana itself now treats the same way) cannot survive under current Second Circuit law regardless of how "administratively necessary" the government might later argue it to be.

## 5. United States v. Rosario

**Citation (confirmed exact):** United States v. Rosario, **386 F.3d 166** (2d Cir. 2004). Argued September 8, 2004; decided October 8, 2004. Before Newman, Calabresi, and Hall, Circuit Judges; opinion by Newman, C.J.

**Binding Status:** Binding, published Second Circuit panel authority — not disturbed by Maiorana (Maiorana cites Rosario approvingly for the general oral-pronouncement-controls rule, 153 F.4th at 310-11).

**Holding (verbatim, with pincite):**

> "It is well settled, as a general proposition, that in the event of variation between an oral pronouncement of sentence and a subsequent written judgment, the oral pronouncement controls... and any burdensome punishments or restrictions added in the written judgment must be removed." 386 F.3d at 168-69 (citing Bartone v. United States, 375 U.S. 52, 53 (1963)).

**Facts (confirmed — matches part16 exactly):** Rosario was orally sentenced to a term including a condition barring "intentional contact with any child under 17 years of age" **except his own son**. The same-day written judgment altered this in two ways: (1) it added "if applicable" to the sex-offender registration requirement (a benign, unchallenged clarification); and (2) it **narrowed the son-contact exception** by adding a requirement that contact with his own son be approved by "the Family Court, or other court having jurisdiction" — a restriction never mentioned orally. The government **conceded** this addition was improper and had to be deleted. The court did not remand for reimposition, reasoning that Rosario had already begun serving his sentence and had a legitimate expectation of finality, and Double Jeopardy principles (drawing on North Carolina v. Pearce and United States v. DiFrancesco) counseled against giving the government a second bite at imposing it. 386 F.3d at 169-71.

**Confirms-or-corrects part16:** **Confirms in full.** Part16's description of the Family Court addition, the government's concession, and the remand-vs.-deletion reasoning is accurate.

**Application to Bernecky's facts:** Confirmed as a good precedent for the specific remedy Bernecky may want if the "silence" clarification theory does not fully persuade the court — Rosario shows that when the government concedes (or a court finds) an unpronounced addition to a written judgment, the ordinary remedy is simple deletion/correction, not a full resentencing, particularly where the defendant already has a settled expectation of finality. This is a useful fallback remedy argument distinct from the primary "clarification" framing.

## 6. Chevron U.S.A. Inc. v. Echazabal

**Citation (confirmed exact):** Chevron U.S.A. Inc. v. Echazabal, **536 U.S. 73** (2002). Argued February 27, 2002; decided June 10, 2002. Souter, J., delivered the opinion for a **unanimous** Court (9-0).

**Binding Status:** Binding, unanimous U.S. Supreme Court authority.

**Holding on expressio unius (confirmed verbatim, at the client's flagged pages 80-81):**

> "The argument follows the reliance of the Ninth Circuit majority on the interpretive canon, expressio unius est exclusio alterius, 'expressing one item of [an] associated group or series excludes another left unmentioned.' United States v. Vonn, 535 U.S. 55, 65 (2002). **The rule is fine when it applies, but this case joins some others in showing when it does not.**" 536 U.S. at 80.

> "The first strike against the expression-exclusion rule here is right in the text... These are spacious defensive categories... That discretion is confirmed, if not magnified, by the provision that 'qualification standards' falling within the limits of job relation and business necessity 'may include' a veto on those who would directly threaten others in the workplace. **Far from supporting Echazabal's position, the expansive phrasing of 'may include' points directly away from the sort of exclusive specification he claims.**" 536 U.S. at 80.

> "Strike two in this case is the failure to identify any such established series... **Expressio unius just fails to work here.**" 536 U.S. at 81, 84.

The Court identified a **third** independent reason the canon failed on these facts (a point part16 did not capture, worth adding): even the EEOC's own prior regulatory practice under the predecessor Rehabilitation Act was not uniform across agencies, so there was no "standard pairing" of threat-to-self/threat-to-others from which an omission could support a clean negative inference. 536 U.S. at 82-83.

**Confirms-or-corrects part16:** **Fully confirms** the client's/part16's determination that Echazabal is a case where the Court **rejected**, not applied, the expressio unius canon — real, correctly located at 80-81, but backwards if cited as if the Court had applied the canon to find an implied exclusion. Part16's recommended correct use (cite it for the doctrinal test: canon requires a genuine closed "series," defeated by illustrative "may include" language) is accurate and is independently reinforced by the fact that **Esteras itself cites Echazabal, 536 U.S. at 80, for exactly that same general-canon-statement purpose** (see Item 2 above) — meaning the Supreme Court's own most recent supervised-release opinion treats Echazabal the same way part16 recommends treating it.

**Application to Bernecky's facts:** Confirmed as usable only as the source of the canon's own limiting test, then affirmatively distinguished: Bernecky's Judgment's Special Conditions (a)-(j) are a closed, fully itemized list with no "including," "such as," or "may include" language — precisely the feature whose absence caused the canon to fail in Echazabal.

## 7. United States v. Chemical Foundation, Inc.

**Citation (confirmed exact):** United States v. Chemical Foundation, Inc., **272 U.S. 1** (1926). Argued December 9-11, 1925; decided October 11, 1926. Butler, J., delivered the opinion of the Court (Sutherland and Stone, JJ., took no part).

**Binding Status:** Binding U.S. Supreme Court authority; a foundational, still-frequently-cited administrative-law case.

**Holding/exact pincite (confirmed, at the client's flagged pages 14-15):**

> "Findings of fact concurred in by two lower courts will not be disturbed unless clearly erroneous... **The presumption of regularity supports the official acts of public officers and, in the absence of clear evidence to the contrary, courts presume that they have properly discharged their official duties.**" 272 U.S. at 14-15 (citing Confiscation Cases, 20 Wall. 92, 108 (1874); United States v. Page, 137 U.S. 673, 679-80 (1891); United States v. Nix, 189 U.S. 199, 205 (1903)).

**Facts (confirmed):** A suit by the United States to void its own prior sales of German-owned patents seized during WWI to the Chemical Foundation, alleging fraud and conflict of interest by the Alien Property Custodian and other officials in structuring the sale. Both lower courts found no fraud or conspiracy. The "presumption of regularity" language appears in the context of the Court accepting the concurrent factual findings of the two lower courts and presuming that the executive-branch officials involved (the Alien Property Custodian, and Frank Polk acting under presidential delegation) properly discharged their official duties absent clear contrary evidence.

**Confirms-or-corrects part16:** **Fully confirms**, exact pincite verified.

**Application to Bernecky's facts:** Confirmed as a serviceable, secondary, footnote-level point: the court should presume the 2018 sentencing judge properly discharged his duties, including the § 5F1.5 two-part occupational-restriction findings requirement — supporting the inference he did not silently impose an occupational restriction without making the required findings. Should remain a minor supporting citation, not a leading one, exactly as part16 recommended.

**§ 5D1.4(b) — exact text:**

> **"(b) Early Termination.**—Any time after the expiration of one year of supervised release and after an individualized assessment of the need for ongoing supervision, the court may terminate the remaining term of supervision and discharge the defendant if the court determines, following consultation with the government and the probation officer, that the termination is warranted by the conduct of the defendant and in the interest of justice. See 18 U.S.C. § 3583(e)(2).")

**Application/significance:** This **fully confirms** — with primary-source text now in hand rather than secondary-source paraphrase — the Task 1(b) point flagged in part16: the Commission wrote an **explicit one-year minimum** into subsection (b) (early termination, keyed to 18 U.S.C. § 3583(e)(1)), but wrote **no minimum time trigger at all** into subsection (a) (ordinary modification of conditions, keyed to § 3583(e)(2) — the exact provision Bernecky's motion invokes). This is now a clean, primary-source-verified expressio unius data point in its own right: the Commission demonstrably knows how to draft a numeric waiting period when it wants one (one year, subsection (b)), and pointedly did not do so in the immediately preceding subsection (a) governing the exact relief Bernecky seeks.
