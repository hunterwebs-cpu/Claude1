# Research Track 26 — Delegation of the Fee/Cost AMOUNT (not just the treatment decision) to Probation
## United States v. Jeffrey Bernecky, No. 6:18-CR-06018-DGL-MWP (W.D.N.Y.) — Second Circuit
### Motion to Modify Conditions of Supervised Release under 18 U.S.C. § 3583(e)(2) / Fed. R. Crim. P. 32.1(c)

**Prepared:** 2026-07-11
**Scope:** Whether case law holds (or comes close to holding) that setting the actual dollar amount, rate, or payment schedule of a fee-bearing supervision condition — as opposed to whether/how broadly the underlying condition applies — is a non-delegable judicial function. This track exists because the motion's current three-case delegation citations (Matta, Browder, Peterson) do not actually address dollar-amount-setting, a gap the client correctly flagged.

This adverse case is actually useful, carefully framed: it confirms that what makes fee-delegation permissible is an ability-to-pay standard, and Bernecky's conditions have none.

## PART A — The Second Circuit's restitution-delegation line (closely analogous, not directly on point)

### A1. United States v. Porter — the seminal Second Circuit case

**United States v. Porter, 41 F.3d 68 (2d Cir. 1994)** and **United States v. Porter, 90 F.3d 64 (2d Cir. 1996)** (same underlying case, two appeals). **[CONFIRMED CITATIONS — both citation strings appeared identically across at least 3 independent searches, including a Justia case-page title and a law.resource.org file path matching "90.F3d.64.95-1219.407.html" — the docket number embedded in that filename (95-1219) is itself independent corroboration this is a real, correctly-paginated opinion.]**

Per multiple consistent search summaries, the district court in Porter had ordered restitution "stayed provided that the defendant makes timely installments on a schedule to be fixed by the U.S. Bureau of Prisons while incarcerated and by the U.S. Probation Office while on supervised release." The Second Circuit held this an impermissible delegation, articulated (per search synthesis, not verified verbatim) as: **a sentencing judge may not authorize a probation officer to dictate the amount of restitution or to establish a schedule for repayment of a restitution order — those decisions are for judges alone.**

**Confidence: moderate-high on the citation and general holding (both appellate numbers corroborated); low-moderate on the exact quoted language (search-summary paraphrase only, not independently read).**

### A2. United States v. Mortimer — extends Porter to Bureau of Prisons/IFRP delegation

**United States v. Mortimer, 52 F.3d 429 (2d Cir. 1995)** (first appeal) and **United States v. Mortimer, 94 F.3d 89 (2d Cir. 1996)** (second appeal, after resentencing). **[CONFIRMED CITATIONS — "52 F.3d 429" and "94 F.3d 89" each appeared independently across 2+ separately-worded searches, and the two-appeal structure (first appeal vacating an immediate-payment restitution order for lack of an ability-to-pay finding and remanding to "devise a reasonable payment schedule"; second appeal after the district court's Dec. 15, 1995 resentencing, addressing whether Porter's holding extends to BOP/IFRP delegation) is internally consistent across sources, which increases confidence this is not a hallucinated compound.]**

Holding (per search synthesis): the district court's delegation of authority to establish the installment amount and timing of Mortimer's restitution payments to the Federal Bureau of Prisons was error requiring vacatur and remand for resentencing; the court may properly draw on IFRP guidelines when fashioning its own restitution order, but discretionary authority to depart from the court's own order cannot be vested in prison officials.

**Confidence: moderate-high on both citations; the holding language is consistent across sources but is paraphrase, not verified opinion text.**

### A3. United States v. Kyles — BOP delegation of restitution schedule, 2010

**United States v. Kyles, 601 F.3d 78 (2d Cir. 2010).** **[CONFIRMED CITATION — "601 F.3d 78" appeared identically across 3 independent searches, including a direct CourtListener title match ("United States v. Kyles, 601 F.3d 78").]** Note: one earlier search result (in an unrelated line of research reflected in part3-fees-financial.md, Task 4(a)) rendered this citation as "601 F.3d 82, 87-88" — that appears to be either a pin-cite-page confusion or a minor synthesis error; every direct citation-matching search this session returned "601 F.3d 78" as the case's starting page, which is more reliable. **Recommend counsel verify the exact starting page before filing.**

Holding (per search synthesis): an order requiring the defendant to participate in the BOP's Inmate Financial Responsibility Program (IFRP) and make restitution "in accordance with the policies of that program" impermissibly delegated judicial power to the BOP, because IFRP payment schedules are set "on an ad hoc basis with the assistance of prison staff" rather than by the court — the Second Circuit vacated that portion of the order and remanded for the district court to set its own schedule, citing Porter.

**Confidence: moderate-high on citation and holding.**

One search surfaced a description of **"United States v. A follow-up targeted search for the citation string "81 Fed. Appx. 364" / "81 F. App'x 364" **failed to reproduce or corroborate it** — no independent search result confirmed this citation exists with this content. **[UNCONFIRMED — do not cite without independent Westlaw/Lexis/PACER verification. The underlying holding description (delegation is impermissible even when "limited and contingent") is useful as an argument concept regardless, since it is consistent with Porter/Mortimer/Kyles, but do not attach the "81 F. Green" (theft of government property, restitution/statute-of-limitations issue) that turned up in searches — do not conflate the two.

### A5. What Part A actually supports

Porter/Mortimer/Kyles are **binding Second Circuit precedent that the AMOUNT and SCHEDULE of a court-ordered financial obligation (restitution) cannot be delegated to the Bureau of Prisons or the probation office** — this is a real, confirmed doctrine, and it directly rebuts any suggestion that "amount-setting delegation" is a made-up problem. But all three arise under 18 U.S.C. § 3664 (the MVRA/restitution statute), which contains an explicit textual command that "the court shall... specify in the restitution order the manner in which... restitution is to be paid" (§ 3664(f)(2)) — a statutory hook the "contribute to the cost of services rendered" language in Special Conditions (b), (g), (h) and the "pay the cost of monitoring services" language in Special Condition (a) do not have. **This is the single most important honest caveat in this memo: Porter/Mortimer/Kyles are closely analogous, not directly on point, because they rest on a statute that has no counterpart governing supervision-fee conditions generally.**

## PART B — Out-of-circuit restitution/fine delegation cases (closely analogous, broader consensus)

### B1. United States v. Miller, 77 F.3d 71 (4th Cir. 1996) — extends the doctrine to FINES, not just restitution

**[CONFIRMED CITATION — "77 F.3d 71" appeared across 3 independent searches, including an OpenJurist title match and a direct fetch-summary with full facts (defendant James Barnett Miller, counterfeiting conviction, argued Dec. 8, 1995, decided Mar. 6, 1996) that is detailed enough to be very unlikely to be a hallucination.]**

Holding (per search synthesis): the district court's order directing Miller to pay fine and restitution installments "at such times and in such amounts as the Bureau of Prisons and/or the Probation Office may direct" was an impermissible delegation. The Fourth Circuit held **"a district court lacks authority to delegate to the probation officer the final authority to determine the amount and timing of restitutionary installment payments, and this principle equally applies to fines."** Sentence vacated as to the fine and restitution order; remanded for resentencing.

**Significance:** This is the best "bridge" case in this research track — it is the clearest confirmed authority that the non-delegation-of-amount principle is **not restitution-specific**; it applies at least to fines as well, which are governed by a different statute (18 U.S.C. § 3572(d)) with parallel "the court shall" language. This supports (without directly deciding) the argument that the underlying principle is about the inherently judicial nature of fixing a defendant's dollar obligation, not merely about MVRA's specific text.

### B2. United States v. Merric, 166 F.3d 406 (1st Cir. 1999) — fine installment AMOUNT delegated to "the supervising officer"

**[CONFIRMED CITATION — "166 F.3d 406" appeared across 4 independent searches, including Justia and FindLaw title matches.]**

The challenged language (quoted consistently in search synthesis): "Any amount that the defendant is unable to pay now shall be paid in monthly installments, **to be determined in amount by the supervising officer**, during the period of his supervised release." The First Circuit held that **the judge, not the probation officer, must have final authority to determine the payment schedule**, and vacated the sentence (not the conviction) and remanded so the district court could make that reservation of authority explicit in the judgment.

**Significance:** This is the closest factual analog located in this entire research track to Bernecky's Special Conditions (a), (b), (g), (h) — a condition that hands the AMOUNT determination to "the supervising officer" with no court-set figure or standard at all. The Bernecky conditions are arguably *worse* than Merric's, because Merric's condition at least fixed a total obligation ($4,000 fine) and left only the installment schedule to probation, whereas Bernecky's conditions leave the entire obligation — total amount and schedule alike — undefined.

### B3. United States v. Albro, 32 F.3d 173 (5th Cir. 1994) — restitution amount/manner is a judicial act

**[CONFIRMED CITATION — "32 F.3d 173" appeared across 2 independent searches with consistent framing.]** Holding (per synthesis): "any decision as to the amount and manner of restitution payments, including the amount and timing of installments... must be made by the court, rather than a probation officer." Also characterized in one source as reasoning that "unauthorized delegation of sentencing authority from an Article III judicial officer to a non-Article III official affects substantial rights." Useful mainly as the Fifth Circuit's own statement of the general principle — important because the Fifth Circuit later distinguished this exact case when it refused to extend the doctrine to treatment costs (see Part C, Warden, below).

### B4. United States v. Gunning, 401 F.3d 1145 (9th Cir. 2005) and United States v. Lemoine, 546 F.3d 1042 (9th Cir. 2008) — Ninth Circuit's categorical rule

**[CONFIRMED CITATIONS — both citation strings appeared identically across 2 independent searches each, with consistent holding descriptions and a clean chronological relationship (Gunning 2005, Lemoine 2008 citing/refining Gunning) that is strong internal corroboration.]**

Gunning holding (per synthesis, strongest quoted language found in this entire research track): the MVRA's language is "categorical," and **"the district court is ultimately responsible for setting a schedule for making restitution"** — a district court **"simply does not have the authority to delegate its own scheduling duties, not to the probation office, not to the BOP, not to anyone else."** Lemoine subsequently held that the BOP's practical "expertise in the payment area" does not relieve the district court of its own scheduling responsibility, though it also clarified that the MVRA does not bar the BOP from running an independent, voluntary program (the IFRP) that encourages *more generous* payments than the court's schedule requires.

**Significance:** This is the broadest, most emphatic statement of the "no delegation, to anyone, for any reason" principle found in this research track. Still restitution/MVRA-specific, but rhetorically the most useful quote for a "core judicial function" framing if counsel wants to argue by extension.

### B5. What Part B supports

A five-circuit consensus (2d, 4th, 5th, 9th, and by clear implication 1st via Merric) holds that fixing the AMOUNT and/or SCHEDULE of a defendant's court-ordered financial obligation — whether restitution or a fine — is an act reserved to the sentencing judge and cannot be handed to probation or the BOP with no court-set standard. Miller is the strongest single citation for arguing this principle is not restitution-specific. **None of these cases, however, involve a "contribute to the cost of services" supervision condition** — they are all restitution or criminal-fine cases. The extension to supervision fee conditions is an analogy, not a holding, in every case found.

## PART C — The case squarely on point for TREATMENT-COST conditions — and it is adverse

### United States v. Warden, 291 F.3d 363 (5th Cir. 2002)

**[CONFIRMED CITATION — "291 F.3d 363" appeared identically across at least 4 independent searches, including Justia, FindLaw, and law.resource.org title/filename matches with consistent underlying facts (defendant Jeffery Lynn Warden, marijuana-distribution conviction, decided May 14, 2002) — this is the best-corroborated citation in this entire memo.]**

**This is the only case located in this entire research run whose fact pattern is squarely the same category of condition at issue in Bernecky — a supervision condition requiring the defendant to pay for treatment/monitoring costs, challenged specifically as an unlawful delegation of the amount-setting function.** The challenged condition (quoted consistently across sources): the defendant "will incur costs associated with such monitoring, **based on ability to pay as determined by the probation officer**," in the context of a required inpatient/outpatient drug and alcohol treatment program.

**Holding:** The Fifth Circuit **rejected** the delegation challenge. It reasoned that the probation officer was given "only the responsibility to make a determination as to Warden's ability to pay, a factfinding determination commonly made by probation officers in other contexts" — and it expressly **distinguished** its own restitution-delegation precedent, United States v. Albro, 32 F.3d 173 (5th Cir. 1994) (Part B3, above), as concerning "restitution payments, rather than payment for the costs of drug treatment and other counseling."

**This is adverse authority and must be reported honestly rather than buried.** If Bernecky's motion argues broadly that "delegating the amount of a cost-of-treatment condition to probation is per se an unlawful delegation," Warden is a squarely-on-point, reasoned rejection of that exact argument from a sister circuit, and the government could be expected to cite it (or a similar case, if one exists in the Second Circuit that I did not find) in opposition.

**However, Warden is also useful, read carefully and narrowly, precisely because of *why* the Fifth Circuit found it permissible:** the condition it upheld tied the probation officer's role to an express **"ability to pay" factfinding standard** — the officer wasn't inventing a number out of nothing; the officer was applying a defined criterion (ability to pay) that cabins the discretion and gives a reviewing court something to check the officer's exercise of discretion against. **Bernecky's Special Conditions (a), (b), (g), and (h) contain no such standard at all** — no "ability to pay" language, no sliding scale, no ceiling, nothing. The argument this supports is not "delegation of amount-setting is always unlawful" (Warden forecloses that broad version in at least one circuit), but rather: **"even the one circuit to squarely approve probation-officer involvement in setting treatment-cost obligations required, as the condition that saved it from being an unlawful delegation, an explicit ability-to-pay standard — the very thing Judicial Conference policy itself says should never be omitted (see part3-fees-financial.md, Task 1(b)) and the very thing missing from every one of Bernecky's four fee-bearing conditions."** Framed this way, Warden is not merely distinguishable — it affirmatively supports the relief Bernecky's motion is already seeking (a court-imposed ability-to-pay/sliding-scale requirement), because it shows what the missing ingredient needs to be to survive scrutiny.

## PART D — Doctrinal framing cases (not fee-specific, but useful for the "core judicial function" argument)

- **United States v. Meléndez-Santana, 353 F.3d 93 (1st Cir. 2003).** **[CONFIRMED CITATION — appeared across 3 independent searches with detailed, mutually consistent holding descriptions.]** Grounds the non-delegation principle in Article III generally: courts may use non-judicial officers to support judicial functions "as long as the judicial officer retains and exercises ultimate responsibility," and held that a probation officer may not decide *whether* a defendant must undergo drug treatment after a positive test — that decision must be made by the court. Useful as a general Article III framing citation, not fee-specific.
- **United States v. Allen, 312 F.3d 512, 515-16 (1st Cir. 2002).** **[CONFIRMED CITATION — appeared across 2 independent searches.]** A *contrast* case: upheld delegating the *scheduling* (session timing, provider selection) of court-ordered mental health treatment to probation, distinguishing "whether treatment is mandatory" (judicial) from "administrative details of treatment" (delegable). Useful to show the line courts draw between administrative/ministerial delegation (permissible) and substantive/amount-or-liberty delegation (impermissible) — but it is about scheduling sessions, not scheduling money, so it cuts only weakly for either side here.

## PART E — Statutory analogy (§ 3664(f)(2) and § 3572(d)) assessed directly

18 U.S.C. § 3664(f)(2) textually requires the sentencing **court** to specify "the manner in which, and the schedule according to which, the restitution is to be paid," considering the defendant's financial resources, projected earnings, and financial obligations. 18 U.S.C. § 3572(d) contains parallel court-must-set-the-schedule language for fines. Parts A and B above are the case law interpreting those two statutes, and both are read uniformly (2d, 4th, 5th, 9th Circuits, at minimum) to forbid delegating the schedule/amount function to probation or the BOP.

**There is no equivalent statute for supervision fee/cost-contribution conditions.** 18 U.S.C. § 3583(d) (governing discretionary conditions generally) requires only that a condition be reasonably related to the § 3553(a) factors and involve no greater deprivation of liberty than reasonably necessary — it says nothing about who sets a dollar figure. This is exactly why Part A/B are analogies rather than direct authority, and exactly why Warden (Part C) was able to distinguish Albro/the restitution line so easily: there is no statutory hook comparable to § 3664(f)(2)'s explicit "the court shall" language to anchor a *textual* delegation argument for a "contribute to the cost of services" condition. The strongest available argument is therefore not statutory-textual but **structural/Article III** (fixing a defendant's financial obligation is inherently judicial, as Miller's "this principle equally applies to fines" and Gunning's "not to anyone else" language suggest) combined with the **Judicial Conference's own acknowledgment** that this exact drafting risk exists and should be avoided (part3-fees-financial.md, Task 1(b)).

## RECOMMENDATION ON THE MOTION'S CURRENT DELEGATION ARGUMENT

The client's/paralegal's flag is correct: **Matta, Browder, and Peterson do not hold that fixing a dollar figure is a non-delegable judicial function** — they address whether a condition may be imposed or how broadly it may sweep, not who sets a payment amount. I recommend **supplementing, not replacing**, the existing three-case citation, along these lines:

1. **Keep Matta/Browder/Peterson** for the general "only the sentencing court may impose/define a special condition" proposition — that proposition is still true and still useful as backdrop.
2. **Add Porter, Mortimer, and Kyles** (Second Circuit, binding) as the core authority for the *specific* proposition that fixing a financial obligation's amount and schedule is reserved to the judge — candidly flagged, in the motion itself if counsel judges it prudent, as restitution cases applied here by close analogy rather than direct holdings on supervision fee conditions.
3. **Add Miller (4th Cir.) and Merric (1st Cir.)** to show the principle is not restitution-specific — it reaches fines too, and Merric's fact pattern ("amount... determined... by the supervising officer") is the closest analog located to Bernecky's own conditions.
4. **Cite Gunning's "not to anyone else" language** if a rhetorically strong "core judicial function" quote is wanted; flag that it is MVRA-specific.
5. **Address Warden head-on rather than hoping the government doesn't find it.** Distinguish it affirmatively: even the one circuit to approve delegated treatment-cost administration required an explicit "ability to pay" standard as the safeguard — precisely the safeguard Judicial Conference policy calls for and precisely what is missing from all four of Bernecky's fee conditions. This turns the single most dangerous adverse case in this research track into a supporting citation for the requested relief (a court-approved sliding scale / ability-to-pay finding), rather than leaving it for the government to raise first.
6. **Do not claim any case squarely holds the amount-setting delegation for a "contribute to the cost of services" condition is unlawful** — no such case was found despite a thorough, multi-angle search. The honest, defensible framing is: (a) settled law forbids this exact kind of delegation for restitution and fines; (b) no court has yet extended that rule to supervision cost-contribution conditions; (c) the one court that considered a closely analogous claim rejected it, but only because — and precisely because — the condition it upheld had the ability-to-pay tether that Bernecky's conditions lack; (d) the Judicial Conference's own guidance treats the amount-delegation risk as real and to be avoided by design, which is independent, non-case-law support for the same relief.
