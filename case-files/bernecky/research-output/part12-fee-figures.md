## PART A — Bearden / Ability-to-Pay Analysis (Lead Section)

### A.1 Bearden v. Georgia, 461 U.S. 660 (1983) — the core holding, confirmed

Multiple independent sources (Wikipedia, Justia, Cornell LII, FindLaw, Fines and Fees Justice Center, and a USSC training document — via WebSearch synthesis; **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING against the actual U.S. Reports text, e.g., via the Cornell LII or Justia links surfaced: `law.cornell.edu/supremecourt/text/461/660`, `supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/461/660/`]**) converge consistently on the same holding:

- A sentencing court **may not automatically revoke probation** (and, by extension in the federal system, supervised release) **for failure to pay a fine, fee, or restitution** without first **inquiring into the reasons for the nonpayment**.
- If the probationer **"willfully refused to pay or failed to make sufficient bona fide efforts legally to acquire the resources to pay,"** the court may revoke and imprison.
- If the probationer **made bona fide efforts and still could not pay**, the court **must consider alternative measures of punishment** short of imprisonment, and may imprison only if the court affirmatively finds those alternatives inadequate to serve the state's interest in punishment and deterrence.
- Doctrinal basis: the Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process and Equal Protection Clauses — it is "fundamentally unfair" to revoke automatically where nonpayment is not the probationer's fault.

This is squarely on point for the restitution obligation in Bernecky's Judgment (10% of monthly gross income) and for the $100 special assessment. **The open question — and the one now prioritized — is whether it extends to the four separate "contribute to the cost of services rendered" / "pay the cost of monitoring services" conditions**, which are conceptually a different animal: they are not court-imposed fines/restitution calculated by the court itself, but open-ended, dollar-figure-free cost-shifting obligations to third-party vendors/providers, with the amount never fixed anywhere in the Judgment.

### A.2 Does Bearden extend to fee-for-service supervision conditions (treatment/monitoring/polygraph cost-contribution), as opposed to fines/restitution? — **Mixed: strong secondary-source support, but no squarely on-point Second Circuit holding located**

**What supports extension:**

1. **General doctrinal consensus in secondary literature.** WebSearch synthesis of commentary states: "While the Bearden case itself explicitly addressed the issue of fines, the Court's language refers to one's 'ability to pay' more broadly, and it is widely accepted that Bearden also applies to other court costs, including fees. This extends to treatment program costs and monitoring fees." This is *commentary*, not a holding, but it reflects a real and recurring argument in the fines-and-fees reform literature. **[VERIFY — identify the specific cases this commentary is generalizing from before relying on the "widely accepted" characterization in the motion.]**

2. **Yale Law Journal, Ryanne Bamieh, "Seeking Equity in Electronic Monitoring: Mounting a *Bearden* Challenge,"** 133 Yale L.J. Its core argument: **litigants should apply *Bearden*'s logic not only to explicit court fines but to the underlying costs associated with any release condition** (the article's running example is electronic monitoring — directly analogous to CIMP computer monitoring). The article's premise is that this is **not yet settled law** — it is an *advocacy* piece arguing courts *should* extend Bearden this way, which itself confirms the extension is **not automatic or already established**. **[VERIFY — pull the full article; it may collect citations to specific lower-court cases already applying Bearden to monitoring/program fees that would be directly useful.]**

The U.S. Courts "Overview of Probation and Supervised Release Conditions," Chapter 3 (Cybercrime-Related Conditions), states the **AO's own model/suggested condition language** for computer-monitoring cost-sharing as: *"You are responsible for computer monitoring and/or management costs for your approved computer devices **based on your ability to pay, as determined by the Probation/Pretrial Services Office**."* (via WebSearch synthesis of the official AO guidance page — **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING against the primary text]**.)
   - **Bernecky's actual CIMP condition contains no ability-to-pay qualifier at all** — it flatly states "The defendant will be required to pay the cost of monitoring services," full stop. Compare the AO's own template language above.
   - This is a strong, concrete textual argument independent of any need to prove Bearden's constitutional holding extends this far: **even under the Administrative Office's own contemplated model for this exact condition, an ability-to-pay determination is supposed to be built into the condition's operation. Bernecky's Judgment omits that qualifier entirely for all four fee-bearing conditions** (CIMP, sex-offense treatment, polygraph, mental health treatment).
   - The same Chapter 3 guidance for mental health/substance-abuse/sex-offense treatment conditions (already noted in the prior draft of this memo) states districts are "encouraged to establish a court-approved sliding scale for treatment services" responsive to the defendant's financial circumstances, and that condition wording must not "inadvertently delegate the establishment of the amount of the co-payment to the probation officer." **No sliding scale, ability-to-pay standard, or rate-setting procedure appears anywhere in Bernecky's Judgment for any of the four conditions** — which is itself the due-process/fair-notice gap, independent of whether Bearden's revocation-specific holding formally extends.

### A.3 Independent due-process/fair-notice argument — does not depend on winning the Bearden-extension question

Separate from whether *Bearden*'s revocation-hearing requirement formally extends, the **total absence of any payment methodology anywhere in the Judgment** is itself independently arguable as a defect:

- None of the four conditions specify **how the amount owed will be calculated** (flat fee? sliding scale? percentage of session cost? per-device?).
- None specify **who sets the rate** — the Judgment's silence, combined with AO guidance's own warning that condition wording must not "inadvertently delegate the establishment of the amount of the co-payment to the probation officer," raises the same **non-delegation concern already being developed for Special Condition (b)'s "follow the rules and regulations of that program" clause** (see `client-directives.md`, Special Condition (b) section, and the *Balon*/*Browder* delegation theory already in the file). The fee-amount-setting problem is arguably a companion non-delegation defect to the rules-setting problem already flagged.
- None specify **any ceiling, cap, or indigency carve-out** — contrast the AO's own model CIMP language ("based on your ability to pay, as determined by the Probation/Pretrial Services Office") and contrast Minnesota's statutory polygraph-cost framework (Minn. Stat. § 609.3456, which expressly allows a court/corrections official to waive the fee "for indigency or economic hardship" — cited here only as an example of how *some* jurisdictions build in an explicit safety valve where this Judgment does not).
- **Practical consequence:** Bernecky has no way, at sentencing or now, to know what he will actually owe, whether it scales with income, or what recourse exists if he cannot pay — which is the fair-notice problem underlying due-process challenges to vague or standardless conditions generally (the same family of concern already animating the CIMP delegation/vagueness theories at Special Condition (a) and the Special Condition (b) provider-rules delegation theory).

**This argument stands on its own** even if a court declines to extend *Bearden*'s formal revocation-hearing/willfulness framework to these conditions, because it is a fair-notice/non-delegation argument about the condition's text and structure, not solely a Bearden-revocation argument about what process is due before a hypothetical future revocation.

### A.4 Turner v. Rogers, 564 U.S. 431 (2011) — relevance assessed, ultimately a weaker fit

Confirmed holding (via WebSearch synthesis of Justia/Cornell/Wikipedia summaries — **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING against primary text]**): the Fourteenth Amendment Due Process Clause does **not** automatically require appointed counsel at a civil contempt hearing for a child-support obligor facing incarceration, provided the state supplies **alternative procedural safeguards** — specifically: (1) notice that ability to pay is the critical issue, (2) a fair opportunity to present and dispute financial information (e.g., a financial affidavit), and (3) an express court finding on the record as to ability to pay. Turner's incarceration there was vacated because South Carolina supplied none of these safeguards.

**Assessment of fit:** *Turner* is a civil contempt / child-support case, not a criminal supervised-release case, and its holding is narrowly about the counsel question, not a general ability-to-pay rule (that piece is closer to *Bearden*). It is best used, if at all, as a **secondary, by-analogy source for the specific procedural safeguards a court should build in** (notice, opportunity to present financial information, express ability-to-pay finding) if the motion requests that the court itself impose a defined procedure before any future revocation proceeding premised on nonpayment of these fee-bearing conditions — i.e., useful for drafting the *requested relief* (what procedural minimum should attach), less useful as authority for the antecedent question of *whether* Bearden's substantive rule extends to these conditions in the first place. **[VERIFY — confirm no Second Circuit case has already imported Turner's three-part safeguard framework into the supervised-release fee context before presenting this as a novel analogy.]**

### A.5 Bottom line on the redirected question

- **Bearden's core rule (no revocation for nonwillful nonpayment; must inquire into ability to pay; must consider alternatives) is squarely established** for the restitution and special-assessment obligations here.
- **Its extension to the four "contribute to cost of services rendered" / "pay the cost of monitoring services" conditions is a live, unresolved, but well-supported argument** — supported by (a) a direct law-review argument for exactly this extension (Bamieh, Yale L.J.), (b) the AO's own model CIMP condition language building in an ability-to-pay qualifier that Bernecky's Judgment conspicuously omits, and (c) general policy-level recognition (HRW, Fines and Fees Justice Center-adjacent commentary) that fee-for-service supervision conditions raise the identical indigency problem *Bearden* was designed to solve — but **no confirmed, directly on-point binding Second Circuit case was found extending it formally**, and the "probation absorbs the cost for indigent defendants" claim (A.2.5 above) requires direct verification before being relied upon, since I could not fetch the primary source this session.
- **Independent of the Bearden-extension question, the fair-notice/non-delegation argument (A.3) is available on the current record** and ties together with theories the client has already developed elsewhere in the file (Special Condition (a) and (b) delegation theories) — this may be the more solid near-term argument to lead with, while preserving the Bearden-extension argument as a complementary, more ambitious theory.
