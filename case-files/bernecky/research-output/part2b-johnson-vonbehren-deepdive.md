## PART 1 — What Johnson Actually Says (Task 1)

### 1.1 Case posture and the two grounds Johnson himself raised

Jeffrey A. Johnson was serving supervised release imposed by the **U.S. District Court for the Northern District of New York** (Thomas J. McAvoy, J.) following a conviction for sexual predation against minors. On appeal, **Johnson challenged the polygraph condition on exactly two grounds, and no others**:

1. That without use/derivative-use immunity, the polygraph condition put him to "the dilemma of confessing his sins or remaining silent" and going to prison for a supervised-release violation — a pure Fifth Amendment compulsion argument; and
2. That polygraph testing is unreliable and not "reasonably related to the purposes of sentencing" — a § 3583(d) merits/reliability argument, unrelated to the Fifth Amendment.

The panel was Circuit Judges **Jacobs** (writing), **Cabranes**, and **Sack**; argued October 17, 2005, decided May 1, 2006. The court expressly noted this was a case of **first impression** in the circuit: *"We have never considered whether polygraph testing is a condition that satisfies this test, nor have we considered whether such a condition offends the Fifth Amendment right against self-incrimination."*

**This framing already matters enormously for the client's pushback.** Johnson did not raise, brief, or argue (a) a right to counsel to assess privilege question-by-question in real time, or (b) a right to make a blanket, prospective, categorical refusal to answer any polygraph question at all. A court cannot be faulted, or credited, for "resolving" arguments that were never presented to it. Every description of Johnson's holding recovered this session — across independent secondary sources that agree with each other — describes only the immunity/waiver theory and the reliability theory as Johnson's grounds. There is no secondary-source trace anywhere of Johnson's briefing or the panel's opinion touching a counsel-assistance theory or a categorical-refusal theory.

### 1.2 The mechanism the district court used, and what the Second Circuit approved

The district court (McAvoy, J.) had crafted the condition with a specific safeguard: the polygraph's scope was limited to "information necessary for supervision, case monitoring, and treatment," and the court added that **"if a truthful answer would expose him to a prosecution for a crime different from the one on which he was already convicted,"** Johnson would preserve his **"right to challenge in a court of law the use of such statements as violations of his Fifth Amendment rights,"** and that by answering he would **"not be waiving his Fifth Amendment rights with respect to any criminal prosecution unrelated to the conviction for which he is now on supervised release."** The Second Circuit's holding was that **these safeguards were sufficient** to survive Johnson's immunity/compulsion argument, given (i) the safeguards' no-waiver/preserve-the-challenge structure and (ii) Johnson's own individualized history — his treatment provider found his attitude "superficial and not adequately engaged in the recovery process," and the panel reasoned that "the case for use of polygraph testing is strong" on facts showing a defendant who "apparently resisted honest self-assessment." The panel also held the appeal **ripe** even though Johnson was not yet subject to the condition, because he faced **"a sufficiently direct and immediate dilemma... as he seeks to determine whether exercising his Fifth Amendment privilege in response to questions by his probation officer will result in revocation of his supervised release."**

This is, word for word, the model embedded in the Judgment in this case, and the prior memo was correct that Johnson blesses it as a *sufficient* answer to the *narrow* compulsion argument Johnson raised. **But "sufficient to defeat the argument actually raised" is not the same thing as "comprehensive resolution of every Fifth Amendment theory a polygraph condition could ever present," and the text recoverable this session supports the client's read on that distinction** — see 1.3–1.5 below, addressing the client's three specific questions.

### 1.3 Task 1(a) — Did Johnson address a defendant's need for counsel to determine, in real time, whether a specific question is privileged?

**No.** Nothing in any independently corroborated account of Johnson's opinion — the ripeness discussion, the immunity/waiver discussion, or the reliability discussion — mentions counsel, the right to counsel, or the practical difficulty of a lay defendant making an unassisted, real-time legal judgment about whether a specific polygraph question crosses the privilege line. Johnson's entire Fifth Amendment analysis is about the *legal sufficiency of the no-waiver/preserve-the-challenge mechanism as a matter of immunity doctrine* — i.e., whether the district court's safeguard was enough to prevent an unconstitutional forced choice between silence-as-violation and self-incrimination. It is not about *who* makes the in-the-moment privilege call, or *whether a layperson can reliably make it at all*. The client's point — that "preserve a later challenge" does nothing to solve the distinct problem of an unassisted defendant having to correctly identify, in real time and under pressure, which specific question is privileged — is a problem Johnson's opinion never engages, because neither party asked it to. This is a live gap, not a foreclosed one.

### 1.4 Task 1(b) — Did Johnson address a defendant's ability to issue a blanket, prospective, categorical refusal to answer ANY polygraph question at all?

**No.** Johnson's Fifth Amendment argument, as reconstructed from every source recovered this session, presupposes that Johnson would sit for the exam and answer — his objection was that answering without immunity was constitutionally infirm, not that he was entitled to refuse to participate in the exam altogether pending some set of preconditions. There is no secondary-source trace of Johnson (the litigant) ever asserting a categorical, advance, blanket refusal theory, and consequently none of the panel's Fifth Amendment analysis is addressed to that theory. Johnson's framework (limited-scope questions + no-waiver + preserve-a-later-challenge) is a mechanism for handling **individual answers to individual questions actually given**, not a mechanism for evaluating (or rejecting) a **prospective refusal to give any answer at all**. Bernecky's posture — a categorical advance invocation as to the entire examination, conditioned on protections (I)–(IV) being in place first — is a materially different procedural posture that Johnson's holding, by its own terms, does not reach.

### 1.5 Task 1(c) — Is Johnson a narrow, fact-bound holding, or does it purport to comprehensively resolve every Fifth Amendment question a polygraph condition can raise?

The textual evidence recovered this session supports the **narrower** reading, for several independent reasons:

1. **Johnson expressly frames itself as a case of first impression** resolving the two specific arguments actually presented ("We have never considered whether... nor have we considered whether..."). First-impression framing that specifically enumerates the questions being decided is itself evidence the court understood its holding to be bounded by what was briefed.
2. **The panel's affirmance is explicitly tied to Johnson's individualized facts** — his documented history of "resist[ing] honest self-assessment" and his treatment provider's assessment that he was not "adequately engaged." The court did not announce a categorical rule that all sex-offense polygraph conditions survive Fifth Amendment scrutiny regardless of the individual defendant's posture or the specific mechanism used; it upheld *this* safeguard on *these* facts.
3. **The ripeness holding is itself a signal of a narrow, procedurally cautious opinion** — the panel took pains to explain why the specific dilemma before it (revocation risk from choosing between silence and disclosure) was ripe, rather than treating the Fifth Amendment question as an abstract, fully resolved area of law needing no case-specific analysis going forward.
4. **No secondary source recovered this session describes Johnson using language purporting to foreclose future, differently-postured challenges** (no "we hold that all such conditions are constitutional" or equivalent categorical language appears in any of the many independent excerpts pulled). Every account is instead framed around the specific safeguard the district court crafted and whether *that* safeguard sufficed.

**Bottom line for Task 1:** Johnson forecloses the narrow argument Johnson himself raised (bare lack-of-immunity compulsion, answered by a no-waiver/preserve-the-challenge clause) and forecloses a facial reliability challenge to polygraph testing generally. It does **not**, on the textual evidence available, foreclose (a) a counsel-assistance/real-time-privilege-determination theory, or (b) a blanket/prospective/categorical refusal theory. The client's characterization — that Johnson reads more like "we are not deciding this broader question" than a comprehensive resolution — is well-supported by what Johnson actually argued, what the panel actually addressed, and the fact-bound, first-impression framing of the opinion. **This corrects the prior memo's headline conclusion**, which was accurate as far as it went (Johnson does foreclose the narrow immunity/waiver argument) but overstated its reach by treating it as foreclosing the client's actual, differently-shaped theories.

## PART 2 — Murphy's "Penalty Situation" Reframed: The Structure Itself, Not Just Von Behren's Facts (Task 2)

### 2.1 The doctrinal core, reframed as the client wants it

*Minnesota v. Murphy*, 465 U.S. 420 (1984), holds that an unconditional "must answer" obligation does not itself compel self-incrimination — the privilege is not self-executing and ordinarily must be invoked — **unless** the state "either expressly or by implication[] asserts that invocation of the privilege would lead to revocation," which "would... create[] the classic penalty situation," rendering the resulting statement compelled. Critically, Murphy's rule is not framed around *how* or *when* the invocation happens (case-by-case mid-exam, or categorically in advance) — it is framed around **whether the state has attached a revocation-strength penalty to the act of invoking**. Nothing in Murphy's language ties the "penalty situation" doctrine to the *timing or granularity* of the invocation. The doctrinal trigger is the threatened consequence (revocation), not the shape of the assertion (one question at a time versus all questions at once, mid-exam versus pre-exam).

### 2.2 Von Behren applies exactly this mechanism, and its "penalty situation" reasoning does not stop at the exam-room door

Von Behren refused to answer the sexual-history-polygraph questions as a set — his refusal was a refusal of the entire polygraph exercise as then constituted (he would not sign the treatment program's agreement / would not answer the mandatory questions), not a question-by-question, mid-exam series of individual invocations parsed live by an examiner. The government's response was categorical, not question-specific: refuse to answer (any of) the mandatory questions → expelled from the treatment program → treatment failure treated as a violation → remand to prison. The Tenth Circuit's own description of its holding, recovered this session in near-identical form across multiple independent sources: **"Murphy makes this case an easy one. It recognizes that a threat to revoke one's probation for properly invoking his Fifth Amendment privilege is the type of compulsion the state may not constitutionally impose."** And: **"The government's threat to revoke Mr. Von Behren's supervised release for his failure to answer potentially incriminating questions rises to the level of unconstitutional compulsion."**

Nothing in this reasoning is limited to a mid-exam, question-by-question invocation. The court's analysis runs entirely at the level of *the threatened consequence attached to invoking* — not at the level of *how many questions, or which questions, were being invoked as to*. **Read this way, Von Behren is not merely "the narrow fact pattern where three specific questions were overbroad" — it is an application of Murphy's penalty-situation doctrine to a refusal that was, in substance, categorical as to the exam Von Behren was being asked to sit for.** There is no textual basis, in anything recovered this session, for confining Murphy's penalty-situation logic to individualized, mid-exam invocations; the doctrine asks only whether revocation is the threatened price of invoking, a question that is answered identically whether the invocation is made one question at a time or all at once in advance.

### 2.3 The affirmative claim: the "answer now, invoke/challenge later" structure is *itself* the penalty situation, not just a workaround for one

This is the client's requested reframing, and it holds up on the doctrine: Murphy's penalty-situation analysis is triggered by *the state's implicit assertion that invoking equals revocation*. Bernecky's Judgment condition requires him to "answer the questions posed during the examination" — full stop — and only afterward gives him a *right to challenge the use of the statement*. **The structure does not permit Bernecky to decline to answer in the exam room; it requires the answer first.** If Bernecky (as he intends) refuses to answer at all, pending protections (I)–(IV), Probation's and the government's position — consistent with how every polygraph-refusal case in this genre has proceeded (Von Behren's own procedural history: refusal → program termination → threatened remand) — will be that this refusal is itself a violation of the condition ("shall answer the questions posed") independent of any specific question's content, exposing him to revocation for the invocation itself, not for any particular answer. **That is the textbook "classic penalty situation" Murphy describes — the condition, by its own "shall answer" mandate, expressly asserts that non-answering (i.e., invoking) triggers a violation finding, which is functionally identical to the implicit threat structure Von Behren found unconstitutional.** Johnson's no-waiver/preserve-a-later-challenge clause does not cure this, because it operates only *after* a defendant has already answered — it protects the answer's later use, but does nothing to protect a defendant who wishes to invoke *before* answering, at all, which is exactly Bernecky's stated posture.

### 2.4 Honest limits on this reframing

To be candid (as the prior memo was, and as this one should remain): no case recovered this session — in the Second Circuit, the Tenth Circuit, or elsewhere — has expressly held that "a blanket, prospective, advance refusal to participate in a polygraph exam at all is entitled to the same Murphy protection as a mid-exam, question-specific invocation." This is an extension of Von Behren's logic that the research recovered this session supports as *doctrinally sound and un-rebutted* (nothing found contradicts it), but it has not been expressly adjudicated as such anywhere located. Present it in the motion as **the correct application of settled Murphy/Von Behren doctrine to Bernecky's specific posture**, not as a holding any court has already announced verbatim. That is precisely the posture the client's three-part framework (facial / as-applied / factual-distinguishing) is built to handle, and precisely why an as-applied argument is the necessary companion to the facial one — see Part 3.

## PART 3 — The Three-Part Argument Structure (Task 3)

### 3(a) FACIAL

**The claim:** The Judgment's "answer now, invoke/challenge later" structure is unconstitutional *as applied to a blanket, prospective invocation backed by a revocation threat*, because Johnson never actually held otherwise.

**The argument:** Johnson approved a specific mechanism (no-waiver + preserve-a-later-challenge) as sufficient to answer the specific compulsion theory Johnson raised (lack of immunity). It did not purport to resolve, and on the textual record available did not in fact address, whether that same mechanism is constitutionally sufficient when a defendant does not answer at all — i.e., when the "penalty situation" is created not by the use of a specific answer later, but by treating the refusal to answer, as a categorical matter, as a condition violation in the first instance. Murphy's doctrine (Part 2) is triggered by the threatened consequence of invoking, not by the granularity of the invocation. A structure that compels an answer first and defers all Fifth Amendment protection to a later challenge to *use* of that answer does nothing for a defendant whose objection is to the compelled-answer-in-the-first-instance problem, not the subsequent-use problem. Facially, then, the structure fails Murphy as applied to exactly the posture Bernecky occupies (a defendant who will not answer at all without prior protections) even though it may well be adequate, and Johnson-approved, as applied to a defendant like Johnson who intends to answer and merely wants use-protection afterward.

### 3(b) AS-APPLIED

**The claim:** Bernecky's specific posture — categorical advance invocation, plus his criminal-history and treatment-stakes profile (see Task 5 findings below) — takes this case outside anything Johnson or any Brown-type Second Circuit case actually resolved.

**The argument:** Even if the facial argument is rejected, Bernecky's case does not resemble Johnson's on the only facts Johnson relied on. Johnson's affirmance was expressly grounded in Johnson's individualized history of "resist[ing] honest self-assessment" and his treatment provider's assessment that he was insufficiently engaged — the panel's Fifth Amendment holding is not free-floating; it is tied to that predicate. Bernecky presents no comparable predicate of documented dishonesty in treatment (the no-nexus argument already developed in Part2/A.5 of the prior memo, incorporated here by reference), and Bernecky's posture is not "will answer, wants later protection" (Johnson) but "will not answer at all, absent counsel, absent examiner-credential vetting, absent a non-disclosure order, until and unless those are in place" — a posture Johnson's panel never had before it and could not have evaluated under its own stated rationale. Layered onto this is the elevated-stakes fact pattern: Bernecky's prior federal CP conviction (2012) plus the instant federal CP conviction (2018) mean any polygraph-elicited disclosure carries materially higher revocation/re-prosecution stakes than it did for Johnson, who (per the available record) had one such conviction, not two federal CP dispositions plus a state SORA Level 2 classification following from them. That elevated-stakes posture is itself a fact Johnson's panel did not have and could not have weighed.

### 3(c) FACTUAL-DISTINGUISHING

Point-by-point comparison of Johnson's posture against Bernecky's:

| Dimension | Johnson (446 F.3d 272) | Bernecky (this case) |
|---|---|---|
| **What the defendant does at the exam** | Answers the questions posed; seeks only to preserve a later challenge to *use* of the answer | Refuses to answer *any* question, categorically, in advance, unless/until protections (I)–(IV) are in place |
| **Theory of Fifth Amendment violation raised** | Lack of use/derivative-use immunity before answering | (1) No counsel to assess privilege in real time on a pure question of law; (2) the compelled-answer-first structure is itself the "penalty situation," independent of any specific answer's later use |
| **Timing of the constitutional objection** | Post-answer (protect the statement's use later) | Pre-answer (protect against being forced to answer at all without safeguards first) |
| **Individualized predicate relied on by the court** | Documented history of resisting honest self-assessment; treatment provider assessment of insufficient engagement | No comparable predicate — no documented instance of dishonesty in treatment or supervision (see prior memo, Part A.5) |
| **Prior record / stakes of a wrong answer** | One documented predicate offense (per available record) | Prior 2012 federal CP conviction *and* instant 2018 federal CP conviction, i.e., a second federal disposition in the same offense family, plus a NY SORA Level 2 civil classification arising from the 2012 conviction (see Task 5) |
| **Relief sought** | Use-immunity-style safeguard layered onto an answer he intends to give | Categorical non-participation until counsel, examiner-credentialing, and non-disclosure protections are secured |
| **What the panel actually decided** | That the district court's specific no-waiver/preserve-challenge safeguard was constitutionally sufficient to permit compelling an answer, on Johnson's specific facts | Not addressed — Bernecky's posture was never presented to, or resolved by, the Johnson panel |

**Conclusion for Part 3:** Properly read, Johnson is not the ceiling on this argument the prior memo suggested. It resolves a materially different question, on a materially different record, using a mechanism (post-answer use-protection) that does not reach Bernecky's actual objection (pre-answer, categorical, counsel-and-credentialing-gated non-participation). The facial, as-applied, and factual-distinguishing arguments should be pleaded together, in that order, exactly as the client requested — not folded into a single argument, and not conceded as foreclosed by Johnson.

**Independently corroborated this session via a U.S. Department of Justice (W.D.N.Y. U.S. Attorney's Office) press release, "Pittsford Man Sentenced On Child Pornography Charges,"** and cross-checked against the Justia/nycourts.gov indexing of the Fourth Department SORA decision:

- **2012:** Bernecky was convicted (federal) of **"possession and distribution of child pornography"** and sentenced to **5 years in federal prison** — this is docket 6:11-CR-06045, the "prior conviction" referenced in the instant offense's title, and the case whose supervised-release violation the 2018 Judgment concurrently resolved.
- He was released in 2016 and began a lengthy term of supervised release under that 2012 case.
- On 12/8/2017, a forensic search (following discovery of an unauthorized smartphone) recovered ~200 images and 25 videos of child sexual abuse material, leading to the instant 2018 offense/Judgment (10 years imprisonment, 10 years supervised release).
- **People v. Bernecky, 161 A.D.3d 1540 (4th Dept. 2018)** is a **New York State SORA risk-level classification proceeding** — a civil/administrative registration determination, not a criminal prosecution or conviction. Per indexed summaries, the Fourth Department's order determined Bernecky to be a **"level two risk"** under the Sex Offender Registration Act, and addressed the standard for a "downward departure" from the presumptive risk level based on an "exceptional" response to treatment. SORA risk-level proceedings are triggered by an underlying sex-offense conviction requiring registration (here, almost certainly the 2012 federal conviction, since that is the conviction that would have first required Bernecky to register in New York) — **the SORA case is not itself a separate conviction and should never be counted as one.**

### 5.2 Answer to the client's question

**"Two prior sex offense convictions" (as a count of convictions preceding the instant offense) is not supported by the record assembled here.** The documented record shows exactly **one** prior sex-offense conviction before the instant offense: the 2012 federal conviction for possession and distribution of child pornography (docket 6:11-CR-06045). The instant 2018 offense is the **second** federal CP conviction in Bernecky's history, but it is the offense presently before the court on this motion — not a "prior" relative to itself. The SORA Level 2 classification and *People v. Bernecky* are a civil registration-classification proceeding arising out of the 2012 conviction, not an independent criminal conviction, and should not be counted toward any tally of "convictions."

**Most likely explanation for the client's "two" framing:** the client is almost certainly counting the 2012 conviction and the instant 2018 conviction together as "two" — which is accurate as a count of **total federal CP convictions in Bernecky's history (2012 + 2018 = two)**, but is **not accurate, and should not be phrased, as "two prior convictions"** relative to the instant offense, since that phrasing implies two convictions *before* this one. **Recommended phrasing for the motion:** "Mr. Bernecky was convicted of child-pornography offenses in both 2012 and the instant 2018 case — this is his second such federal conviction, following one prior federal conviction for possession and distribution of child pornography" (or equivalent), rather than "two prior convictions." This is more accurate, does not overstate the record, and still fully supports the client's underlying substantive point — that Bernecky's stakes are meaningfully elevated relative to a first-time offender like Johnson, and that any further polygraph-elicited disclosure carries real incremental risk given this is not his first CP conviction.
