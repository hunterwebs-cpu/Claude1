## 1. Moore v. City of East Cleveland, 431 U.S. 494 (1977)

**Binding status:** U.S. Supreme Court — binding on all courts, including the sentencing court and the Second Circuit.

**Holding (as reconstructed from multiple independent secondary sources — Wikipedia, Justia case summary, Cornell LII, Encyclopedia.com, Casebriefs — that are highly consistent with each other and with the case's well-established treatment in constitutional law scholarship):**

Justice Powell's plurality opinion struck down an East Cleveland, Ohio housing ordinance that restricted occupancy of a dwelling to members of a single "family" as narrowly defined by the ordinance — a definition that excluded a grandmother (Inez Moore) living with her grandson, whose cousin relationship to another grandchild in the household did not fit the ordinance's nuclear-family definition. The Court held the ordinance unconstitutional as applied.

Key holding elements:
- The Due Process Clause protects "freedom of personal choice in matters of marriage and family life," and this protection is **not confined to the nuclear family**. Per the plurality: the "choice of relatives in this degree of kinship to live together may not lightly be denied by the State," and the sanctity-of-family line of cases "has consistently been interpreted broadly" to protect extended-family living arrangements, not merely "an arbitrary boundary drawn at the limits of the nuclear family."
- Because the ordinance intruded on "the sanctity of the family," it was subject to **"careful scrutiny"** of the government's proffered justification — not mere rational-basis review. The city's asserted interests (avoiding overcrowding, traffic/parking congestion, easing the burden on the school system) were held to be served "only marginally" by the ordinance and did not survive that scrutiny.
- The opinion was a **plurality** (Powell, joined by Brennan, Marshall, and Blackmun in relevant part), with Justice Stevens concurring in the judgment on narrower (irrebuttable-presumption/arbitrary-classification) grounds and four justices dissenting (White, Stewart, Rehnquist, Burger, in various combinations). This plurality status matters: *Moore*'s "careful scrutiny" standard is often cited as persuasive/foundational rather than treated as announcing a single clean test with five votes behind identical reasoning.

**Verification status:** Holding and "careful scrutiny" language confirmed consistently across five-plus independent secondary sources (Wikipedia, Justia, Cornell LII case index, Encyclopedia.com, Casebriefs/Quimbee summaries). I was not able to retrieve the slip opinion text directly (WebFetch 403 on Justia, Cornell LII, and Oyez) to confirm the *exact* verbatim wording and page pincites for the quotations above. **The quoted phrases ("careful scrutiny," "sanctity of the family," "arbitrary boundary drawn at the limits of the nuclear family") are consistently reported across sources as close paraphrases/quotations of the actual opinion language, but exact pincites (431 U.S. at ___) could not be confirmed. [VERIFY BEFORE FILING — confirm exact quotation and pincite against 431 U.S. 494 before using as a direct quote in the motion.]**

**Application to Bernecky's facts:** *Moore* is the strongest available Supreme Court authority for the proposition that constitutionally protected family-associational interests extend beyond the nuclear family to collateral relatives — aunts, uncles, cousins, nieces, nephews, grandchildren. This directly supports the argument that Special Condition (c)'s carve-out, which protects only Bernecky's own biological/adopted children (a null set, since he has none), fails to account for a constitutionally cognizable family-associational interest in contact with his nieces, nephews, and other minor relatives. *Moore* does not itself address supervised-release conditions or sex-offender restrictions, and it does not hold that this interest is absolute — it requires "careful scrutiny" of the government's justification, which is exactly what the client's argument says never happened here (no individualized nexus-to-danger finding). Use *Moore* for the general constitutional proposition and the "careful scrutiny" framing; do not overstate it as directly controlling supervised-release conditions.

## 2. Troxel v. Granville, 530 U.S. 57 (2000)

**Binding status:** U.S. Supreme Court — binding.

**Holding (reconstructed from Justia, Wikipedia, Cornell LII, Oyez, and multiple family-law secondary sources, consistent across all):**

The Court (plurality opinion by Justice O'Connor, joined by Rehnquist, Ginsburg, and Breyer; six justices agreed on the judgment via different rationales) struck down, as applied, a Washington state statute that allowed *any* person to petition for visitation rights over a child at *any* time, with visitation to be granted whenever a court found it in the child's "best interest" — with no requirement of deference to a fit parent's own decision. The Court held this violated the substantive due process right of a fit parent (Granville) to make decisions concerning the "care, custody, and control" of her children, calling this "perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court." Because the Washington statute gave **no special weight to the fit parent's own determination** about which third-party associations were in the child's best interest, and instead subordinated the parent's decision to a judge's own view of the child's best interest, it swept too broadly.

**Verification status:** Holding confirmed consistently across five+ independent sources. Exact pincites for direct quotation not independently confirmed against primary text due to WebFetch failures. `[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — confirm pincites against 530 U.S. 57 before quoting directly.]`

**Candid assessment — this cuts both ways, exactly as flagged in the task:**

*Troxel* is a double-edged sword for Bernecky's argument, and it should not be cited without acknowledging the cut against him:

- **Supports Bernecky:** *Troxel* reinforces that decisions about a child's associations are a core parental liberty interest deserving heightened protection from government override — reinforcing the general principle that the state needs a strong justification before dictating who a child may or may not see.
- **Cuts against Bernecky:** *Troxel*'s right belongs to the **parent of the child**, not to the would-be third-party visitor. In the Bernecky fact pattern, the relevant *Troxel*-type right holders are the parents of the nieces/nephews (Bernecky's siblings) — deciding whether their own children may see their uncle. Special Condition (c) does not restrict those parents' right to decide; it restricts *Bernecky's* ability to have contact, leaving the parents free to decide their children may visit him if the condition is modified or waived. In other words, *Troxel* protects *parents'* authority to include Bernecky in their children's lives (an argument that cuts in Bernecky's favor derivatively, since the condition also burdens the parents' Troxel-protected choice to allow that association if they judge it appropriate and safe) but it is not, strictly speaking, *Bernecky's own* fundamental right under *Troxel*'s own holding. The better framing is: the condition burdens the *combined* associational interest of Bernecky and his willing family members (who, per the client's proposed relief, would be present and would have actual knowledge of the conviction) — with *Troxel* supplying the parents'-side authority and *Moore*/general familial-association doctrine supplying Bernecky's side.
- A prosecutor could also cite *Troxel* to argue that the very "special weight" due to a fit parent's judgment is precisely what the government is standing in for here: probation/the court is not overriding a parent's welcoming decision, but rather regulating the *offender's* conduct as a condition of a criminal sentence — a fundamentally different posture than a third-party visitation statute. This distinction (criminal supervision vs. private third-party visitation dispute) should be anticipated and addressed head-on in the motion rather than ignored.

**Application to Bernecky's facts:** Cite *Troxel* for the "care, custody, and control" fundamental-right framing and to bolster the argument that the *parents* of Bernecky's nieces/nephews (who would be the ones granting/facilitating contact under the requested carve-out) have their own constitutionally protected interest in deciding this is appropriate — not just Bernecky. Do not present *Troxel* as if it were squarely about a convicted offender's own right to contact extended family; be candid in the motion that *Troxel*'s holder-of-the-right is the parent, and frame the argument accordingly.

## 3. United States v. Myers, 426 F.3d 117 (2d Cir. 2005)

**Binding status:** Second Circuit — binding.

**Citation confirmed:** Yes — independently located via multiple sources (Justia: `law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/F3/426/117/627177/`; OpenJurist: `openjurist.org/426/f3d/117/united-states-v-myers`). Caption: *United States v. Duane Arthur Myers*, decided September 27, 2005 (argued March 24, 2005), on appeal from the **Western District of New York** — the same district as Bernecky's case.

**Facts:** Myers pleaded guilty to one count of receiving child pornography in interstate commerce (18 U.S.C. § 2252A(a)(2)(A)) and was sentenced to 78 months' imprisonment and 5 years' supervised release. As a special condition, the district court prohibited Myers from **"spending time alone with his [own] child absent advance authorization from the U.S. Probation Office."** Myers challenged this condition on appeal. Neither party received advance notice of the condition prior to sentencing.

**Holding, as reconstructed from consistent independent secondary-source reporting (Justia case summary, OpenJurist, and multiple case-brief aggregators, all in agreement):**

- The Second Circuit held that where a special condition of supervised release **implicates a fundamental liberty interest** (here, a parent's relationship with his own child), the district court must **"carefully examine"** the condition to ensure it is "reasonably related" to the applicable statutory factors and "involves no greater deprivation of liberty than is reasonably necessary" — and that **the application of this standard must reflect heightened constitutional concern**, not the ordinary abuse-of-discretion tolerance given to garden-variety conditions.
- The court held the district court must make **findings** as to (1) whether the defendant has a constitutionally protected liberty interest in the relationship at issue, and if so, (2) what deprivation of that liberty is actually necessary to achieve the sentencing goal(s) — i.e., an **individualized nexus finding**, not a generic recitation.
- The court noted that imposing a special condition implicating a potentially fundamental constitutional interest **"strongly counsels in favor of notice"** to the defendant before sentencing, both for the defendant's own benefit (opportunity to be heard) and so the district court can adequately **"think through"** a condition burdening those rights before imposing it.
- Because the government offered no evidence that Myers's own son (a male child) was in any danger from his father, and the district court made no findings explaining how the condition would deter misconduct toward that child, the record did not permit the appellate court to assess the condition's validity. **The Second Circuit vacated the condition and remanded for resentencing** consistent with the opinion.

**Verification status:** The core holding — heightened-scrutiny standard, individualized-findings requirement, notice requirement, vacatur/remand disposition — is corroborated consistently across at least four independent secondary sources search-derived from Justia and OpenJurist summaries (which themselves purport to draw from the actual opinion text) plus case-brief aggregators, with no contradictory account found anywhere in the research. I was **not able to WebFetch the slip opinion directly** (403 on every attempt against Justia, OpenJurist, Leagle) to confirm exact pincites (e.g., which page the "reasonably related"/"no greater deprivation" quote appears on, or the exact wording of the notice-requirement passage). `[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — confirm exact quoted language and pincites (e.g., 426 F.3d at 124-25 or wherever the relevant passages fall) against the F.3d reporter or a Westlaw/Lexis pull before quoting Myers directly in the motion.]`

**Application to Bernecky's facts — this is the single most important case for this condition:**

*Myers* is on all fours with the structural defect the client identifies in Special Condition (c), for three independent reasons:

1. **Same district, same circuit, same kind of condition.** *Myers* arose in the W.D.N.Y. — the same sentencing district as Bernecky — and involved a nearly identical condition structure: contact with a family member (there, the defendant's own child; here, by the condition's own terms, non-existent biological/adopted children, but functionally reaching all extended family) gated behind advance PO/court authorization.
2. **Myers requires exactly the individualized nexus finding the client says never happened.** The Judgment in Bernecky's case contains no finding — on its face — that Bernecky poses any risk to his nieces, nephews, or any specific family member, nor any finding of what "deprivation of liberty" is actually necessary given his history. *Myers* holds that is precisely the analysis a heightened-scrutiny standard demands before such a condition may stand, and the Second Circuit vacated for the district court's failure to do exactly this.
3. **The "no notice" defect may recur here.** *Myers* flags the absence of advance notice to the defendant of a condition implicating a fundamental liberty interest as an independent problem, both for the defendant's participation and for ensuring the sentencing court actually "thought through" the condition. This is worth investigating factually (via PACER/sentencing transcript) — did the Judgment or a PSR addendum give Bernecky advance notice of Special Condition (c) prior to the 2018 sentencing, or was it presented without prior notice the way it was in Myers?

**Important candid distinction to build into the motion:** *Myers* addressed a condition restricting contact with the defendant's **own** child — a relationship as to which the "constitutionally protected liberty interest" question was at least debatable but presumptively strong (parent-child). Bernecky's case is one step removed: the family members at issue are nieces, nephews, and other extended relatives, not his own children. The government will likely argue *Myers*'s heightened-scrutiny holding was keyed to the parent-child bond specifically and doesn't automatically extend to collateral relatives. The correct response, using *Moore* (above) as the bridge, is that the "familial association" interest recognized in constitutional doctrine has never been confined to parent-child relationships (see *Moore*'s extension to grandparent-grandchild and cousin relationships), and that *Myers*'s framework — carefully examine, require individualized findings, require notice — is the right *methodology* to import here even if the precise "how fundamental is this specific family bond" question requires additional argument for the extended-family context. This is an extension argument, not a direct-holding argument, and the motion should be candid about that gradation.

## 4. United States v. Bleau, 930 F.3d 35 (2d Cir. 2019)

**Binding status:** Second Circuit — binding.

**Citation confirmed:** Yes — cited consistently and repeatedly, with a specific pincite of **930 F.3d 35, 42–43**, across multiple independent secondary sources discussing the case (case commentary aggregators, and citing discussions in other Second Circuit opinions — *United States v. Forney*, and others — that quote Bleau directly).

**Facts:** The Second Circuit affirmed a below-Guidelines 78-month sentence for **receipt and possession of child pornography**, but **vacated/remanded** as to a special condition of supervised release **prohibiting contact with minors**, because — per the consistently reported holding — **"the offense conduct did not entail inappropriate contact with minors."** There is no indication in any source located that Bleau had a prior contact offense or any allegation of hands-on conduct with a minor; the condition was imposed on a pure CP-possession/receipt record.

**Holding, as reconstructed from multiple independent, mutually consistent sources:**

- **A district court must ordinarily conduct an individualized assessment of the necessity of a special condition of supervised release** before imposing it.
- **Unless the justification is obvious from the record, a district court's failure to articulate its reasons for imposing a special condition is error** — this is the core, frequently-cited holding, pincited to 930 F.3d at 43.
- Applying that rule to the no-contact-with-minors condition specifically, the Second Circuit held the condition's justification **was not obvious from the record** where there were **no allegations of inappropriate contact with minors** — i.e., a pure-possession CP record, standing alone, does not automatically justify a blanket contact-with-minors restriction without further explanation from the sentencing court.
- One source (discussing *United States v. Forney*, a later Second Circuit case quoting Bleau) reports the language: **"While there may be reasonable arguments to support the imposition of this special condition, we cannot conclude that, on the present record, this condition is warranted"** — attributed to the Bleau court's own reasoning, and separately: **"The District Court's failure to explain its rationale is plain error because it is not obvious from the record why such a condition was reasonably necessary."**

**Verification status:** The core "individualized assessment" / "not obvious from the record" holding, with the 930 F.3d at 42–43 pincite, is corroborated across at least three independent sources, including at least one (the Forney discussion) that purports to quote Bleau directly rather than merely paraphrasing it. I was **not able to retrieve the Bleau slip opinion itself** to confirm the quoted language firsthand. `[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — confirm the "While there may be reasonable arguments..." and "not obvious from the record" quotations, and the 42-43 pincites, against the F.3d reporter directly.]`

**Application to Bernecky's facts — important candid distinction:**

*Bleau* is directly useful for the general **"individualized assessment required, silence is plain error"** principle, and it is squarely a contact-with-minors case, which makes it more directly on point than *Myers* in that specific sense. But there is a material factual distinction that must be addressed honestly rather than glossed over:

- **Bernecky's record is not a clean first-offense CP-possession case like Bleau's.** Bernecky's offense of conviction is expressly captioned as "Possession of Child Pornography Involving Prepubescent Minors, **Following a Prior Conviction** for Possession and Distribution of Child Pornography" (18 U.S.C. § 2252A(a)(5)(B), (b)(2)) — meaning this is (at least) his **second** CP-related federal conviction. The government will argue this recidivism record, unlike Bleau's apparent single-offense record, supplies at least some "obvious from the record" basis for *some* contact-with-minors restriction, distinguishing Bleau.
- **The client's requested relief is not elimination of the condition — it is a narrower carve-out.** This is actually the more defensible position given the Bleau distinction: rather than argue (as Bleau's defendant successfully did) that the entire contact-with-minors condition lacks any record basis, the stronger and more honest argument for Bernecky is that *even accepting* some restriction is justified by the recidivism record, the specific **scope** of the restriction — categorically excluding all contact with any extended-family minor absent case-by-case advance approval, with no exception for supervised/accompanied contact — is broader than reasonably necessary, and Bleau's "individualized assessment" / "no greater deprivation than necessary" framework applies with full force to that scope question even if it might not support wholesale elimination.
- Use Bleau primarily for the **procedural principle** (individualized assessment required; unexplained blanket conditions are plain error) and for underscoring that the Second Circuit does **not** treat contact-with-minors conditions as automatically justified merely because the underlying offense is CP-related — some nexus explanation is required, and by extension, some nexus explanation is required for *how broad* the family-contact restriction needs to be, not just whether one exists at all.

## 5. United States v. Dupes, 513 F.3d 338 (2d Cir. 2008)

**Binding status:** Second Circuit — binding.

**Citation confirmed:** Yes — independently located (CourtListener: `courtlistener.com/opinion/1270919/united-states-v-dupes/`; also referenced across Justia's 2008 Second Circuit case index).

**Facts (important and somewhat surprising — confirmed independently, candidly reported):** Dupes's *instant* offense of conviction was **securities fraud and conspiracy to commit securities fraud** (not a sex offense) — he was sentenced to 38 months' imprisonment and 3 years' supervised release before Judge William H. Pauley III (S.D.N.Y.). Despite the instant offense being non-sexual, the district court imposed special conditions tied to Dupes's **prior sex-offense history**, including: sex-offense treatment, sex-offender registration, a restriction on contact with children and places children congregate, and a restriction on internet use to access child pornography or solicit minors. Special Condition 10 specifically **barred Dupes from having "deliberate contact with any child under the age of seventeen"** and required him to stay more than **100 feet from "places primarily used by children," such as schoolyards, playgrounds, and arcades** — language extremely close to (though not identical in the age cutoff to) Bernecky's Special Condition (c).

**Holding, as reconstructed from multiple independent sources (CourtListener summary snippets, secondary discussion of the case's core holding, and a related case — *United States v. Gibson*, 9th Cir. — that itself discusses and applies the same "primarily used by children" phrasing as not unconstitutionally vague):**

- The Second Circuit held that 18 U.S.C. § 3583(d) **authorizes a district court to impose conditions tied to a defendant's history/characteristics — including a prior, uncharged-in-the-instant-offense sex-offense history — even where the instant conviction is for an unrelated (non-sex) offense**, so long as the conditions are not overly broad or vague and are otherwise reasonably related to the statutory sentencing factors.
- The court rejected Dupes's argument that these conditions were an unauthorized/successive punishment for his prior sex offenses (Double Jeopardy/Tenth Amendment theory) — holding instead that the conditions were an authorized part of the sentence for the securities-fraud conviction, reasonably related to "his history and characteristics as a sex offender, his need for treatment, and the public's need for protection from him."
- On the **vagueness/specificity** question (directly relevant to Bernecky's Special Condition (c), which uses identical "primarily used by children" / "schoolyards, playgrounds, arcades" language): the "places primarily used by children" formulation has been treated, in the *Dupes* line of cases and in parallel authority from other circuits, as **sufficiently specific to survive a vagueness challenge** — it gives a person of ordinary intelligence fair notice of what is prohibited (a school, playground, or arcade, as opposed to, e.g., a public park or grocery store that children merely sometimes visit).

**Verification status:** The core facts (securities-fraud instant offense, prior sex-offense-based conditions, Special Condition 10's near-identical text to Bernecky's condition) are independently confirmed via CourtListener's own case metadata/snippets, which is a primary-source aggregator (though I could not get past its 403 block to pull the full opinion text). The specificity/non-vagueness treatment of "primarily used by children" language is corroborated by a parallel case (*Gibson*) applying essentially the same standard, though I was **not able to confirm that *Dupes itself* contains an explicit vagueness holding on this exact phrase** — it is possible Dupes did not need to reach vagueness because Dupes's appeal focused on the Double Jeopardy/authorization theory rather than a vagueness challenge. `[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — confirm whether Dupes itself expressly addresses and rejects a vagueness challenge to "primarily used by children," or whether that specificity point is better sourced to a different case (e.g., Gibson, or a different 2d Cir. case) that does address vagueness directly. Do not represent Dupes as a vagueness holding unless this is confirmed against the full opinion.]`

**Application to Bernecky's facts:**

*Dupes* is useful for two distinct purposes, but candor requires flagging that it does **not** support the client's family-carve-out theory — if anything, it cuts the other way on the "how broad can this condition be" question:

1. **It confirms the loitering/100-foot/"primarily used by children" portion of Bernecky's condition tracks well-established, court-approved Second Circuit language** — this part of Special Condition (c) is likely *not* a fruitful target for a vagueness challenge, and the motion should not spend significant effort attacking that clause's specificity. Use Dupes narrowly, for the loitering-clause specificity point, and do not overstate its relevance to the family-contact carve-out argument.

## 6. United States v. Peterson, 248 F.3d 79 (2d Cir. 2001)

**Binding status:** Second Circuit — binding.

**Citation confirmed:** Yes — independently located (CourtListener: `courtlistener.com/opinion/773009/united-states-v-larry-peterson/`; OpenJurist: `openjurist.org/248/f3d/79/united-states-of-america-v-larry-peterson`). Arose from the **Western District of New York**.

**Facts:** Peterson was convicted of bank larceny and sentenced to five years of **probation**, with special conditions imposed based on a **prior state-court incest conviction** (not the instant offense). The conditions at issue were (1) broad computer/internet restrictions, and (2) a condition requiring Peterson to notify future employers of his conviction, with the manner/scope of notification left to the probation officer's discretion.

**Holding, as reconstructed from multiple independent sources:**

- **On the computer/internet condition:** the court held that broad restrictions on Peterson's computer ownership and internet access were **not "reasonably related"** to the nature and circumstances of the offense (bank larceny) or to Peterson's history and characteristics, and were not "reasonably necessary" to the § 3553(a)(2) sentencing purposes, because **there was no indication that Peterson's prior incest offense had any connection to computers or the internet.**
- **On the employer-notification condition:** the court held that the sentencing **court itself** — not the probation officer — must determine whether employer notification is required, and if the court delegates any part of the implementation to probation, it **may specify guidelines to direct the probation officer's discretion but may not simply leave the issue to the probation officer's unfettered discretion.** This is a **non-delegation** holding: the core judicial decision (whether/how much liberty-restricting notification is required) cannot be handed off standardless to an executive-branch officer.
- The Second Circuit **vacated in part and remanded for resentencing.**

**Verification status:** Both holdings (nexus requirement for computer/internet condition; non-delegation requirement for employer-notification condition) are corroborated across at least three independent sources (CourtListener metadata, OpenJurist, and a Federal Defenders of New York blog post discussing Peterson's internet-monitoring holding in later case law). Exact pincites not confirmed against primary text. `[VERIFY BEFORE FILING]`

## 7. The General Second Circuit Standard, and U.S.S.G. § 5D1.3

**Statutory framework (18 U.S.C. § 3583(d)):** A special condition of supervised release must be (1) reasonably related to the § 3553(a) sentencing factors (nature/circumstances of the offense, defendant's history/characteristics, deterrence, protection of the public, and the defendant's correctional/treatment needs); (2) involve **no greater deprivation of liberty than is reasonably necessary** to serve those purposes; and (3) be consistent with any pertinent Sentencing Commission policy statements. This statutory text is not in dispute and does not need independent web verification — it is the governing statute.

**Second Circuit gloss (confirmed via *Myers* and *Bleau*, both independently verified above, plus the broader line of cases those two belong to — *Sofsky*, *Johnson*, *Balon* — which are well-documented in multiple independent sources as the "individualized assessment" family of Second Circuit supervised-release cases):**

- **An individualized assessment is required, not a categorical rule.** Multiple independent sources (a CaseMine commentary on the "individualized assessment" doctrine, a Federal Defenders of New York blog post, and the *Bleau* holding itself) consistently confirm the Second Circuit's position that a **"careful and sensitive individualized assessment is always required"** before imposing conditions like internet bans or contact restrictions on a sex offender — the court has expressly **declined to hold that a categorical restriction is appropriate for any sex offender** merely because the offense involved similar conduct. This directly supports the client's "no nexus finding" argument: a categorical, offense-title-triggered restriction on all contact with all minor relatives, without any offense-specific or offender-specific finding of danger to those relatives, is not what the Second Circuit's framework permits.
- **Where a fundamental liberty interest is implicated, scrutiny is heightened** (this is *Myers*'s specific contribution, discussed above) — ordinary deferential review does not apply.
- **Failure to explain is independently reversible** where the justification is not "obvious from the record" (this is *Bleau*'s specific contribution).

**U.S.S.G. § 5D1.3 commentary on family-contact conditions specifically:** I was unable to retrieve the current Guidelines Manual text of § 5D1.3(d) directly (WebFetch 403 on ussc.gov). ## 8. The "Supervising / Knowledgeable Adult" Carve-Out as a Term of Art

**Finding: this is a recognized, real-world carve-out structure — not an invented one — with at least one confirmed federal appellate example closely on point.**

**United States v. DaSilva, 844 F.3d 8 (1st Cir. 2016).**

- **Binding status:** First Circuit — **persuasive only** in the Second Circuit, not binding, but directly on point and worth citing as persuasive authority.
- **Citation confirmed:** Yes, independently (FindLaw: `caselaw.findlaw.com/court/us-1st-circuit/1757702.html`; Justia: `law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/ca1/15-2103/15-2103-2016-12-16.html`).
- **Facts and holding, as reconstructed from independent sources:** DaSilva's supervised-release Special Condition 8 required that he **"have no contact with any child under the age of 18, with the exception of your own children, without the presence of an adult who is aware of your history and who is approved in advance by the probation officer."** This is materially similar in structure to Bernecky's condition (own-children exception; otherwise, PO-approved supervising-adult gate). On appeal, the First Circuit provided a **construction/clarification** of the condition rather than striking it: it held that "the restrictions on interaction with minors are not to be construed so strictly as to prohibit any manner of contact with other children," that **"associational restrictions are usually read to exclude incidental encounters,"** and gave the illustrative example that a defendant dropping his own child off at a birthday party, checking in only with the supervising adult and not speaking with other children, "likely should not be read to violate the conditions of his release." The court further suggested the district court likely did not intend to restrict contact with children who are "in the presence of or being supervised by other adults."
- **Verification status:** Confirmed via two independent sources (FindLaw case page, Justia docket page) plus a Prison Legal News article discussing the same holding, all consistent. Exact pincites for the quoted passages not confirmed against the F.3d reporter directly. `[VERIFY BEFORE FILING]`

**Application to Bernecky's facts:** *DaSilva* is directly useful for research task #7 in two ways: (1) it confirms that a "presence of an adult aware of the history" gate is a **real, recognized structure already used in federal supervised-release conditions** — Bernecick's proposed relief is not a novel invention, it mirrors condition language that other districts already impose as the *starting* condition (not merely as relief granted after litigation); and (2) it shows that even where such a condition still requires **advance PO approval** (as DaSilva's did, and unlike the broader carve-out Bernecky's client wants, which asks to drop the case-by-case advance-approval requirement for family gatherings), courts read in a **common-sense incidental-contact exception** that softens the practical bite of a strict-liability reading. This supports, at minimum, a fallback/alternative argument: even if the court declines to strike the advance-approval requirement altogether, it should adopt a *DaSilva*-style construction making clear that incidental family contact in the presence of a knowledgeable adult relative (the client's own example: a sister arriving with her kids at a family gathering) is not a per se violation requiring Bernecky to "evacuate like there's a fire."

I did not find any case or Guidelines/U.S. Courts policy document that **rejects** this kind of carve-out as improper — the concept appears to be an accepted, unremarkable feature of ordinary federal supervision drafting practice (the Georgia state-probation analog uses nearly identical language, for what limited value a state practice has here), not a fringe or disfavored one. This is a meaningful practical point: the requested relief is not asking the court to invent a new legal category, only to write Bernecky's condition the way many other sex-offender contact conditions are already written elsewhere, and to go one step further than *DaSilva* by removing the case-by-case advance-approval requirement for family gatherings specifically.

## 9. What This Research Supports — Recommended Relief for Special Condition (c)

**Strongest, most defensible version of the argument, built candidly from what this research actually shows (not overclaiming):**

1. **The current condition's family carve-out is functionally illusory and should be broadened**, because it excepts only Bernecky's biological/adopted children — a null set — while affording no protection at all to the extended-family relationships (nieces, nephews, siblings' children, grandchildren if any) that both *Moore* (extended family) and, derivatively, *Troxel* (parents' authority to include Bernecky in their children's lives) recognize as implicating constitutionally significant associational interests.
2. **No individualized nexus-to-danger finding appears anywhere on the face of the Judgment**, and *Myers* and *Bleau* — both Second Circuit, both independently verified above, both arising from records with no allegation of hands-on contact with minors — establish that (a) heightened scrutiny applies when a condition implicates a fundamental family-associational interest, and (b) a district court's failure to explain the necessity of a contact-with-minors restriction, where the offense conduct did not involve inappropriate contact with minors, is plain error. Bernecky's offense (CP possession, following a prior CP conviction) likewise involves no allegation of hands-on contact with any child — the same gap *Bleau* found dispositive. This is the strongest, most direct argument in the file for this condition.
3. **The honest limiting principle:** Bernecky's recidivist CP record (a second CP-related conviction) is a real distinguishing fact from Bleau's apparent single-offense record, and the government will likely argue it supplies at least some record-based justification for *some* restriction. The research therefore supports **not** arguing for wholesale elimination of the condition, but for the **narrower, more defensible relief the client has already proposed**: a carve-out permitting contact with minor relatives when in the presence of an adult (e.g., mother, father, sister) who has actual knowledge of the conviction, without a separate case-by-case advance-PO-approval requirement for that specific circumstance — modeled on, and going one step further than, the *DaSilva* First Circuit condition structure, which already reflects this kind of "knowledgeable supervising adult" gate as an accepted feature of federal supervision practice.
4. **A secondary, independent delegation argument** (via *Peterson*, used carefully and not overstated) can be layered on: the condition's "unless approved by the probation officer or by the Court" language, with no stated standard guiding that discretion, independently supports requiring either (a) court-articulated guidelines cabining what the PO may/must approve, consistent with *Peterson*'s non-delegation holding, or (b) removing the advance-approval requirement altogether for the narrow, low-risk category of supervised family contact the client has identified.
5. **The employability/no-occupational-restriction clarification** the client also requested for this condition (ordinary incidental workplace contact with a minor coworker or customer should not be read as "deliberate contact") is well-supported by *DaSilva*'s "incidental encounters are excluded" construction, applied by analogy from the family context to the workplace context, and should be requested as clarifying language regardless of how the family-carve-out argument itself fares.
6. **What this research does not support:** an argument that Bernecky has an unqualified, unrestricted right to contact with minor relatives regardless of circumstance, or that no restriction whatsoever may be imposed. Given his offense history, *Bleau*'s own facts (single-offense CP possession) are more favorable than Bernecky's (recidivist CP possession), and the honest reading of this line of cases is that some family-contact condition is likely sustainable — the argument should be pitched at narrowing the scope and removing the illusory-carve-out defect and the case-by-case pre-approval bottleneck for low-risk supervised contact, not at eliminating the condition outright.
