## TASK 1 — Plain-Error Review and Precedential Effect

### 1.1 The general doctrine, stated honestly (not a foregone conclusion either way)

The attorney's opening framing — that plain-error review "by its nature[] may not be capable of producing binding circuit precedent on the underlying legal question" — is **too strong as a categorical proposition**, but it is reaching for a real and well-recognized doctrinal distinction. The correct, narrower statement of the doctrine is:

**A published panel opinion's discussion of governing law binds future panels — regardless of the standard of review under which the case arrived — to the extent that discussion was actually, necessarily resolved by the panel and not merely assumed for the sake of argument.** This is not a special plain-error rule; it is the ordinary holding/dictum distinction, applied to a plain-error posture. The seminal articulation is *Webster v. Fall*, 266 U.S. 507, 511 (1925): "Questions which merely lurk in the record, neither brought to the attention of the court nor ruled upon, are not to be considered as having been so decided as to constitute precedents." A proposition a court assumes arguendo, without deciding, is not a holding on that proposition and does not bind later panels on it.

Plain error's own four-part structure (*United States v. Olano*, 507 U.S. 725, 732 (1993); *Puckett v. United States*, 556 U.S. 129, 135 (2009); accord *United States v. Marcus*, 560 U.S. 258, 262 (2010)) makes this distinction concrete rather than abstract, because the four prongs are sequential and a panel can dispose of an appeal at different points in the sequence:

- **Prong one — "there is an error."** To answer this, a panel must actually identify what the governing legal rule is and determine whether the district court's action violated it. A panel that resolves prong one by holding there was **no error at all** has necessarily decided, as a matter of law, what the rule is and that the district court's conduct satisfied it. That is a holding on the merits — full stop — and the fact that the procedural vehicle carrying it to judgment is "plain error" (because the point was unpreserved) does not diminish its stare decisis weight one bit. A merits ruling does not become non-precedential merely because forfeiture, rather than preservation, is what put the question before the court.
- **Prongs two through four — "clear or obvious," "affected substantial rights," "seriously affect[s] fairness/integrity."** A panel can also dispose of an appeal by **assuming, arguendo, that prong one is satisfied** (i.e., assuming an error occurred) and then resolving the case on the ground that any such error was not "plain," did not affect substantial rights, or does not warrant the discretionary remedy. This is the paradigm *Webster v. Fall* scenario: the panel never decides whether the district court was actually right or wrong; it assumes the defendant's premise and holds only that the premise, even if true, doesn't warrant relief on this record. That kind of disposition does **not** bind future panels on the assumed premise, because the premise was never actually adjudicated — it was borrowed for the sake of argument. The Second Circuit's own plain-error jurisprudence reinforces why this distinction matters: for an error to be "plain," it must be "clear or obvious ... under current law," and the Circuit will typically decline to find plain error where "the operative legal question is unsettled" (see, e.g., the *Weintraub* line of Second Circuit authority applying this principle). A panel that disposes of a case by saying only "the law was unsettled, so this wasn't plain" is, by definition, **declining to resolve** what the law actually requires — that disposition is intrinsically non-precedential on the merits, because unsettled law is the very reason the panel gave for not deciding it.

**So the doctrinal distinction the attorney is pointing to is real, and it does have a name in ordinary appellate practice** — it is simply the holding/dictum and "assumed-without-deciding" line of doctrine, applied to the specific procedural setting of plain-error review, rather than a bespoke plain-error-specific doctrine with its own separate label. Practically, the question in any given case reduces to: **did the panel's own sentences affirmatively resolve what the law is (category (i) in the attorney's framing — a genuine holding, fully precedential), or did the panel's own sentences merely assume a violation and hold only that it wasn't clear/obvious (category (ii) — non-precedential on the assumed premise)?** That is a close-reading question about the actual opinion, not a question that can be answered categorically for "plain-error opinions" as a class. This is exactly why Task 1(b) below — a fresh, sentence-level read of Brown's actual text — is the right way to resolve it, rather than assuming the answer from the standard-of-review label alone.

### 1.2 Applying the doctrine to Brown's actual text — the operative sentences, verbatim

Having re-pulled the complete slip opinion directly (not a secondary transcription), the full Part III.B passage, verbatim, is:

> "Brown also asserts that the component of the special condition restricting his 'use of any devices in the course of employment' is 'tantamount to an occupational restriction' since it 'effectively requires him to notify any prospective employers of his fraud conviction.' Brown Br. at 14, 17. **Because Brown did not object to this condition at sentencing, we once again review for plain error.** Dupes, 513 F.3d at 343.
>
> "Section 5F1.5(a) of the Sentencing Guidelines provides that the trial court may impose a special condition 'prohibiting the defendant from engaging in a specified occupation, business, or profession, or limiting the terms on which the defendant may do so' only if (1) 'a reasonably direct relationship existed between the defendant's occupation, business, or profession and the conduct relevant to the offense of conviction'; and (2) 'imposition of such a restriction is reasonably necessary to protect the public because there is reason to believe that, absent such restriction, the defendant will continue to engage in unlawful conduct similar to that for which the defendant was convicted.' U.S.S.G. § 5F1.5(a)....
>
> "Here, **the challenged monitoring condition is not an occupational restriction.** The condition does not bar Brown from a specific occupation, business, or profession, see U.S.S.G. § 5F1.5(a), restrict him from using a computer or computer equipment such that he would be functionally barred from occupations requiring such use, see United States v. Peterson, 248 F.3d 79, 83 (2d Cir. 2001), or compel Brown to notify his employers of the nature of his conviction. **And even if the condition functionally required Brown to notify future employers about the nature of his conviction, such a requirement would not transform the challenged monitoring condition into an occupational restriction** because the condition does not target 'a specified occupation, business, or profession.' U.S.S.G. § 5F1.5(a) (emphasis added).
>
> "... We thus **decline to follow Gorychka** here.
>
> "Given that the challenged computer-monitoring condition neither restricts Brown from 'specified occupations' nor functionally bars him from jobs requiring the use of a computer, **we find that this special condition is not an occupational restriction as contemplated by section 5F1.5. Accordingly, we conclude that the district court did not plainly err** in imposing this special condition of supervised release."

173 F.4th at 50-51 (emphasis added throughout to isolate the operative verbs).

### 1.3 Sentence-by-sentence classification: affirmative holding, or hedged plain-error language?

Reading this fresh, verb by verb, the passage is **overwhelmingly cast in unconditional, declarative, holding-type language, not hedged plain-error language**:

- "the challenged monitoring condition **is not** an occupational restriction" — unconditional declarative statement of law, not "we cannot say the district court plainly erred in concluding X."
- "The condition **does not** bar Brown ... **does not** target 'a specified occupation, business, or profession'" — declarative negatives about what the condition/statute actually is, not about the clarity of the district court's error.
- "We thus **decline to follow** Gorychka" — this is language of doctrinal engagement and rejection of contrary authority. Panels do not "decline to follow" other authority as a matter of unresolved assumption; this is the language of a court affirmatively choosing one reading of the law over another.
- "**we find** that this special condition is not an occupational restriction as contemplated by section 5F1.5" — an affirmative finding of law, stated in the panel's own voice, not attributed conditionally to what the district court might reasonably have believed.
- Only the **final clause** of the entire five-paragraph passage — "Accordingly, we conclude that the district court did not plainly err in imposing this special condition" — is cast in plain-error terms, and it functions as the mechanical, non-independent **consequence** of the holding already announced in the immediately preceding sentence ("we find that this special condition is not an occupational restriction"), not as an independent, freestanding hedge. If there is no error, it is a logical truism that there was no "plain" error either; that final sentence is doing no separate analytical work of its own.

**The one clause that could be mistaken for hedged language — "And even if the condition functionally required Brown to notify future employers ... such a requirement would not transform the challenged monitoring condition into an occupational restriction" — is not actually the hedge pattern the attorney is asking about, and the distinction matters.** The attorney's category (ii) is: "even if X might be Y, it was not plain/obvious error for the district court to conclude otherwise" — a hedge on the **clarity of law**, leaving the ultimate legal question (is X actually Y?) unresolved. Brown's "even if" clause is structured differently: it assumes arguendo a **factual predicate** (that the condition functionally compels notification) and, even granting that predicate, still reaches an **unconditional legal conclusion** ("such a requirement would not transform ... into an occupational restriction"). That is an a fortiori affirmative holding, not a hedge on legal clarity — the panel is not saying "it wasn't obvious whether functionally-compelled notification makes this an occupational restriction"; it is saying, flatly, that it does not, even on the defendant's own most favorable reading of the facts. This is, if anything, a **broader and more confidently stated** rule than a simple "notification isn't required here" holding would have been.

**A directly on-point contrast exists within the very same opinion, one section earlier, and it proves the panel knew how to write the hedged version when that is what it meant.** Part III.A (the separate overbreadth holding) contains genuinely hedged, exactly-attorney's-category-(ii) language: "On this record, **we cannot say that the district court plainly erred in concluding** that the monitoring condition was related to the factors outlined in section 5D1.3(b)." 173 F.4th at 49. That sentence does not say the condition *is* related to the § 5D1.3(b) factors as a matter of law; it says only that the district court's own conclusion to that effect was not plainly wrong — precisely the hedge the attorney is asking whether Brown contains. **It does contain that hedge — but only in Part III.A, not in Part III.B.** Part III.B's occupational-restriction passage never uses "we cannot say the district court plainly erred in concluding X" phrasing anywhere; it uses "is not," "does not," "we find," and "we decline to follow" — the language of a panel resolving the legal question itself, not merely assessing whether the district court's resolution of it was clearly wrong.

### 1.4 Honest bottom line on Task 1

**The attorney's underlying legal-doctrine premise is correct and identifies a genuine, recognized distinction in federal appellate practice** — plain-error dispositions that merely assume an error and hold it wasn't "plain" do not resolve the merits and do not bind future panels on the assumed premise (Webster v. Fall's "lurking question" principle, operating through plain error's own sequential-prong structure). **But applying that correct doctrine to Brown's actual text yields a result adverse to the attorney's hoped-for conclusion, not favorable to it.** Part III.B is written in the affirmative-holding register, not the hedged-assumption register; it affirmatively resolves the definitional question under § 5F1.5(a) (a condition must "target a specified occupation, business, or profession" to qualify), states that resolution three times in different words across the passage, expressly rejects a contrary summary order (Gorychka) on the merits of that resolution, and treats the "did not plainly err" conclusion as the automatic byproduct of that resolution rather than as an independent basis for affirmance. **This is a genuine holding, fully entitled to ordinary law-of-the-circuit weight on the specific proposition it announces, and the plain-error posture does not diminish that weight.** The correct point of attack is not "Brown's plain-error posture makes Part III.B non-precedential" — that argument should not be made, because a careful reader (including any judge reviewing the motion) will find it contradicted by the opinion's own text and will discount the motion's credibility on this point. The correct, sustainable point of attack remains the one already developed in Track 7: the rule Brown actually announced is narrow in what it covers (a single textual/definitional proposition about conditions that reference employment generically without naming an occupation), not narrow in its formal bindingness.

## TASK 2 — Brown's Actual Theory of Harm

### 2.1 How Brown's own brief framed the claim, verbatim

The Questions Presented section of Brown's appellant brief frames the entire occupational-restriction claim around notification, and only notification:

> "[require] that any computer devices that Appellant could use 'in the course of employment will be subject to monitoring restriction as permitted by the employer' —— **effectively requiring him to notify any prospective employer of the instant conviction**, which could be tantamount to an occupational restriction." Brown Appellant Br. at 2.

Point V of the brief (the operative argument section) confirms this is not an isolated phrase but the entire theory of the claim:

> "The monitoring of a defendant's work computers would likely **function as a de facto employer notification condition**: the condition allows Probation to install software on any computer device he will '[u]se ... in the course [of] employment' to monitor his use of the devices.
>
> "**This Court treats a condition of supervision requiring a defendant to notify an employer (or client) of his or her conviction as an occupational restriction.** See, e.g., United States v. Doe, 79 F.3d 1309, 1320-21 (2d Cir. 1996); United States v. Peterson, 248 F.3d 79, 85 (2d Cir. 2001). This is so because the Guideline governing 'occupational restrictions' defines such conditions as either 'prohibiting the defendant from engaging in a specified occupation' or 'limiting the terms on which the defendant may do so.' U.S.S.G. § 5F1.5(a) (emphasis added). **A notification condition falls within the latter.**" Brown Appellant Br. at 28-29 (emphasis added; footnote citing Souser, 10th Cir., and Britt, 9th Cir., both notification cases, omitted from quotation but confirmed present at n.6).

And the closing of the argument, tying the whole theory together:

> "Thus, this Court in Jenkins struck a special condition requiring that the defendant 'notify his prospective employer of the nature of his conviction and the fact that his conviction was facilitated by the use of a computer' if his employment involved a computer because the relationship between the restriction and his offense was not 'readily apparent.' Jenkins, 854 F.3d at 181, 187, 195.
>
> "**Here also, the relationship between the de facto employer-notification condition is not 'readily apparent.'** Jenkins, 854 F.3d at 185. Nothing in the record demonstrates that Mark Brown used a work computer to commit the instant offense, 'nor, indeed, was his crime connected in any way to his occupation.' See Lombardi, 727 F.App'x at 20-21.
>
> "Thus, **the condition requiring the monitoring of any computer devices Brown will [use] 'in the course of employment' is unlawful because his offense was unrelated to his job.**" Brown Appellant Br. at 30-31 (emphasis added).

**This fully confirms the attorney's factual premise, with no qualification needed.** Every authority Brown's brief cites for the occupational-restriction argument — Doe, Peterson, Jenkins, Lombardi, and the footnoted Souser and Britt — is a compelled-notification case. The brief never argues that monitoring itself (apart from any notification effect), or any burden imposed on the employer independent of notification, is what makes the condition an occupational restriction. The entire theory is: monitoring → functions as compelled notification → notification is a "term[] on which the defendant may [engage in his occupation]" under § 5F1.5(a)'s second prong → therefore an occupational restriction requiring the two-part nexus/necessity findings. There is no alternate or independent theory anywhere in the brief.

### 2.2 Is Brown's holding keyed specifically to the notification theory? — a careful, two-part answer

The panel's own framing of what it was rejecting mirrors the brief exactly: "Brown also asserts that the component of the special condition ... is 'tantamount to an occupational restriction' since it 'effectively requires him to notify any prospective employers of his fraud conviction.'" 173 F.4th at 50 (quoting Brown Br. at 14, 17). The panel plainly understood, and was answering, a notification-based theory. But the panel's disposition contains **two analytically separate rationales**, and they are not equally narrow:

**Rationale one (fact-specific, tied to notification):** "The condition does not ... compel Brown to notify his employers of the nature of his conviction." This is a factual/definitional rejection of the premise that Brown's own clause ("as permitted by the employer") even functions as compelled notification in the first place — a conclusion this office's own Task 2 analysis in part7 (§ 2.2-2.4) independently reached on textual grounds. This rationale is genuinely narrow and tied to notification; it says nothing about a condition (like Bernecky's) that imposes a wholly different kind of burden.

**Rationale two (general, definitional, burden-type-agnostic):** "[E]ven if the condition functionally required Brown to notify future employers ... such a requirement would not transform the challenged monitoring condition into an occupational restriction **because the condition does not target 'a specified occupation, business, or profession.'**" This is a broader rule, and it is **not, on its own terms, limited to notification-type burdens**. Its logic is: § 5F1.5(a) only regulates conditions that target a "specified occupation, business, or profession" by name; a condition that references employment generically (without naming a job, industry, or profession) falls outside § 5F1.5(a) regardless of what functional burden results from it — whether that burden is compelled notification, a capability-parity demand, or anything else. **Read honestly, this second rationale is not textually confined to the notification harm Brown happened to allege; it is a rule about what kinds of conditions § 5F1.5(a) reaches at all, stated at a level of generality that would, if squarely invoked, extend to other functional burdens flowing from a generically-worded, non-job-specific condition.**

This means the attorney's point requires a real qualification, and the honest answer is not a clean "yes, the holding is keyed to notification and Bernecky falls outside it." **If Bernecky's claim were pled as a straight, competing § 5F1.5(a) occupational-restriction theory** — i.e., "Probation's capability-parity demand functions as a de facto occupational restriction under the Guideline, just via a different mechanism than notification" — **Brown's second, general rationale would still be available to the government and would need to be engaged and distinguished on its own terms** (which the existing part6/part7 distinguishing arguments — no employment language at all in Bernecky's condition, the "as permitted by employer" contrast, Love/Jenkins nexus reasoning — already do, independent of the notification-versus-capability-parity distinction). Simply re-labeling the operative harm from "notification" to "capability-parity" does not, by itself, take a § 5F1.5(a) claim outside Brown's stated rule, because that rule was framed at the level of the Guideline's own textual trigger ("specified occupation, business, or profession"), not at the level of which specific harm resulted.

### 2.3 Where the attorney's point is fully correct and does real work

The attorney's point lands with full force, however, on a different and more fundamental level: **Bernecky's actual claim is not most naturally, and need not be, pled as a § 5F1.5(a) occupational-restriction claim competing on Brown's own doctrinal turf at all.** Per client-directives.md, Bernecky's complaint is that Probation is making an unauthorized, extra-textual demand of a non-consenting third party (the employer) — a demand with **no textual basis anywhere in his CIMP condition**, directed at an entity that is not a party to the case and over whom neither the court nor Probation has been shown to have any authority. That is an **ultra vires / delegation / no-authority-over-a-non-party theory**, already identified in client-directives.md as Theory 2 for Special Condition (a), and it is a fundamentally different legal question from "is this condition an occupational restriction under § 5F1.5(a)." Section 5F1.5(a) asks whether a condition restricts *the defendant's* occupational terms in a way requiring nexus/necessity findings; the ultra vires theory asks whether Probation has *any authority at all* to impose a demand on a third party's own systems and processes as a precondition of the defendant's employment, wholly apart from how that demand is classified under the Guidelines. **Brown never had, briefed, or decided anything resembling this question** — his own condition's employer-facing clause ("as permitted by the employer") was deferential to the employer's own choice, never demanding anything of the employer, so no ultra vires or no-authority-over-a-non-party question was ever presented to that panel. On this antecedent theory, Brown's holding — confined by its own text to construing § 5F1.5(a)'s "specified occupation" trigger — simply has nothing to say, and does not reach Bernecky's claim, **regardless of how the § 5F1.5(a) rule is stated or how generally it sweeps**, because the ultra vires theory does not depend on § 5F1.5(a) being satisfied or not satisfied in the first place.

### 2.4 Honest bottom line on Task 2

**Confirmed without qualification:** Brown's brief and the panel's own framing of it are keyed entirely to a compelled/de facto-notification theory of harm; no other theory appears anywhere in the briefing.

**Partially confirmed, with a necessary qualification:** the panel's disposition contains a broader, general rationale (the "specified occupation" definitional rule) that is not, on its face, limited to notification-type burdens — so a claim that simply substitutes "capability-parity demand" for "notification" while still pressing a § 5F1.5(a) occupational-restriction theory on a generically-worded condition would still need to grapple with that general rule; it does not automatically fall outside Brown's holding merely by changing the label of the harm.

**Where the attorney is fully vindicated:** to the extent Bernecky's actual, primary claim is the ultra vires/no-authority-over-a-non-party theory already identified in client-directives.md (Special Condition (a), Theory 2) — a claim that does not depend on classifying the demand as an "occupational restriction" under § 5F1.5(a) at all — Brown's holding, keyed as it is to construing that specific Guideline provision, simply does not reach it. That claim sits entirely outside the four corners of what Brown decided, independent of, and in addition to, the distinguishing arguments already developed in part6 and part7.

## PART 3 — Corrections and Refinements to Part6/Part7's Integrated Argument

The Part 5 draft in part7-brown-love-carlineo-reexamination.md ("Brown Is Adverse Authority That Must Be — and Can Be — Distinguished on Its Own Terms...") requires the following refinements in light of this memo's findings. These are additions/sharpenings, not retractions — no distinguishing point already developed in part6 or part7 is undermined by this memo.

1. **Do not argue, and affirmatively avoid arguing, that Brown's plain-error posture reduces Part III.B's precedential weight.** This memo's close read confirms Part III.B is written in affirmative, unconditional holding language (contrast the genuinely hedged "we cannot say ... plainly erred in concluding" language the same panel used one section earlier, in Part III.A, 173 F.4th at 49). A motion that argued plain-error review categorically weakens Part III.B's bindingness would be factually contradicted by the opinion's own words and would cost credibility. The motion should continue to concede, candidly, that Brown's occupational-restriction holding is fully precedential on the specific proposition it announces (a condition must "target a specified occupation, business, or profession" to trigger § 5F1.5(a)) — consistent with part7's own Task 1 conclusion — and should confine its distinguishing work to showing that Bernecky's condition lacks the textual feature that proposition was interpreting, and that Bernecky's ultra vires theory is a different question entirely.

2. **Add an explicit doctrinal footnote or sentence distinguishing the "assumed-without-deciding" plain-error pattern from what Brown actually did, framed as an act of candor rather than as an affirmative argument for Bernecky.** Since the government may itself invoke Brown's plain-error posture defensively (e.g., to argue the holding is somehow limited), it is worth noting affirmatively, in the motion's candor-to-the-tribunal vein already used in part7's draft, that Part III.B is not the sort of "assumed error, not plain" disposition that would leave the underlying rule undecided (contrast, e.g., a hypothetical panel statement of "even if this were an occupational restriction, it was not plain error for the district court to conclude otherwise") — Brown's panel instead affirmatively resolved the definitional question. This preempts any suggestion that the defense is trying to have it both ways (relying on Brown's binding character where convenient while disputing it elsewhere).

3. **Sharpen the Love/Jenkins-based, straight § 5F1.5(a) portion of the argument to confront Brown's general "specified occupation" rule head-on, rather than relying on a notification-vs.-capability-parity relabeling to escape it.** The existing distinguishing points (no employment language at all in Bernecky's CIMP condition; the "as permitted by the employer" contrast; the Love/Jenkins offense-nexus reasoning) remain the correct and sufficient basis for distinguishing Brown's general rule, because they attack the premise the rule depends on (a condition that at least references employment) rather than attempting to argue that a differently-labeled harm falls outside a rule that was, in fact, stated at the level of the Guideline's textual trigger. Part6/part7's existing "Third" and "Fourth" paragraphs already do this correctly and require no substantive change; this memo simply confirms that the argument's success does not additionally rest on, and should not be represented as resting on, a claim that Brown's rule is inherently confined to notification-type harms.

4. **Lead with the ultra vires/delegation theory as the primary, most protected vehicle for Bernecky's actual complaint, with the § 5F1.5(a)/Love a fortiori argument as an independent, alternative ground — not the reverse.** This memo's Task 2 analysis confirms that Bernecky's actual grievance (an unauthorized capability-parity demand on a non-consenting third-party employer, imposed after voluntary disclosure was already made and accepted) is best framed as antecedent to, and independent of, the § 5F1.5(a) occupational-restriction question Brown resolved. Brown has essentially nothing to say about that antecedent theory. The motion's structure should present the ultra vires/no-authority-over-a-non-party argument (client-directives.md, Special Condition (a), Theory 2) as the lead argument for relief from the specific "like kind and degree" demand, with the § 5F1.5(a)/Love/Jenkins occupational-restriction argument presented as an independent, alternative basis that does not depend on the first argument's success — exactly the structure already used in part7's Part 5 draft, which this memo confirms is correctly sequenced and requires no reordering, only the sharpening described in points 1-3 above.
