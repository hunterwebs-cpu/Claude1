## BOTTOM LINE UP FRONT

1. **The client's factual/legal premise is correct, and better-supported than the client's own framing assumed.** A post-revocation term of supervised release under § 3583(h) is not an automatic continuation of the original 2018 judgment — it is a capped-length, newly-authorized term that the court decides, at the revocation proceeding, whether to impose at all. Every condition attached to that new term — including one textually identical to a condition never objected to in 2018 — is imposed as part of that fresh sentencing act, and this case's own docket (*Betts*, W.D.N.Y., decided under Bernecky's district) is a direct, primary-source illustration of a revocation court imposing conditions that were then freely reviewed and partly **vacated** on direct appeal, with no forfeiture penalty traced back to the original sentencing. No "law of the case" or diminished-scrutiny doctrine for reimposed, previously-unobjected conditions was found anywhere in this research — to the contrary, the Second Circuit's 2025 en banc decision in *Maiorana* reinforces that a "contemporaneous opportunity to object" attaches at every sentencing event that imposes a non-mandatory condition, which by its own logic includes a revocation resentencing under § 3583(h)/Rule 32.1(b).
2. **No case anywhere squarely holds that a court should resolve a supervised-release conditions dispute now, via § 3583(e)(2), rather than defer it to a later revocation proceeding, as a named judicial-economy doctrine.** This is a genuine gap, honestly disclosed. The argument must be built as a synthesis — from Rule 32.1's own stated purpose (already established in part14), the November 2025 Guidelines amendment's commencement-of-supervision timing (already established in part14), *Kunz* footnote 8's express treatment of a § 3583(e)(2) motion as "the correct vehicle" for resolving an employer/device tension identical in kind to Bernecky's (already found in part19), and the general federal-courts ripeness framework (*Abbott Laboratories v. Gardner*, 387 U.S. 136 (1967)) that asks exactly the question the client is asking: is there a *hardship* in withholding review now. This is presented candidly as a first-principles argument, not a citation to an existing holding, exactly as part19 modeled for Theory 7.
3. **Void-for-vagueness/fair-notice doctrine is a strong, and unusually well-matched, reinforcing analogy — and this case file already contains its own best example of it, previously unused for this purpose.** *United States v. Carlineo*, 998 F.3d 533 (2d Cir. 2021) — already read in full in this case file (part1, part5) for its delegation holding — independently held the challenged condition vague because it "fail[ed] to inform Carlineo what he must do... to avoid a **violation proceeding**." That is, verbatim, the client's own point: a defendant should not have to guess, under threat of a violation proceeding, what a condition requires. This is not an out-of-circuit analogy needing to be imported — it is binding Second Circuit language, already in this case's own citation set, that says exactly what the client is arguing, just not yet connected to the timing/judicial-economy framing.
4. **The client's argument does *not* meaningfully cut against *Traficante* — and the reason why is itself the sharpest, most useful part of the answer.** *Traficante*'s ripeness holding rests on the ordinary two-part ripeness test (fitness for decision; hardship from withholding review), and it came out the way it did because *neither* factor was satisfied there: no risk-determination had been made, might never be made, and Traficante suffered no concrete injury from waiting. Bernecky's CIMP dispute is different **in kind, not merely degree**: it is not a future contingent event that may never occur — it is a live, currently-administered dispute (an actual job actually suspended, an actual PO demand already made) causing concrete, non-speculative, present-tense harm. Properly understood, the client's judicial-economy point does not conflict with *Traficante* — it *explains* why *Traficante* came out the way it did, and confirms that Bernecky's CIMP/occupational theory satisfies both ripeness factors *Traficante*'s own claim failed. **Recommendation, stated directly: the attorney's draft outline is correct to press Special Condition (a)/CIMP's merits in full now, and correct to hold Standard Condition 12/Boles as a brief preservation paragraph rather than a fully-litigated merits argument — this new timing argument reinforces, and should not be used to disturb, that existing strategic allocation.**

## PART 1 — The Statutory/Procedural Mechanics of Revocation and Reimposition (Research Question 1)

### 1(a). § 3583(e)(3) and § 3583(h), read in full (primary source)

Both subsections were read directly from the Drive folder's LII copy of § 3583 (Drive ID `1L-aBH-OCLbbAy8PM5grHvpwFN7uKadSv`) — the same primary-source file part14 already used and confirmed for §§ (d) and (e)(2).

**§ 3583(e)(3)** (revocation):

> "revoke a term of supervised release, and require the defendant to serve in prison all or part of the term of supervised release authorized by statute for the offense that resulted in such term of supervised release without credit for time previously served on post release supervision, if the court, pursuant to the Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure applicable to revocation of probation or supervised release, **finds by a preponderance of the evidence that the defendant violated a condition of supervised release**, except that a defendant whose term is revoked under this paragraph may not be required to serve on any such revocation more than 5 years in prison if the offense that resulted in the term of supervised release is a class A felony, more than 3 years in prison if such offense is a class B felony, more than 2 years in prison if such offense is a class C or D felony, or more than one year in any other case."

**§ 3583(h)** (supervised release following revocation):

> "SUPERVISED RELEASE FOLLOWING REVOCATION.— When a term of supervised release is revoked and the defendant is required to serve a term of imprisonment, **the court may include a requirement that the defendant be placed on a term of supervised release after imprisonment.** The length of such a term of supervised release shall not exceed the term of supervised release authorized by statute for the offense that resulted in the original term of supervised release, less any term of imprisonment that was imposed upon revocation of supervised release."

**Two textual points do real work for the client's premise:**

1. **§ 3583(h) uses "may," not "shall," and speaks only to whether a new term of supervised release is imposed at all and its outer length — it says nothing about conditions.** There is no statutory text anywhere in § 3583 stating that conditions from the original judgment simply continue by operation of law into a new § 3583(h) term. The new term, if the court elects to impose one, is a **new, freestanding authorization**, capped by (and measured against) the original statutory maximum less time already served — not an extension or continuation of the 2018 term.
2. **§ 3583(e)(3) itself is expressly conditioned on a fresh judicial finding** — "if the court... finds by a preponderance of the evidence that the defendant violated a condition" — made **at the revocation proceeding**, under Rule 32.1's revocation procedures, not by reference back to anything decided in 2018.

Neither subsection answers, on its own text, what conditions attach to a new § 3583(h) term. That is answered by ordinary sentencing procedure and case law, addressed next.

### 1(b). A revocation/reimposition proceeding is a fresh sentencing act, at which special conditions of supervised release are imposed (or reimposed) under the same individualized-assessment framework as the original sentencing — confirmed directly from this district's own case law

**United States v. Betts, 886 F.3d 198 (2d Cir. 2018)** — read in full from the Drive folder — is the single most useful primary source located for this question, and it comes from **Bernecky's own district (W.D.N.Y.)**, arising on direct appeal from a **revocation** judgment (Betts was on supervised release for a prior bank-fraud conviction, violated it, and was resentenced by the district court to 10 months' imprisonment followed by a **new** four-year term of supervised release with special conditions). The Second Circuit:

- Reviewed the **newly imposed special conditions** (a total alcohol ban, a "zero tolerance" drug-testing condition) under the **identical** framework used at an original sentencing: "[a] district court is required to make an individualized assessment when determining whether to impose a special condition of supervised release, and to state on the record the reason for imposing it; the failure to do so is error," citing *United States v. Balon*, 384 F.3d 38, 41 n.1 (2d Cir. 2004), and applying U.S.S.G. § 5D1.3(b) and *United States v. Myers*, 426 F.3d 117, 123-25 (2d Cir. 2005) — the same authorities this case file already relies on for original-sentencing conditions review (part14, part19).
- **Vacated** the alcohol-abstinence special condition as "not reasonably related to any of the factors outlined in Section 5D1.3(b)," remanding "for further consideration of whether it is necessary to impose any special conditions related to alcohol use and, if so, for the District Court to explain the basis for imposing them."
- Applied ordinary appellate review — nothing in the opinion treats Betts's challenge as forfeited, diminished, or subject to plain-error review merely because the conditions arose at a revocation proceeding rather than an original sentencing, and nothing in the opinion suggests that a condition's similarity to, or difference from, anything in Betts's original 2013 judgment affected the standard of review applied to the newly-imposed condition.

**Betts confirms the client's premise directly, from this exact district:** a revocation proceeding that results in a new § 3583(h) term of supervised release is a genuine, freestanding sentencing event, at which special conditions are imposed (or reimposed) under the ordinary individualized-assessment/reasonably-related standard, and are **freely appealable on the merits** — not merely reviewable for an abuse of discretion confined to whatever changed since the last judgment.

**Candid limitation:** *Betts*'s conditions were **newly added** at the revocation sentencing (they did not exist in the 2013 judgment) — it does not present the precise fact pattern the client's directive asks about (a condition **identical** to one already in the original judgment, never objected to, simply carried forward unchanged at the revocation proceeding). No case squarely presenting that exact fact pattern was located this session. What *Betts* does establish, and what answers the client's underlying concern even without that exact fact pattern, is addressed next.

### 1(c). Does a condition merely *carried forward unchanged* get treated differently — law of the case, or diminished scrutiny? No such doctrine was found, and the trend runs the other way

**No Second Circuit (or other) case was located holding, or even suggesting, that a condition reimposed unchanged at a revocation proceeding receives diminished appellate scrutiny, is treated as "law of the case," or is otherwise insulated from a fresh objection merely because it is identical to a previously-unobjected-to condition.** This was tested through multiple, differently-phrased searches (Part 5 below documents the search strategy). What was found instead cuts affirmatively **against** any such diminished-scrutiny theory:

- **United States v. Maiorana, 153 F.4th 306 (2d Cir. 2025) (en banc)** — **[VERIFY: reconstructed from consistent, multiply-corroborated WebSearch results (CaseMine commentary, Justia docket page, FindLaw); not independently read from the opinion text this session, though it is already discussed, without a citation, in client-directives.md and has been the subject of a controlling client correction in this case file (demoted for the CIMP-scope/silence argument — see client-directives.md, "CORRECTION — *Maiorana* demoted").]** *Maiorana* overruled *Truscello* and held that **"the right to be present at sentencing entails notice and a contemporaneous opportunity to object to every non-mandatory condition of supervised release."** This is a **different use of *Maiorana* than the one client-directives.md correctly rejected** for the CIMP silence/clarification argument (where *Maiorana*'s formation/oral-pronouncement holding was the wrong doctrinal fit for an interpretation question). Here, the question is not interpretive — it is procedural: does a defendant get a **fresh** opportunity to object when a condition is imposed at a later sentencing event? *Maiorana*'s own holding answers that affirmatively, and by its terms applies to **every** sentencing event that imposes a non-mandatory condition — which, under § 3583(h), plainly includes a revocation resentencing. Nothing in *Maiorana*, or any case citing it, carves out an exception for a condition that happens to duplicate one from an earlier, unappealed judgment.
- **Esteras v. United States, 606 U.S. ___ (2025)** — **[VERIFY: WebSearch-sourced synthesis, not independently read; multiply corroborated across Cornell LII, Justia, Ballotpedia, and the Federal Defenders' own training-office summary, giving reasonable confidence in the holding stated.]** The Supreme Court held a district court **may not** consider 18 U.S.C. § 3553(a)(2)(A) retribution "vis-à-vis the defendant's underlying criminal offense" when deciding whether to revoke supervised release — confirming that a revocation proceeding is analytically its **own**, forward-looking sentencing decision, not a backward-looking enforcement of whatever was decided at the original sentencing. This reinforces, from the Supreme Court's own 2025 treatment of revocation proceedings generally, that revocation/reimposition is not treated as a mechanical continuation of the 2018 judgment.
- **General appellate-review authority located via WebSearch (not independently verified from a primary opinion this session, flagged accordingly)** describes revocation-sentence appeals reviewed for reasonableness under the ordinary *Gall*/*Booker* framework, with **de novo resentencing** required where the district court's discretion was exercised under a mistaken legal premise. Nothing located suggests a separate, more forgiving standard for conditions that happen to repeat prior, unobjected-to language.

**Bottom line for 1(c):** the "void ab initio"/forfeiture analysis this case file already built in part14 (Part 3) — that Rule 52(b) plain-error review is a direct-appeal doctrine with no application to matters not previously appealed, and that there is no forfeiture doctrine operating on an original § 3583(e)(2) motion in the first place — has a clean structural parallel here: **there is likewise no doctrine holding that a condition's mere textual identity to an unobjected-to 2018 condition strips it of full, fresh review when it is reimposed at a later sentencing event.** The right to object, and to appeal, attaches anew at whatever proceeding actually imposes the condition — the original 2018 sentencing for that judgment, and (per *Maiorana*'s own logic, if it comes to that) a revocation resentencing for a new § 3583(h) judgment. **This confirms, rather than merely assumes, the client's premise: a violation proceeding really would "allow objection on all grounds and even an appeal," exactly as the client stated it.**

### 1(d). Rule 32.1(b)'s revocation-hearing procedure — reconstructed, flagged [VERIFY]

**[VERIFY — Rule 32.1's primary text could not be independently opened this session, for the same reason part14 could not open it (session-wide WebFetch failure/403 across every host attempted, including Cornell LII, uscode.house.gov, casetext.com, and web.archive.org). The following is reconstructed from consistent, multiply-corroborated WebSearch snippets and should be checked against the current rule text before filing.]** Rule 32.1(b) provides, in substance, that before a court may revoke supervised release, the person is entitled to (subject to a preliminary-hearing stage for probable cause where the person is held in custody): written notice of the alleged violation; disclosure of the evidence; an opportunity to appear, present evidence, and (upon request) question any adverse witness unless the court finds good cause not to require the witness's appearance; notice of the right to counsel; and an opportunity to make a statement and present mitigating information. This is the same procedural apparatus — notice, opportunity to be heard, right to counsel, right to allocute — that governs an original sentencing, reinforcing 1(b)-(c)'s conclusion that a revocation/reimposition proceeding is not a truncated or diminished process as to the conditions it sets.

## PART 2 — Judicial-Economy / Avoid-Piecemeal-Litigation Case Law (Research Question 2)

### 2(a). No case squarely holds this — confirmed gap, tested from several directions

**No Second Circuit, or other circuit, case was located holding that a court should resolve a supervised-release conditions dispute proactively, via § 3583(e)(2), rather than defer it to a future revocation proceeding, framed as a judicial-economy or piecemeal-litigation rationale.** This was tested via multiple search strategies: (i) direct searches for judicial-economy/piecemeal-litigation rationale specific to Rule 32.1(c) modification proceedings; (ii) searches for cases discussing the purpose of resolving ambiguous conditions before a violation occurs; (iii) searches for language about the "perversity" of forcing a defendant to violate, or risk violating, a condition to obtain review of its scope. None produced a squarely-on-point holding. This should be disclosed candidly, consistent with this file's own established practice (part19's honest gap list).

### 2(b). What exists instead — real, usable building blocks, none independently sufficient

1. **Rule 32.1(b)/(c) advisory committee's notes, already confirmed in part14 (Part 6(b)) via a primary source read in full:** *Lussier* itself quotes the Notes' description of the modification mechanism's purpose — "the sentencing court must be able to respond to changes in the [defendant's] circumstances **as well as new ideas and methods of rehabilitation**," 104 F.3d at 36 (quoting *United States v. Truss*, 4 F.3d 437, 439 (6th Cir. 1993)). This confirms Rule 32.1(c)'s modification mechanism is understood, by its own drafting history, as a responsive, present-tense tool — not one that exists merely to ratify decisions already made. This is not itself a "resolve now, not later" holding, but it supports the proposition that the mechanism's whole design purpose is to let a court act **before** the next event forces its hand, consistent with the client's framing.

2. **The November 2025 Guidelines amendment, already fully established in part14 (Parts 4-5), is the single strongest existing building block, and it is squarely about *timing*.** U.S.S.G. § 5D1.4(a) directs that "[t]he court **is encouraged to conduct such an assessment in consultation with the probation officer after the defendant's release from imprisonment**" — i.e., at the exact moment Bernecky is in right now (supervision commencing ~July 27, 2026). This does not independently supply a "resolve now vs. later" rationale, but it supplies the Sentencing Commission's own affirmative, recent (effective Nov. 1, 2025) institutional judgment that **this specific moment** — commencement of supervision, before any violation — is the right moment for exactly this kind of individualized reassessment. Combined with the client's point, the argument becomes: the Commission has already told courts *when* to do this (now); the client's argument tells the court *why* now is better than later (the alternative is a higher-stakes, not lower-stakes, version of the identical legal dispute).

3. **Kunz footnote 8, already found in part19 (Section 6), is the closest thing to a Second Circuit statement on this exact question, and is worth leading with.** Confronted with an employer/VPN tension within the very same CIMP condition family at issue here, the *Kunz* panel called the concern "a hypothetical worry" and stated that "**a simple modification request should a change in [the defendant's] employment circumstances warrant as much**" was the correct response, citing *Balon* for the proposition that "changing technology is an appropriate factor to authorize a modification... under Section 3583(e)(2)." 68 F.4th at 766 n.8. This is the Second Circuit **affirmatively treating § 3583(e)(2) as the right, proactive tool** for resolving exactly this kind of employer/device-scope tension — not a "wait for a violation" mechanism. Bernecky's facts are not hypothetical (an actual job is actually suspended right now), which makes this footnote's own logic apply with **more**, not less, force to Bernecky than it did to Kunz.

4. **General federal-courts doctrine on the purpose of declaratory and clarifying relief is a real, if generalized, analogy — flag as analogy, not as SR-specific authority.** **[VERIFY — WebSearch-sourced, not independently read from a primary opinion this session.]** *Steffel v. Thompson*, 415 U.S. 452 (1974), rejected the notion that a person must first violate a law and face prosecution before securing judicial resolution of a live legal dispute about that law's scope, quoting Professor Borchard's observation about the alternative being to force a person "to violate the statute and take his chances in testing constitutionality on a criminal prosecution, or else to [forgo], in the fear of prosecution, the exercise of his claimed rights," and concluding: **"Into this dilemma no civilized legal system operating under a constitution should force any person."** This is not a supervised-release case, and the procedural posture (a live federal declaratory-judgment action against a still-enforceable state statute) differs meaningfully from a § 3583(e)(2) modification motion. But the underlying logic — that a legal system should not force a person to choose between forgoing a claimed right and risking punishment to test it — is structurally identical to the client's own point, and is worth a single clean citation as reinforcing background, candidly flagged as an analogy from a different procedural context, not as controlling or even persuasive supervised-release authority.

5. **Abbott Laboratories v. Gardner, 387 U.S. 136 (1967)**, supplies the actual doctrinal test that does the analytical work the client is describing, and is addressed fully in Part 4 below because it is also the key to the *Traficante* question: ripeness turns on "**both the fitness of the issues for judicial decision and the hardship to the parties of withholding court consideration**." This is not a case *about* supervised release, but it is the general framework any court (including the Second Circuit in *Traficante*, see Part 4) actually uses to decide whether to resolve a dispute now or wait — and it gives the client's "why not now" intuition a real, established doctrinal name and structure, rather than leaving it as pure policy argument.

### 2(c). Candid assessment

**No case says what the client wants said, in so many words. But the building blocks above are real, not manufactured, and they assemble into a coherent argument:** Rule 32.1(c)'s own purpose is responsive and present-tense (part14, confirmed); the Sentencing Commission has just (Nov. 2025) told courts this exact moment (commencement of supervision) is the right time to conduct exactly this kind of reassessment (part14); the Second Circuit has already, in this same condition family, called a § 3583(e)(2) motion the "simple," correct response to an employer-related tension rather than something to defer (*Kunz* fn. 8, part19); and general federal-courts doctrine (*Steffel*, *Abbott Laboratories*) independently recognizes that forcing a person to await a violation/enforcement event to resolve a live legal question is not favored, and turns on a hardship inquiry Bernecky's facts satisfy. This should be presented as a synthesis argument, in the same disclosed, non-overclaiming register part19 used for Theory 7 — not as a citation to a controlling holding.

## PART 3 — Void-for-Vagueness/Fair-Notice Doctrine as a Reinforcing Analogy (Research Question 3)

### 3(a). The best version of this argument is already inside this case file, unused for this purpose

**Most useful finding of this entire memo:** *United States v. Carlineo*, 998 F.3d 533 (2d Cir. 2021) — already read in full in this case file for its delegation holding (part1, part5-primary-source-verification.md, Section 1) — **independently held the challenged condition unconstitutionally vague**, and did so in language that is, in substance, the client's own argument stated by the Second Circuit itself:

> Carlineo's condition "would leave a reasonable person guessing as to what the requirements of the Program might be... fails to inform Carlineo what he must do... **to avoid a violation proceeding**." (as quoted and confirmed via primary-source read, part5-primary-source-verification.md, Section 1, Master Verification Table.)

This is not a generic vagueness holding about criminal statutes — it is a **binding Second Circuit application of the fair-notice rationale specifically to a supervised-release condition**, and it frames the harm specifically as uncertainty about **what is needed to avoid a violation proceeding** — i.e., exactly the dynamic the client says the court should not force him into by declining to resolve the CIMP dispute now. Bernecky's argument is not that his own conditions are vague in *Carlineo*'s sense (that is a different, already-developed theory for Special Condition (b), see part1/part7) — the point here is that *Carlineo* supplies binding, in-case, Second Circuit language confirming that **the whole point of the fair-notice principle in this specific context is to spare a defendant from having to find out, via an actual violation proceeding, what a condition requires or permits.** That is precisely the client's own rationale for resolving the CIMP dispute now rather than at a future revocation proceeding.

### 3(b). Reinforcing, already-verified authority elsewhere in this case file

- ***United States v. Reeves*, 591 F.3d 77, 80-81 (2d Cir. 2010)**, already cited and quoted (via *Carlineo*'s own citation of it, confirmed in part5): **"A condition is too vague if it requires a reasonable person to speculate as to its meaning."** Same register — a condition should not require guesswork.
- ***United States v. Mike*, 632 F.3d 686, 696-97 (10th Cir. 2011)**, read in full in this case file for part19, remanded a computer-monitoring condition for vagueness precisely because a probation officer could reasonably read it to reach devices (including an employer's) the condition's drafters may not have intended — and the **remedy** the Tenth Circuit ordered was exactly a prospective **clarification**, not a wait-and-see approach requiring Mike to violate the condition (by using or not using an employer device) before finding out its scope. This is a second, independently-confirmed example of a court's actual remedial choice tracking the client's "resolve now" logic, even though *Mike* does not frame it in judicial-economy terms.

### 3(c). Out-of-circuit supplemental authority — flagged, not load-bearing

**United States v. Loy, 237 F.3d 251 (3d Cir. 2001)** — **[VERIFY: WebSearch-sourced, not independently read from the opinion this session.]** Per multiply-corroborated secondary-source description, the Third Circuit held a supervised-release condition prohibiting "all forms of pornography, including legal adult pornography" unconstitutionally vague because it supplied no method to distinguish prohibited from permitted material, risking that the condition's real content would be whatever a given probation officer personally found objectionable. This is useful only as a second circuit's confirmation that vagueness doctrine is actively applied to supervised-release conditions specifically (not just criminal statutes generally) — it does not add anything *Carlineo* does not already supply within the Second Circuit itself, and should be cited, if at all, as a brief "accord" citation, not as load-bearing authority, given it has not been independently read this session and is out-of-circuit besides.

### 3(d). Candid assessment of fit

**The vagueness/fair-notice analogy is a strong, well-fitted reinforcing point — arguably the best-supported piece of this entire memo — but it remains an analogy, not a separate cause of action.** Bernecky is not arguing any condition at issue is void for vagueness in this section (that is separately argued, condition-specific, elsewhere in the motion). The move here is narrower and cleaner: **the same underlying value vagueness doctrine protects — that a person should not have to guess, under threat of a violation proceeding, what is required of him — is the value this timing argument vindicates by resolving the CIMP dispute now, on a clean record, rather than making Bernecky wait to find out (possibly via an actual violation) what the condition requires or permits.** *Carlineo*'s own language, already binding in this case's citation set, makes this connection nearly automatic and should be quoted directly in the Jurisdiction/Standard-of-Review section for exactly this purpose.

## PART 4 — *Traficante* and the Standard Condition 12/*Boles* Strategic Question (Research Question 4)

### 4(a). What *Traficante* actually held, restated precisely

As already confirmed in this case file (part5-primary-source-verification.md, Section 5, and part14 Part 1(b)) — *United States v. Traficante*, 966 F.3d 99 (2d Cir. 2020), arising from **Bernecky's own sentencing judge's docket** (Larimer, J., W.D.N.Y.) — held that a facial vagueness/delegation challenge to the WDNY's post-*Boles* "consultation with the court" risk-notification standing order was **not ripe**, because it depended on "contingent future events that may not occur as anticipated, or indeed may not occur at all" — i.e., an actual risk-determination by the district court that had not yet happened (and, on Traficante's own facts, might never happen).

### 4(b). Traficante's ripeness holding is an ordinary application of the two-factor test — not a freestanding "always wait" rule

**Abbott Laboratories v. Gardner**, 387 U.S. 136 (1967) — **[VERIFY: WebSearch-sourced, not independently read this session, but the two-factor test is uncontroversial, black-letter federal-courts doctrine.]** — supplies the doctrinal skeleton *Traficante*'s own language implicitly applies: ripeness asks (1) whether the issue is **fit** for judicial decision, and (2) what **hardship** the parties would suffer from the court withholding consideration now. *Traficante*'s "contingent future events... may not occur at all" language is a fitness-prong holding: there was, quite literally, no live administrative decision yet in existence to review, because no risk-determination had been made. And because nothing concrete had happened to Traficante yet, there was no showing of the second, hardship prong either.

**This means *Traficante* is not a holding that courts should generally prefer to wait rather than resolve supervised-release conditions disputes proactively.** It is a holding that **this particular kind of dispute** — a facial challenge to a procedural mechanism that only activates upon a future, not-yet-triggered administrative event — fails ripeness's ordinary two-factor test on its own facts, because there was nothing yet to review and no injury yet to redress.

### 4(c). Applying the same two factors to Bernecky's CIMP/occupational dispute shows the opposite result on both prongs

| Ripeness Factor | *Traficante*'s Standard Condition 12 posture | Bernecky's CIMP/Special Condition (a) posture |
|---|---|---|
| **Fitness for decision** | No risk-determination had been made; the standing order's "consultation with the court" mechanism had not yet been invoked against Traficante at all. The challenge was to a hypothetical future application. | The CIMP condition is **already being actively administered and disputed right now**: the PO has already made an actual, concrete demand (employer must prove "like kind and degree" monitoring capability); Probation has already had the relevant conversation with Bernecky; the dispute is over the **current meaning and scope** of an existing condition as it is presently being applied, not a facial challenge to a mechanism that has not yet fired. |
| **Hardship from withholding review** | None shown or arguably even possible — nothing concrete had happened to Traficante as a result of the standing order at the time of his challenge. | Concrete, ongoing, non-speculative harm **right now**: an actual job offer suspended, actual lost income, a signed lease and documented financial precarity, and (per Standard Condition 7's mandatory full-time-employment requirement) a live Catch-22 in which one condition compels what another condition's administration is simultaneously preventing. |

**This is not a marginal distinction — it is the difference between a case that has not yet ripened at all and a case that is unusually, even urgently, ripe.** *Traficante*'s claim failed both of ripeness's ordinary prongs; Bernecky's CIMP claim satisfies both.
