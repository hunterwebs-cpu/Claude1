## PART 1 — U.S.S.G. § 5F1.5 Occupational Restriction: The Two-Part Test and the Consequence of Its Absence

### The test itself

U.S.S.G. § 5F1.5(a) permits a court to impose a condition "prohibiting the defendant from engaging in a specified occupation, business, or profession, or limiting the terms on which the defendant may do so," **only if the court determines that:**
1. **"a reasonably direct relationship existed between the defendant's occupation, business, or profession and the conduct relevant to the offense of conviction"**; AND
2. **imposition of such a restriction is reasonably necessary to protect the public because there is reason to believe that, absent such restriction, the defendant will continue to engage in unlawful conduct similar to that for which the defendant was convicted.**

§ 5F1.5(b) additionally requires such a restriction to be "reasonably necessary" and to impose "no greater restriction than necessary" to accomplish its purpose — an explicit **least-restrictive-alternative requirement layered on top of the two-part nexus/necessity test.** [VERIFY BEFORE FILING — confirm exact guideline text and 2025 Guidelines Manual numbering/commentary, since the Guidelines were amended effective Nov. 1, 2025, see Part 6 below]

**Binding status:** U.S.S.G. § 5F1.5 is a Sentencing Guidelines provision, not case law, but courts (including the Second Circuit) treat its two-part test as the operative substantive standard for evaluating whether a condition functions as an occupational restriction. Any Second Circuit case applying it is binding; sister-circuit application is persuasive only.

### Application to Bernecky

**Nothing in the Judgment shows this analysis was ever performed.** Per the case-file intake (conditions-list.md § 8(a)): "No occupational restriction condition appears anywhere in the Judgment. There is no special condition restricting Mr. Bernecky's occupation, employer type, or work duties, and no U.S.S.G. § 5F1.5 finding of a 'reasonably direct relationship' between an occupation and the offense conduct appears on the face of the Judgment." This is a factual predicate independently confirmed by the intake team's direct reading of the AO 245B Judgment — not something this research track re-verified against the Judgment itself, but it is treated as reliable given the intake methodology.

**The relief that follows:** where a condition is being administered in a way that functions as a de facto occupational bar (here: CIMP's "owned or operated by the defendant" language, as administered by the PO through an extra-textual "monitoring-parity" demand on Bernecky's prospective employer, is preventing him from taking a specific job), and the sentencing court never made the § 5F1.5 findings, the available relief is (1) a declaration/clarifying order that the Judgment imposes **no occupational restriction** and that CIMP may not be administered so as to function as one without the court first making the required findings; and, in the alternative or in addition, (2) a request that if the government/Probation believes an occupational-type restriction is in fact warranted going forward, it must seek it through a proper § 3583(e)(2) modification proceeding with the § 5F1.5 findings made on the record — not through ad hoc administrative demands on a non-party employer. This is squarely the theory the client's directives already identify (Special Condition (a), theory 1; cross-referenced at Special Condition (c) and Standard Condition 7).

**Directly on point, and important adverse authority to confront:** *United States v. Brown*, No. 24-1227 (2d Cir. ## PART 2 — Computer/Internet Monitoring Case Law (CIMP-type conditions)

### Case: United States v. Lifshitz
**Citation:** 369 F.3d 173 (2d Cir. 2004)
**Binding status:** Binding (Second Circuit)
**Verification status:** **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — FULL OPINION NOT DIRECTLY READ; reconstructed from a law-review student note (Shauna Curphey, *United States v. Lifshitz: Warrantless Computer Monitoring and the Fourth Amendment*, 38 Loy. L.A. L. Rev. (2004-05)), a Federal Defenders of NY 2d Cir. blog post, Justia case summary, and multiple WebSearch snippets. These sources were consistent with each other but I could not independently open the F.3d text.]**

**Holding (as reconstructed):** Lifshitz challenged a supervised-release condition permitting the probation office to monitor his computer/internet use following a child-pornography conviction. The Second Circuit applied the "special needs" doctrine (rather than a traditional probable-cause/reasonable-suspicion Fourth Amendment framework) to hold that suspicionless computer monitoring conditions **can** be justified by the government's special supervisory interest, but held that the monitoring condition, as imposed, may be **overbroad**, and **vacated and remanded** for the district court to (a) evaluate the comparative privacy intrusiveness of monitoring software versus filtering/blocking software, and (b) impose a condition "consistent with th[e] opinion" — i.e., narrowly tailored to the government's actual interest. One secondary source paraphrase: the court noted that "[i]ndividuals generally possess a reasonable expectation of privacy in their home computers," and that the government's chosen monitoring method must bear a "close and substantial relation" to its interest in pursuing the search/monitoring. Another secondary source (the law review note) states the court suggested a narrower condition would "limit[] the monitoring condition to Internet-related activity and e-mail" and use "monitoring software that searches for particular suspect words and phrases rather than recording all varieties of computer-related activity" as an example of a more narrowly tailored alternative — i.e., **less intrusive than recording "all activity."**

**Does Lifshitz address keystroke logging specifically?** No square finding could be confirmed. Based on all available secondary sources, Lifshitz's discussion is about monitoring/filtering software **generally** (2004-era technology) — it does not appear to name or specifically analyze keystroke-level logging as a discrete, more-intrusive category distinct from lower-intrusion methods like URL/website-history review. **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — this is a gap; I could not locate any Second Circuit or other circuit authority squarely holding that keystroke-level logging is subject to a higher intrusiveness standard than general internet-activity monitoring.]** The client's instinct that keystroke logging is meaningfully more intrusive than web-history logging is intuitively sound (keystroke logs capture password entry, private correspondence text before it's even sent, drafts never transmitted, etc. — an even greater intrusion than transmitted-content logging), but I found **no case squarely adopting a keystroke-specific heightened standard**. This should be argued as an application of Lifshitz's own "narrow tailoring"/comparative-intrusiveness framework to today's more granular monitoring technology (i.e., an a fortiori extension of Lifshitz's logic), not as an established separate doctrine.

**Application to Bernecky:** Lifshitz is Bernecky's foundational authority that (1) CIMP-type conditions are evaluated under a special-needs/narrow-tailoring framework, not rubber-stamped; (2) courts must consider less intrusive alternatives; and (3) "surveil all activity" language — precisely Bernecky's condition's phrase — is the kind of broad, unqualified authorization Lifshitz's remand was aimed at narrowing. Bernecky's condition text ("authorized to install any application as necessary to surveil all activity") is, if anything, broader and less cabined than the condition that was found potentially overbroad in Lifshitz twenty-plus years ago, and was imposed without any on-the-record narrow-tailoring analysis specific to Bernecky.

### Case: United States v. Brown
**Citation:** No. 24-1227 (2d Cir. Apr. 21, 2026)
**Binding status:** Binding (Second Circuit) — **and extremely recent; decided roughly 10 weeks before this memo**
**Verification status:** **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — FULL OPINION NOT DIRECTLY READ; reconstructed entirely from WebSearch snippets and a secondary-source case tracker (changeflow.com/govping). This is the single most important case in this memo to verify against the actual slip opinion before filing, given its recency, its direct application of § 5F1.5 to a computer-monitoring/employment fact pattern, and the risk that this office is not yet aware of it as adverse authority.]**

**Facts (as reconstructed):** Brown was convicted of fourteen counts of making false claims (18 U.S.C. § 287) and one count of theft of government funds (18 U.S.C. § 641), arising from fraudulently obtaining prisoners' PII and filing false tax returns for over $130,000 in refunds. His supervised-release conditions included a condition "authorizing the probation office to conduct constant, suspicionless monitoring of 'all activity' on internet-capable devices used by Brown, **including those at his place of employment**."

**Brown's argument (as reconstructed) — directly parallel to Bernecky's:** Brown argued that "the component of the special condition restricting his 'use of any devices in the course of employment' is 'tantamount to an occupational restriction' since it 'effectively requires him to notify any prospective employers of his fraud conviction'" — because, per the reconstructed reasoning, **the employer must know of and consent to probation's monitoring** for Brown to use employer devices at all. Brown argued this violated U.S.S.G. § 5F1.5 because no court ever made the two-part occupational-restriction finding.

**Holding (as reconstructed):** The panel (Sullivan, J., joined by Wesley & Park, JJ.) **rejected** the § 5F1.5 argument, reasoning that the condition "does not prohibit Brown from engaging in a specific occupation, does not limit the terms on which he may do so, and is reasonably necessary to protect the public." The panel grounded the "reasonably necessary" conclusion in individualized aggravating facts specific to Brown: **"Brown's repeated use of electronic devices to engage in unlawful conduct, his continuation of unlawful conduct while on probation for a previous state offense, and his history of lying to probation."** The court affirmed.

**Critical distinctions from Bernecky's facts — this must be the centerpiece of any effort to distinguish Brown:**
1. **Individualized aggravating record.** Brown's affirmance rested explicitly on his *specific history* of repeated device-based reoffending, reoffending *while already on supervision*, and a documented *history of lying to probation*. Nothing in the Bernecky case file alleges any comparable history — no violation history, no prior supervision misconduct, no documented dishonesty with Probation. Brown's holding is fact-bound to aggravators Bernecky's record does not share; it is not a categorical rule that CIMP-type monitoring of employer devices can never function as an occupational restriction.
2. **Theoretical versus actualized harm.** Brown's occupational-restriction argument, as reconstructed, appears to have been argued prospectively/in the abstract — the panel could say the condition "does not prohibit" or "limit the terms" of his employment in a general sense. Bernecky's case is not abstract: **a specific, real employer has specifically and presently refused to finalize a specific, real job offer because of this exact condition**, and the job is "effectively suspended" as a matter of undisputed, contemporaneous fact. Bernecky is not asking the court to speculate about a hypothetical chilling effect on future employability in the abstract — he has a concrete, documented instance of the condition, as administered by the PO's extra-textual "like kind and degree" demand, functioning as an actual occupational bar, right now, before supervision has even begun. This is precisely the kind of concrete factual predicate an occupational-restriction-as-applied claim needs that Brown's more generalized/prospective framing may have lacked.
3. **No PO overlay of an extra-textual employer-facing demand in Brown (as reconstructed).** Nothing in the reconstructed Brown facts suggests the probation officer imposed an independent "monitoring-parity"/"like kind and degree" keystroke-logging-capability demand on Brown's employer that has no textual basis in the condition itself. Bernecky's fact pattern is arguably worse: it is not just the condition's text causing friction with an employer's own Fourth Amendment sensitivities (as in Brown), but a **PO-invented additional demand, untethered to the condition's text, imposed on a non-party**, that is the actual proximate cause of the job's suspension — see Part 3 below (ultra vires/delegation).

**Application to Bernecky:** Brown is adverse authority that defense counsel must candidly address, not ignore — a court doing its own research will find it, and it is recent enough (April 2026) that it may not yet be widely known even to experienced practitioners. The correct strategy is not to argue Brown was wrongly decided, but to argue Brown is **factually inapposite** on exactly the axis that matters here: Brown's affirmance depended on individualized bad-conduct findings entirely absent from Bernecky's record, and Brown did not involve (so far as reconstructed) an actual, present, documented loss of a specific job opportunity caused by a PO's extra-textual demand on a non-party employer. If the § 5F1.5/occupational-restriction argument is to succeed, it should be pled as narrower and more fact-specific than a facial attack on CIMP-monitoring-of-work-devices in general (which Brown forecloses on its facts), and instead framed as: (1) an ultra vires challenge to the PO's specific extra-textual demand (Part 3), and (2) an as-applied § 5F1.5 argument resting on the absence of any Brown-type aggravating individualized findings as to Bernecky specifically.

## PART 3 — Delegation Doctrine

### Case: United States v. Matta
**Citation:** 777 F.3d 116 (2d Cir. 2015)
**Binding status:** Binding (Second Circuit)
**Verification status:** **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — FULL OPINION NOT DIRECTLY READ; reconstructed from Findlaw/Justia/Casemine summaries, cross-corroborated across three independent secondary sources with consistent detail.]**

**Holding (as reconstructed):** Matta pleaded guilty to being a felon in possession of a firearm; the district court's supervised-release conditions left it "to the discretion of Probation" to decide whether Matta should undergo inpatient or outpatient drug treatment. The Second Circuit held this delegation improper, reasoning that **"granting the probation officer discretion to decide whether such conditions will be imposed is tantamount to allowing him to decide the nature or extent of the defendant's punishment,"** and that **"any condition affecting a significant liberty interest ... must be imposed by the district court and supported by particularized findings."** The court **vacated and remanded** the specific delegated condition (with instructions that the district court itself select the form of treatment) while otherwise affirming the sentence.

**The general framework this establishes:** Matta distinguishes permissible delegation of **"minor details"** of implementation (e.g., selection of a specific therapy provider or treatment schedule) from impermissible delegation of the **"nature or extent of the liberty interest itself"** (e.g., whether the defendant must undergo a more restrictive form of treatment, inpatient versus outpatient, at all). The touchstone is whether the delegated decision determines *whether/how much* liberty is restricted, versus merely *how* an already-judicially-fixed restriction is administered.

### Case: United States v. Kunz
**Citation:** 68 F.4th 748 (2d Cir. 2023)
**Binding status:** Binding (Second Circuit)
**Verification status:** **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — FULL OPINION NOT DIRECTLY READ; reconstructed from WebSearch snippets and a Casemine commentary on the related Keleher decision, which discusses Kunz's holding as background.]**

**Holding (as reconstructed):** Kunz challenged a condition making his internet/computer access contingent on compliance with computer-monitoring terms devised by Probation, on procedural/substantive reasonableness and delegation grounds. The Second Circuit affirmed, holding that a district court must **"unambiguously authorize"** the restriction itself, after which "details" — described as things like "time limits, specific websites, or apps" — may be left to Probation as "minor details," **provided an objective, rule-based program constrains Probation's discretion** rather than leaving Probation with open-ended, standardless authority. Kunz is understood (per the Keleher commentary) as "supply[ing] the template for upholding [] delegation" where "a rule-based framework limits probation's discretion."

### Standard Condition 6 — Home visits / "or elsewhere" / plain-view seizure at the workplace

No case squarely addressing an employer's right to bar a probation officer from a workplace, or a probationer's lack of authority to "permit" seizure of property he does not own or control at a third-party worksite, was located in this session. This appears to be an argument that must be built from first principles rather than a single controlling case:

- The condition's plain-view seizure authority is a *quasi-consent* mechanism — it operates because Bernecky himself is the one "permitting" the seizure of items in his own space. At an employer's premises, Bernecky has no possessory or supervisory authority over the premises or over co-workers' or the employer's property, and therefore cannot, as a matter of basic property/consent law, "permit" anything with respect to it. This tracks the general Fourth Amendment principle (see Part 4, Georgia v. Randolph and the third-party-consent line of cases) that consent authority runs with possessory control, not simply presence.
- Separately, as a practical/administrative matter, the U.S. Courts' own guidance documents (persuasive only) confirm that "or elsewhere" is understood to include workplace visits as a matter of course — meaning the friction point (an employer's security/insurance rules barring probation officer entry) is a real, foreseeable administrative problem the Judgment's boilerplate text does not address, not a fanciful hypothetical.
- **Recommended framing:** rather than searching for a single case, this should be argued as a logical extension of ordinary third-party-property/consent principles combined with the practical, factual record here (an employer already raising exactly this concern) — paired with the CIMP employer-systems argument as a single consolidated "third-party/employer premises and property" section, per the client's own cross-reference note.

## PART 4 — Ultra Vires / No Jurisdiction Over the Non-Party Employer; Third-Party Fourth Amendment Interests

**No case was located squarely holding "a probation officer/court lacks authority to impose a supervised-release-derived requirement directly on a non-party employer."** This appears to be an argument of first impression on these particular facts, to be built from the following established building blocks:

1. **The condition's text has no employer-facing component at all.** Confirmed directly against the Judgment's verbatim language (client-directives.md, Special Condition (a)): the word "employ"/"employer"/"employment" does not appear anywhere in the CIMP condition. The condition addresses only computers/devices "owned or operated by the defendant." A probation officer's after-the-fact demand that a third-party employer "independently demonstrate 'like kind and degree' monitoring capability" has **zero textual anchor** in the Judgment. This is not a matter of construing ambiguous text charitably or narrowly — there is no text purporting to reach the employer at all. An agency (Probation) generally may not impose a requirement that has no basis whatsoever in the instrument (the Judgment) that is its sole source of authority over the defendant, let alone over a stranger to the case.

2. **Fourth Amendment rights are personal and cannot be administered away by assurances of government reasonableness offered to someone else.** *Georgia v. Randolph*, 547 U.S. 103 (2006) **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — reconstructed from secondary sources, not independently read]** — holding that a co-occupant's consent to a search cannot override a present, objecting co-occupant's own refusal, because Fourth Amendment protection of a space belongs personally to each person with a possessory interest in it, and one person's waiver does not extend to bind another's independent interest. Although Randolph is a co-occupant case, not an employer case, its core principle — that consent and waiver of Fourth Amendment interests are personal and cannot be supplied by someone else's say-so — supports the structural point underlying the client's theory 3: the government cannot satisfy *the employer's* Fourth Amendment concern about its own business systems merely by assuring the court (or Bernecky) that monitoring will be administered "reasonably." The employer's own interest in its systems is not before the court, was never consented to by the employer, and is not extinguished by a "common sense" or narrowing construction adopted to protect Bernecky's interests, because that canon has no logical purchase on a non-litigant's separate rights.

3. **Practical framing for the motion:** the relief that follows is not a request that the court adjudicate the employer's Fourth Amendment rights (the employer is not a party and the court likely lacks a live case or controversy to do so directly) — rather, it is a request that the court **clarify that the condition itself does not authorize, and Probation has no delegated authority to impose, any employer-facing monitoring-capability-parity requirement**, because (a) no such requirement appears in the condition's text, (b) no court ever found it necessary via the § 5F1.5 process (if the practical effect is occupational), and (c) Probation may not manufacture new, unauthorized conditions in the course of "implementation" under the Matta/Kunz/Carlineo delegation framework (Part 3). This reframes an otherwise-difficult third-party-standing problem into a delegation-and-textual-authority argument that the court can resolve without adjudicating the employer's own rights at all — the cleanest and most defensible way to plead this theory.

4. **Employer-owned-computer expectation-of-privacy doctrine (context, not directly dispositive):** general employment-law authority holds that *employees* often lack a reasonable expectation of privacy in employer-owned systems as against their *employer*. That doctrine is not on point here — the question is not whether Bernecky has privacy rights in his employer's systems (he doesn't need to assert any), but whether *the employer* can be compelled, indirectly, to submit its own systems to government-mandated monitoring-capability requirements neither the Judgment nor any statute imposes on it. No authority located addresses this specific configuration; flag as an argument built on principle rather than direct precedent. **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING]**

## PART 5 — The "Common Sense Reading" Doctrine and Its Limits

As noted in Part 2, I was **not able to confirm** in this session that *Browder* is the source of the "common sense reading" doctrine the client attributes to Second Circuit practice. What I could confirm:

- A Federal Defenders of NY blog post (title only, full text not read) describes Second Circuit practice of **"constru[ing] supervised-release conditions ... in the defendant's favor in order to avoid constitutional, statutory, or delegation problems."** **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — identify and read the case(s) behind this post before citing it.]**
- This is consistent with, but not confirmed to be the same doctrine as, the general canon of constitutional avoidance applied to ambiguous supervision conditions.

**The limiting principle to argue (regardless of which specific case anchors the doctrine):** a construe-in-the-defendant's-favor canon is, by definition, a tool for protecting the defendant's own rights against an overbroad literal reading. It has no logical application to protecting a third party's separate constitutional interests, because:
(a) the third party is not the object of the canon's protective purpose;
(b) the third party has not had, and structurally cannot have, an opportunity to be heard on how the condition should be construed as to their own systems; and
(c) "the government will administer this reasonably" is not a cognizable substitute for the third party's own Fourth Amendment protection — reasonableness assurances offered to a court in a case the third party isn't part of cannot bind the government's actual conduct toward that third party, and even if they could, they are not enforceable by the third party, who has no vehicle to compel compliance.

This should be pled as a legal/structural argument, explicitly flagging that the specific "common sense reading" case(s) require further verification, while making clear the argument does not depend on any single case — it follows from the nature of constitutional-avoidance canons themselves.

## PART 6 — U.S. Sentencing Commission Policy: 2025 Supervised Release Amendment

**Source:** ussc.gov (via WebSearch snippets only — direct ussc.gov page fetch failed with the same 403 tool error affecting all direct fetches this session). **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING by pulling the actual Federal Register notice, 90 Fed. **What I could confirm:**
- The Commission promulgated amendments to the supervised-release guidelines (Chapter 5, and revocation guidance in Chapter 7) that took effect **November 1, 2025**.
- The amendment's stated purpose is to require **individualized determinations** by courts at "all relevant stages" — imposition, modification, extension, termination, and revocation of supervised release — responding to "widespread concern that supervised release often is ordered reflexively."
- Courts must now **"explain on the record their reasoning for imposing, not imposing, or modifying supervised release,"** taking into account statutory factors and case-specific circumstances.

- A "graduated response" framework for supervised-release **violations** (Chapter 7/Part C) encourages courts to consider the "least restrictive" available options that still promote public safety, rather than automatic revocation.

The 2025 amendment's individualized-findings and least-restrictive-means themes are **generally supportive background** for Bernecky's overarching argument (that conditions imposed without individualized findings, and administered in a maximally restrictive manner, run against current Commission policy direction) but **should not be cited as if the Commission specifically addressed CIMP, occupational bars, or delegation** — it did not, so far as this research could confirm.
