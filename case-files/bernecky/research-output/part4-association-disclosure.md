## PART 1 — Standard Condition 8: No Communication/Interaction With Felons

**Condition text:** "You must not communicate or interact with someone you know is engaged in criminal activity. If you know someone has been convicted of a felony, you must not knowingly communicate or interact with that person without first getting the permission of the probation officer."

### 1.1 Threshold correction to client's characterization of *Reeves*

The client-directives document cites *United States v. Reeves*, 591 F.3d 77 (2d Cir. 2010), as "narrowing an association condition to require knowledge + a sustained relationship." **This citation is confirmed as a real, correctly cited Second Circuit case, but the client's characterization of its subject matter is incorrect and should not be used as drafted.** *Reeves* did not involve a "no association with felons" condition at all. It involved a special condition requiring Reeves to notify his probation officer and any new partner, "upon commencing a significant romantic relationship," of his prior sex-offense conviction. The Second Circuit held that condition void for vagueness and not reasonably necessary, reasoning that "no source provides guidance ... as to what constitutes" a "significant romantic relationship," and that Reeves's liberty should not "hinge on the accuracy of his prediction" of how a probation officer, prosecutor, or judge would later characterize a relationship. **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — confirm exact quotes and pincites against the slip opinion; WebFetch could not retrieve full text in this session.]**

*Reeves* remains useful to Bernecky, but only for its **general doctrinal framework**, not as a felon-association case:
- Conditions of supervised release must be "reasonably related" to the § 3553(a) sentencing factors and must "involve no greater deprivation of liberty than is reasonably necessary" — the statutory standard under § 3583(d), which *Reeves* applied to vacate an overbroad condition.
- Due process requires that a condition be "sufficiently clear to give a person of ordinary intelligence a reasonable opportunity to know what is prohibited."
- *Reeves* also confirms that the Second Circuit recognizes a "right to enter into and maintain intimate personal relationships" as a liberty interest the government conceded was "well established" — useful, by analogy, for the familial-association argument in Part 2 below, though it arose in the romantic-partner, not family, context.

**Binding/persuasive status:** Binding Second Circuit authority for the general vagueness/overbreadth/§3583(d) framework. Not on-point precedent for the specific felon-communication condition.

### 1.2 No squarely on-point Second Circuit case distinguishing "communication" from "association" bans could be confirmed

Despite a substantial search effort (WebSearch across multiple phrasings; attempted direct retrieval of the U.S. Courts' own treatise chapter on this exact condition, which was blocked by the same 403 issue), **no Second Circuit decision could be located and confirmed that squarely holds a "communicate or interact" condition unconstitutional or narrows it to require a "sustained relationship."** This should be treated as a genuine gap, not a client error to be silently corrected away — it means the argument as to Standard Condition 8 will likely need to be framed as new/argued from first principles rather than presented as compelled by existing Second Circuit precedent. Do not represent to the court that Second Circuit precedent already resolves this; that would be filing a citation you could not verify.

What **can** be documented and used:

**(a) The 2016 revision history of the condition's text is a strong textual argument, independently of case law.** Multiple archived AO 245B judgment forms confirm that the pre-2016 standard language was: *"The defendant shall not associate with any persons engaged in criminal activity, and shall not associate with any person convicted of a felony ..."* The post-2016 form — the one used in Bernecky's own 2018 Judgment — instead reads: *"You must not communicate or interact with someone you know is engaged in criminal activity. If you know someone has been convicted of a felony, you must not knowingly communicate or interact with that person without first getting the permission of the probation officer."* **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — pull the actual Judicial Conference Advisory Committee note/2016 revision history, if any exists, from uscourts.gov or the Federal Register once WebFetch access is restored; this session could not retrieve the underlying committee rationale.]** Two textual points follow regardless of intent behind the change:
  - "Communicate" is not synonymous with "associate." Ordinary usage of "communicate" reaches a single, one-way, or momentary exchange (an autograph request, a letter, a shouted greeting at a rally) that does not require an ongoing relationship, whereas "associate"/"interact" more naturally connotes a relationship or course of conduct. The condition uses **both** words disjunctively ("communicate **or** interact"), which on its face reaches more than an association-only condition would.
  - The added word "knowingly" (present in the felon clause but read into the "engaged in criminal activity" clause by at least the "you know" language) addresses *mens rea* vagueness but does nothing to narrow the *scope* of prohibited conduct once knowledge exists — it does not import a duration, materiality, or relationship requirement.

**(b) The general Second Circuit vagueness/overbreadth framework (from *Reeves* and its progeny) applies with full force to Standard Condition 8** even without a squarely on-point felon-association case: the condition must be (i) sufficiently clear that a person of ordinary intelligence understands what is prohibited, (ii) reasonably related to the §3553(a) factors, and (iii) no greater a deprivation of liberty than reasonably necessary. Apply these three prongs to the client's specific hypotheticals:
  - **Structural housing conflict** — sex offenders are frequently housed adjacent to or among other registered offenders/felons because of residency restrictions and landlord reluctance; a condition that criminalizes ordinary greeting-level neighbor contact absent advance PO permission is not "reasonably necessary" where the government's own registration/residency regime is what created the proximity.
  - **Direct conflict with Special Condition (b)** (mandatory sex-offense group treatment) — the Judgment simultaneously *compels* contact with other convicted felons (fellow group members) and *bans* it elsewhere without reconciling the two, which is itself evidence the condition as globally applied is not "reasonably related" in a coherent way and was not the product of the individualized analysis *Betts* requires (see 1.2(d) below).
  - **Workplace scenario** — a coworker's felony status is often incidental and not learned until after an employment relationship exists; requiring Bernecky either to cut off routine workplace communication or preemptively seek PO permission before he can know whether permission is even needed is unworkable and, per *Reeves*'s vagueness logic, improperly conditions his liberty on guessing how the PO/court will later characterize incidental workplace contact.
  - **First Amendment hypotheticals** (autograph from a convicted-felon celebrity; attendance at a political rally advocating felon voting-rights restoration; a letter to a public figure) — these implicate speech and petition-clause-adjacent activity that has no logical relationship to protecting the public from re-offense, and a "communication" ban (as opposed to a ban on *sustained relationships* or *cohabitation*) reaches this core protected activity with no de minimis or incidental-contact carve-out.

**(c) Overbreadth-by-the-numbers.** Client's ~20% figure is directionally consistent with widely cited estimates (e.g., Sentencing Project/BJS-derived estimates that roughly one in five, or somewhat lower depending on methodology, adult Americans has a felony conviction), but the precise figure varies substantially by source and methodology and **must be sourced to a specific, current BJS/Sentencing Project publication before filing** — do not use "20%" without a footnote to an actual dataset. **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING.]**

**(d) *United States v. Betts*, 886 F.3d 198, 202 (2d Cir. 2018)** — confirmed via WebSearch (Federal Defenders of New York Second Circuit blog commentary) to hold that a district court imposing a special condition of supervised release must "make an individualized assessment" and "state on the record the reason for imposing it," and that "the failure to do so is error." **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — confirm exact quote and pincite against the slip opinion.]** This is directly useful across every condition in this track: nothing in the Judgment's boilerplate Sheet 3B reflects an individualized *Betts* finding for Standard Condition 8, Special Condition (c)'s family restriction, or Special Condition (e)'s PSR-disclosure authorization.

**Requested relief supported by this research:**
1. Narrow the condition from a blanket communication/interaction ban to a **cohabitation/harboring restriction** (client's suggested narrower alternative) — supported by the *Reeves* "no greater deprivation than reasonably necessary" framework and the coherence problem created by Special Condition (b).
2. In the alternative, add an express **de minimis/incidental-contact carve-out** and clarify that "knowingly communicate" does not reach one-off, non-substantive, or constitutionally protected expressive contact (autographs, rallies, correspondence to public figures).
3. Request a *Betts*-compliant individualized statement of reasons if the condition is retained in any form.

## PART 2 — Special Condition (c): Family-Association Piece

**Condition text (family-relevant portion):** "The defendant shall not have deliberate contact with any child under 18 years of age, excluding his biological or adopted children, unless approved by the probation officer or by the Court."

### 2.1 Familial association as a protected liberty interest

**Moore v. City of East Cleveland, 431 U.S. 494 (1977)** (U.S. Supreme Court, plurality opinion by Powell, J.) — confirmed via WebSearch/Justia/Cornell LII summaries. Struck down a municipal zoning ordinance that criminalized a grandmother's cohabitation with her grandson because it defined "family" too narrowly (nuclear family only). Holding, as reconstructed from secondary sources: "the freedom of personal choice in matters of family life is one of the liberties protected by the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment," and this protection is **not limited to the nuclear family** — it extends to the "extended family" pattern (grandparent-grandchild, and by the same logic, aunt/uncle-niece/nephew, cousin-cousin) which has "roots equally venerable and equally deserving of constitutional recognition." The Court held that when government intrudes on family living/associational choices, "the usual deference to the legislature is inappropriate" and courts must "examine carefully the importance of the governmental interests advanced and the extent to which they are served by the challenged regulation." **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — confirm exact quoted language against the U.S. Reports; this is Supreme Court authority, binding nationwide, and should be locatable without difficulty once WebFetch access is restored.]**

**Application to Bernecky:** *Moore* is directly on point for the client's central complaint — that Special Condition (c)'s carve-out for "biological or adopted children" (which Bernecky has none of) is not a meaningful family exception at all, because it excludes the *extended* family relationships (nieces, nephews, cousins, his sister's children) that *Moore* recognizes as constitutionally significant in their own right, not merely as a proxy for parent-child relationships.

**Troxel v. Granville, 530 U.S. 57 (2000)** — confirmed as a real, frequently cited Supreme Court case recognizing that a fit parent's decisions about their child's associations (there, whether grandparents may visit) are entitled to a presumption of validity, because "there is a presumption that fit parents act in the best interests of their children." **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — confirm holding and pincite; note *Troxel* is usually invoked to protect the *parent's* authority against third-party/state override, so it must be cited carefully — it supports Bernecky's position only derivatively, i.e., that his sister (or mother/father) as the minor's parent should be trusted to decide, subject to her own knowledge of Bernecky's conviction, whether her children may be present, rather than requiring Bernecky himself to seek advance case-by-case PO approval every time.]**

### 2.2 No balancing test performed; no nexus finding

There is nothing in the Judgment (per intake, Section 6.3(c) and Section 8) reflecting any individualized finding that Bernecky poses a physical danger to family members specifically, as opposed to the generic risk addressed by the offense-based conditions generally. Under *Betts*, 886 F.3d 198 (2d Cir. 2018) (see Part 1.2(d) above), the absence of a stated, individualized reason for extending a contact restriction to family members beyond biological/adopted children is itself a procedural defect independent of the substantive familial-association liberty interest. Combining *Betts* (procedural: no individualized statement of reasons) with *Moore* (substantive: extended family relationships are constitutionally protected, triggering "careful examination" rather than rubber-stamp deference) gives two independent grounds for relief.

### 2.3 The "supervising adult" alternative

No case law could be located in this session that squarely blesses or rejects a "contact permitted in the presence of a knowledgeable supervising adult" carve-out as a term of art in federal supervised-release practice; however, the U.S. Courts' own treatise chapter on Association and Contact Restrictions (accessible via search-result caching before the WebFetch block) confirms that probation-condition drafting practice already recognizes categories like reporting incidental/unplanned contact and structuring conditions around specific "risk factors in the individual case" rather than blanket bans — supporting the argument that a supervising-adult carve-out is a recognized *type* of narrowing tool within the existing conditions toolkit, even without a single case adjudicating this exact carve-out. **[This is a persuasive-authority/policy argument, not a holding — do not overstate it as "courts have approved a supervising-adult carve-out"; the honest framing is that no court has rejected it either, and it is consistent with the individualized, risk-based approach *Betts* requires.]**

**Requested relief supported by this research:**
1. Extend the family carve-out beyond "biological or adopted children" to cover contact with minor relatives (nieces, nephews, cousins) when in the presence of an adult relative — e.g., mother, father, sister — who has actual knowledge of the conviction, without requiring case-by-case advance PO approval, grounded in *Moore*'s extended-family liberty interest and the absence of any *Betts*-compliant nexus finding to the contrary.
2. In the alternative, at minimum, require the sentencing court to make the individualized nexus-to-danger finding *Betts* requires before the condition can be enforced against extended-family contact.

## PART 3 — Special Condition (e): Sex Offender Registration / PSR Disclosure

**Condition text:** "The defendant shall register with the state sex offender registration agency ... The probation office is authorized to release the defendant's presentence report to the New York State Board of Examiners of Sex Offenders. Further disclosure to the county court and to the parties involved in the determination of the defendant's final classification level is also authorized."

### 3.1 *United States v. Charmer Industries, Inc.*, 711 F.2d 1164 (2d Cir. 1983)

**Confirmed** via CourtListener search-result metadata (opinion ID 421656, docketed as *United States v. Charmer Industries, Inc., and Peerless Importers, Inc.*, argued April 29, 1983, decided June 28, 1983) and via WebSearch snippets summarizing the opinion's reasoning. The case arose when the Arizona Attorney General sought to use/publish a federal presentence report prepared for one of the Charmer/Peerless corporate defendants; Peerless (the entity whose PSR was at issue) moved to prevent disclosure.

**Holding, as reconstructed from secondary-source summaries [VERIFY BEFORE FILING — full text could not be retrieved in this session]:**
- "[A] district court should not authorize disclosure of a presentence report to a third person in the absence of [a] compelling demonstration that disclosure ... is required to meet the ends of justice."
- The burden is on **the party seeking disclosure** to establish a **particularized need** for the material.
- Rationale: PSRs routinely contain hearsay, information with no direct relationship to the offense of conviction, and information given to the probation officer **in confidence** (financial data the defendant did not want made public, psychiatric evaluations meant to be restricted to the court, law-enforcement source information); treating PSRs as freely disclosable "is contrary to the public interest" because it would compromise the sentencing court's ability to obtain candid information in future cases, both from defendants and from independent sources.

**Binding/persuasive status:** Binding Second Circuit authority. This is the controlling Second Circuit case on PSR confidentiality and third-party disclosure standards, and it is the same case the client cited in his own pro se January 2026 emergency TRO filings (per intake Section 10) — so this track's research corroborates rather than originates the client's own theory.

**Application to Bernecky — does a Judgment-level blanket pre-authorization satisfy or violate *Charmer*?** On the reconstructed *Charmer* standard, the answer is a strong **"likely violates."** *Charmer* places the burden of demonstrating a "compelling" and "particularized" need for disclosure on **the party seeking it** at the time disclosure is sought, and contemplates individualized judicial evaluation of that need. Special Condition (e), by contrast, is a **blanket, standing, Judgment-level pre-authorization** entered at sentencing — years before any actual disclosure request from the NY State Board of Examiners of Sex Offenders — with no particularized showing of compelling need ever made, no opportunity for Bernecky to contest the specific disclosure at the time it occurs, and no judicial reassessment of whether "the ends of justice" require it in this specific instance. A blanket advance waiver of the *Charmer* particularized-need showing is difficult to reconcile with a standard that expressly places the burden on the requesting party and requires a case-specific "compelling" showing. This is the single strongest doctrinal hook in this entire research track, because it is **binding, on-point, and squarely contrary to the condition's structure** — not merely persuasive-by-analogy the way most of the other conditions in this track are.

**Caveat:** *Charmer* itself did not arise in the sex-offender civil-classification context, and courts *have* recognized that state sex-offender classification boards have a specific and legitimate need for PSR information to perform SORNA-adjacent risk classification. Expect the government to argue that (a) Congress/SORNA contemplates exactly this kind of information-sharing with state registration authorities, and (b) *Charmer*'s "particularized need" standard is satisfied categorically by the state board's statutory classification function. The rebuttal is that even if some disclosure could survive *Charmer* if requested and evaluated case-by-case, the Judgment's **blanket, standing, pre-authorized** structure — with no opportunity for Bernecky to be heard on the "particularized need" question at the time of actual disclosure — is what fails *Charmer*, independent of whether disclosure could ever be justified in some individualized form.

### 3.2 People v. Mingo, 12 N.Y.3d 563 (2009)

**Confirmed** via multiple sources (Justia, NYCourts.gov, Cornell/NYCTAP case summaries). This is a **New York Court of Appeals** decision (not Second Circuit; persuasive, not binding, in the federal § 3583(e)(2) motion, though directly relevant to the client's parallel/related state SORA proceeding). Holding, as reconstructed: SORA risk-level hearings are governed by a "reliable hearsay" standard, not the Confrontation Clause; presentence reports and case summaries "meet the reliable hearsay standard for admissibility at SORA proceedings" because they are prepared under statutory mandate and their "origins and function are well-known to SORA courts" — but a court **need not credit** information in a PSR/case summary that is "unduly speculative" or is undermined by more compelling evidence. **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING.]**

**Relevance:** *Mingo* is not a disclosure-standard case — it is an admissibility/weight-of-evidence case for the *state* SORA risk-classification hearing itself, addressing what happens once a PSR is already before the SORA court. It does **not** independently justify (or forbid) the *federal* disclosure of a sealed PSR to the state Board in the first place; that threshold disclosure question is governed by *Charmer*, not *Mingo*. Client's pro se filings' reliance on *Mingo* appears therefore best used as a **downstream** point (i.e., once/if a PSR reaches the state SORA court, *Mingo* governs how it may be used and constrains over-reliance on speculative PSR content) rather than as authority for blocking disclosure at the federal level. The two cases should not be conflated in drafting: *Charmer* is the disclosure-authorization hook for the § 3583(e)(2) motion; *Mingo* is a separate, persuasive, state-law point about downstream use once disclosed.

### 3.3 Registration-clause redundancy

Confirmed as a straightforward textual point, not requiring case law: Mandatory Condition 6 (per conditions-list.md § 6.1) already requires compliance with SORNA (34 U.S.C. § 20901 et seq.) registration "as directed by the probation officer, the Bureau of Prisons, or any state sex offender registration agency." Special Condition (e)'s opening clause ("shall register with the state sex offender registration agency in any state where the defendant resides, is employed, carries on a vocation, or is a student") duplicates this obligation. This is a clean-up/surplusage point for drafting, not a rights violation — recommend it be raised as a request to strike the redundant registration clause while preserving (or striking, per the *Charmer* argument) the PSR-disclosure clause separately, since the two clauses serve different functions and should not be treated as a package for relief purposes.

**Requested relief supported by this research:**
1. Strike or substantially narrow the PSR-disclosure authorization in Special Condition (e) as inconsistent with *Charmer*'s particularized-need, burden-on-the-requester standard; at minimum, require that any future disclosure request go through a *Charmer*-compliant judicial determination at the time disclosure is actually sought, with notice and an opportunity for Bernecky to be heard, rather than a standing blanket pre-authorization.
2. Strike the redundant "shall register with the state sex offender registration agency" clause as surplusage duplicative of Mandatory Condition 6.

## PART 4 — Standard Condition 12: Risk Notification / *Boles*

**Condition text:** "If the probation officer determines that you pose a risk to another person (including an organization), the probation officer may require you to notify the person about the risk and you must comply with that instruction. The probation officer may contact the person and confirm that you have notified the person about the risk."

### 4.1 *United States v. Boles*, 914 F.3d 95 (2d Cir. 2019)

**Citation confirmed.** Docket No. 17-1138, decided January 25, 2019 (Justia); the case is *United States v. Donald Ray Boles*, appeal from a District of Vermont child-pornography-possession conviction (18 U.S.C. § 2252(a)(4)(B)) and 120-month sentence with a 10-year term of supervised release. **This is the same "risk" condition language at issue in Bernecky's Standard Condition 12, word for word** (confirmed independently by the W.D.N.Y. standing order itself, see 4.2 below, which states the district's Standard Condition 12 "is worded identically" to the condition *Boles* struck down).

**Holding, as reconstructed from WebSearch snippets of the opinion:** The Second Circuit agreed with Boles that the risk condition **"is vague and affords too much discretion to the probation officer,"** because the condition does not define "risk" and leaves "too much discretion[] to the supervising officer" — including discretion to require notification to employers based on an undefined risk standard. The court found the condition "largely indistinguishable" from a similar condition it had previously struck down. **Remedy: the risk condition was vacated and the case remanded to the district court "to clarify the scope of the 'risk' condition."** The remainder of the conviction and sentence was affirmed. **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — confirm exact quoted language and the precise remand instructions against the slip opinion; note that the Second Circuit's remand instruction was to "clarify," which is significant — see 4.3 below.]**

**Binding/persuasive status:** Binding Second Circuit authority, squarely on point — the condition text in Bernecky's Judgment is (per the W.D.N.Y. standing order's own representation) identical to the one *Boles* invalidated.

### 4.2 The W.D.N.Y. standing order

Retrieved directly from the case's Google Drive folder (file `wdny standing order re risk notification boles.pdf`, filed and entered March 22, 2019, signed by Hon. Frank P. Geraci, Jr., then-Chief Judge, W.D.N.Y.). **Full verbatim text of the operative paragraph:**

> "In *United States v. Boles*, 914 F.3d 95 (2d Cir. 2019), the Second Circuit held that the standard 'risk' condition of supervised release is vague and affords too much discretion to the probation officer. The United States District Court for the Western District of New York currently uses a standard condition (number 12) that is worded identically. In light of the *Boles* decision, the United States District Court for the Western District of New York will amend the Judgment and Commitment order in all criminal cases in which a term of probation or supervised release is imposed by removing the standard 'risk' condition and replacing it with the following language:
>
> 'If the court determines in consultation with your probation officer that, based on your criminal record, personal history and characteristics, and the nature and circumstances of your offense, you pose a risk of committing further crimes against another person (including an organization), the probation officer may require you to notify the person about the risk and you must comply with that instruction. The probation officer may contact the person and confirm that you have notified the person about the risk.'"

This is a certified, primary-source, verbatim quote (pulled directly from the case file, not reconstructed from search snippets) — no [VERIFY] flag needed on this specific text.

### 4.3 Does "the court determines... in consultation with your probation officer" satisfy *Boles*, or does it just relocate the delegation problem?

This is the central legal question of this section, and the client's theory is well-founded on the text of both documents:

1. **The standing order's fix is aimed at the wrong half of the vagueness problem.** *Boles*'s stated defect was that the condition (a) did not define "risk" and (b) delegated too much discretion to the probation officer. The standing order responds to (a) by adding a list of factors the "risk" determination should rest on (criminal record, personal history/characteristics, nature/circumstances of offense) — this is a genuine improvement on the vagueness-of-"risk" problem. But its response to (b) is only to say the **court** — rather than the officer alone — makes the risk determination, "in consultation with" the probation officer. **The order does not describe any procedure by which the court makes that determination**: no hearing, no notice to the defendant that a risk-notification determination is being considered, no standard of proof, no right to be heard, and no role for counsel. The phrase "in consultation with the probation officer" is not defined and could mean anything from a formal recommendation-and-response process to an informal conversation with no defendant involvement whatsoever.

2. **"Consultation with the court" is not equivalent to adjudication by the court.** Nothing in the standing order requires the risk determination to occur on the record, with notice to the defendant, or with any opportunity for the defendant to contest either (a) whether he in fact poses a "risk" under the listed factors, or (b) the scope/content of any notification the officer proposes to require him to make. As drafted, it is entirely possible for the "consultation" to occur through an ex parte conversation or written note between the probation officer and the judge (or the judge's chambers staff), with the defendant learning of the determination only when he is instructed to comply. This is the structure the client characterizes as recreating "the ancient condemned practice of private tribunals" — a closed-door determination between officer and court, adversarial to no one, with the affected party absent from the process entirely.

3. **No case law was located in this session either approving or rejecting "consultation with the court" as an adequate substitute for a genuine adversarial process.** This appears to be an issue of first impression as to the W.D.N.Y. standing order specifically — no reported Second Circuit decision post-*Boles* addressing the adequacy of this or a materially similar standing order could be confirmed via WebSearch. **[This should be presented candidly to the court as an unresolved question, not overclaimed as settled by existing case law.]** The strongest available argument is not "case X holds this insufficient," but rather a structural argument built directly from *Boles* itself: if unconstrained probation-officer discretion is the defect *Boles* condemned, then relocating the same undefined, unreviewable, one-sided decision-making process to a "court" that acts on nothing more than an undefined "consultation" with the same probation officer — with no hearing, no notice, no standard of proof, and no opportunity for the defendant or counsel to be heard — does not cure the delegation/discretion problem; it merely adds a rubber stamp on top of it. The core vice *Boles* identified was not literally *who* holds the pen, but the **absence of any ascertainable standard and any process by which the defendant could know or contest the basis for a determination that will require him to broadcast his own criminal history to a third party** (with all the reputational, employment, and safety consequences that entails). A "consultation" that produces no findings the defendant can see or challenge does not supply that missing standard/process merely by virtue of a judge nominally being in the loop.

4. **Additional supporting authority:** *United States v. Peterson*, 248 F.3d 79 (2d Cir. 2001) — confirmed via WebSearch/OpenJurist/CourtListener metadata as a **W.D.N.Y.-origin** Second Circuit case (same district as Bernecky) in which the court vacated a condition requiring the defendant to notify potential employers and vocational/educational programs of his prior sex-offense and bank-larceny convictions, holding the notification requirement not "reasonably related" to the offense or the defendant's history/characteristics and not "reasonably necessary" to the statutory sentencing purposes. **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — confirm exact holding and pincite.]** *Peterson* pre-dates *Boles* by nearly two decades and shows the Second Circuit has *long* been skeptical of broad, officer-driven third-party notification conditions in this exact district — useful as corroborating, independent binding authority alongside *Boles* itself, not merely as background.

**Requested relief supported by this research:**
1. Argue that Standard Condition 12, even as it would be reformed under the W.D.N.Y. standing order, remains constitutionally deficient under *Boles* because "consultation with the court" supplies no ascertainable procedure for notice, hearing, or counsel before a risk-notification determination is made or acted upon.
2. Request that any risk-notification condition include express procedural protections: advance notice to Bernecky of a proposed risk determination, an opportunity to be heard (with counsel) before the determination is finalized or acted upon, and a written statement of the basis for any risk finding referencing the standing order's own listed factors (criminal record, personal history/characteristics, nature/circumstances of offense).
3. In the alternative, argue that Probation's apparent presentation of the **pre-*Boles*, unmodified** condition language to Bernecky (per client-directives.md item 3, flagged as needing document confirmation) independently entitles him to correction — Probation is not even applying the district's own corrective standing order to his case. **[ACTION ITEM — confirm whether Probation's "expected conditions" document referenced by the client actually uses the pre-*Boles* language; this document was not located in the Drive folder search performed in this session and needs a follow-up targeted search or a request to the client for the specific file.]**

## PART 5 — Standard Condition 10 (Weapons/Pepper Spray) — Brief, Low-Priority

Per client instruction, minimal time was spent here. Findings:

- The condition's definition of "dangerous weapon" — "anything that was designed, or was modified for, the specific purpose of causing bodily injury or death to another person" — is drawn from, and tracks, the U.S. Sentencing Guidelines' general "dangerous weapon" concept (an instrument "capable of inflicting death or serious bodily injury").
- General (non-supervised-release) case law confirms that pepper spray/OC spray **has** been treated as a "dangerous weapon" in some contexts — e.g., under 18 U.S.C. § 930 (weapons at federal facilities) and in at least Ninth Circuit sentencing-guideline enhancement case law, courts have found pepper spray capable of causing "serious bodily injury" for guideline purposes. **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — this line of authority arises in different statutory contexts (federal-facility weapons ban; guideline enhancement) and has not been confirmed as applied to the specific "designed/modified for the specific purpose of causing bodily injury or death" definition used in this Judgment's supervised-release condition.]**
- No case was located addressing a personal-safety/self-defense accommodation carve-out for a supervisee in a documented elevated-threat category (e.g., registered sex offenders facing vigilante-violence risk). This appears to be genuinely uncharted territory.
- **Bottom line, consistent with client's own assessment: this is likely academic.** Pepper spray's stated design purpose (incapacitation/deterrence via irritation, not death or "bodily injury" in the sense the condition's illustrative examples — nunchakus, tasers — suggest) creates a colorable textual argument that it falls outside the condition's plain definition, but no authority was found either confirming or foreclosing that reading in the supervised-release context specifically. Do not invest further research time absent a specific triggering event (e.g., Probation actually objecting to pepper spray possession).

## PART 6 — U.S. Sentencing Commission Policy-Statement Developments

Confirmed via ussc.gov: the Commission's **2025 amendments to U.S.S.G. § 5D1.3** (effective November 2025) reflect a general policy shift toward more individualized, evidence-based supervised-release practice — the amended guideline provides that discretionary conditions are "no longer the standard," and instead requires courts to conduct an "individualized assessment" tailored to "the nature of the offense, the defendant's history, and the specific purposes of supervision as applied to that defendant's case," with encouragement to reassess conditions early in the supervision term for possible modification or reduction. **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — confirm exact amendment text against the 2025 Guidelines Manual, § 5D1.3 and accompanying commentary, at ussc.gov/guidelines/2025-guidelines-manual.]**

**Important limitation, stated candidly:** this 2025 amendment is a **general** policy shift toward individualized assessment of supervised-release conditions overall. **No provision specific to associational restrictions, family-contact conditions, or PSR/disclosure confidentiality was identified** in this research session. Do not overclaim that the Commission has specifically addressed felon-communication conditions, family-association carve-outs, or PSR disclosure to state agencies — it has not, so far as this research could confirm. The amendment is useful only as general reinforcement of the *Betts* individualized-assessment argument that runs through Parts 1 and 2 above (i.e., the current, evolving policy consensus — now reflected in the Guidelines themselves — is that boilerplate, one-size-fits-all conditions imposed without individualized justification are disfavored), not as condition-specific authority.
