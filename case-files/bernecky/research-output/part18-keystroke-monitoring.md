## BOTTOM LINE UP FRONT

1. **Lifshitz does not go beyond footnote 10 on keystroke logging**, and — newly confirmed this pass — **it says nothing whatsoever about attorney-client privilege.** The word "attorney" appears in the opinion exactly once, in the case caption (counsel's name). "Privilege"/"privileged" does not appear at all. This closes out, on a primary-source basis, both halves of the open question flagged at the outset of this research task.
2. **A real, binding, and previously under-leveraged hook exists: *United States v. Kunz*, 68 F.4th 748, 766-67 (2d Cir. 2023).** Kunz construes the *identical* WDNY boilerplate privilege clause that appears in Bernecky's own Judgment — "designed to avoid, as much as possible, reading any privileged information or any private material that is not illegal" — and holds that this clause is a **real, judicially enforceable constraint on Probation's conduct**, not surplusage: it "constrains the supervisors in their surveillance ... by requiring them not to abuse their power and access." 68 F.4th at 767. This is the single most valuable new finding of this research track — it means Bernecky's argument does not need to persuade a court to recognize a *new* privilege carve-out; it can instead argue that **keystroke-level logging, as a category of technology, is incapable of honoring a privilege-avoidance clause the Second Circuit has already held Probation must actually comply with** — an as-applied argument grounded in the condition's own text, not a facial constitutional claim requiring new law.
3. **No case — not Lifshitz, not Kunz, not any source located this session — uses the words "attorney-client privilege" in the computer-monitoring-condition context.** No case squarely holds that a privilege carve-out or minimization procedure is *constitutionally required* for such a condition. The client's privilege theory is analytically sound and well-supported by combining Kunz (the clause is real and enforceable) with a technical/factual argument about what keystroke logging actually does, but it is **not yet the subject of any on-point holding** — this must be honestly disclosed in the motion, not papered over.
4. **The pictorial/text nexus theory is well-supported by analogy but not squarely decided anywhere.** *United States v. Sofsky*, 287 F.3d 122, 126-27 (2d Cir. 2002) — quoted at length in Lifshitz, 369 F.3d at 189 — already establishes, for an image-based receipt-of-child-pornography offense materially identical to Bernecky's, that "a more focused restriction, limited to pornography sites and images" is an available, adequate, narrower alternative to a blanket ban/broad monitoring regime. Lifshitz's own footnote 11 independently suggests, as a narrowing option, "monitoring software that searches for particular suspect words and phrases rather than recording all varieties of computer-related activity." Neither case, however, frames its narrowing suggestion as a rule that **the type of underlying offense conduct (image vs. text)** must set the *outer boundary* of permissible monitoring scope — that is the client's own extension of the doctrine, a reasonable and well-grounded one, but an extension, not an existing holding.
5. **No case squarely addresses keystroke logging as a distinct doctrinal category apart from monitoring/filtering generally**, and no circuit split or trend narrowing or vacating a keystroke-logging requirement *specifically* (as opposed to internet/computer monitoring generally) was found. This is a genuine, confirmed gap, not a research failure — it was affirmatively tested against the two cases most likely to contain it (Lifshitz, Kunz) and against WebSearch across multiple phrasings.
6. **§ 5F1.5 is not, on its own terms, the operative test for a monitoring condition's scope** (it is the occupational-restriction guideline); its relevance here is by analogy. But it is a useful analogy: even in the occupational-restriction context, the Guideline conditions the nexus specifically on "the conduct relevant to the offense of conviction" — not on the mere instrumentality involved. That structure supports, by analogy, the client's argument that a monitoring condition's *scope* should likewise track the actual offense conduct (images), not merely the fact that a computer was used.

**Overall strength assessment:** This is a **credible, well-supported argument for narrowing relief, not a slam-dunk facial challenge.** It is strongest when framed narrowly — as an as-applied request to modify how CIMP is technically implemented as to Bernecky specifically — and weakest if framed as a claim that any binding authority already holds keystroke logging unlawful or requires an attorney-client carve-out, because no such holding exists. Full analysis and a candid gap list follow below.

## 1. *United States v. Lifshitz* Re-Examined in Full — Keystroke Logging and Narrow Tailoring

**Citation:** 369 F.3d 173 (2d Cir. 2004) (Katzmann, J., for Calabresi, Katzmann & B.D. Parker, JJ.). Read in full from the Drive-folder PDF (`US v. Lifshitz, 369 F. 3d 173 - Court of Appeals, 2nd Circuit 2004 - Google Scholar.pdf`, file ID `1lq4zYMoo8fqPPdNIBJS10xiQ0DBn-dqC`).

### 1.1 The keystroke-logging footnote (fn. 10) — confirmed, and its precise scope

The relevant text, at 369 F.3d at 191-92, describes "two principal axes" along which monitoring methods diverge: (1) software installed locally versus reliance on ISP records, and (2) content-specific vs. all-activity monitoring:

> "Second, some software focuses attention upon specific types of unauthorized materials, whereas other kinds monitor all activities engaged in by the computer user.[10]"

Footnote 10 itself, in full:

> "On the more comprehensive side, some monitoring software boasts the ability to track all of an individual's uses of his or her computer through what is called 'keystroke' monitoring. See Matt Richtel, *A Different Type of Computer Monitor*, N.Y. Times, Aug. 27, 1998, at G3 (explaining that '[a] new generation of software is emerging to permit employers to record not just which Web sites employees browse, but also which programs they use, memos they write, E-mails they send — in short, every keystroke'). By contrast, other software may be more attuned to detecting specific impermissible activities. See John Schwartz, *Internet Leash Can Monitor Sex Offenders*, N.Y. Times, Dec. 31, 2001, at C1 (describing how, with a program called Cyber Sentinel, '[w]henever the system detects suspicious activity, it captures whatever is on the screen at the time, stores it on the user's hard drive and sends an e-mail message to the county probation officer,' and explaining that the 'program works by monitoring everything that crosses the computer screen and comparing the sites visited and the words appearing on the screen with a database of sexually oriented sites and phrases')." 369 F.3d at 191-92 n.10.

**This confirms, and slightly refines, part5's prior finding.** Keystroke monitoring is named exactly once, in a footnote, as the *most comprehensive/intrusive example* on one axis of a two-axis taxonomy the court draws to help decide whether the Lifshitz condition (as it then stood) was adequately tailored. It is not the subject of any separate holding, test, or heightened standard — it functions as an illustrative data point for the general narrow-tailoring inquiry, not as an adjudicated category with its own rule.

The main text immediately following this footnote reinforces that the taxonomy is doing real analytical work, not merely descriptive:

> "These distinctions may be material to determining whether the scope of the monitoring condition's infringement on privacy is commensurate with the 'special needs' articulated in this case. Constant inspection of the documents that Lifshitz creates on his computer might be more like searching his diary or inspecting his closets than it is like the highly targeted diagnosis accomplished by drug testing. ... By contrast, software that alerted a probation officer only when Lifshitz was engaging in impermissible communications over e-mail or the Internet would bear much greater resemblance to screening a probationer's urine for particular drugs — as opposed to investigating a sample to ascertain all medical conditions from which the individual suffered." 369 F.3d at 192.

The "searching his diary" comparison is squarely on point for Bernecky's theory: it is the court's own language for describing what constant, content-blind capture of everything a person writes looks like, as against a targeted, offense-specific alternative.

### 1.2 The remand instructions (fn. 11) — the narrowing menu the court itself proposed

At 369 F.3d at 193, the court held the condition "may ... be overbroad" and remanded, "leav[ing] the resolution of this issue to the discretion of the district court," 369 F.3d at 193 n.11. The footnote's key language:

> "[T]wo ways in which the condition might be more narrowly tailored would be by limiting it to Internet-related activity and e-mail and by implementing monitoring software that searches for particular suspect words and phrases rather than recording all varieties of computer-related activity. This is not to suggest that the condition must necessarily be restricted to the monitoring of online conduct." 369 F.3d at 193 n.11.

Two things are worth flagging honestly here. First, this passage is genuinely useful: it is the Second Circuit itself, in the leading case, contemplating a menu of narrower alternatives to "recording all varieties of computer-related activity" (i.e., to what keystroke logging does), and it expressly approves keyword/phrase-triggered monitoring as one such alternative. Second, and important not to overstate: the suggested narrower alternative here is *itself* text-based ("particular suspect words and phrases") — it is not an image-specific alternative. Lifshitz's own suggested narrowing menu does not independently support restricting monitoring to *images* specifically; that leap comes from Sofsky (Section 2, below), not from Lifshitz's footnote 11.

### 1.3 Attorney-client privilege — confirmed absent from the opinion

A full-text search of the opinion for "attorney," "privilege," "privileged," "counsel," and "First Amendment"/"petition" was conducted directly against the retrieved primary-source text. Results:

- **"Attorney" appears exactly once** — in the case caption, identifying Jennifer G. Rodgers as an Assistant United States Attorney for the government. Not a substantive reference.
- **"Privilege"/"privileged" appears zero times.**
- **No discussion of attorney-client communications, legal mail, correspondence with public officials, or the petition clause appears anywhere in the opinion.**

**This affirmatively confirms the gap the client's directive flagged as unresolved.** Lifshitz simply does not address privilege in any form. Nothing about its "special needs"/narrow-tailoring framework depends on, or forecloses, a privilege-based argument — it is silent on the question, not adverse to it. Any privilege-based argument in the motion must be built without Lifshitz's help on this specific point (Kunz, discussed next, is the more useful case for this).

## 2. *United States v. Sofsky* — the Pictorial-Offense Alternative (as Quoted in Lifshitz and Kunz)

**Citation:** 287 F.3d 122 (2d Cir. 2002). **[VERIFY — the full Sofsky opinion was not located in the Drive folder and could not be independently opened this session due to the WebFetch 403 failures; everything below is quoted from Lifshitz's and Kunz's own quotations of Sofsky, both of which were read directly from primary-source PDFs. The quoted language itself is therefore reliable — it comes from two different Second Circuit panels' own verbatim quotations of Sofsky, not from a secondary source — but the full context, facts, and disposition of Sofsky itself should be independently confirmed before citing it as an independent authority in the motion.]**

Lifshitz describes and quotes Sofsky as follows, at 369 F.3d at 189:

> "This Court has previously confronted a case in which an individual, who pled guilty to receiving child pornography, challenged a condition of probation prohibiting access to computers or the Internet. See United States v. Sofsky, 287 F.3d 122 (2d Cir. 2002). Although we recognized that, under certain circumstances, such a condition may be 'reasonably related to the purposes of sentencing,' we held in that case that it resulted in a deprivation of liberty 'greater ... than is reasonably necessary.' Id. at 126. As an alternative, we suggested that 'a more focused restriction, limited to pornography sites and images, can be enforced by unannounced inspections of [a probationer's] premises and examination of material stored on his hard drive or removable disks.' Id. at 127."

Kunz, in 2023, separately cites Sofsky (again independently confirming it as real, current, and cited authority) for the proposition that Sofsky is among the "early computer monitoring cases from the late 1990s and early 2000s." 68 F.4th at 762.

**Significance for the pictorial-nexus theory:** Sofsky is, on its facts, the closest existing Second Circuit authority to the client's theory. It involves the *same offense type* as Bernecky's (receiving/possessing child pornography — image-based conduct), and it holds that a **narrower restriction focused specifically on "pornography sites and images"** — implemented through hard-drive/device examination — is an adequate, "more focused" means of achieving the government's supervisory interest, as against a broader access ban. This is real support for the proposition that, for this offense type, an image-and-file-focused monitoring regime is not merely *permissible* but has already been identified by the Second Circuit as the *appropriate* narrower alternative when a broader restriction is found excessive.

**What Sofsky does *not* do:** it does not hold that a *broader* monitoring condition (as opposed to a total ban, which is what Sofsky itself involved) must be narrowed to images only because the offense is image-based. Sofsky's holding addresses a computer/Internet-access ban, not an already-in-place monitoring/logging regime. The extension from "a ban must be replaced with image-focused device inspection" to "an existing broad keystroke-logging regime must be narrowed to image-focused monitoring because the offense is image-based" is the client's own analogical move — a sound one, resting on the same "reasonably related to the offense" / "no greater deprivation than necessary" framework Sofsky itself applies, but an extension rather than a case directly so holding.

## 3. *United States v. Kunz* — The Privilege Clause Is Real, and Its Limits

**Citation:** 68 F.4th 748 (2d Cir. 2023) (Lynch, J., for Livingston, C.J., and Calabresi, J.), Docket No. 21-2577-cr, decided May 22, 2023. On appeal from W.D.N.Y. (Siragusa, J.). Read in full from the Drive-folder PDF (file ID `1N9l4wc1XXg6rigfQL_68O5qrej_qnRi6`).

### 3.1 Why Kunz matters here specifically

Part1 and part5 already identified Kunz as central authority for the CIMP delegation theory (Special Condition (a)), because Kunz's WDNY condition is textually near-identical to Bernecky's ("owned or operated by the defendant... surveil all activity... consistent with the computer monitoring policy in effect by the probation office"). **Neither prior memo, however, flagged that Kunz's condition — like Bernecky's — also contains the exact privilege-avoidance sentence at issue in this research track**, and that Kunz's panel directly construed it. This is the new finding of this pass.

The Kunz condition (quoted at 68 F.4th at 758) includes:

> "Any such monitoring or examinations shall be designed to avoid, as much as possible, reading any privileged information or any private material that is not illegal or reasonably likely to lead to illegal material or evidence related to illegal activity."

This is **word-for-word the same sentence** that appears in Bernecky's Judgment (conditions-list.md, Special Condition (a), Sheet 3B) — confirming this is standardized WDNY boilerplate, not language unique to either defendant, and that Kunz's construction of it applies with full force to Bernecky's own condition.

### 3.2 The holding on the privilege clause

Kunz argued that a *different* CIMP-program term — a clause requiring him to "waive any expectations of privacy from the supervising probation officer, his or her designee, and the monitoring company" — contradicted the special condition's privilege-avoidance sentence, and that this contradiction was itself an unlawful delegation (letting Probation's own program terms silently override the court-ordered privilege protection). The panel disagreed that the two provisions conflict, but in doing so it necessarily construed the privilege-avoidance clause's function and gave it independent legal effect:

> "[W]e read the two together to mean simply that Kunz agrees that he is entitled to no greater expectation of privacy than that which is implied by the special condition. Although they tread similar ground, those two provisions target different things. **The special condition's text constrains the supervisors in their surveillance of Kunz by requiring them not to abuse their power and access**; the CIMP provision instructs Kunz that he has no standalone expectation of privacy beyond that. Together, the two provisions convey to Kunz that he can expect no greater privacy from his supervisors than what is guaranteed to him by the special condition. Though they may diverge in focus and in thrust, they are not contradictory, and pose no delegation problems." 68 F.4th at 766-67 (emphasis added).

**This is the single most valuable holding located in this research track.** The Second Circuit has already said, in a fully binding, on-point opinion construing the identical clause, that the "designed to avoid ... reading any privileged information" language is a genuine, judicially-recognized **constraint on how Probation may conduct its surveillance** — not aspirational language that means nothing in practice, and not language whose content is left to Probation's own discretion to define away. That is exactly the predicate Bernecky's privilege argument needs: a court does not have to be persuaded to invent a privilege protection from scratch; it only has to be shown that (a) this exact protection already exists in Bernecky's own Judgment, (b) the Second Circuit has already held it must be given real effect, and (c) keystroke-level logging cannot, as a technical matter, be reconciled with it.

### 3.3 What Kunz does *not* do — the honest limits

1. **Kunz does not address keystroke logging.** Kunz's technical/vagueness challenges concerned software-alteration notifications, password-change timing, and encryption ("https," messaging-app encryption) — not keystroke capture. The court had no occasion to consider whether continuous, content-blind character-by-character capture is compatible with the privilege clause; it addressed only whether a *different* CIMP clause (the privacy waiver) textually conflicted with the privilege clause, not whether privilege-sensitive material can, in practice, be captured without ever being "read."
2. **Kunz does not use the words "attorney-client."** The privilege clause it construes protects "privileged information" and "private material that is not illegal" generically — it is not limited to, nor does the opinion discuss, attorney-client communications specifically, correspondence with elected officials, or petition-clause material. Extending the clause's protection to those specific categories is a reasonable reading of "privileged information" (attorney-client communications are the paradigm example of "privileged information" in ordinary legal usage) but is, again, an extension this specific opinion does not make explicit.
3. **Kunz reads "designed to avoid ... reading" as regulating the humans who supervise the monitoring** ("constrains the supervisors ... by requiring them not to abuse their power and access") — it does not squarely address whether automated, indiscriminate *capture and storage* of privileged content (before any human ever "reads" it) is itself a violation of a clause phrased in terms of avoiding "reading," or whether the clause is satisfied so long as no human at Probation actually reviews the privileged portions of what was captured. This is the crux technical/legal question the client's theory raises, and it remains open — Kunz did not need to resolve it on its facts, because Kunz's challenge was to a different, non-privilege CIMP term.
4. **Kunz's own remand/construction methodology cuts both ways.** The court's general approach throughout the opinion is to construe ambiguous or arguably-overbroad CIMP terms narrowly to avoid constitutional problems, rather than to strike them (see, e.g., its construction of the "alterations"/encryption terms, 68 F.4th at 762-64). A court following Kunz's own methodology on Bernecky's facts might well prefer to **construe** the existing privilege clause to already require excluding keystroke-level capture of privileged material (or to require some segregation/minimization procedure), rather than to vacate or eliminate keystroke logging outright. This is actually a favorable data point for framing the *requested relief* (see Section 6 below) — Kunz suggests the court has a ready, low-friction path (a construing order) rather than needing to declare the condition facially invalid.

## 4. Is There a Circuit Split or Trend on Keystroke Logging Specifically? — Confirmed Gap

**Finding: no.** No case — Second Circuit or otherwise — squarely addressing keystroke logging as a *distinct* doctrinal category (as opposed to internet/computer monitoring generally), and no circuit split or trend narrowing, vacating, or specially limiting keystroke logging as such, could be located through either primary-source reading (Lifshitz, Kunz) or WebSearch across a dozen phrasings this session. This should be stated candidly in the motion rather than implied to exist.

What *is* available, and should be used honestly as general supporting context rather than overstated as squarely on point:

- **General appellate trend toward narrowing/vacating overbroad computer-monitoring and internet-ban conditions.** Multiple WebSearch results (law-firm and practitioner commentary, not independently read in primary form) describe the Tenth Circuit vacating a condition that gave probation officers discretion to impose a complete computer/Internet ban, and a Fifth Circuit decision finding a ten-year total internet/computer/electronic-device ban substantively unreasonable. **[VERIFY — neither case's citation or holding was independently confirmed against a primary source this session; do not cite without first pulling and reading the actual opinions.]** These are useful only as general-trend background (courts increasingly demand tailoring in this area), not as keystroke-specific authority.
- **United States v. Cope, 527 F.3d 944 (9th Cir. 2008)** — a Ninth Circuit case addressing a condition barring possession of material "depicting **and/or describing**" child pornography. Per WebSearch snippets (the primary opinion could not be opened due to the WebFetch failures), Cope's holding was that the **"describing"** (text) prong was unconstitutionally overbroad because it could sweep in protected material such as statutes, case law, and the defendant's own sex-offender-treatment writings, while the **"depicting"** (image) prong survived. **[VERIFY — this citation and holding are reconstructed entirely from WebSearch snippets and have not been independently confirmed from the primary opinion; treat with real caution before relying on it.]** If confirmed, Cope would be a useful, though out-of-circuit and not squarely parallel, data point: a federal appellate court in this same offense area treating a *text*-based restriction as more constitutionally fraught than the corresponding *image*-based restriction — conceptually supportive of, though not identical to, Bernecky's theory that text/keystroke capture is a different (and more troubling) kind of intrusion than image-focused monitoring. It is not a monitoring-condition case (it is a possession-prohibition case) and should not be cited as if it were.
- **No case anywhere located ties nexus/narrow-tailoring analysis explicitly to the image-vs.-text *type* of underlying offense conduct** in the computer-monitoring-condition context specifically. This is the honest state of the law: the closest thing to it is Sofsky's fact-specific suggestion of an image-focused alternative for an image-based offense (Section 2, above), not a generalized doctrinal rule that monitoring scope must be keyed to offense-conduct type.

## 5. U.S.S.G. § 5F1.5 — Nexus Framework, Checked for Offense-Conduct-Type Language

§ 5F1.5 governs **occupational restrictions**, not computer-monitoring conditions; it is not, on its own terms, the operative test for the scope of a CIMP condition. (The operative general test for special-condition scope is the "reasonably related to the offense"/"no greater deprivation of liberty than reasonably necessary" standard under 18 U.S.C. § 3583(d) and U.S.S.G. § 5D1.3(b)/(c) — the very standard Lifshitz and Sofsky both apply, see 369 F.3d at 188-89.) This research task asked specifically whether § 5F1.5's own text or interpretive case law ties condition *scope* to offense-*conduct type* — as already independently confirmed in this case file (part1; Brown, No. 24-1227 (2d Cir. Apr. 21, 2026), quoting the guideline), § 5F1.5(a) requires:

> "a reasonably direct relationship existed between the defendant's occupation, business, or profession and **the conduct relevant to the offense of conviction**" (emphasis added).

**Relevance by analogy:** even where the Guidelines are regulating restrictions tied to an *instrumentality* (an occupation), the operative nexus test is expressly keyed to "the conduct relevant to the offense of conviction" — not to the occupation itself, and not to the mere fact that the occupation created an opportunity for wrongdoing. This textual structure is a useful, though indirect, analogy: it shows the Guidelines' own drafting convention, when constraining the scope of a restriction tied to a tool/instrumentality (an occupation; by extension, a computer), is to anchor permissible scope to the *actual offense conduct*, not to the instrumentality's full range of capabilities. Bernecky's argument — that CIMP's scope should track his actual offense conduct (images) rather than everything a computer or keyboard is capable of doing — fits comfortably within that drafting logic, but § 5F1.5 itself does not address computer monitoring, and no case was found applying § 5F1.5's specific two-part test to a monitoring condition's scope. This should be used as **persuasive structural analogy in a framing/introduction section**, not cited as if it were directly on point.

## 6. Synthesized Argument and Requested Relief

Combining the confirmed primary-source material above, the strongest available version of this argument is built in three layers, in descending order of how directly it is supported by existing authority:

**Layer 1 (strongest — as-applied, textual, no new law required):** Bernecky's Judgment already contains the same privilege-avoidance clause construed in Kunz, 68 F.4th at 766-67, which the Second Circuit has already held is a real, enforceable constraint requiring Probation "not [to] abuse their power and access" as to "privileged information." Keystroke-level logging — which by definition captures every character typed, including the full text of privileged attorney-client communications, draft court filings, and correspondence to public officials, indiscriminately and before any determination of privilege is possible — cannot be reconciled with a monitoring method "designed to avoid ... reading" that same material. The requested relief at this layer is narrow and textual: a clarifying/modifying order holding that the existing privilege clause requires Probation to implement CIMP using a monitoring method (e.g., website/domain-level review, file-type and image-hash scanning, or keyword-triggered flagging of the kind Lifshitz's footnote 11 itself approved) that does not capture privileged content indiscriminately, rather than full keystroke capture. This tracks Kunz's own preferred remedial approach (construing conditions to avoid problems, rather than invalidating them) and asks the court to do the same thing it has already done once with this identical WDNY boilerplate.

**Layer 2 (well-supported by analogy, but an extension of existing doctrine):** Lifshitz's own narrow-tailoring framework (369 F.3d at 190-93) and Sofsky's image-focused alternative (287 F.3d at 126-27, quoted at 369 F.3d at 189) together support narrowing CIMP's *scope* — independent of the privilege point — to match Bernecky's actual, exclusively image-based offense conduct, on the theory that blanket keystroke/text capture is not "reasonably related" to, and imposes "greater deprivation of liberty than is reasonably necessary" for, an offense that involved no chat, no grooming, no solicitation, and no text-based communication of any kind. This is a sound extension of both cases' own logic but should be presented candidly as an extension — neither case holds that offense-conduct type is itself the limiting principle for monitoring scope.

**Layer 3 (weakest — no square authority, do not overclaim):** A freestanding claim that the Constitution, or Second Circuit precedent, *requires* an attorney-client-privilege carve-out in electronic-monitoring conditions of supervised release, or that keystroke logging is categorically unlawful as a supervision tool. No case anywhere located this session so holds. This layer should not appear in the motion as an affirmative holding-based claim; it may appear only as a policy/first-principles argument (the Sixth Amendment right to counsel and the attorney-client privilege are foundational, and a monitoring method that makes compliance with a court's own privilege-avoidance clause technologically impossible should not survive on that basis alone), clearly flagged as argument from principle rather than from binding authority.

## 7. Master Gap List — What Is Still Missing Before This Argument Can Be Relied On As Drafted

1. **Sofsky, 287 F.3d 122 (2d Cir. 2002), has not been independently read in full.** Every quotation used above comes from two other panels' (Lifshitz's and Kunz's) own quotations of it — reliable as far as they go, but the full opinion (facts, complete reasoning, disposition) should be pulled and read directly before Sofsky is cited as an independent authority rather than as "quoted in Lifshitz."
2. **United States v. Cope, 527 F.3d 944 (9th Cir. 2008), is entirely unverified** — reconstructed only from WebSearch snippets. Do not cite until independently read. If confirmed, it is out-of-circuit, persuasive-only, and not a monitoring-condition case, so its usefulness will be limited to conceptual support for treating text/description restrictions as more overbreadth-prone than image/depiction restrictions.
3. **The Tenth Circuit and Fifth Circuit cases referenced in Section 4** (complete-ban discretion vacated; ten-year ban found unreasonable) are unverified citations/holdings from secondary commentary only. Identify and confirm the actual case names and citations before using them, even as general-trend background.
4. **No court has been asked, in any case located this session, to decide whether "designed to avoid ... reading" privileged information is violated by automated capture/storage of privileged content that no human subsequently reviews, as opposed to only by a human actively reading it.** This is the central technical/legal question the Layer 1 argument turns on, and it appears to be a genuinely open question — helpful to the client because no adverse authority forecloses it, but also meaning the argument will need to be built from Kunz's general framework plus a factual/technical showing (e.g., expert declaration on how keystroke-logging software actually operates) rather than from a case squarely deciding the point.
5. **No factual record yet exists on how Probation's actual CIMP software captures, stores, transmits, and reviews keystroke data** — whether it flags/quarantines anything before a human review, whether logs are searchable/filterable after the fact, whether any minimization already occurs administratively even though the Judgment doesn't require it. This evidentiary gap should be closed (via a hearing, a Probation declaration, or a request for the "computer monitoring policy in effect by the probation office" referenced in the condition's own text but never produced in this file, per part1) before the motion is filed, since Kunz's reconciling-construction approach may depend heavily on exactly this kind of factual detail.
6. **The client's own theory frames the condition as having "no carve-out, filter, or minimization procedure ... as currently administered."** That framing should be revised in light of this research: **the condition's text does contain a privilege clause**, and Kunz holds it is a real constraint. The stronger and more accurate framing is that the *existing* clause is not honored (or cannot be honored) by keystroke-level logging specifically — an as-administered/as-implemented theory — not that no privilege language exists at all. Drafting counsel should adjust the motion's framing accordingly; overstating "no carve-out exists at all" when the Judgment's own text says otherwise would be an easily-exploited weakness for the government to point out.
