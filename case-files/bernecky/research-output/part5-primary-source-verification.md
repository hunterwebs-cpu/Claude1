## 2. Johnson — Highest-Priority Item (Full Textual Confirmation)

**446 F.3d 272 (2d Cir. 2006), Docket Nos. 04-4992, 05-0248(L), 05-0256(CON), argued Oct. 17, 2005, decided May 1, 2006, panel of Jacobs (writing), Cabranes, and Sack, arising from the Northern District of New York (McAvoy, J.).**

Having now read the complete opinion (not a reconstruction), **the part2b memo's conclusions are confirmed, not corrected**, on every point the client's pushback raised. This is the single most important finding of this verification pass.

### 2.1 The two grounds Johnson actually raised — confirmed exactly

The opinion states Johnson's Fifth Amendment argument in full at the outset: "Johnson objected to polygraph testing on two grounds. First, he argued that, without use and derivative use immunity, he could be deprived of his Fifth Amendment rights against self-incrimination and face the dilemma of confessing his sins or remaining silent (and going to jail for violating a condition of supervised release). Second, Johnson argued that the district court should not impose polygraph testing because such testing is unreliable and not reasonably related to the purposes of sentencing." No other ground appears anywhere in the opinion. There is no mention anywhere in the text of a right-to-counsel-for-invocation theory, nor of a blanket/categorical prospective refusal theory.

### 2.2 First-impression framing — confirmed verbatim

"We have never considered whether polygraph testing is a condition that satisfies this test, nor have we considered whether such a condition offends the Fifth Amendment right against self-incrimination." This is the court's own description of the scope of what it was deciding.

### 2.3 The mechanism approved — confirmed verbatim

The district court's safeguard, which the panel upheld, was that Johnson "must answer the questions posed to him, but, by answering, he will not be waiving his Fifth Amendment rights with respect to any criminal prosecution unrelated to the conviction for which he is now on supervised release," preserving his "right to challenge in a court of law the use of such statements as violations of his Fifth Amendment rights." This is, as the prior memos found, essentially identical in structure to Bernecky's own polygraph condition.

### 2.4 The actual merits holding — confirmed, and its narrowness is textually apparent

On the merits, the panel held: "Second Circuit precedent allows the revocation of supervised release of an offender who fails to answer questions even if they are self-incriminating," relying on *Asherman v. Meachum*, 957 F.2d 978 (2d Cir. 1992) (en banc), for the proposition that "revocation is an administrative decision that may be made based on a refusal to answer relevant questions, so long as the administrator does nothing to impair the later invocation of the privilege." The panel then states: **"Johnson's dilemma is harsh but it is not of constitutional salience. The only distinction between Asherman and this case is the polygraph. But as Johnson admitted at oral argument, the use of the lie detector has no impact on Fifth Amendment considerations. Asherman therefore applies directly."**

This confirms the part2b memo's reading precisely: Johnson's holding is that the *Asherman* framework (revocation for refusal-to-answer is permissible so long as a later challenge to use of any statement is preserved) applies with equal force whether or not a polygraph is the instrument used to ask the question. The panel never engages with a scenario where the defendant refuses to sit for the examination at all, pending appointment of counsel and other preconditions — because Johnson never presented that scenario. Johnson's own posture, as the opinion's individualized-history discussion confirms, was that he would answer; his objection was to the absence of immunity, not to being asked to answer at all.

### 2.5 The individualized predicate the panel relied on — confirmed

The panel expressly tied its holding to Johnson's specific facts: "The district court crafted supervised release conditions for a serial offender who apparently resisted honest self-assessment. Johnson's treatment provider labeled Johnson's attitude 'superficial and not adequately engaged in the recovery process. He has an increasingly antagonistic attitude…[featuring an] inadequate level of accountability for his offenses.'" The court found "[t]he case for use of polygraph testing is strong here in light of 'the nature and circumstances of the offense and the history and characteristics of the defendant.'" No comparable predicate has been identified for Bernecky in the case file to date.

### 2.6 Conclusion for Task 1

**The part2b memo's reconstruction is CONFIRMED IN FULL against the actual opinion text, not corrected.** Johnson (a) raised only the immunity/waiver and reliability grounds; (b) never addressed a counsel-for-invocation theory; (c) never addressed a blanket/categorical prospective-refusal theory; and (d) is expressly framed as a first-impression, fact-bound decision resting on Johnson's individualized history. The three-part argument structure built in part2b (facial / as-applied / factual-distinguishing) remains sound and is now supported by direct quotation rather than secondary-source reconstruction. Counsel can cite the passages above with confidence as to their accuracy — they were transcribed directly from the retrieved opinion text.

One additional, newly available fact: *Boles* (2019) independently reaffirmed Johnson's holding thirteen years later without qualification (see table above), confirming Johnson has not been narrowed or questioned by any later panel — it remains good law exactly as construed in part2b.

## 4. United States v. Love — Full Facts, Holding, and Application to Bernecky's CIMP/Occupational Track

**2d Cir., No. 24-1706-cr, summary order, decided April 8, 2025.** Appeal from the U.S. District Court for the Northern District of New York (Anne M. Nardacci, J.). Non-precedential (summary order), but citable under 2d Cir. Local Rule 32.1.1.

### Facts
Thomas Love pleaded guilty to one count of receipt of child pornography, 18 U.S.C. § 2252A(a)(2)(A). He was sentenced to 121 months' imprisonment (30 months below his 151-181-month Guidelines range) followed by a 20-year term of supervised release. The district court's stated basis for the sentence included that Love possessed "over 300 images of child pornography," including images "portray[ing] sadistic conduct and other depictions of violence or sexual abuse," and that he had distributed at least some of what he possessed.

Love challenged two special conditions on appeal:
- **Special Condition 12:** limited him to possessing **one internet-capable device**, absent court approval, "to facilitate the US Probation Office's ability to effectively monitor [his] internet-related activity."
- **Special Condition 13:** required him, **if his employment involves computer use**, to notify his employer of (1) "the nature of [his] conviction" and (2) "the fact that [his] conviction was facilitated by the use of the computer," with Probation confirming compliance.

### Holding
The Second Circuit **vacated both conditions and remanded**, on a joint concession by both parties that the district court's explanation was inadequate.

**On the one-device limit:** Citing *Kunz*, 68 F.4th at 767 ("a restriction limiting a supervisee to just one internet-connected device would pose a significant burden on his liberty, and therefore would need to be imposed by the court and justified by particularized on-the-record findings"), the court held that although the district court *had* offered some justification (Love's use of multiple devices/aliases to trade CP), it failed to grapple with Love's argument that Probation's own CIMP technology **could monitor multiple devices just as easily as one** — a point the government itself conceded on appeal ("technological improvements to probation's monitoring capabilities have enhanced its ability to monitor multiple devices simultaneously, a fact of which the district court may not have been aware at the time it imposed the condition"). Vacated and remanded for the district court to reconsider in light of that concession.

**On the employer-notification condition:** The court held this is squarely an "occupational restriction[]" under U.S.S.G. § 5F1.5(a), requiring (1) "a reasonably direct relationship... between the defendant's occupation... and the conduct relevant to the offense of conviction," and (2) that the restriction be "reasonably necessary to protect the public." Citing *Jenkins*, 854 F.3d at 195, for having "vacated the same condition at issue here because 'the relationship between the restrictions on [the defendant's] employment and [his] offense and circumstances [wa]s not readily apparent,'" and *Ruff*, 795 F. App'x 6, 8 (2d Cir. 2019), for vacating an identical condition where "the defendant had only ever held employment in the food services industry" with "no evidence that he [had] used a workplace computer to access child pornography," the court held: **"the [district] court did not... explain what, if any, connection existed between Love's employment and his offense of conviction"** — this was **plain error**, conceded by the government.

### Application to Bernecky

This is a very strong, current, and directly analogous case for the CIMP/occupational-restriction track — arguably the single best-fitting precedent in the entire folder for that theory, because it involves:
1. The **identical doctrinal defect** the client's directives flag: no § 5F1.5(a) findings were ever made connecting Bernecky's occupation (at a contact-lens manufacturer, unrelated to his offense) to his CIMP condition's employer-facing effects.
2. A **government concession** that Probation's own CIMP technology can, as a technical matter, monitor multiple devices without the burden the district court assumed — directly supporting Bernecky's parallel point that the PO's "like kind and degree"/keystroke-parity demand on his employer may likewise be unnecessary given current CIMP capabilities.
3. The Second Circuit's continued, active application of *Jenkins*'s "relationship... not readily apparent" standard to modern CIMP-adjacent employer-facing conditions, confirming this is a live, winning theory as of April 2025 — not a stale or abandoned doctrine.

**One caveat, stated candidly:** the attorney's description of this case as involving "**reasonable-suspicion language**" does not match the text. Nothing in *Love* discusses a reasonable-suspicion standard; its holding rests entirely on the § 5F1.5(a) occupational-restriction framework and, for the device limit, the *Kunz* delegation/particularized-findings framework. If the attorney is recalling a "reasonable suspicion" element from a different case or from the general CIMP search-condition language, that association should not be carried into *Love* specifically. The case is still a very strong parallel — just not for the reason involving "reasonable suspicion."

## 5. Traficante, Jenkins, Gorychka (both), and Burnash — Brief Identification and Relevance

**Traficante**, 966 F.3d 99 (2d Cir. 2020) — WDNY case, **Judge Larimer** (Bernecky's own sentencing judge). About cyberstalking/drug-distribution sentencing and the WDNY *Boles* standing order. **Relevant** to Standard Condition 12 (risk notification): confirms the standing order's exact text and holds that a facial vagueness/delegation challenge to the reformed "consultation with the court" condition is **not ripe** until an actual risk-finding occurs. This qualifies (does not defeat) the part4 memo's proposed argument — counsel should anticipate a ripeness objection and consider framing the challenge as a request for procedural clarification going forward rather than a present facial attack, or be prepared to argue Bernecky's imminent-supervision posture (as in the Birkedahl-distinguishing argument already used elsewhere) makes this riper than in Traficante.

**Jenkins**, 854 F.3d 181 (2d Cir. 2017) — NDNY case. About substantive unreasonableness of a 225-month sentence and 25-year supervised-release term for CP transportation (Dorvee/§2G2.2 critique), plus vacatur of several conditions (contact-with-minors, employer-notification, credit restrictions) as excessive/unexplained. **Relevant only generally** — it is not about any single Bernecky condition specifically, but supplies (a) the leading citation *Love* and *Burnash* both rely on for vacating employer-notification conditions, and (b) useful general authority for a "cumulative effect of many severe, unexplained conditions is itself unreasonable" framing argument, which fits the client's Catch-22 framing (Standard Condition 7's mandatory-employment requirement vs. the CIMP-driven de facto occupational bar).

**Gorychka** (SDNY, No. 23-6457-cr — Aug. 28, 2024 partial remand; Feb. 25, 2025 affirmance on remand) — **Relevant** to the occupational-restriction/CIMP-employer track as the "other side" of the *Love*/*Burnash* pattern: shows that an employer-device-monitoring condition **can** survive if the district court makes genuine, individualized § 5F1.5(a) findings (here, actual offense conduct occurring at the defendant's workplace via a traceable IP address, plus an IT-specialist's ability to evade monitoring). By contrast, nothing in Bernecky's record ties his contact-lens-manufacturer job to his offense conduct — reinforcing that his case is on the *Love*/*Burnash*/*Jenkins* side of the line, not the *Gorychka* side.

**Burnash** (NDNY, Nos. 23-8024-cr & 23-8025-cr, Dec. 20, 2024) — Same NDNY judge (Nardacci) as *Love*. **Relevant**: vacated (1) a one-device CIMP limit as an impermissible *Kunz* delegation, and (2) an employer-notification condition for lack of § 5F1.5(a) findings — the same two conditions vacated in *Love*, by the same judge, within months. This is strong evidence that this fact pattern is currently, actively being corrected by the Second Circuit on a recurring basis, which supports framing Bernecky's motion as consistent with, not contrary to, current circuit practice.
