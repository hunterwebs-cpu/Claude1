## BOTTOM LINE UP FRONT

1. **No case — in the Second Circuit or any circuit located this session — squarely holds that the geographic/locational scope of a computer-monitoring condition must be tied to where the underlying offense conduct occurred (private residence vs. public terminals or third-party/employer systems), as a named, independent doctrinal test.** This is a genuine gap, not a research failure — it was tested against Lifshitz, Sofsky (as quoted), Kunz, and the FJC Guide's full circuit survey. **Theory 7(a)'s "place" component is a sound, well-grounded synthesis of existing doctrine, not an existing holding**, and should be presented as such.
2. **However, a real, directly-read, on-point (though out-of-circuit) primary source exists that reaches functionally the same result Theory 7 seeks:** *United States v. Mike*, 632 F.3d 686 (10th Cir. 2011), applying and quoting *United States v. Matteson*, 327 F. App'x 791 (10th Cir. 2009), held that a computer-monitoring condition is **unconstitutionally vague** where its text could reasonably be read to reach computers the defendant does not own — **including the defendant's employer's computers** — and remanded for the district court to clarify and narrow the condition's scope to devices the defendant actually owns. This is the single strongest piece of authority located for Theory 7's "monitor only what he owns and brings home" component — not because it rests on a place-of-offense theory, but because it independently reaches the conclusion that monitoring authority reaching third-party/employer devices, where the condition does not clearly and deliberately say so, is legally infirm and must be narrowed.
3. **Device-capability-based (rather than "any connected device") scope definitions are an established, multi-circuit drafting convention, not a novel invention.** The FJC Guide collects several circuit cases upholding or applying conditions that define the restricted-device category by **capability** — e.g., *United States v. Granger* (4th Cir.) ("any computer which is connected or has the **capacity to be connected** to any network"); *United States v. Rath* (5th Cir.) (computer permitted for school/work "as long as the computer is **not capable of Internet access**"); *United States v. Ullman* (10th Cir.) ("devices **without** Internet access, such as gaming systems, are not restricted"); *United States v. Fletcher* (8th Cir.) ("device with Internet **capabilities**"); *United States v. Phillips* (6th Cir.) ("internet-**capable** electronic device"). **[VERIFY — these holdings are drawn from the FJC Guide's own summaries and citations, not independently read from the reporters; the Guide itself, however, was read directly.]** These all use *Internet-capability* as the threshold, not *image-capability* — Bernecky's proposed image-capability threshold is a one-step, modest extension of an existing, judicially-accepted drafting convention, not an unprecedented ask.
4. **Bernecky's own condition already contains a capability-based textual hook that has gone unnoticed:** the CIMP condition (and the entire WDNY boilerplate family Kunz discusses — Savastio, Leone, Swartz, Petix, Asch, DeCapua) opens with "any **internet capable** device" as the gate for CIMP participation. **The condition already uses a capability qualifier — it simply fails to carry that qualifier through to the separate "surveil all activity on computer(s) or connected device(s) **owned or operated by the defendant**" monitoring-authorization clause**, which drops the capability language entirely. This textual seam is a concrete, case-specific hook for the argument that a capability-based (here, image-capability-based, given the offense) limit is not an invention but a restoration of internal consistency within the condition's own drafting logic.
5. **No case anywhere located criticizes an "any connected device" definition specifically because it swept in IoT/smart-appliance devices lacking the relevant capability (the client's recurring networked-refrigerator example).** This is a confirmed gap (tested via direct WebSearch and against the FJC Guide). The closest available analogy is *Ullman*'s exclusion of "gaming systems" lacking Internet access — real, but not an IoT/smart-appliance case, and not a case actually invalidating an "any connected device" clause on this ground; it is a case where the sentencing court's own condition already excluded such devices, not a case vacating a broader condition for failing to do so.
6. **On the "forbid rather than monitor" third-party-device component (Theory 7(a)):** no case was located affirmatively blessing this specific structure as a required or even a used less-restrictive alternative to imposing monitoring/access obligations on a third party's property. This is best understood as a novel but low-risk, logically compelled application of principles **already established elsewhere in this case file** — the least-restrictive-means requirement of § 3583(d)(2)/Lifshitz/Sofsky, the ultra vires/no-authority-over-non-parties analysis (part1, Part 4), and the standing analysis (part13, built on *Bond v. United States*) — combined into an affirmatively *cleaner* remedial structure than even the employer-veto ("Path 2") alternative already proposed in client-directives.md, because a pure behavioral prohibition on Bernecky needs no third-party cooperation, consent, or veto mechanism at all.
7. **On offense-type/content-significance as a general narrow-tailoring principle (Research Question 4):** the FJC Guide documents a genuine, multi-circuit doctrinal category — courts distinguish "simple possession" child-pornography cases from **"child pornography plus"** cases (solicitation, chat-facilitated contact, threats, distribution for profit) when assessing how broad a computer/Internet restriction may be, with possession-only cases more likely to have restrictions narrowed or vacated. **This is a broader, multi-circuit articulation of essentially the same underlying principle Track 18 already found in Sofsky/Lifshitz (2d Cir. only), not a separate doctrine** — it generalizes the image/text axis into a broader offense-conduct-type axis (did the offense involve solicitation/communication/contact beyond the image content, or not), which is exactly the "broader doctrinal frame" the client's directive asked this track to look for. It should be cited as reinforcing, persuasive, largely out-of-circuit authority for a principle Sofsky/Lifshitz already support within the Second Circuit, not as new binding law.

**Overall strength assessment:** This is a **credible, well-supported narrowing/modification theory built substantially by analogy and synthesis, with one genuinely on-point (if out-of-circuit) primary-source holding (Mike/Matteson) doing real work.** It is not a facial challenge with square Second Circuit authority behind it. It is strongest when presented as a request to **restore internal consistency to the condition's own capability-based drafting logic** and to **adopt the least restrictive available structure** (forbid third-party devices; monitor only owned, image-capable devices) that the record already shows achieves the government's stated interest — not as a claim that any court has already held this is constitutionally compelled. Full analysis and a candid gap list follow.

## 1. Research Question 1 — Does Monitoring Scope Track Where the Offense Occurred?

**Finding: no case squarely so holds.** Neither Lifshitz, Sofsky (as quoted in Lifshitz/Kunz), nor Kunz itself discusses the location where the offense conduct occurred as an independent variable bearing on a monitoring condition's *geographic* or *ownership* scope. Lifshitz's narrow-tailoring taxonomy (part18, Section 1) concerns *what kind of data* is captured (content-specific vs. all-activity), not *where* or *whose* devices are reached. Sofsky's "focused restriction, limited to pornography sites and images" concerns *content type*, not location. Kunz does not discuss the defendant's residence, workplace, or any location-based limitation at all — its closest brush with the issue (footnote 8, discussed below) treats an employment-related CIMP tension as an unripe hypothetical, not a location-based holding.

**What does exist, assembled from several directions, none squarely on point:**

- ***United States v. Mike*, 632 F.3d 686 (10th Cir. 2011)**, read in full — see Section 3, below. This is the closest real authority, and it approaches the problem through *vagueness* and *ownership*, not "location of the offense," but it reaches a functionally similar destination: monitoring conditions that could reach devices beyond those the defendant owns (specifically flagging *employer* devices) are constitutionally infirm as written and must be narrowed on remand.
- **§ 5F1.5's "conduct relevant to the offense of conviction" language** (already established as an analogy in Track 18, part18 Section 5) supports, by extension, that monitoring scope should track the *actual conduct* — here, conduct that took place entirely within Bernecky's own residence on his own devices — rather than reaching instrumentalities or locations having no relationship to that conduct. This is the same analogy Track 18 already used for the image/text axis; it applies with equal logical force to the place/ownership axis, since § 5F1.5's text does not distinguish between the two.
- **The FJC Guide's "Nexus Between Offense History and the Internet" doctrine** (Section IV, below) is the closest generalizable circuit-level principle: courts across multiple circuits ask whether the *offense itself* involved the Internet/computer at all before upholding a restriction keyed to that instrumentality. This is an instrumentality-nexus test, not a place-nexus test, but the underlying logic — restrict only what the offense conduct actually implicates — is the same logic Theory 7(a) asks the court to apply one level further down, to *which locations/devices* within "computer use" the offense conduct actually implicates.
- ***United States v. T.M.*, 330 F.3d 1235 (9th Cir. 2003)** — per the FJC Guide, the Ninth Circuit rejected a condition barring computer/online-service access "at any location (including place of employment)," reasoning that the predicate incidents (decades old) did not justify such a sweeping restriction. **[VERIFY — reconstructed from the FJC Guide, not independently read.]** The court's rationale was temporal remoteness, not a residence-specific nexus theory, but the case is useful because it shows a circuit court expressly scrutinizing and rejecting the "any location, including employment" formulation as part of its overbreadth analysis — the same formulation problem Bernecky's own "owned or operated" language raises.
- ***United States v. Knight*, 86 F. App'x 2 (5th Cir. 2003) (unpublished)** — per the FJC Guide, the Fifth Circuit upheld a condition barring computer use with Internet/e-mail access "at home or at work," reasoning the defendant's livelihood did not depend on computer access. **[VERIFY — FJC Guide summary only.]** This shows courts do treat "home" and "work" as distinct, separately-analyzed loci within the same condition — supporting the general proposition that location is a live variable in the reasonableness calculus, even though this particular case did not narrow the condition to home-only.

**Honest assessment:** Theory 7(a)'s place-based component is **an assembled, analogical argument, not a citation to a squarely-holding case.** It should be framed as a synthesis of the § 5F1.5 conduct-nexus analogy, the FJC Guide's instrumentality-nexus doctrine, and the Mike/Matteson ownership-vagueness holding — not as resting on any single "place of the offense" precedent, because none exists.

## 2. Research Question 2 — Device-Capability-Based Limits vs. "Any Connected Device"

**Finding: capability-based device definitions are an established, accepted, multi-circuit drafting convention — just not, so far as located, using *image* capability specifically.** The FJC Guide's Appendix A ("Cases Upholding Qualified Bans") collects several examples where courts have approved or applied conditions whose device-category boundary is drawn by *technical capability* rather than blanket "any device" language:

- ***United States v. Granger*, 117 F. App'x 247 (4th Cir. 2004) (unpublished)** — upheld a condition barring "any computer which is connected or has the **capacity to be connected** to any network," reasoning the defendant's work was manual labor and the restriction would not prevent him from earning a living. **[VERIFY — FJC Guide summary.]**
- ***United States v. Rath*, 2015 WL 3559160 (5th Cir. 2015)** — upheld conditions prohibiting access to "any computer **capable of Internet access**," reasoning the defendant could still use a computer for school/work "as long as the computer is **not capable of Internet access**." **[VERIFY — FJC Guide summary.]** This is the single closest analogue to Bernecky's proposal: a court explicitly defining the restricted-device category by a technical capability (Internet access), with devices lacking that capability falling outside the restriction entirely — precisely the move Theory 7(b) asks for, substituting image-related capability for Internet-related capability given this offense's different content-nexus.
- ***United States v. Ullman*, 2015 WL 3559221 (10th Cir. 2015)** — upheld an Internet-access restriction, expressly noting that "**devices without Internet access, such as gaming systems, are not restricted**." **[VERIFY — FJC Guide summary.]** This is the closest available analogue to the client's IoT/smart-appliance concern: a circuit court accepting, as part of a condition's own scope, that some networked-adjacent consumer devices are properly excluded because they lack the relevant capability.
- ***United States v. Fletcher*, 435 F. App'x 578 (8th Cir. 2011) (unpublished)** and ***United States v. Phillips*, 370 F. App'x 610 (6th Cir. 2010) (unpublished)** — both use "device with Internet **capabilities**" / "internet-**capable** electronic device" as the operative category-defining phrase. **[VERIFY — FJC Guide summaries.]**
- ***United States v. Ellis*, 720 F.3d 220 (5th Cir. 2013)** — upheld a condition listing specific device categories requiring approval ("including, but not limited to Xbox, PlayStation, Nintendo, or similar device"), showing courts do draft device-category lists tailored to function/capability rather than using undifferentiated catch-all language. **[VERIFY — FJC Guide summary.]**

**No case found criticizing, narrowing, or vacating an "any connected device"/"any electronic device" clause specifically because it swept in IoT or smart-appliance devices incapable of the relevant conduct.** This was tested directly via WebSearch (multiple phrasings) and against the FJC Guide's full text; the result is a confirmed gap, not a search failure. The client's networked-refrigerator hypothetical remains, so far as located, **entirely unbriefed territory** — a real, sound argument from first principles (a condition's own purpose cannot be served by monitoring a device with no capacity to locate, access, store, or display the relevant content), but not one any court has yet addressed in these terms.

**The strongest, most case-specific hook is textual, not extrinsic:** Bernecky's own condition (and the entire WDNY CIMP boilerplate family) already opens with "any **internet capable** device" as a threshold qualifier (confirmed directly from the Kunz opinion's quotation of the identical condition language, and from Kunz's own footnote 11 string-citing Savastio, Leone, Swartz, Petix, Asch, and DeCapua as sister judgments using the same phrase). The condition's *drafters* already accepted the principle that device scope should be capability-bounded — they simply did not carry that qualifier through consistently into the separate "surveil all activity on computer(s) or connected device(s) owned or operated by the defendant" monitoring-authorization sentence, which drops all capability language. **This is not a request to import a foreign concept into the condition; it is a request to make the condition internally consistent with a capability-based limiting principle its own first sentence already embraces**, recalibrated to the capability actually relevant to this offense (image-related) rather than the capability relevant to Internet-facilitated offenses generally.

**Combine with Sofsky (already established, part18):** Sofsky's "focused restriction, limited to pornography sites and images" is the Second Circuit's own content-based narrowing example for this exact offense type. Pairing Sofsky's content-boundary (images) with the multi-circuit capability-boundary drafting convention (Granger/Rath/Ullman/Fletcher/Phillips) produces exactly the synthesis Theory 7(b) proposes — a device-capability threshold defined in image-related terms. No single case does this; it is a combination of a Second Circuit content-nexus holding with an out-of-circuit device-capability drafting convention, honestly presented as such.

## 3. Research Question 1(cont.)/3 — *United States v. Mike* / *Matteson*: Ownership-Based Vagueness, and Its Fit for the "Forbid Rather Than Monitor" Structure

**Citation:** *United States v. Mike*, 632 F.3d 686 (10th Cir. 2011) (Melgren, J., sitting by designation, for Briscoe, C.J., and Holloway, J.). Read in full, primary source, from the Drive-folder PDF. Quotes *United States v. Matteson*, 327 F. App'x 791 (10th Cir. 2009) (unpublished), at length.

**Facts:** Mike, sentenced for a non-sex-offense assault, was subject to the district's standard sex-offender computer-monitoring conditions because of a 1997 sex offense. The conditions required consent to computer examinations and monitoring software, and — critically — required Mike to "warn others of the probation officer's ability to place monitoring software on his computer **or any such computer he may have access to**," and to submit monthly records "accrued in the use of **a** computer" (not "his" computer).

**Holding on vagueness/ownership:**

> "Recently, in *United States v. Matteson*, 327 Fed. Appx. 791 (10th Cir. 2009) (unpublished), we were faced with a case that involved a computer monitoring condition very similar to one of the conditions at issue here. There, the defendant argued that the condition was infirm for two reasons: first, it was overly vague, and second, it was too intrusive to comply with either § 3583(d)(2)'s requirements or the Fourth Amendment. ... Because we agreed with the defendant that the condition was vague, as it could be reasonably interpreted to apply to any computer he used, not just the one he owned, we remanded the matter back to the sentencing court for clarification.
>
> Here, we are presented with essentially the same problem we faced in Matteson: it is not clear to which computers the conditions in question apply. In its response, the Government argues that the conditions 'cannot be read fairly to encompass public computers or computers owned by employers.' ... We disagree. ... [W]e find that the probation officer could reasonably believe that the computer monitoring conditions apply not only to computers that Mike owns, but also to those owned by others, **including Mike's employer**. As a consequence, we remand these conditions to the district court to clarify their scope. If on remand the court decides that it intends for the computer monitoring conditions to apply to computers owned by Mike's employers, **it must make the necessary findings**, which we outline infra in our discussion of the condition limiting the types of occupations that Mike may engage in." 632 F.3d at 696-97 (record citations omitted).

**Why this matters for Bernecky, precisely:**

1. **The government made exactly the argument Probation implicitly makes here — "the condition cannot fairly be read to reach the employer's computers" — and the Tenth Circuit rejected it.** Bernecky's condition ("owned or operated by the defendant") is, if anything, broader in one respect (it says "operated," not merely "used" or "accessed," and contains no "warn others" clause narrowing it), but it shares Mike's core vice: nothing in the text expressly *excludes* employer or third-party devices, and a probation officer could reasonably read it — indeed, per the case-file record, a probation officer already *has* read it — to reach employer systems (see part1, the "like kind and degree" demand).
2. **The remedy Mike/Matteson supply is precisely Theory 7(b)'s remedy: remand/modification to clarify and narrow the condition's scope to devices the defendant actually owns**, not a facial invalidation. This maps directly onto the relief the client seeks — a modification defining the monitored-device category as those Bernecky owns and keeps/brings home, expressly excluding devices owned by others.
3. **Mike/Matteson's conditional holding — "if the court intends the condition to reach employer computers, it must make the necessary [§ 5F1.5] findings" — is the same integration point already built into this case's occupational-restriction theory** (part1, Part 1; part13). Mike supplies independent, out-of-circuit, directly-on-point (read in full) confirmation that courts treat "does this monitoring condition reach employer property" and "has an occupational-restriction finding been made" as linked questions, exactly as Bernecky's own theory already argues.
4. **Necessary candor:** *Mike* is a Tenth Circuit case — persuasive, not binding, in the Second Circuit. Its holding rests on vagueness/notice grounds, not on a "the crime happened at home, so monitoring should stay home" theory. It should be cited as strong persuasive authority for the *narrowing remedy* Theory 7 seeks, not mischaracterized as controlling Second Circuit law or as a location-of-offense holding.

**On the "forbid rather than monitor" structure specifically:** no case — Mike/Matteson included — was found affirmatively addressing whether a court *should* prefer a pure behavioral prohibition (forbid use of another's device) over authorizing monitoring that reaches third-party property, as a less-restrictive-means matter. This appears to be genuinely unbriefed territory. It should be argued from principle, drawing on:

- **The least-restrictive-means logic already established via Lifshitz/Sofsky/§ 3583(d)(2)** (part18): where a narrower alternative achieves the same government interest, it must be preferred. A flat prohibition on using another person's device achieves the same objective (preventing Bernecky from accessing image content on an unmonitored device) with *zero* reach into anyone else's property — it is, by definition, less intrusive than any structure that authorizes monitoring, inspection, or capability-parity demands directed at a third party's system, because it imposes no burden on the third party at all.
- **The ultra vires/no-jurisdiction-over-non-parties analysis already built in part1 (Part 4) and part13** (standing, *Bond v. United States*): a forbid-only structure sidesteps that entire analysis, since it creates no occasion for Probation to make any demand of a non-party in the first place. It is, in that sense, a *stronger* and administratively simpler solution than even the "Path 2" employer-veto modification language already proposed in client-directives.md (which still requires an employer-facing consent mechanism); forbidding borrowed-device use requires no third-party involvement whatsoever.
- **Analogous "personal access" narrowing language already accepted by courts:** *United States v. Angle*, 598 F.3d 352 (7th Cir. 2010), per the FJC Guide, upheld a condition prohibiting "**personal** access to computer Internet services," reasoning the restriction was not absolute precisely because it was limited to personal (as opposed to third-party/work-mediated) access. **[VERIFY — FJC Guide summary.]** This shows the "personal"/"own devices only" qualifier is a familiar, judicially-accepted narrowing technique in this exact doctrinal area, not a novel structure — supporting the plausibility, though not the compelled necessity, of the forbid-based approach.

## 4. Research Question 4 — Content-Significance-of-Conduct as a General Narrow-Tailoring Principle (Beyond Image vs. Text)

The FJC Guide's Section III.B (**"Nature of Defendant's Offense History"**) documents a real, multi-circuit doctrinal category directly responsive to this question — broader than, but related to, Track 18's Sofsky/Lifshitz image-vs-text finding:

> "Courts are more likely to reject restrictions when computers and the Internet are used exclusively for possession of child pornography. ... On the other hand, courts are more likely to uphold conditions when the defendant uses a computer or the Internet for '**child pornography plus**' cases 'involving not merely possession but additional conduct that threatens the welfare of children.'" (quoting Cheryl A. Krause & Luke A.E. Pazicky, *An Un-Standard Condition: Restricting Internet Use as a Condition of Supervised Release*, 20 Fed. Sent'g Rep. 201, 202 (2008)).

Examples of "plus" conduct the Guide collects (facilitating real-time molestation via chat, soliciting a fictitious minor online, developing an online relationship culminating in sexual contact, posting images to a public file-sharing program) are contrasted with cases rejecting or narrowing restrictions where the offense was **pure possession/receipt with no communicative or contact component** — the Guide's own Appendix C is titled "Cases Rejecting Bans Based on Lack of Conduct Beyond Possession or Receipt of Child Pornography." Representative citations (per the Guide; **[VERIFY — not independently read]**): *United States v. Doyle*, 711 F.3d 729 (2013); *United States v. Maxwell*, 483 F. App'x 233 (6th Cir. 2012) (unpublished); *United States v. Springston*, 650 F.3d 1153 (8th Cir. 2011); *United States v. Heckman*, 592 F.3d 400 (3d Cir. 2010). Contrast: *United States v. Ristine*, 335 F.3d 692 (8th Cir. 2003) (upholding restriction where defendant "more than merely possessed images... he exchanged the images with other Internet users, and he attempted to arrange sexual relations with underage girls"); *United States v. Laureys*, 653 F.3d 27 (D.C. Cir. 2011) (upholding logging/disclosure requirement where defendant "used the internet to facilitate criminal sexual conduct with minors").

**Is this "simply the same doctrine already found" in Track 18? No — it is a broader, related generalization, and should be presented that way, not conflated with it.** Track 18 found a Second-Circuit-specific, image-vs-text axis (Sofsky's "pornography sites and images" vs. Lifshitz footnote 11's keyword/phrase-triggered alternative). This FJC-Guide-documented doctrine is a **multi-circuit, offense-conduct-type axis** — simple possession vs. "CP-plus" (solicitation/chat/contact/distribution-for-profit) — that operates one level of generality higher: it is not about *what kind of data* is monitored (images vs. text), but about *what the defendant actually did* (passively possessed images vs. actively used communications/contact to victimize). It is the general doctrinal frame the client's directive asked this track to look for, and it independently reinforces Track 18's narrower finding: both doctrines point the same direction (image-only, non-communicative CP possession offenses support narrower monitoring than offenses with a communicative/contact component), but they are analytically distinct bases for the same conclusion, and both should be pled, cross-referenced to each other, not merged into one citation.

**Application to Bernecky:** so far as the case file reflects, Bernecky's offense is CP possession with no alleged solicitation, chat-facilitated contact, distribution, or threats — squarely on the favorable side of this doctrine (the Appendix C category), reinforcing (from a second, independent, multi-circuit doctrinal direction) the same conclusion Track 18 already reached via Sofsky: a content/communication-blind, all-activity monitoring regime is broader than this specific offense conduct supports.

**Honest caveat:** every case in this section is sourced from the FJC Guide's own summary, not independently read from the reporters, and the Guide itself — while a real Federal Judicial Center publication I read directly — is a secondary compilation, not primary case law. **[VERIFY all citations in this section before filing.]**

## 5. Research Question 5 — § 5D1.3(b)/(c) and § 3583(d): Circuit Case Law Applying Location- or Capability-Based Narrowing

**No case was found applying § 5D1.3(b)/(c) or § 3583(d)'s "reasonably related"/"no greater deprivation" standards with an express, named "location-based" or "capability-based" sub-test.** What exists is that the numerous capability-based device-definition cases collected in Section 2 above (Granger, Rath, Ullman, Fletcher, Phillips) are **themselves applications of this exact statutory standard** — each is a court finding that a capability-bounded device category satisfies (or a broader, uncapped category fails) the "no greater deprivation of liberty than reasonably necessary" requirement. In other words: capability-based narrowing is not a separate doctrinal test additional to § 3583(d)(2) — it **is** one of the several forms § 3583(d)(2) narrowing has already taken in multiple circuits, just not yet in the Second Circuit specifically (Sofsky/Lifshitz apply the same statutory standard via a content-based rather than a device-capability-based narrowing). This should be presented as such: Theory 7(b) does not need a freestanding "capability doctrine" citation — it needs only to show that capability-based narrowing is a well-established *species* of the § 3583(d)(2)/§ 5D1.3 narrow-tailoring analysis already squarely governing this case, for which multiple circuits (though not yet the Second) provide concrete drafting templates.

## 6. Kunz Footnote 8 — The Adjacent, Unresolved Employer/Third-Party-Device Tension Already in the Case Law

Worth flagging as directly adjacent, though not dispositive: Kunz's own footnote 8 records that Kunz's expert raised a concern that the CIMP term barring use of any service that "conceals or spoofs" an IP address could "make life difficult for any user who works for a company that requires the use of a VPN." 68 F.4th at 766 n.8. The panel did not resolve this on the merits — it called the concern "a hypothetical worry" and suggested "a simple modification request should a change in Kunz's employment circumstances warrant as much," citing *Balon*'s "changing technology is an appropriate factor to authorize a modification... under Section 3583(e)(2)." *Id.* **This confirms that the Second Circuit has already brushed up against an employer/third-party-device tension within this exact condition family and treated the correct vehicle as a § 3583(e)(2) modification motion — precisely the posture Bernecky is in now, except his facts are not hypothetical (an actual job is actually suspended).** This is a useful, directly-on-point (same condition family, same circuit) confirmation of the *procedural* correctness of bringing this as a modification motion now, though the court did not resolve any capability- or location-based substantive standard.

## 7. Synthesis — How the Pieces Fit Together for the Motion

1. **Lead with the internal-textual-consistency point (Section 2, above):** Bernecky's own condition already uses a capability qualifier ("internet capable device") at its opening; the monitoring-authorization clause drops it. Ask the court to restore a capability qualifier to the operative monitoring clause, recalibrated to the capability relevant to this offense (image-related), consistent with Sofsky's own "pornography sites and images" framing.
