## 1. Tenth Circuit case — IDENTIFIED WITH HIGH CONFIDENCE

**Case:** *United States v. Blair*, 933 F.3d 1271 (10th Cir. 2019) (No. 18-1220, decided Aug. 13, 2019).
Defendant: Michael Lyle Blair (matches the name flagged in the prior cut-off attempt).

**Facts:** Blair pleaded guilty to possession of child pornography (a hard drive with 700,000+ images); sentenced to 10 years' imprisonment plus 7 years' supervised release.

**Challenged condition:** A special condition stating the defendant's "use of computers and Internet access devices must be limited to those the defendant requests to use, and which the probation officer authorizes."

**Holding (matches the part18 description):** The Tenth Circuit held this condition violates 18 U.S.C. §§ 3583(d)(2) and 3553(a)(2)(B)-(D) because it places no limit on the probation officer's discretion — the officer could, in practice, ban all computer/Internet use entirely by simply never authorizing any device. The court found the district court abused its discretion, **vacated** the condition, and remanded for the district court to reformulate it consistent with the opinion.

This is a clean, confirmed match to the original description: "Tenth Circuit vacating a condition that gave probation officers discretion to impose a complete computer/Internet ban." Confidence: **high** — name, circuit, year, procedural posture (vacated/remanded), and the specific mechanism (unbounded probation-officer discretion functioning as a de facto total ban) all align.

Sources: [Criminal Legal News summary](https://www.criminallegalnews.org/news/2019/sep/17/tenth-circuit-vacates-special-condition-supervised-release-gave-probation-officers-discretion-ban-computer-and-internet-usage/); [Justia](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/ca10/18-1220/18-1220-2019-08-13.html); [Leagle](https://www.leagle.com/decision/infco20190813067); [FindLaw](https://caselaw.findlaw.com/court/us-10th-circuit/2014898.html)

## 2. Fifth Circuit case — IDENTIFIED WITH HIGH CONFIDENCE

**Case:** *United States v. Becerra*, 977 F.3d 373 (5th Cir. 2020).
Defendant: Carlos Saul Becerra (matches the name flagged in the prior cut-off attempt).

**Facts:** Becerra pleaded guilty (2019) to receipt, possession, and distribution of child pornography; sentenced to 151 months' imprisonment followed by **10 years'** supervised release, with special conditions barring Internet, computer, and other electronic-device use for that full 10-year term.

**Holding (matches the part18 description):** The Fifth Circuit held the 10-year absolute ban on Internet/computer/electronic-device use was **substantively unreasonable** — noting the ban wouldn't even begin for ~13.5 years after sentencing (post-incarceration), by which point computers/Internet would be even more essential to daily/economic life, and the district court failed to ensure "no greater deprivation of liberty than reasonably necessary." The court **vacated** the conditions and remanded for resentencing, with guidance that if a long-term restriction is imposed, it must be narrowed (e.g., prior-approval mechanism via probation/court) rather than an absolute ban.

This is a clean, confirmed match to the original description: "Fifth Circuit decision finding a ten-year total internet/computer/electronic-device ban substantively unreasonable." Confidence: **high** — name, circuit, year, the exact "ten-year" detail, and the substantive-unreasonableness holding all align precisely.

Sources: [Criminal Legal News summary](https://www.criminallegalnews.org/news/2020/dec/15/fifth-circuit-special-conditions-supervised-release-barred-use-internet-computers-and-electronic-devices-10-years-not-substantively-reasonable/); [FindLaw](https://caselaw.findlaw.com/court/us-5th-circuit/2089760.html)
