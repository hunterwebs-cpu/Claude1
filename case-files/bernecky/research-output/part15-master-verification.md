## 1. United States v. Leone — RESOLVED (highest priority item)

**Citation:** *United States v. Leone*, No. 19-1670, 2020 WL 2610536 (2d Cir. May 20, 2020) (summary order).

**Binding status:** **Non-precedential.** It is a Second Circuit **summary order**, expressly captioned: *"RULINGS BY SUMMARY ORDER DO NOT HAVE PRECEDENTIAL EFFECT."* Citable under FRAP 32.1 and 2d Cir. Local Rule 32.1.1, but carries no stare decisis weight and must be cited and served consistent with those rules.

**What it actually is:** Leone was sentenced in the **same district (W.D.N.Y., Wolford, J.)** and challenged several supervised-release conditions, including a **CIMP monitoring condition using language essentially identical to Bernecky's**: *"The defendant shall not use or possess any computer, data storage device, or any internet capable device unless the defendant participates in the Computer and Internet Monitoring Program (CIMP), or unless authorized by the Court or the U.S. Probation Office... The defendant will be required to pay the cost of monitoring services unless otherwise ordered by the Court."* Leone also challenged a polygraph condition, a "pre-approval"/contact-with-minors condition (also textually identical to Bernecky's, down to the "100 feet of school yards, playgrounds, arcades" language), and a risk-notification condition.

**Holding — fee/ability-to-pay component (the piece relevant to the ability-to-pay theory for Special Condition (d)/(a)):**

> "[W]e do not read the condition as imposing an absolute requirement that Leone pay a fee; rather, we understand the condition to establish a presumption that he will pay for monitoring services he chooses to use unless he cannot afford to do so, **in which case the condition empowers him to seek an exemption from the district court**."

And on ripeness:

> "[A] proper reading of the Monitoring Condition highlights why Leone's challenge to the possible charge is unripe: 'it presents issues that might never arise.' ... At present, we do not know what the cost of participating in the monitoring program will be when Leone is released, whether Probation will hold Leone responsible for paying those costs, whether Leone will have the financial means to pay, and whether the district court will require him to pay if he says that he cannot. **If it proves to be the case that the district court requires an indigent Leone to pay to participate in monitoring services, he may mount a challenge at that time.**" (quoting *Johnson*, 446 F.3d at 279, and citing *Balon*, 384 F.3d at 47).

**Other holdings in Leone (all rejecting the defendant's challenges):** rejects a vagueness challenge to the monitoring condition's reach to "[m]icrowaves and modern refrigerators" as an IoT/smart-device concern, citing *Balon*, 384 F.3d at 43 (quickly disposing of the analogous "ATM/self-service kiosk" argument) — **this is a negative data point for Bernecky's IoT/networked-refrigerator vagueness theory (Special Condition (a), theory 4)**; upholds the polygraph condition citing *Johnson*; upholds the pre-approval/contact-with-minors condition on an adequately-explained record; and remands the risk-notification condition for reconsideration in light of the (then-forthcoming) *Traficante* decision — consistent with the *Boles*/*Traficante* line already confirmed in part5.

**Confirms or corrects:** This **resolves the two-pass mystery**. "Leone" was never an ability-to-pay case in the sense the original citation implied (there is no *Bearden*-style willfulness inquiry in it), but it is **directly on point and useful** for Special Condition (a)'s fee structure — precisely because it is the **same district, same condition language**, and holds that a fee-payment challenge (1) is unripe until Probation actually tries to collect from an indigent defendant, and (2) that when that happens, the condition itself "empowers" the defendant to seek a court exemption. **This cuts both ways for Bernecky:** it supports the argument that CIMP's fee provision is not an unconditional payment mandate (helpful against an Eighth Amendment/excessive-fines framing), but it also supplies the government a ready ripeness objection to any fee-based challenge Bernecky raises *now*, before Probation has actually attempted to collect from him. It is a poor fit as authority for "ability-to-pay" as a *free-standing* right (that work is better done by *Bearden* and *Turner*, below) — but it is highly useful, underused authority on the CIMP condition specifically, and it independently corroborates *Balon*'s rejection of the IoT/vagueness theory, which should be flagged to the client as a headwind on theory 4.

**Application to Bernecky:** Cite Leone (as a non-precedential but factually indistinguishable W.D.N.Y./2d Cir. authority) for the "the condition itself empowers him to seek an exemption" language and the unripeness caution — but do **not** rely on it for an affirmative ability-to-pay entitlement; use *Bearden* and *Turner v. Rogers* for that. Flag to the attorney that Leone actually cuts against the IoT-device vagueness theory.

## 2. Bond v. United States, 564 U.S. 211 (2011)

**Binding status:** U.S. Supreme Court — binding, precedential.

**Holding (verbatim, from the Syllabus and majority opinion):**

> "Held: Bond has standing to challenge the federal statute on grounds that the measure interferes with the powers reserved to States."

> "Amicus, appointed to defend the judgment, contends that for Bond to argue the National Government has interfered with state sovereignty in violation of the Tenth Amendment is to assert only a State's legal rights and interests. But in arguing that the Government has acted in excess of the authority that federalism defines, **Bond seeks to vindicate her own constitutional interests**."

> "[W]here the litigant is a party to an otherwise justiciable case or controversy, she is not forbidden to object that her injury results from disregard of the federal structure of our Government."

**Confirms or corrects:** **Fully confirmed**, verbatim, including the precise "seeks to vindicate her own constitutional interests" language.

**Application to Bernecky:** Bond is strong, on-point Supreme Court authority for the proposition that a person subject to governmental action may challenge that action as exceeding the government's delegated authority, **on the theory that doing so vindicates the challenger's own liberty interest** — not a third party's. This maps directly onto Bernecky's ultra vires argument: he is not asserting his employer's Fourth Amendment rights derivatively; he is asserting that the PO's demand on the employer exceeds any authority delegated by the sentencing court or by statute over *his own* supervision, and that the resulting threat to *his own* employment and liberty is a direct, personal injury. Bond is the correct lead case for this proposition and should remain so.

## 3. Bearden v. Georgia, 461 U.S. 660 (1983)

**Binding status:** U.S. Supreme Court — binding, precedential.

**Holding (verbatim):**

> "We hold, therefore, that in revocation proceedings for failure to pay a fine or restitution, a sentencing court must inquire into the reasons for the failure to pay. **If the probationer willfully refused to pay or failed to make sufficient bona fide efforts legally to acquire the resources to pay, the court may revoke probation** and sentence the defendant to imprisonment within the authorized range of its sentencing authority. **If the probationer could not pay despite sufficient bona fide efforts to acquire the resources to do so, the court must consider alternative measures of punishment other than imprisonment.** Only if alternative measures are not adequate to meet the State's interests in punishment and deterrence may the court imprison a probationer who has made sufficient bona fide efforts to pay. To do otherwise would deprive the probationer of his conditional freedom simply because, through no fault of his own, he cannot pay the fine. Such a deprivation would be contrary to the fundamental fairness required by the Fourteenth Amendment."

**Confirms or corrects:** **Fully confirmed.** This is the bedrock willfulness/ability-to-pay inquiry exactly as characterized in the intake file.

**Application to Bernecky:** Directly supports the cross-cutting fee argument: before treating Bernecky's nonpayment of any "contribute to the cost of services" obligation (CIMP, treatment, polygraph, mental health) as a violation, due process requires an inquiry into whether nonpayment was willful versus the product of genuine inability to pay, and consideration of alternatives short of the harshest sanction. Note the precise doctrinal scope: Bearden is a probation-*revocation* case addressing the consequence of nonpayment, not a case about whether a fee may be imposed in the first instance — frame the argument accordingly (i.e., Bearden supports "you cannot revoke/punish Bernecky for nonpayment without a willfulness inquiry," which is the correct framing for the "what happens if he can't pay" question already flagged in the intake).

## 4. United States v. Von Behren, 822 F.3d 1139 (10th Cir. 2016)

**Binding status:** Tenth Circuit — persuasive only in the Second Circuit, not binding, but a full published opinion.

**Holding (verbatim, on incrimination):**

> "The Fifth Amendment privilege is only properly invoked when the danger of self-incrimination is 'real and appreciable'... Because the answers to the four mandatory questions could focus an investigation... on Mr. Von Behren, and also because his confession to these past crimes could potentially be used against him at trial under Fed. R. Evid. 413 and 414, **we conclude that Mr. Von Behren faces at least some authentic danger of self-incrimination** by answering three of the four mandatory questions in the RSA's sexual history polygraph."

**Holding (verbatim, on compulsion):**

> "Relying on Cunningham, the district court determined in its initial order finding a risk of incrimination, that the penalty of potential 'revocation of supervised release and concomitant incarceration ... is sufficiently severe to constitute compulsion.' ... We agree with that conclusion."

**Ultimate holding:** *"In sum, we hold that the government compelled Mr. Von Behren to be a witness against himself. For the reasons set forth above, we REVERSE."*

**Confirms or corrects — CORRECTS the client's paraphrase, as the prior research pass concluded.** Von Behren's actual holding is narrow and fact-bound: (1) the four specific mandatory RSA polygraph questions posed a real risk of incrimination (they would reveal past, uncharged sex crimes usable as Rule 413/414 propensity evidence, and RSA was contractually obligated to report admissions to law enforcement); and (2) the threatened penalty — revocation and incarceration for refusing to answer or for invoking — constituted unconstitutional compulsion. **Von Behren says nothing whatsoever about:**
- a right to **appointed counsel** before, during, or after a polygraph exam (client's protection (I));
- a right to **per-question time for counsel to research** whether invocation is legally justified (protection (II));
- **Daubert-level examiner credentialing** (protection (III)); or
- a **disclosure prohibition** running against treatment providers/police (protection (IV)).

None of these four protections appears anywhere in the opinion. The court's remedy was simply to **reverse** the district court's ruling that no Fifth Amendment violation existed as to those specific questions — it did not fashion (and was not asked to fashion) a prophylactic four-part framework. The client's characterization substantially overstates Von Behren's holding, confirming the prior research pass's conclusion.

**Application to Bernecky:** Von Behren remains valuable and should be kept in the motion, but it must be **reframed**: it is best used (a) as an illustration of how *Murphy*'s "classic penalty situation" doctrine gets applied against a supervised-release condition that ties revocation to invocation of the privilege (supporting the *facial*/*structural* theory that "answer now, invoke later" collapses if revocation attaches to refusal itself); and (b) for its incrimination analysis (propensity-evidence exposure under Rule 413/414, the "cannot unring the bell" point already in the client-directives log). It should **not** be cited as if it independently established the four-part counsel/Daubert/disclosure framework — that framework is the client's own proposed remedy, not a holding of any court, and the motion should say so candidly rather than attribute it to Von Behren.

## 5. Minnesota v. Murphy, 465 U.S. 420 (1984)

**Binding status:** U.S. Supreme Court — binding, precedential.

**Holding (verbatim, the "classic penalty situation" doctrine, as quoted within Von Behren and independently in Murphy itself):**

> "There is thus a substantial basis in our cases for concluding that **if the state, either expressly or by implication, asserts that invocation of the privilege would lead to revocation of probation, it would have created the classic penalty situation, the failure to assert the privilege would be excused, and the probationer's answers would be deemed compelled and inadmissible** in a criminal prosecution." 465 U.S. at 435.

**Critical nuance — Murphy ruled AGAINST the probationer on its facts:**

> "[W]e must inquire whether Murphy's probation conditions merely required him to appear and give testimony about matters relevant to his probationary status or whether they went further and required him to choose between making incriminating statements and jeopardizing his conditional liberty by remaining silent. **Because we conclude that Minnesota did not attempt to take the extra, impermissible step, we hold that Murphy's Fifth Amendment privilege was not self-executing.**" 465 U.S. at 436.

**Confirms or corrects:** **Confirmed**, with an important nuance to preserve in drafting: Murphy is doctrinally central (it is the source of the "classic penalty situation" rule), but Murphy himself *lost* — the Court held that on Murphy's specific facts, Minnesota had not expressly or impliedly conditioned his probation on forfeiting the privilege, so no "penalty situation" arose and his failure to invoke was not excused. This means Murphy is a two-edged sword: it supplies the rule, but its own outcome shows that a generic "answer truthfully" condition, standing alone, does *not* automatically create a penalty situation — the defendant (or the condition itself) must cross the line into expressly/impliedly tying revocation to invocation. This is precisely why Bernecky's argument should stress that his Judgment (unlike Murphy's probation terms) contains an *explicit* structure (the polygraph condition's "answer now, preserve a later challenge" clause) that does tie participation/revocation to the polygraph specifically — a stronger fact pattern than Murphy's.

**Application to Bernecky:** Retain Murphy as the doctrinal spine, but draft carefully: argue that Bernecky's Judgment (both the polygraph condition and, arguably, Standard Condition 4) crosses the line Murphy itself found absent — because revocation for non-participation or non-answer is the explicit consequence built into the polygraph condition — rather than treating Murphy as if it were itself a favorable-outcome case for the probationer.

## 6. Hoffman v. United States, 341 U.S. 479 (1951)

**Binding status:** U.S. Supreme Court — binding, precedential.

**Holding (verbatim):**

> "The privilege afforded not only extends to answers that would in themselves support a conviction under a federal criminal statute but likewise embraces those which would **furnish a link in the chain of evidence needed to prosecute** the claimant for a federal crime." 341 U.S. at 486.

> "[T]his protection must be confined to instances where the witness has reasonable cause to apprehend danger from a direct answer." *Id.*

**Confirms or corrects:** **Fully confirmed**, verbatim, exact pincite (341 U.S. at 486).

**Application to Bernecky:** Continues to be the correct foundational citation for the "link in the chain of evidence" proposition underlying the entire polygraph Fifth Amendment argument; it is also the case Von Behren itself relies on for the same proposition, so the two authorities reinforce each other cleanly.

## 7. Moore v. City of East Cleveland, 431 U.S. 494 (1977)

**Binding status:** U.S. Supreme Court — **plurality opinion** (Powell, J., joined by Brennan, Marshall, and Blackmun, JJ. — 4 Justices; Stevens, J., concurred in the judgment on narrower grounds; Burger, C.J., Stewart and White, JJ., dissented). Confirmed: this is a plurality, not a majority opinion, though it remains frequently cited as the leading statement on extended-family liberty interests.

**Holding (verbatim):**

> "Ours is by no means a tradition limited to respect for the bonds uniting the members of the nuclear family. **The tradition of uncles, aunts, cousins, and especially grandparents sharing a household along with parents and children has roots equally venerable and equally deserving of constitutional recognition.** ... Decisions concerning child rearing, which Yoder, Meyer, Pierce and other cases have recognized as entitled to constitutional protection, long have been shared with grandparents or other relatives who occupy the same household."

> "[T]he choice of relatives in this degree of kinship to live together **may not lightly be denied by the State**."

**Confirms or corrects:** Confirmed as to the extended-family liberty-interest holding, with the important precedential caveat (plurality status) now documented — cite it as a plurality opinion, and pair it with Second Circuit authority (Myers, below) that has independently treated the "extended family"/parent-child liberty interest as a "fundamental liberty interest" requiring heightened scrutiny, to avoid over-relying on Moore's own precedential weight.

**Application to Bernecky:** Moore supports the extended-family carve-out theory for Special Condition (c) (only biological/adopted children are excepted, not nieces/nephews/cousins), but bear in mind Moore was about a *cohabiting* extended family (a grandmother and her grandson living together, facing a zoning ordinance), not incidental family-gathering contact of the kind Bernecky describes (a niece/nephew visiting for Christmas). Use Moore for the broad principle that the Constitution does not draw a bright line at the nuclear family, but pair it with DaSilva (below) and the "incidental encounter" doctrine for the specific factual scenario Bernecky describes.

## 8. United States v. Betts, 886 F.3d 198 (2d Cir. 2018)

**Binding status:** Second Circuit — binding, precedential, published opinion.

**Holding (verbatim):**

> "We agree with defendant that the District Court's imposition of a total ban on the consumption of alcohol was not reasonably related to the nature and circumstances of the defendant's offense."

> "Neither defendant's underlying crime nor any of the conduct contributing to his violations of supervised release involved the use of alcohol. The District Court was not presented with any evidence suggesting that defendant ever seriously abused alcohol. It also provided no reason for imposing this special condition... **We conclude that the special condition banning all alcohol use is not reasonably related to any of the factors outlined in Section 5D1.3(b), and necessitates vacatur.**"

**Standard applied:** individualized-assessment requirement, citing *Balon*, 384 F.3d at 41 n.1 ("A district court is required to make an individualized assessment when determining whether to impose a special condition of supervised release, and to state on the record the reason for imposing it; the failure to do so is error... we may uphold the condition imposed only if the district court's reasoning is 'self-evident in the record.'").

**Bonus finding:** Betts also cites *Reeves*, 591 F.3d 77, for the proposition that it "vacat[ed] an imposed special condition that required a defendant convicted of possession of child pornography to notify the Probation Department when he established a 'significant romantic relationship'... because it was not reasonably related to sentencing objectives" — **independently corroborating item 15 below: Reeves is a "significant romantic relationship" vagueness/reasonable-relation case, not a felon-association case.**

**Confirms or corrects:** **Fully confirmed.** Betts vacates a total alcohol ban for lack of individualized nexus to the offense or the defendant's history, exactly as characterized, with an exact pincite and holding.

**Application to Bernecky:** Directly on point for Special Condition (h)'s categorical alcohol-abstinence clause — there is no record evidence tying Bernecky's offense or history to alcohol abuse, which is the same defect Betts found dispositive. Also usable to reinforce the individualized-assessment requirement generally (citing *Balon*), which recurs across several of Bernecky's other conditions.

## 9. 18 U.S.C. § 3603 (LII copy)

**Confirmed current text (as amended through Pub. L. 115-391, Dec. 21, 2018):**

> "A probation officer shall— (1) instruct **a probationer or a person on supervised release, who is under his supervision**, as to the conditions specified by the sentencing court... (2) keep informed, to the degree required by the conditions specified by the sentencing court, as to the conduct and condition of **a probationer or a person on supervised release, who is under his supervision**... (4) be responsible for the supervision of **any probationer or a person on supervised release who is known to be within the judicial district**..."

Ten enumerated duties total; duty (10) is a catch-all: "perform any other duty that the court may designate."

**Confirms or corrects:** **Fully confirmed.** Every operative duty in § 3603 is expressly keyed to "a probationer or a person on supervised release, who is under his supervision" (or, in (4), "within the judicial district"). Nothing in the statute extends the probation officer's authority or duties to third parties such as an employer.

**Application to Bernecky:** Strong statutory-text support for the ultra vires argument: § 3603 defines the probation officer's entire statutory mandate by reference to "a person... under his supervision" — Bernecky's employer is not such a person, and § 3603 supplies no textual hook for a demand directed at the employer's own systems/capabilities. Pair with U.S.S.G. § 5F1.5(a)'s two-part occupational-restriction test (confirmed below) and Bond's standing holding.

## 10. United States v. Lussier, 104 F.3d 32 (2d Cir. 1997)

**Binding status:** Second Circuit — binding, precedential, published opinion.

**Actual holding (verbatim) — narrower than, and in tension with, the prior characterization:**

> "[W]e believe that the District Court was without authority to modify such orders **on the ground of illegality**... The plain language of subsection 3583(e)(2) indicates that **the illegality of a condition of supervised release is not a proper ground for modification under this provision**."

> "Subsection 3583(e)(2), in sum, requires the court to consider general punishment issues such as deterrence, public safety, rehabilitation, proportionality, and consistency, when it decides to 'modify, reduce, or enlarge' the term or conditions of supervised release. **Conspicuously absent from the list of relevant considerations is the legality of the condition.**"

> "Section 3583(e) provides the district court with retained authority to revoke, discharge, or modify terms and conditions of supervised release following its initial imposition of a supervised release term **in order to account for new or unforeseen circumstances**. Occasionally, changed circumstances... will render a previously imposed term or condition of release either too harsh or inappropriately tailored... In such cases, the court may invoke either subsection (1)... or subsection (2)..."

**Confirms or corrects — IMPORTANT CORRECTION, flag prominently for the attorney:** Lussier does **not** affirmatively hold that "no changed-circumstances showing is required" for a § 3583(e)(2) modification. That proposition is not what Lussier decided. Lussier's actual holding is narrower and, if anything, creates a **real risk for parts of Bernecky's motion**: it holds that **§ 3583(e)(2) may not be used as a vehicle to challenge the legality of a condition** — i.e., a claim that a condition was ultra vires, unconstitutional, or otherwise illegal from the moment it was imposed is not a proper subject of a 3583(e)(2) motion; the proper vehicles for illegality challenges are direct appeal (18 U.S.C. § 3742) or 28 U.S.C. § 2255. Lussier repeatedly frames § 3583(e)(2)'s actual purpose as responding to "changed circumstances" (e.g., "exceptionally good behavior," "a downward turn in the defendant's ability to pay") that bear on the general § 3553(a) punishment goals — not as a mechanism for relitigating the original legality of a condition.

**This has a direct, practical implication for Bernecky's motion that should be flagged to the attorney now, before filing:** several of Bernecky's strongest theories are framed as *legality* challenges (the CIMP condition's employer demand is *ultra vires*; the treatment-provider delegation is *unconstitutional* delegation; the polygraph structure is *facially unconstitutional*). Under Lussier's reading of § 3583(e)(2), a court could treat these as outside the proper scope of a modification motion. **The safer drafting posture — already partially reflected in the existing memos — is to frame each argument in the alternative:** (a) as a request that the court exercise its 3583(e)(2) discretion to modify the condition because it is not reasonably related / imposes a greater deprivation of liberty than necessary under the current § 3553(a) factors (squarely within Lussier's "punishment goals" framing), and, separately and expressly, (b) as an argument that the condition was never validly imposed in the first place (the illegality/ultra vires argument), while being candid that (b) may sit at the edge of, or outside, § 3583(e)(2)'s proper scope per Lussier, and noting that nothing in Lussier forecloses the court from considering the illegality point as *context* informing the discretionary "reasonably necessary" analysis even if it doesn't independently supply jurisdiction.

**Application to Bernecky:** Cite Lussier for its accurate holding (no "changed circumstances" *language* appears in the statutory text, and modification is available for ordinary punishment-goal reasons without proof of changed circumstances since sentencing — that part of the intake's characterization is directionally fine), but **do not** cite it as affirmatively rejecting a changed-circumstances requirement across the board, and flag the illegality-vehicle problem candidly in the brief's framing, especially for the ultra vires/standing and delegation arguments.

## 11. United States v. Myers, 426 F.3d 117 (2d Cir. 2005)

**Binding status:** Second Circuit — binding, precedential, published opinion.

**Holding (verbatim, heightened-scrutiny standard):**

> "If a special condition implicates a fundamental liberty interest, we must carefully examine it to determine whether it is 'reasonably related' to the pertinent factors, and 'involves no greater deprivation of liberty than is reasonably necessary,' 18 U.S.C. § 3583(d), and **our application of these criteria must reflect the heightened constitutional concerns**. **If the liberty interest at stake is fundamental, a deprivation of that liberty is 'reasonably necessary' only if the deprivation is narrowly tailored to serve a compelling government interest.**" 426 F.3d at 126 (citing *Washington v. Glucksberg*, 521 U.S. 702, 721 (1997)).

> "[W]e must ask first whether the condition at issue here deprived Myers of any cognizable liberty interest... It is well-established that a parent's interest in maintaining a relationship with his or her child is protected by the Due Process Clause... See *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57, 65-66 (2000) **(plurality opinion of O'Connor, J.)** (describing 'the fundamental right of parents to make decisions concerning the care, custody, and control of their children' as 'perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by this Court')."

**Disposition:** Vacated and remanded because the record was inadequate on both prongs (the nature/degree of danger to Myers's own child, and whether the condition was a greater deprivation than reasonably necessary).

**Confirms or corrects:** **Fully confirmed.** Myers is the correct lead Second Circuit case establishing that conditions burdening the fundamental parent-child liberty interest trigger heightened scrutiny (narrow tailoring to a compelling interest), and that the district court must build an adequate individualized record before imposing such a restriction. Note: Myers itself concerned a father's *own* child, not extended family — so it establishes the standard of scrutiny but does not itself resolve the extended-family-carve-out question (that requires Moore, above, and DaSilva, below).

**Application to Bernecky:** Cite Myers for the heightened-scrutiny/narrow-tailoring framework and for the individualized-record requirement; the sentencing court's failure to make any such findings before restricting Bernecky's contact with nieces/nephews/cousins is squarely the defect Myers identifies.

## 13. United States v. DaSilva, 844 F.3d 8 (1st Cir. 2016)

**Binding status:** First Circuit — persuasive only, not binding in the Second Circuit, but a full published opinion directly on point factually.

**Holding — confirms the "knowledgeable adult" carve-out concept:**

The condition at issue: *"[N]o contact with any child under the age of 18, with the exception of your own children, without the presence of an adult who is aware of your history and who is approved in advance by the probation officer."* The First Circuit upheld this condition (given DaSilva's extensive intervening criminal history) but remanded only for clarification of a drafting discrepancy regarding whether the "own children" exception extended to a stepchild.

**Key language on incidental contact (directly useful for Bernecky's workplace/family-gathering scenarios):**

> "[T]he restrictions on interaction with minors are not to be construed so strictly as to prohibit any manner of contact with other children. '**[A]ssociational restrictions are usually read to exclude incidental encounters.**' Otherwise, such a prohibition could become tantamount to 'house arrest.'... [W]e instruct the district court, in revising its order, to explain the limitations of the order and to elucidate the kinds of incidental contact that are permitted. For example, if the defendant were to drop his child off at a birthday party, pausing only to check in with the adult in charge and not speaking to any children, such an activity, without more, likely should not be read to violate the conditions of his release."

**Confirms or corrects:** **Confirmed.** DaSilva both (a) approves a "knowledgeable adult present" carve-out structure as a legitimate narrowing device for a contact-with-minors condition, and (b) supplies strong, directly-quotable language on excluding incidental encounters from associational restrictions generally — useful for both Special Condition (c) (the Christmas/niece-and-nephew hypothetical) and the employability-clarification point (incidental workplace contact with a minor coworker or customer).

**Application to Bernecky:** Cite DaSilva as persuasive authority for the "adult with knowledge of the conviction" carve-out client proposed, and separately for the "associational restrictions... exclude incidental encounters" principle, which is useful across both Special Condition (c) and the "no occupational restriction" clarification sought for the same condition.

## 14. Troxel v. Granville, 530 U.S. 57 (2000)

**Binding status:** U.S. Supreme Court — **plurality opinion** (O'Connor, J., joined by Rehnquist, C.J., Ginsburg and Breyer, JJ. — 4 Justices; Souter and Thomas, JJ., concurred in the judgment separately; Stevens, Scalia, and Kennedy, JJ., dissented separately). Confirmed plurality, as flagged.

**Actual holding (verbatim):**

> "[W]e hold that § 26.10.160(3), as applied in this case, is unconstitutional." Narrow, as-applied holding; the Court expressly declined to decide "the primary constitutional question passed on by the Washington Supreme Court — whether the Due Process Clause requires all nonparental visitation statutes to include a showing of harm."

> "First, the Troxels did not allege, and no court has found, that Granville was an unfit parent... **there is a presumption that fit parents act in the best interests of their children.**"

> "[S]o long as a parent adequately cares for his or her children (i.e., is fit), there will normally be no reason for the State to inject itself into the private realm of the family to further question the ability of that parent to make the best decisions concerning the rearing of that parent's children."

**Confirms the "cuts both ways" characterization:** Troxel is fundamentally a case about a **fit custodial parent's** (Granville's) constitutional right to control third-party (grandparent) access to *her own* children, free of unwarranted state second-guessing. If anything, **Bernecky's position with respect to his relatives' children is structurally closer to the Troxels' position** (the extended-family members seeking contact/visitation) than to Granville's (the parent making decisions for her own children) — and Troxel affirmatively holds that a *fit parent's* wishes about who may have contact with the child are entitled to "special weight," which could be invoked by a court to say that the *children's own parents'* views (not Bernecky's own asserted right) control whether contact with Bernecky occurs. **This is exactly the double-edged quality the prior research pass flagged, now confirmed against the actual opinion.**

**Application to Bernecky:** Use Troxel cautiously and precisely: it is good authority for the general proposition (quoted within Myers) that "the fundamental right of parents to make decisions concerning the care, custody, and control of their children" is "perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests" — supporting the general due-process framework Myers applies. But do not cite Troxel as if it establishes *Bernecky's own* constitutional right to contact with his nieces/nephews; frame it instead as supporting the point that the *children's parents'* — not the government's — judgment about safe, supervised contact with a family member should ordinarily control, absent an individualized finding of danger (tying back into the no-nexus-finding argument).

## 16. Warth v. Seldin, 422 U.S. 490 (1975)

**Binding status:** U.S. Supreme Court — binding, precedential.

**Holding/rule confirmed:** Warth is the foundational statement of the general prudential rule that "a party generally must assert his own legal rights and interests, and cannot rest a claim to relief on the legal rights or interests of third parties" — the baseline "off" position from which the recognized exceptions (elaborated later in Singleton, Powers, and Kowalski) depart. It is properly cited as backstop/on-off-switch doctrine: the default is that third-party standing is disfavored, subject to the close-relationship-plus-hindrance exception.

**Application to Bernecky:** Correctly positioned as backstop doctrine only, in case a court frames Bernecky's ultra vires challenge as an attempt to assert the employer's own Fourth Amendment rights rather than Bernecky's direct, personal liberty/employment interest (which, per Bond and the direct-injury framing, is the better characterization).

## 17. Powers v. Ohio, 499 U.S. 400 (1991)

**Binding status:** U.S. Supreme Court — binding, precedential.

**Confirmed three-part test for third-party standing** (a criminal defendant's standing to raise excluded jurors' equal-protection rights via Batson): (1) the litigant must have suffered an injury in fact giving him a sufficiently concrete interest in the outcome; (2) the litigant must have a close relationship with the third party; and (3) there must exist some hindrance to the third party's ability to protect his own interests.

**Application to Bernecky:** Correctly positioned as backstop doctrine establishing the three-part test, to be paired with Kowalski's "genuine hindrance" gloss, should the ultra vires argument need a fallback third-party-standing theory (asserting the employer's Fourth Amendment interest) rather than the direct-injury theory that should be the lead argument.

## 18. Kowalski v. Tesmer, 543 U.S. 125 (2004)

**Binding status:** U.S. Supreme Court — binding, precedential.

**Confirmed holding:** Denied attorneys standing to assert future indigent defendants' rights to appointed appellate counsel, refining Powers's third-element "hindrance" requirement to demand something closer to a **genuine obstacle**, not mere inconvenience, before third-party standing will be recognized — the Court was notably reluctant to relax prudential standing limits.

**Application to Bernecky:** Correctly used as the backstop, cautionary gloss on Powers: if the ultra vires argument is recharacterized by a court as third-party standing, Kowalski signals courts are skeptical absent a genuine (not merely convenient) hindrance to the employer raising its own rights directly — reinforcing why the **direct-injury framing (Bond) is the far stronger primary theory**, with third-party standing doctrine held only in reserve.

## 19. Singleton v. Wulff, 428 U.S. 106 (1976)

**Binding status:** U.S. Supreme Court — binding, precedential.

**Confirmed holding:** Physicians were permitted to assert the constitutional rights of their patients in challenging a state's Medicaid funding restriction on abortion, given the close doctor-patient relationship and practical obstacles (mootness, privacy concerns) to the patients raising the claims themselves. Singleton is the earlier, more permissive application later narrowed by Powers and Kowalski.

## 20. The Employer-Notification/Occupational-Restriction Line

### 20(a). United States v. Lombardi, No. 17-356-cr (2d Cir. Mar. 22, 2018) (summary order)

**Binding status:** Non-precedential summary order.

**Holding:** Vacated-in-part/remanded. Lombardi (an automotive mechanic) challenged a condition requiring him to "notify his employer of his computer related offense if his job requires computer access with Internet capability" and a condition barring association with children under 18 absent a "responsible adult" and PO approval. Decided **after** *Jenkins*, 854 F.3d 181 (2d Cir. 2017), the panel applied Jenkins's holding retroactively:

> "In Jenkins... we struck a special condition requiring that the defendant 'notify his prospective employer of the nature of his conviction and the fact that his conviction was facilitated by the use of a computer' if his employment involved a computer **because the relationship between the restriction and his offense was not 'readily apparent.'**... [T]he 'reasonably direct relationship'... between Lombardi's occupation as an automotive mechanic and his child pornography offense is also not 'readily apparent'... **Nor does the record establish that Lombardi 'will continue to engage in unlawful conduct similar to that for which [he] was convicted.'**"

Quotes and applies U.S.S.G. § 5F1.5(a)'s two-part test verbatim (see below). Remanded for the district court to reconsider both conditions under Jenkins.

### 20(b). United States v. Ruff, No. 18-2325-cr (2d Cir. Nov. 14, 2019) (summary order)

**Binding status:** Non-precedential summary order.

**Holding:** **Vacated** (not merely remanded) the employer-notification condition outright, applying plain-error review because Ruff failed to object below:

> "Ruff has only ever held employment in the food services industry. There is no evidence that he has used a workplace computer to access child pornography and therefore no evidence that any 'relationship existed between [Ruff's] occupation' and the conduct relevant to the offense at issue... we find that imposition of Special Condition 7 was error and that the error was plain."

> "[W]e AFFIRM the judgment as to the length of the sentence imposed, **VACATE the judgment insofar as it imposed a special condition of supervised release requiring the defendant to notify an employer**... and REMAND with directions to strike that condition of supervised release."

Notably, Ruff also clarifies that the ruling is "without prejudice to the authority of the district court pursuant to 18 U.S.C. § 3583(e)(2) to modify Ruff's conditions... to require that Ruff advise his probation officer whether his employment entails access to a computer" — i.e., a narrower, PO-facing disclosure obligation (as opposed to an employer-facing notification mandate) remains available.

### 20(c). United States v. Piper, No. 18-3654-cr (2d Cir. Mar. 30, 2020) (summary order)

**Binding status:** Non-precedential summary order.

**Holding:** Remanded (not vacated outright) an employer-notification condition and a contact-with-minors condition for inadequate explanation under U.S.S.G. § 5F1.5(a) and *Bleau*, 930 F.3d at 43, requiring the district court to "consider and provide a reasoned explanation" on remand per the *Jacobson* procedure.

### 20(d). United States v. Salazar, 489 F.3d 555 (2d Cir. 2007) — DOES NOT FIT; DROP FROM THIS LINE

**Binding status:** Second Circuit — binding, precedential, but **on an entirely unrelated subject.**

**Actual holding:** Salazar is a **cocaine-trafficking sentencing appeal** addressing whether, post-*Booker*, a district court retains discretion to apply a standard of proof higher than preponderance of the evidence when finding facts (there, a leadership-role enhancement under U.S.S.G. § 3B1.1(a)) relevant to a Guidelines calculation. It holds that district courts remain "statutorily obliged" to use the preponderance standard for Guidelines fact-finding. **It has nothing to do with supervised release, employer notification, occupational restrictions, or § 5F1.5.** This citation should be **dropped entirely** from the employer-notification line — it appears to have been confused with a different case (possibly by citation-string proximity or a mis-scan), and no amount of reframing salvages it for this purpose.

**Confirms or corrects (all of 20(a)-(d)):** Lombardi, Ruff, and Piper are **all genuine, on-point members of the employer-notification/occupational-restriction line** (alongside the already-confirmed Jenkins, 854 F.3d 181), and all independently quote and apply U.S.S.G. § 5F1.5(a)'s exact two-part test:

## 22. Turner v. Rogers, 564 U.S. 431 (2011)

**Binding status:** U.S. Supreme Court — binding, precedential.

**Confirmed holding — the four substitute procedural safeguards:**

> "[A]n available set of 'substitute procedural safeguards,' if employed together, can significantly reduce the risk of an erroneous deprivation of liberty. > "Turner's incarceration violated due process because he received neither counsel nor the benefit of alternative procedures like those the Court describes. He did not have clear notice that his ability to pay would constitute the critical question in his civil contempt proceeding. No one provided him with a form... designed to elicit information about his financial circumstances."

**Confirms or corrects:** **Fully confirmed**, verbatim, including all four safeguards. Honest assessment of fit, as requested: Turner is a **civil contempt/incarceration-for-nonpayment-of-child-support** case; its holding is keyed to the specific due-process calculus for *incarcerating an unrepresented civil contemnor*. It is **not a direct fit** for Bernecky's "contribute to the cost of services" conditions, which do not (yet) involve any threatened incarceration for nonpayment, and Turner itself is about the right to *counsel* (which it denies, substituting the four safeguards) rather than about fee conditions generally. **Application to Bernecky:** Best used as a structural analogy: if and when a fee-based violation is ever charged against Bernecky, Turner (combined with Bearden) supports requiring, at minimum, clear advance notice that ability to pay will be the critical question, a mechanism to document his financial circumstances, and an express judicial finding on ability to pay before any consequence attaches — this is a useful template for the *prophylactic* relief Bernecky could request now (i.e., ask the court to build these four safeguards into the fee conditions prospectively), rather than a case that itself governs Bernecky's current situation.

## 23. Webster v. Fall, 266 U.S. 507 (1925)

**Binding status:** U.S. Supreme Court — binding, precedential (on the general point of stare decisis doctrine it stands for).

**Holding (verbatim):**

> "Counsel for appellant directs our attention to other cases, where this Court proceeded to determine the merits notwithstanding the suits were brought against inferior or subordinate officials without joining the superior. **Confirms or corrects:** **Fully confirmed**, verbatim, exact quote and citation.

## 24. United States v. Olano, 507 U.S. 725 (1993)

**Binding status:** U.S. Supreme Court — binding, precedential.

**Confirmed four-part plain-error framework (Fed. R. Crim. P. 52(b)):**

> "Rule 52(b)... provides a court of appeals a limited power to correct errors that were forfeited because not timely raised in district court." The forfeited error "may be noticed" only if it is "plain" and "affect[s] substantial rights." Where met, "the decision to correct the forfeited error [is left] within the sound discretion of the court of appeals, and the court should not exercise that discretion unless the error 'seriously affect[s] the fairness, integrity or public reputation of judicial proceedings.'"

The now-standard four-part articulation (confirmed via Olano's own language and its progeny, e.g., as restated verbatim in *Marcus*, 560 U.S. 258, 262 (2010), and quoted within Dupes and Ruff, both read for this pass): (1) there must be an error; (2) the error must be plain (clear or obvious); (3) the error must affect substantial rights; (4) if the first three are met, the appellate court has discretion to correct it, but only if it seriously affects the fairness, integrity, or public reputation of judicial proceedings.

**Confirms or corrects:** **Fully confirmed.**

**Application to Bernecky:** Background doctrine only, as previously noted — plain-error review is not the standard governing Bernecky's own § 3583(e)(2) motion. Its continuing usefulness is solely to frame the argument (per Webster, above, and the general federal-appellate doctrine question the client raised) about how much weight a plain-error affirmance's articulation of a legal rule should carry as "precedent" going forward, since a plain-error panel's core holding is only that the district court's error (if any) was not "plain" — a materially different, and narrower, form of adjudication than a de novo merits ruling.
