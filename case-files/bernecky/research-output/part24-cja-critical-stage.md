## BOTTOM LINE UP FRONT

1. **The affirmative critical-stage argument is genuinely weak on the doctrine's own terms, and the weakness is structural, not just a matter of no case being squarely on point.** Sixth Amendment "critical stage" analysis is not a freestanding fairness inquiry — it is the second half of a two-step framework (*Rothgery v. Gillespie County*, 554 U.S. 191 (2008)) in which the right must first **attach** (adversary judicial proceedings must have commenced against the defendant **for that specific offense** — *Kirby v. Illinois*, 406 U.S. 682 (1972); *McNeil v. Wisconsin*, 501 U.S. 171 (1991); *Texas v. Cobb*, 532 U.S. 162 (2001)) before any "critical stage" question can even be asked. For the scenario that actually worries the client — a polygraph question eliciting an admission to an **uncharged, separate** sex offense — no adversary proceeding has been initiated on that offense, so under conventional doctrine the right has not attached to it at all, and critical-stage analysis is doctrinally premature, not merely disfavored. For the offense of conviction itself, the Sixth Amendment right attached long ago, but the mainstream doctrine treats ordinary post-conviction supervision determinations (polygraphs, probation-officer meetings, and revocation-type proceedings) under **due-process** frameworks (*Gagnon v. Scarpelli*, 411 U.S. 778 (1973); *Morrissey v. Brewer*, 408 U.S. 471 (1972)), not the Sixth Amendment critical-stage line, and at least one on-point (if out-of-jurisdiction, non-binding) authority — *J.B. v. New Jersey State Parole Board*, 229 N.J. 21 (2017) — squarely rejected a right-to-counsel-at-polygraph claim on reasoning that a supervision-compliance polygraph is not the kind of proceeding that triggers the right. **This should be disclosed as real, on-point-flavored adverse authority, not omitted.**
2. **That said, the argument is not frivolous and should be made — honestly, as an extension of real doctrine, not as settled law.** *Estelle v. Smith*, 451 U.S. 454 (1981) (compelled psychiatric examination held a critical stage because it directly affected the pending capital sentencing proceeding), supplies a genuine, citable principle: an examination that functions as an interrogation and that bears directly on a defendant's liberty in a proceeding to which the right has already attached can be a critical stage, even outside a courtroom and even without custody. The honest limitation is that *Estelle* worked because the examination was tied to the **same, still-pending** case (capital sentencing); Bernecky's supervision term is technically still part of the same criminal judgment, and a serious, good-faith argument can be built that revocation exposure flowing directly from the polygraph makes this analogous — but this cuts against the mainstream *Gagnon*/due-process treatment of supervision-phase proceedings, and no court has adopted the analogy in this context. **This is the honest frontier of the argument: real, arguable, and never adopted anywhere this session's research could find.**
3. **§ 3006A(a)(1) already lists supervised-release *modification* proceedings as a category requiring mandatory (not merely discretionary) appointment of counsel for financially eligible persons** — this is confirmed, independently, across every primary-source-adjacent citation recovered this session, and it means Bernecky's own § 3583(e)(2) motion may **already** qualify him for CJA counsel as a matter of statutory right, **without needing the "interests of justice" provision at all.** This is a more direct and more immediately useful finding than the client's own proposed theory.
4. **The "interests of justice" provision is real, but it is not the broad, general catch-all the client's directive assumes.** § 3006A(a)(2), as confirmed across multiple independent sources, extends the "interests of justice" standard to exactly two enumerated categories — Class B/C misdemeanants and infraction defendants facing authorized confinement, and habeas petitioners under 28 U.S.C. §§ 2241, 2254, or 2255 — **not** to a general "whenever justice requires it, appoint counsel for anything" standard. On the statute's plain text, it does not reach a request for counsel to vet Fifth Amendment implications of polygraph questions. **The client's proposed reading is a stretch on the statute as actually written, and this should be said plainly rather than forced.**
5. **No case anywhere located this session appoints, or even discusses appointing, counsel under § 3006A's interests-of-justice provision to protect a supervisee's Fifth Amendment rights during a polygraph or any analogous administrative examination.** This is a confirmed, honest gap.
6. **Recommendation:** lead with the discretionary condition-crafting/administrative-safeguard theory as the primary vehicle (already developed elsewhere in this case), present the critical-stage argument affirmatively but candidly labeled as a genuine extension of *Estelle*'s logic rather than settled law, disclose *J.B. v. New Jersey* and the *Gagnon*/due-process line as real adverse material, and use § 3006A(a)(1)(E)'s modification-proceeding category — not the interests-of-justice provision — as the CJA hook, since it is the stronger, better-supported statutory argument for getting Bernecky appointed counsel for this very motion.

## THREAD 1 — The Affirmative Critical-Stage Argument

### 1.1 The actual "critical stage" test and its doctrinal architecture

The Sixth Amendment right to counsel is not a single, undifferentiated guarantee that applies wherever a proceeding "feels" important. It has two analytically distinct components, and both must be satisfied:

**Step one — attachment.** The right to counsel "attaches" only "at or after the initiation of adversary judicial criminal proceedings — whether by way of formal charge, preliminary hearing, indictment, information, or arraignment." *Kirby v. Illinois*, 406 U.S. 682, 689 (1972) [VERIFY pincite]. This is "the starting point of our whole system of adversary criminal justice," because "it is only then that the government has committed itself to prosecute, and only then that the adverse positions of government and defendant have solidified." *Id.* [VERIFY]. Critically, the right is **offense-specific**: it "cannot be invoked once for all future prosecutions," *McNeil v. Wisconsin*, 501 U.S. 171, 175 (1991) [VERIFY], and attaches only as to the specific offense for which adversary proceedings have commenced. *Texas v. Cobb*, 532 U.S. 162 (2001), confirms this and rejects a "closely related offense" exception — the Court held that the *Blockburger* same-offense test, not a "factually related" or "same criminal episode" test, defines the boundary of what counts as "the offense" for attachment purposes. 532 U.S. at 173 [VERIFY].

**Step two — critical stage.** Only after attachment does the question become whether a particular proceeding or event, occurring within that same, already-attached prosecution, is a "critical stage" requiring counsel's presence. *United States v. Wade*, 388 U.S. 218 (1967), is the source formulation: the Sixth Amendment guarantees counsel "at any critical confrontation by the prosecution at pretrial proceedings where the results might well determine his fate and where the absence of counsel might derogate from his right to a fair trial." 388 U.S. at 226-27 [VERIFY]. A stage is "critical" where "potential substantial prejudice to defendant's rights inheres in the ... confrontation" and "the ability of counsel [to] help avoid that prejudice" is meaningful — i.e., where "the accused requires aid in coping with legal problems or ... assistance in meeting his adversary." *Id.* [VERIFY]. *Wade* itself applied this to post-indictment lineups.

**These two steps are analytically separate, and courts have specifically warned against collapsing them.** *Rothgery v. Gillespie County*, 554 U.S. 191 (2008), expressly identifies "the analytical mistake" of "assum[ing] that attachment necessarily requires the occurrence or imminence of a critical stage," clarifying that "[t]he question whether [a proceeding] signals the initiation of adversary judicial proceedings ... is distinct from the question whether [that proceeding] itself is a critical stage requiring the presence of counsel." 554 U.S. at 212 [VERIFY]. This two-step structure is the single most important piece of doctrinal architecture for assessing Bernecky's theory, because it means **a "critical stage" argument cannot succeed at all unless attachment is independently established first** — and attachment is where this theory runs into its hardest problem.

### 1.2 Applying the two-step framework to Bernecky's polygraph — the honest problem

The client's theory conflates two factually distinct risks the polygraph presents, and the two-step framework treats them very differently:

**(a) Risk of self-incrimination as to the offense of conviction, or matters bearing on the existing supervision.** Bernecky's Sixth Amendment right attached long ago, at indictment on the CP offense. The hard question is whether that attachment **persists** through the supervision phase for purposes of a polygraph examination. The mainstream doctrine says no, or at least not automatically: the Supreme Court has treated **post-sentencing parole and probation determinations as a category the Sixth Amendment critical-stage line has not been invoked to reach** [VERIFY — this characterization is drawn from the Constitution Annotated's synthesis of the Court's post-conviction-proceedings case law at congress.gov, itself a secondary compilation of primary holdings, and should be confirmed against the specific cases it cites before being relied upon]. Instead, counsel at revocation-type, post-sentencing supervision proceedings is analyzed under the **due-process** framework of *Gagnon v. Scarpelli*, 411 U.S. 778 (1973), and *Morrissey v. Brewer*, 408 U.S. 471 (1972) — a **case-by-case** inquiry turning on the complexity of the issues and whether the probationer has a "timely and colorable claim," not a categorical Sixth Amendment critical-stage entitlement. 411 U.S. at 790 [VERIFY]. Separately, *Pennsylvania v. Finley*, 481 U.S. 551 (1987), holds there is no Sixth Amendment right to counsel in proceedings "attacking ... a conviction that has become final upon exhaustion of the appellate process," 481 U.S. at 555 [VERIFY] — direct appeal is generally treated as the outer boundary of the Sixth Amendment's ordinary reach for a given prosecution. **The honest reading: nothing squarely closes the door on treating supervision as "the same case," but the weight of the doctrine (Gagnon/Morrissey due process, Finley's post-appeal cutoff) runs the other way, and no case was found affirmatively holding that a supervised-release-phase examination is a Sixth Amendment critical stage of the original prosecution.**

**(b) Risk of self-incrimination as to an uncharged, separate future offense (the client's real concern, and the closest fit to Von Behren's facts).** This is the scenario that most worries the client — a sexual-history question eliciting an admission to conduct that was never charged. Under *McNeil* and *Cobb*, the Sixth Amendment right to counsel has **not attached at all** to that hypothetical future offense, because no adversary judicial proceeding has been initiated on it. This is not a close call on the doctrine's own terms: attachment is offense-specific, and there is no "offense" yet for the right to attach to. **This means the affirmative critical-stage argument, applied to the scenario the client cares most about, faces a threshold, structural attachment problem that has nothing to do with how compelling the facts are** — it is not that courts have weighed the interests and found against the client; it is that, under conventional Sixth Amendment doctrine, the question of whether this is a "critical stage" cannot even be reached, because step one (attachment) fails before step two (critical stage) is ever asked. This is the central reason the "safer fallback" theory exists in the first place, and it should be presented to the client as a structural doctrinal wall, not merely an unfavorable balance of case law.

**Massiah does not provide a workaround.** *Massiah v. United States*, 377 U.S. 201 (1964), does establish that once the right has attached (post-indictment), custody is irrelevant — the government may not "deliberately elicit" incriminating statements about the charged offense even through an uncounseled, non-custodial conversation. This confirms that **custodial status is not the doctrinal gatekeeper** for Sixth Amendment purposes (unlike Fifth Amendment *Miranda* doctrine, where custody is essential) — but *Massiah*'s protection is still confined to the **charged** offense for which the right has attached. It does not extend Sixth Amendment protection to elicitation of statements about a *different, uncharged* offense. So while it is true (and worth saying candidly) that critical-stage doctrine does not require custody, it is not true that it operates independent of charge-status — and charge-status (attachment) is exactly what is missing for the new-offense scenario.

### 1.3 The closest genuine analogy — *Estelle v. Smith* — and why it does not fully transfer

The single most useful affirmative building block located this session is *Estelle v. Smith*, 451 U.S. 454 (1981). There, a court-ordered psychiatric examination, administered to assess future dangerousness for a **still-pending** capital sentencing proceeding, was held a "critical stage" because "the decision to submit to the examination" without notice to counsel deprived the defendant "the assistance of counsel in making [a] significant decision" bearing directly on "a life or death matter." 451 U.S. at 471 [VERIFY]. This case is genuinely useful because it confirms: (1) an examination need not occur in a courtroom, and need not be custodial, to be a critical stage; (2) an examination that functions as an interrogation-equivalent — extracting information used against the defendant in a proceeding affecting his liberty — can trigger the critical-stage doctrine; and (3) the Court did not treat the psychiatric/clinical framing of the exam as placing it outside Sixth Amendment scrutiny.

**The honest limitation, which must be confronted rather than glossed over:** *Estelle*'s examination was tied to the **same, still-pending prosecution** (the capital sentencing phase of the very case in which Smith had already been indicted) — attachment was never in question in *Estelle*, only whether the sentencing-phase psychiatric exam was a critical stage of that already-attached case. *Estelle* therefore does the least work for exactly the scenario that worries the client most (new, uncharged conduct), and does more work for the "is supervision itself still part of the same case" argument in 1.2(a) above — which is itself contested by the *Gagnon*/*Finley* line. **The fair characterization: *Estelle* supplies real doctrinal DNA for the proposition that an interrogation-like examination bearing on liberty can be a critical stage regardless of custody or formal courtroom setting, but it does not resolve the attachment problem for new-offense risk, and it has never been extended to a supervised-release-phase examination by any court this session's research located.**

### 1.4 Is there a doctrine treating "functional interrogation" as a critical stage regardless of custodial or charge status?

No — and this needs to be stated candidly because it is the crux of the client's question 1(c). The custody-independence in Sixth Amendment doctrine (confirmed via *Massiah*, above) is real, but it operates **only after attachment** — i.e., custody doesn't matter, but charge-status (attachment) still does. There is no doctrine, located anywhere this session, treating the functional equivalent of interrogation as a critical stage in the complete absence of an already-attached, charged offense. The Fifth Amendment (*Miranda*, and the *Murphy*/*Von Behren* "classic penalty situation" line already developed in part2/part2b) is the doctrinal home for protecting against self-incrimination regardless of charge status — that is precisely why the Fifth Amendment theory, not the Sixth Amendment critical-stage theory, is this case's stronger vehicle for the new-offense risk scenario. This is not a matter of the client's theory being poorly argued; it reflects a genuine, structural division of labor between the Fifth and Sixth Amendments that no court has blurred in this context.

### 1.5 Adverse authority that must be disclosed, not avoided

*J.B. v. New Jersey State Parole Board*, 229 N.J. 21 (2017) [VERIFY — New Jersey Supreme Court, state law, not binding in the Second Circuit or under the federal Constitution, but genuinely on-point and worth knowing about], squarely rejected a parolee's claim to a right to counsel's presence during a sex-offender polygraph examination. The court's reasoning — that "the right to counsel does not attach to a polygraph examination because it is a routine interview" for compliance monitoring, and that counsel's presence "is likely to diminish the positive potential therapeutic benefits of the polygraph testing" and would "inject adversarial elements into the procedure" [VERIFY, reconstructed from secondary sources] — is exactly the kind of reasoning a federal court considering Bernecky's affirmative theory is likely to find persuasive, even though the case is not controlling. It should be cited and distinguished (not ignored) in any motion making the affirmative argument, both because candor requires it and because a court that finds it on its own will notice if the motion did not address it.

### 1.6 Candid strength assessment (Thread 1(d))

Applying the assignment's own three-way framework:

**Is this theory supported by real, on-point authority?** No. No court — Second Circuit, other circuit, or Supreme Court — has held that a supervised-release polygraph (or any analogous post-conviction examination) is a Sixth Amendment critical stage. The one closely analogous state-court decision to consider a version of this exact claim (*J.B.*) rejected it.

**Is it a plausible, honest extension of real doctrine?** Partially, and unevenly across the two risk scenarios. For the risk of eliciting an admission to a **new, uncharged** offense — the scenario that actually drives the client's concern — the theory runs into the attachment requirement (*Kirby*/*McNeil*/*Cobb*) as a threshold, structural problem that no amount of favorable facts overcomes on conventional doctrine; this is the honest reason a "critical stage" label does not fit well here, not a matter of unfavorable precedent balance. For the risk of eliciting information that bears on the **existing** conviction/supervision, the theory has a genuine doctrinal analog in *Estelle v. Smith*'s "interrogation-equivalent examination bearing on a pending liberty determination" principle, but it swims against the mainstream *Gagnon*/*Morrissey*/*Finley* treatment of post-conviction supervision matters as due-process (not Sixth Amendment critical-stage) territory, and no court has made this extension.

**Recommendation on Thread 1:** Make the argument affirmatively, as the client wants, but structure it honestly along the fault line above — lead with *Estelle v. Smith*'s functional-interrogation-bearing-on-liberty principle as applied to the supervision-continuation risk, candidly acknowledge the *Gagnon*/*Finley* due-process counter-authority, disclose *J.B. v. New Jersey* directly, and be explicit that the new-uncharged-offense risk scenario (the client's central worry) faces the attachment problem as a matter of doctrinal structure rather than unfavorable weighing — which is precisely why the discretionary condition-crafting/administrative-safeguard fallback exists and should remain the load-bearing argument. This is not a theory that should be abandoned unheard, but the motion should not represent it as anything stronger than a genuine, first-impression extension of *Estelle*, honestly limited by *McNeil*/*Cobb* attachment doctrine as to new-offense risk.

## THREAD 2 — The Criminal Justice Act, 18 U.S.C. § 3006A

### 2.1 The actual text of § 3006A(a)(1) — mandatory appointment categories

> "(a)(1) Representation shall be provided for any financially eligible person who—
> (A) is charged with a felony or a Class A misdemeanor;
> (B) is a juvenile alleged to have committed an act of juvenile delinquency as defined in section 5031 of this title;
> (C) is charged with a violation of probation;
> (D) is under arrest, when such representation is required by law;
> (E) is charged with a violation of supervised release or faces modification, reduction, or enlargement of a condition, or extension or revocation of a term of supervised release;

**The single most important finding for this thread: subparagraph (E) already lists "faces modification ... of a condition ... of a term of supervised release" as a category requiring mandatory (not merely discretionary) appointment of counsel for a financially eligible person.** This is confirmed with high confidence — the phrase appeared, worded identically, across independent search results drawing on Cornell LII, FindLaw, Justia, and govinfo.gov. **On its plain text, Bernecky's own § 3583(e)(2) motion — a request to modify/reduce the polygraph and CIMP conditions — appears to fall within this mandatory category, independent of any critical-stage argument and independent of the "interests of justice" provision discussed below.** If Bernecky is financially eligible (client-directives.md already documents that he currently "lacks the funds" to order his own sentencing transcript, which is at least suggestive of financial eligibility), this is a directly actionable, statute-grounded basis to seek CJA counsel appointment for this motion as a matter of right, not discretion.

**A genuine complication that must be disclosed, not buried:** a pending (as of this writing, not yet enacted) bill, the **Safer Supervision Act of 2025** (H.R. 5883, 119th Congress, introduced by Rep. Laurel Lee, companion bill by Sens. Mike Lee and Chris Coons) [VERIFY current status via congress.gov before relying on this], would specifically "make appointed counsel available to people seeking early termination" of supervised release or modification of conditions [VERIFY exact bill language]. **The existence of a pending bill specifically designed to add appointed-counsel availability for defendant-initiated modification/early-termination motions is meaningful evidence that, in current practice, courts and CJA administrators do not uniformly treat every defense-initiated, defense-favorable modification motion as automatically triggering (a)(1)(E)'s mandatory category** — otherwise the bill's sponsors would have no gap to fill. The most likely reconciliation: (a)(1)(E)'s language ("faces modification ... or extension or revocation") reads most naturally as covering proceedings where something adverse is being done to the defendant (consistent with the other items in the same list — violation, extension, revocation), and it is genuinely unsettled whether it was drafted to reach a defendant's own request to loosen/reduce a condition. **This should be disclosed as a live, unresolved textual ambiguity, not represented as a clean win** — but it is still the better statutory argument available, better than reaching for the interests-of-justice clause discussed next, and worth raising with the court directly (and worth checking against the W.D.N.Y.'s own local CJA plan, which may resolve the ambiguity as a matter of local practice).

### 2.2 The actual text of § 3006A(a)(2) — the "interests of justice" provision, and its real scope

Also reconstructed from multiple independently-worded but consistent sources [VERIFY against the official U.S. Code]:

> "(a)(2) Whenever the United States magistrate judge or the court determines that the interests of justice so require, representation may be provided for any financially eligible person who—
> (A) is charged with a Class B or C misdemeanor, or an infraction for which a sentence to confinement is authorized; or
> (B) is seeking relief under section 2241, 2254, or 2255 of title 28."

**This is the central, honest finding for Thread 2: the "interests of justice" standard, as actually codified, is not a general, open-ended catch-all provision that can be invoked to reach any situation a court thinks justice requires.** It is a discretionary standard attached to exactly **two enumerated categories** — low-level misdemeanants/infraction defendants, and federal or state habeas petitioners. **On the statute's plain text, it does not extend to a request for counsel to vet the Fifth Amendment implications of polygraph questions, or to any other administrative/supervisory process not falling within (A) or (B).** This directly qualifies the premise embedded in the client's own directive — that "§ 3006A ... already permits appointment 'in the interests of justice' regardless of critical-stage status" as a freestanding, general standard. That premise does not match the statute as actually written.

### 2.3 Has any court appointed, or discussed appointing, counsel under the interests-of-justice provision to protect a supervisee's Fifth Amendment rights during a polygraph or an analogous process?

No. No case was located this session — through either the Google Drive materials or WebSearch — appointing or even discussing appointing counsel under § 3006A(a)(2)'s interests-of-justice standard for this purpose, or for any functionally analogous purpose (protecting a supervisee's constitutional rights during an administrative or supervisory examination). This is consistent with the narrow textual scope confirmed in 2.2: since (a)(2) is limited to misdemeanor/infraction defendants and habeas petitioners, there would be no textual hook for a court to reach for it in the polygraph-counsel context in the first place, and none appears to have tried.

### 2.4 Assessing the client's proposed reading candidly

The client's theory — that ensuring a supervisee's Fifth Amendment rights are protected during a polygraph, where privilege determinations are "pure questions of law" a layperson cannot reliably make in real time, is "squarely the kind of thing the interests-of-justice standard is meant to reach" — is **a sound intuition about what "interests of justice" ought to mean as a matter of policy, but it is not a sound reading of § 3006A(a)(2) as the statute is actually drafted.** Congress did not write a general interests-of-justice catch-all; it wrote a specific, two-category discretionary extension layered on top of the broader mandatory list in (a)(1). Using (a)(2) for this purpose would require a court to read language into the statute that is not there — a stretch that should be disclosed as such, not papered over with confident citation.

**However, this does not mean the CJA route is a dead end — it means the better argument lives in (a)(1), not (a)2).** Two independent statutory hooks exist and should both be presented:

1. **The direct, practical hook:** § 3006A(a)(1)(E)'s mandatory modification-proceeding category, discussed in 2.1 above, which may already entitle Bernecky to appointed counsel for this very § 3583(e)(2) motion, regardless of how the critical-stage or Fifth Amendment merits arguments come out. This should be raised as a request for CJA appointment for the motion itself — a practical, immediate, and independently useful outcome regardless of the outcome of Thread 1.
2. **The contingent, larger hook:** if the affirmative critical-stage argument in Thread 1 were ever to succeed (acknowledged above to be a hard, honestly uncertain argument), that would trigger § 3006A(a)(1)'s own catch-all clause for persons "entitled to appointment of counsel under the Sixth Amendment to the Constitution" [VERIFY exact catch-all language and lettering] — which is a **mandatory**, constitutionally-grounded entitlement, stronger than anything the discretionary interests-of-justice provision could supply. **This means Threads 1 and 2 are not two independent, equally-weighted arguments — success on Thread 1 would automatically moot the need for Thread 2's interests-of-justice theory (a constitutional right subsumes a discretionary statutory one), and if Thread 1 fails, Thread 2's interests-of-justice clause, as actually written, does not independently reach this scenario either.** The two threads should be presented as sequenced, not parallel: (1) critical-stage, as the ambitious lead theory; (2) the modification-proceeding mandatory-appointment hook in (a)(1)(E), as the practical, freestanding request that does not depend on Thread 1's outcome at all; and (3) the interests-of-justice provision should **not** be presented as an independent third ground, because it does not reach this fact pattern on the statute's own terms — using it anyway risks the same credibility cost this case's other research has flagged for overclaiming (part19, part20).

## BOTTOM-LINE RECOMMENDATION

**Thread 1 (affirmative critical-stage argument):** Make it, as the client wants, but honestly. It is not supported by any on-point authority anywhere located this session, and it runs into a real structural problem — the Sixth Amendment's offense-specific attachment requirement — for exactly the new-uncharged-offense scenario that worries the client most. The strongest honest version leads with *Estelle v. Smith*'s "interrogation-equivalent examination bearing directly on a pending liberty determination can be a critical stage" principle, applied to the argument that supervision is a continuation of the same case, candidly confronts the *Gagnon*/*Morrissey*/*Finley* due-process counter-authority and *J.B. v. New Jersey*'s direct rejection of the same theory, and is explicit that the new-offense-risk scenario faces a threshold attachment problem that is structural, not a matter of unfavorable weighing. This should be presented as the ambitious lead theory, tested honestly rather than conceded away untested — but the discretionary condition-crafting/administrative-safeguard theory should remain the load-bearing fallback the motion actually rests on, exactly as this case's other close-call theories (Theory 7, Theory 8) have been positioned: principle and ambition in the lead, doctrine that actually exists carrying the real weight.

**Thread 2 (CJA "interests of justice" ground):** The client's instinct that § 3006A supplies an independent ground worth checking was well-founded, but the specific provision the client had in mind (the "interests of justice" catch-all) does not reach this fact pattern on the statute's actual text — it is narrowly limited to misdemeanor/infraction defendants and habeas petitioners, and no case has ever stretched it further. **The genuinely valuable finding this thread produced is a different one:** § 3006A(a)(1)(E) already lists supervised-release modification proceedings as a **mandatory** appointment category, which may entitle Bernecky to CJA counsel for this very § 3583(e)(2) motion as a matter of statutory right, independent of the critical-stage debate entirely — a practical, immediately actionable point that should be raised regardless of how Thread 1 is ultimately resolved, though the client should be told candidly that it is not free from ambiguity (a pending bill suggests current practice may not treat defendant-initiated favorable modifications as automatically covered). Do **not** present the interests-of-justice provision as an independent, freestanding ground in the motion — doing so invites exactly the kind of citation-drift/overclaiming problem this case's Drive-document review has already flagged repeatedly, on a provision whose text plainly does not reach this scenario.
