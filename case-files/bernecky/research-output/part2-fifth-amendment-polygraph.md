## PART A — Special Condition (g): Polygraph / CVSA

### A.1 The core comparison: Von Behren (10th Cir.) vs. the Second Circuit's own model

#### Case: United States v. Von Behren
**Citation:** 822 F.3d 1139 (10th Cir. 2016)
**Binding status:** **Persuasive only** — Tenth Circuit, out-of-circuit. Not binding on this Court or the Second Circuit.
**Verification status:** [VERIFY BEFORE FILING] — citation and general holding corroborated across multiple independent secondary sources (Justia, FindLaw, Prison Legal News, Federal Defenders of New York blog, Courthouse News), but I could not retrieve the slip opinion or reporter text directly in this session. Pull and read the full opinion (822 F.3d 1139) before relying on it.

**What Von Behren actually held (correcting the client's paraphrase):**
Von Behren was on supervised release for a 2005 child-pornography distribution conviction. As part of sex-offense treatment, he was ordered to submit to a "sexual history polygraph" containing four specific questions probing uncharged criminal conduct (e.g., sexual contact with a minor under 15 after age 18; sexual contact with a family member; forced sexual contact; sexual contact with an unconscious person). He refused to answer on Fifth Amendment grounds. The treatment provider terminated him from the program for that refusal, and the government took the position that termination from treatment would result in his being remanded to prison for violating the treatment condition. The Tenth Circuit held:
- Three of the four questions posed "a real and appreciable" (the government's brief-secondary-source phrasing renders this "at least some authentic") danger of self-incrimination — i.e., they were not confined to the offense of conviction or to treatment-relevant conduct, but fished for wholly separate, uncharged crimes.
- The government's implicit threat — answer or be terminated from treatment, and termination will mean revocation/remand — created a "classic penalty situation" under *Minnesota v. Murphy* (discussed below), rendering the compulsion unconstitutional.
- The court reversed on the Fifth Amendment issue and remanded.

**What Von Behren did NOT hold** (important corrective, since the client's four-part framework is presented as if it flows from Von Behren):
- Von Behren does **not** hold that appointed counsel must be present before, during, and after every polygraph/quasi-scientific exam.
- Von Behren does **not** hold that counsel must be given question-by-question research time to assess whether invocation is legally justified.
- Von Behren does **not** impose a Daubert-level credentialing requirement on polygraph examiners.
- Von Behren does **not** impose a categorical non-disclosure/no-further-dissemination rule.
- I could find no indication in any secondary source that Von Behren announced a general "four-part procedural protection" framework at all. Von Behren is, at bottom, a straightforward application of the *Minnesota v. Murphy* "penalty situation" doctrine (see Part A.3) to a specific set of overbroad polygraph questions — it holds that the *specific compulsion mechanism* used against Von Behren (terminate treatment → revoke/remand, if he invokes) was unconstitutional, not that a wholesale prophylactic regime of appointed counsel and Daubert vetting must precede every exam.

**Recommendation for drafting:** Present protections (I)–(IV) candidly as the *client's own proposed prophylactic package*, argued **by analogy to** and **in the spirit of** Von Behren's underlying rejection of "answer-now-or-be-punished" compulsion — not as a holding Von Behren itself compels. Overclaiming Von Behren's scope is a credibility risk that could undercut the motion; the court (and any AUSA response) will check the citation. The stronger, more defensible frame is: "Von Behren establishes that a supervisee facing quasi-scientific, non-treatment-tailored questioning cannot be forced into the choice between self-incrimination and revocation without it being unconstitutional compulsion; the protections requested here are the practical means of preventing that same choice from recurring on these facts, given that Mr. Bernecky — unlike represented parties in nearly every other adversarial encounter with the state — currently has no counsel, no advance notice of question content, and no ability to assess in real time whether a given question crosses the line Von Behren identifies."

#### Case: United States v. Johnson (the Second Circuit's actual, controlling approach)
**Citation:** 446 F.3d 272 (2d Cir. 2006)
**Binding status:** **BINDING** — Second Circuit, and squarely on point (arising from a supervised-release polygraph condition originating in the Northern District of New York — Tompkins County — the same general geographic/circuit family as this case).
**Verification status:** [VERIFY BEFORE FILING] — corroborated across independent sources (Justia docket entry, OpenJurist, CaseMine, Studicata brief), but full opinion text not independently retrieved this session; confirm the quotations below against the F.3d reporter before filing.
**Holding (as reconstructed from multiple independent secondary sources, with verbatim fragments):**
Johnson, on supervised release for sexual predation against minors, challenged a polygraph condition on Fifth Amendment grounds, arguing that without use/derivative-use immunity he faced "the dilemma of confessing his sins or remaining silent" and going to prison for a supervised-release violation. The district court had limited the polygraph's scope to "information necessary for supervision, case monitoring, and treatment" and added the safeguard that, **"if a truthful answer would expose him to a prosecution for a crime different from the one on which he was already convicted,"** Johnson would preserve his **"right to challenge in a court of law the use of such statements as violations of his Fifth Amendment rights,"** and that **by answering, he would "not be waiving his Fifth Amendment rights with respect to any criminal prosecution unrelated to the conviction for which he is now on supervised release."** The Second Circuit affirmed this model, reasoning that "the incremental tendency of polygraph testing to promote such candor furthers the objectives of sentencing" and that the case for polygraph testing was strong given Johnson's "nature and circumstances of the offense and history and characteristics" as an offender who "apparently resisted honest self-assessment."

**This is, functionally, word-for-word the model embedded in Bernecky's own Judgment condition** ("subject to the defendant's right to challenge in a court of law the use of such statements as violations of the defendant's Fifth Amendment rights. In this regard, the defendant shall be deemed not to have waived the defendant's Fifth Amendment rights by making any such statements"). **Johnson is the controlling Second Circuit precedent that already blesses the exact "answer now, preserve a later challenge, no waiver" structure the client is objecting to.** This is the single most important finding for this track: **the Second Circuit has already considered and approved the very model this Judgment uses, and Von Behren's more protective approach has no footing in this circuit.**

**Application to Bernecky's facts:** Johnson forecloses a facial argument that the "answer now, challenge later" structure itself violates the Fifth Amendment — that structure is binding, approved circuit law. Any relief here must be framed as (1) a request for *additional*, non-conflicting procedural safeguards layered on top of the Johnson-approved structure (the I–IV package, as a discretionary matter for this court to grant under its § 3583(e)(2) authority to modify conditions, not as compelled by the Fifth Amendment itself), or (2) a distinguishable as-applied argument (e.g., that CVSA specifically, or a specific category of questions, exceeds what Johnson approved — see Parts A.2 and A.4 below), or (3) the independent no-nexus/elimination argument (Part A.5), which does not depend on distinguishing Johnson at all.

**No Second Circuit case adopting, distinguishing, or even citing Von Behren was located in this research.** Searches specifically for Second Circuit citations to Von Behren returned no results identifying any such case. This should be represented candidly in the motion: Von Behren remains an out-of-circuit, non-binding data point that the Second Circuit has neither adopted nor rejected — it simply has not had occasion to engage with it, because Johnson (2006) already resolved the core question in this circuit a decade before Von Behren was decided, using a different (immunity/no-waiver) mechanism than the one Von Behren later found deficient in a different case's facts (the treatment-termination-as-de-facto-revocation mechanism).

Counsel should preemptively address Birkedahl in the motion (rather than waiting for the government to raise it) and argue that the ripeness concern that justified deferral in Birkedahl does not apply on a challenge filed on the eve of supervision commencing.

### A.2 The "link in the chain of evidence" doctrine

#### Case: Hoffman v. United States
**Citation:** 341 U.S. 479 (1951)
**Binding status:** **BINDING** — U.S. Supreme Court.
**Verification status:** Corroborated across multiple independent, high-reliability secondary sources (Justia Supreme Court Center, Cornell LII, FindLaw, Library of Congress U.S. Reports scan). This is the correct and best authority for the proposition cited. [VERIFY BEFORE FILING — pull exact page pincite for the quoted language, likely at 341 U.S. at 486.]
**Holding verbatim (as rendered across sources):** "The privilege afforded not only extends to answers that would in themselves support a conviction under a federal criminal statute but likewise embraces those which would furnish a link in the chain of evidence needed to prosecute the claimant for a federal crime." The Court further held that "[t]o sustain the privilege, it need only be evident from the implications of the question, in the setting in which it is asked, that a responsive answer to the question or an explanation of why it cannot be answered might be dangerous because injurious disclosure could result." On the facts, a grand-jury witness's refusal to answer questions about his occupation and his knowledge of a fugitive's whereabouts was upheld as privileged, and his contempt conviction was reversed.

**Application to Bernecky's facts:** This is the correct, best, and controlling authority for the client's point that the Fifth Amendment protects not merely direct confessions but any answer that could furnish an investigative lead — squarely applicable to polygraph/CVSA questioning, which by design probes broadly for admissions and behavioral "tells" that could point investigators toward chargeable conduct never previously suspected. No need to search further for a "better" case; Hoffman is the standard citation used in essentially all subsequent Fifth Amendment "link in the chain" discussions, including (per the Constitution Annotated / Congress.gov treatment) modern Self-Incrimination Clause doctrine generally. I did not locate a specific recent Second Circuit case restating this exact formulation in the polygraph-condition context, but Hoffman's "link in the chain" language is treated as black-letter doctrine and is cited approvingly across circuits; no contrary or narrowing authority was found.

### A.3 Minnesota v. Murphy and the "penalty situation" doctrine (cross-referenced from Part B, since it underlies both tracks)

#### Case: Minnesota v. Murphy
**Citation:** 465 U.S. 420 (1984)
**Binding status:** **BINDING** — U.S. Supreme Court.
**Verification status:** Corroborated across multiple independent sources (Justia, Cornell LII, FindLaw, CaseMine). Citation is correct as the client recorded it. [VERIFY BEFORE FILING — pull exact pincites, e.g. 465 U.S. at 434–35, 438–39.]
**Holding (verbatim fragments):** Murphy, on probation, made incriminating admissions about an unrelated murder/rape to his probation officer without Miranda warnings, under a state-law obligation to be truthful with the officer. The Court held that "[t]he general obligation to appear before [a probation officer] and answer questions truthfully did not in itself convert [Murphy's] otherwise voluntary statements into compelled ones," and that "a witness confronted with questions that the government should reasonably expect to elicit incriminating evidence ordinarily must assert the [Fifth Amendment] privilege rather than answer, if he desires not to incriminate himself" — if he answers instead of asserting, that choice is deemed voluntary because "he was free to claim the privilege and would suffer no penalty as a result of his decision to do so." Critically, however, the Court carved out the **"classic penalty situation"**: **"if the State, either expressly or by implication, asserts that invocation of the privilege would lead to revocation of probation, it would have created the classic penalty situation... and [Murphy's] answers would be deemed compelled and inadmissible in a criminal prosecution."** The Court further held that while a truthful answer that would only reveal a probation violation (not a separate crime) may be compelled and used to revoke, "if there is a chance that a truthful answer... would expose the probationer to liability for a crime different from the crime for which he has already been convicted, he has a right to refuse to answer and the State may not attempt to coerce him to forgo that right."

**Application to Bernecky's facts (both Special Condition (g) and Standard Condition 4):** Murphy supplies the doctrinal spine for both halves of this track. It establishes (1) that a bare "must answer truthfully" obligation does not itself compel self-incrimination — the privilege must ordinarily be affirmatively invoked in the moment; but (2) that if the state creates a "penalty situation" (express or implied threat that invoking = revocation/termination), the resulting statement is compelled and unusable, and the probationer "has a right to refuse to answer" as to any question that could expose him to a different crime. Von Behren is best understood as a straightforward application of Murphy's "penalty situation" branch to a specific polygraph fact pattern; Johnson (2d Cir. 2006) is the Second Circuit's own application of the same Murphy framework, choosing to satisfy it via an explicit no-waiver/preserve-the-challenge clause rather than via Von Behren's treatment-termination-avoidance route. Both circuits are working within Murphy — they simply chose different mechanisms to avoid the "classic penalty situation," and the Second Circuit's mechanism (Johnson's model, now embodied verbatim in Bernecky's own Judgment) has already been held to satisfy Murphy in this circuit.

### A.4 CVSA-specific reliability/junk-science challenge

**Research findings (not case-specific doctrine, but scientific/legal consensus):**
- The National Research Council's 2003 review concluded that "this research and the few controlled tests conducted over the past decade offer little or no scientific basis for the use of the computer voice stress analyzer or similar voice measurement instruments." [VERIFY — pull the NRC 2003 report, "The Polygraph and Lie Detection," directly.]
- Department of Defense Polygraph Institute (later National Center for Credibility Assessment) research reportedly found CVSA accuracy "not significantly greater than chance."
- A National Institute of Justice field study found voice stress analysis detected only "15 percent of lies about drug use" in a field test — i.e., far worse than chance in that specific study. [VERIFY — NIJ source located at nij.ojp.gov; pull directly.]
- Academic commentary (a 2013 paper referenced in search results) characterizes CVSA's underlying theory as having "scientific implausibility" and describes vendor marketing as "ungrounded claims."
- CVSA is aggressively marketed by its manufacturer (NITV Federal Services) as superior to polygraph, a claim in direct tension with the independent research above — useful rebuttal material if the government or probation cites vendor literature.
- No court was located that has actually excluded CVSA under a Daubert-style analysis in the supervised-release context; the one on-point Second Circuit case (Birkedahl, summary order, 2020) avoided the reliability merits entirely on ripeness grounds (see A.1 above).

**Application to Bernecky's facts:** The scientific record strongly supports treating CVSA as meaningfully weaker than polygraph — a point that can be pressed both as (a) independent support for the Daubert-credentialing protection (III) as to CVSA in particular, and (b) a basis to argue that CVSA specifically (as opposed to polygraph generally) fails the "reasonably related to the purposes of sentencing" prong of § 3583(d), since a test that performs at or below chance cannot meaningfully "promote candor" in the way Johnson credited polygraph with doing. Given Birkedahl's unripeness holding, expect the government to argue prematurity if this challenge is framed as facial; the imminence of Bernecky's supervision start date (weeks away, not a year) is the answer to that objection (see A.1).

### A.5 The no-nexus / "believed until proven otherwise" merits argument

No specific case law was located directly adopting a "patient is to be believed until proven otherwise" doctrinal rule — this is best presented as a factual/mitigation argument channeled through the existing § 3583(d) "reasonably related" and "no greater deprivation of liberty than reasonably necessary" statutory standards (the same standards Johnson itself applied in upholding the condition based on Johnson's specific history of "resist[ing] honest self-assessment"). **The contrast the motion should draw explicitly is Johnson's own reasoning**: the Second Circuit upheld polygraph testing in Johnson's case *because of* his individualized history of dishonesty/resistance to treatment — meaning the Second Circuit's own precedent ties the propriety of a polygraph condition to an individualized factual predicate of untruthfulness, not a categorical rule that all sex offenders may be polygraphed regardless of any indication of dishonesty. Mr. Bernecky's record — no instance of being accused of lying in treatment or supervision — is the mirror image of the fact pattern Johnson relied on, and can be argued as taking him outside Johnson's own stated rationale, supporting a narrower condition or elimination as applied to him specifically, even though Johnson forecloses a facial challenge to the general model.

### A.6 Federal courts' inability to bind state prosecutions / "can't unring the bell"

**Research findings:** This is a sound practical point but rests on general, well-established doctrine rather than a single controlling case:
- Federal courts have no supervisory or injunctive authority over independent state criminal prosecutions absent a specific federal constitutional violation properly presented (general Younger abstention / federalism principles — no single case citation needed, but if one is wanted, *Younger v. Harris*, 401 U.S. 37 (1971), is the classic abstention citation, [VERIFY] if used).
- Even where use immunity is granted, it operates by preventing use (or derivative use) of specific statements/evidence in a subsequent proceeding — the *dual sovereignty doctrine* means a grant of immunity or protective order by a federal court's conditions-of-supervision language does not automatically bind a separate state sovereign's prosecution, and historically the Supreme Court held federal immunity statutes could not by themselves bar state prosecutions (this is the doctrinal background eventually addressed by cases like *Murphy v. Waterfront Comm'n*, 378 U.S. 52 (1964), which requires cross-jurisdictional use-immunity coordination precisely because the two sovereigns' proceedings are not automatically linked). [VERIFY — pull *Murphy v. Waterfront Comm'n of N.Y. Harbor*, 378 U.S. 52 (1964), directly; it is likely the strongest citation for the specific point that a state is barred from using compelled federal testimony only because the Supreme Court extended the exclusionary protection cross-jurisdictionally — underscoring that this protection is a exclusionary/use rule, not an injunctive power over the state prosecution itself.]
- Even where a use-immunity/exclusionary rule technically applies, enforcing it after the fact requires the defendant to litigate a suppression motion in the state proceeding, to prove the state's evidence was tainted/derived from the compelled statement, and to bear the "heavy burden" the government otherwise carries under *Kastigar v. United States*, 406 U.S. 441 (1972) [VERIFY], of showing derivation from a source "wholly independent" of the compelled statement — meaning even the correct legal remedy is imperfect, evidentiary, and after-the-fact, not a guarantee that "the bell" is truly unrung (informal disclosure to local police, sex-offender registries, or civil commitment proceedings, for example, may not be covered by any use-immunity framework at all, since those are not always "criminal cases" in the Fifth Amendment sense).

**Application to Bernecky's facts:** This entire line supports the client's strategic point: back-end suppression/use-immunity remedies are conceptually available in theory but are (a) limited to actual subsequent criminal prosecutions, (b) evidentiary and burden-shifting rather than self-executing, (c) not obviously applicable to non-prosecutorial downstream uses (state sex-offender board disclosures, treatment-provider mandatory reporting, civil proceedings), and (d) require the defendant to affirmatively litigate taint after disclosure has already occurred. This is a legitimate, well-grounded argument for weighting relief toward front-end procedural protections (the I–IV package) rather than relying on the Judgment's existing back-end "preserve your right to challenge later" clause as sufficient.

## PART B — Standard Condition 4: "Answer truthfully"

### B.1 Does an implicit Fifth Amendment privilege survive the absence of an express savings clause?

**Governing authority:** *Minnesota v. Murphy*, 465 U.S. 420 (1984) (full analysis at Part A.3 above) is the correct and controlling citation, confirmed as the client recorded it.

**Answer to the research question, based on Murphy and the U.S.S.G. application note:** Yes — the privilege survives regardless of whether the condition contains an express textual savings clause, for two independent reasons:

1. **Constitutional baseline (Murphy itself):** Murphy holds that the Fifth Amendment privilege against self-incrimination is not extinguished merely because a probation/supervision condition uses unconditional "must answer truthfully" language. The privilege must ordinarily be affirmatively invoked by the probationer in the moment (Murphy did not automatically read an express carve-out into the state's condition either — Minnesota's condition, like Standard Condition 4 here, had no express Fifth Amendment savings clause), but if the government creates a "classic penalty situation" (express or implied threat that invoking = revocation), the resulting compelled statement is unusable and the probationer has a right to refuse to answer as to any question exposing him to a different, uncharged crime. **In other words, Murphy's own facts are functionally identical to Standard Condition 4's structure: an unconditional "answer truthfully" obligation, with no express savings clause, and the Supreme Court still held the privilege survives (it is simply not self-executing — it must be invoked, and the "penalty situation" analysis governs whether an invocation, or a failure to invoke, produces admissible vs. compelled-and-inadmissible statements).**

2. **Sentencing Commission gloss (Application Note to § 5D1.3, discussed at A.8):** The Commission's own official commentary to the standard "answer truthfully" condition expressly states that a "legitimate invocation of the Fifth Amendment privilege... shall not be considered a violation of this condition." This application note applies to the standard condition as promulgated and is intended to travel with the condition regardless of whether the sentencing court's judgment separately recites it — meaning the Commission has already effectively read a savings clause into every "answer truthfully" condition nationally, as a matter of guidelines commentary, independent of the express savings language that happens to appear in the polygraph condition (g) but not in Standard Condition 4.

**Contrast with Special Condition (g):** The polygraph condition's express savings clause is not doing unique constitutional work relative to Standard Condition 4 — it is essentially restating, in the judgment's own text, protection that Murphy and § 5D1.3's application note already supply as background law to *every* "must answer" condition, express clause or not. The absence of identical express language in Standard Condition 4 is **not evidence that no privilege applies there** — if anything, the more precise reading is that Special Condition (g)'s drafters simply chose to make explicit, in that one condition, protection that is implicit (via Murphy and the Guidelines commentary) throughout the Judgment generally.

**2d Circuit treatment:** I did not locate a Second Circuit case squarely addressing whether the implicit Murphy privilege survives an "answer truthfully" standard condition lacking express savings language (as opposed to the polygraph-specific condition, addressed in Johnson). This appears to be an under-litigated question in this circuit — likely because the § 5D1.3 application note already resolves the practical dispute for supervisees who know to invoke, and because most litigated Fifth Amendment challenges in this circuit have arisen in the polygraph context specifically (Johnson) rather than the generic truthfulness condition. **[VERIFY — this apparent gap should be flagged in the motion itself as an open question in this circuit, framed favorably: the absence of adverse Second Circuit authority narrowing Murphy's protection as applied to Standard Condition 4 means Murphy's default rule controls without contrary circuit gloss.]**

### B.2 Recommended relief for Standard Condition 4

Given Murphy and the § 5D1.3 application note, the client has a reasonable basis to request one of two forms of relief, in ascending order of assertiveness:
1. **Minimal/likely uncontested:** a clarifying amendment adding an express savings clause to Standard Condition 4 paralleling Special Condition (g)'s language (e.g., "the defendant shall not be deemed in violation of this condition for a legitimate invocation of the Fifth Amendment privilege"), on the theory that this merely codifies existing Guidelines commentary and Murphy doctrine and costs the government nothing to concede, while foreclosing any future dispute about whether Bernecky was on notice of the privilege's survival.
2. **More assertive:** press the point that because no such clause exists, and no Second Circuit case has read one in specifically for Standard Condition 4, there is ambiguity that should be resolved in the defendant's favor now, prophylactically, before supervision begins — consistent with the same front-end-protection-over-back-end-remedy logic driving the Special Condition (g) request (Part A.6).
