### 1(b). What I could confirm instead: Administrative Office of the U.S. Courts / Judicial Conference guidance on the "contribute to the cost" language

The Administrative Office of the U.S. Courts publishes the official commentary districts and probation offices use to administer this exact AO 245B condition language ("contribute to the cost of services rendered"). This guidance is not binding case law, but it is the **official interpretive source for the very form-language condition imposed on Bernecky**, and is highly persuasive/authoritative for a motion arguing that the condition as administered departs from the framework the condition's own drafters intended:

- **Source:** U.S. Courts, "Chapter 3: Mental Health Treatment (Probation and Supervised Release Conditions)," https://www.uscourts.gov/about-federal-courts/probation-and-pretrial-services/post-conviction-supervision/overview-probation-and-supervised-release-conditions/chapter-3-mental-health-treatment-probation-and-supervised-release
- **Materially identical language also appears at:** U.S. Courts, "Chapter 3: Substance Abuse Treatment, Testing, and Abstinence" (same URL family) — confirming this is a standardized cross-condition policy, not a one-off comment.
- **Substance (confirmed via multiple independent search snippets returning matching language, though the page itself could not be opened directly — [VERIFY BEFORE FILING] against the live page):**
  > "The wording of the requirement to contribute to the cost of treatment should not inadvertently delegate the establishment of the amount of the co-payment to the probation officer, and districts are encouraged by Judicial Conference policy to establish a court-approved sliding scale for treatment services that is responsive to changes in a defendant's financial circumstances."
  > "When evaluation and treatment services are ordered, defendants should be required to pay for these services to the degree that they are financially able, as the collection of reasonable co-payments for services meets both administrative and treatment objectives."

**Application to Bernecky:** This is the AO's own gloss on the identical "contribute to the cost of services rendered" language used in Special Conditions (b), (g), and (h) of Bernecky's Judgment (and the parallel "pay the cost of monitoring services" language in Special Condition (a)). It establishes two things directly useful to the motion: (1) **Judicial Conference policy itself calls for a court-approved sliding scale responsive to the defendant's ability to pay** — which does not exist anywhere in this case file (confirmed at intake: no CIMP fee schedule, no probation cost sheet, no billing document of any kind); and (2) the AO's own guidance warns against exactly what appears to have happened here — a bare "contribute to the cost" condition with no court-set amount or scale, leaving the amount to be set unilaterally by the probation officer or provider, which is functionally an unlawful delegation of a judicial sentencing function (the delegation theory the client independently raised as to Special Condition (b), citing "Balon," is reinforced by this AO guidance as to the fee-amount-setting function specifically, not just the treatment-rules function).

**Relief supported:** A request that the court either (i) vacate/modify the four fee-bearing conditions to require the probation office to develop and present a court-approved sliding-scale fee schedule tied to Bernecky's actual ability to pay before any "contribution" can be assessed, consistent with Judicial Conference policy, or (ii) at minimum require an on-the-record ability-to-pay finding before any dollar amount is set or collected under any of the four conditions.

### 1(c). General due process framing (statutory, not case-specific)

18 U.S.C. § 3583(d) requires that any discretionary condition (i) be "reasonably related" to the § 3553(a) factors and (ii) "involve no greater deprivation of liberty than is reasonably necessary." An open-ended financial obligation with no ceiling, no rate, and no ability-to-pay gate is difficult to reconcile with the "no greater deprivation than reasonably necessary" requirement, because the deprivation's actual magnitude is unknowable and unbounded on the face of the Judgment. This is a straightforward, low-risk textual argument that does not depend on any single case citation and should be the backbone of the fee argument regardless of whether Leone can be located.

## TASK 2 — Is the total absence of any dollar figure, rate, or payment procedure itself a due process/fair notice/vagueness problem?

I found the general vagueness-doctrine framework (verified as a correct, uncontroversial statement of law — this is black-letter due process doctrine, not a contested citation):

> A condition "must provide specific standards to avoid arbitrary and discriminatory enforcement," and a law/condition is void for vagueness if it is "so vague that men of common intelligence must necessarily guess at its meaning and differ as to its application."
(General due-process/vagueness doctrine, collected at Cornell LII, "Void for Vagueness and the Due Process Clause: Doctrine and Practice," https://www.law.cornell.edu/constitution-conan/amendment-5/void-for-vagueness-and-the-due-process-clause-doctrine-and-practice — secondary/annotative source, not itself binding authority, but a reliable restatement of settled doctrine. **[VERIFY current edition/section before citing.]**)

I was **not** able to locate a case squarely holding that a fee-bearing supervision condition lacking any dollar figure is unconstitutionally vague (as opposed to overbroad/an impermissible-delegation problem, which is the theory Task 1(b) and the client's own Balon theory for Special Condition (b) already target). My assessment: **the stronger, better-supported theory here is not "vagueness" in the classic notice-of-criminality sense (this is not a condition whose violation triggers revocation for guessing at its meaning — Bernecky is not being asked to comply with an ambiguous behavioral command), but rather (i) the delegation problem identified in Task 1(b), and (ii) a fair-notice-of-financial-exposure argument sounding in due process but framed through § 3583(d)'s "reasonably necessary" requirement rather than through vagueness doctrine proper.** I recommend the motion frame this as a due-process/§ 3583(d) argument, not a stand-alone vagueness claim, unless counsel independently locates a case squarely applying vagueness doctrine to an unquantified financial condition (I did not find one and could not confirm one exists).

**Relief supported:** Supports the request for a court-set dollar ceiling/rate/payment procedure as a condition of the fee-bearing conditions remaining in effect at all — i.e., an as-modified condition rather than outright vacatur, since (unlike Special Condition (i)) there is an ongoing programmatic justification for CIMP/treatment/polygraph costs generally.

## TASK 3 — Insurance-first sequencing and Medicaid balance-billing conflict

### 3(a). Insurance-first sequencing

I found no case or regulation directly requiring that private insurance be exhausted before a "contribute to the cost" supervision condition can be assessed. This appears to be an unaddressed gap in the case law generally, not something specific to this case file. The strongest hook remains the AO/Judicial Conference "sliding scale... responsive to changes in a defendant's financial circumstances" guidance discussed in Task 1(b) — a properly constructed sliding scale would necessarily account for any insurance offset, since a defendant's true out-of-pocket ability to pay is a function of what insurance already covers. **Recommend framing the insurance point as a component of the requested sliding-scale/ability-to-pay relief, not as an independent legal theory with its own case law**, since no such freestanding case law was located.

### 3(b). Medicaid balance-billing prohibition

This is the strongest, most concrete legal hook in this research track.

**Regulatory text (confirmed consistently across multiple independent sources — Cornell LII, eCFR.gov, govregs.com, LawServer — high confidence this text is accurate as currently codified):**

> **42 C.F.R. § 447.15 — Acceptance of State payment as payment in full.**
> "A State plan must provide that the Medicaid agency must limit participation in the Medicaid program to providers who accept, as payment in full, the amounts paid by the agency plus any deductible, coinsurance or copayment required by the plan to be paid by the individual... The provider may only deny services to any eligible individual on account of the individual's inability to pay the cost-sharing amount imposed by the plan..."

**Statutory hook / correction to the research-task's proposed citation:** The task prompt suggested **42 U.S.C. § 1396a(a)(25)** as the source of this rule. Based on my research, **§ 1396a(a)(25) is actually the Medicaid third-party-liability (TPL) provision** (requiring states to identify legally liable third parties — e.g., insurers — and seek reimbursement from them), **not** the anti-balance-billing/payment-in-full provision. The payment-in-full rule that actually does the work here is implemented at **42 C.F.R. § 447.15**, whose statutory authority traces to 42 U.S.C. §§ 1302 and 1396a generally (the Secretary's general rulemaking authority and the state-plan requirements provision), with related state-plan payment-methodology requirements appearing elsewhere in § 1396a(a) (e.g., the payment-methodology and provider-participation clauses, not specifically paragraph (a)(25)). **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — confirm the precise U.S.C. paragraph cross-reference for § 447.15's statutory basis before citing a specific "(a)(__)" paragraph number in the motion; cite 42 C.F.R. § 447.15 directly, which is the operative rule, and avoid over-specifying the exact U.S.C. subparagraph unless independently confirmed.]** Regardless of the precise cross-reference, the **substantive rule is solid and well-established**: Medicaid providers may not bill a Medicaid beneficiary beyond the plan's permitted cost-sharing amount for a Medicaid-covered service, and may not deny services for inability to pay that cost-sharing amount.

**Application to Bernecky:** If Bernecky is or becomes Medicaid-eligible (a realistic possibility given his financial precarity, described in the client-directives file — recently signed lease, suspended job, ten-year supervision term with four separate fee-bearing conditions) and if his sex-offense treatment, mental-health treatment, or (less likely) polygraph/CIMP services are billed through or covered by Medicaid, then a probation-imposed "contribute to the cost of services rendered" obligation **could require the provider to bill Bernecky an amount beyond what federal Medicaid law permits the provider to collect from him** — a direct conflict between a federal supervision condition and a federal Medicaid payment-in-full rule. This is a strong, mechanically clean argument: it does not depend on any contested case law, just the interaction of two federal rules.

**Relief supported:** Language in the modified conditions expressly stating that (i) the "contribution" obligation applies only to the extent not covered by insurance or Medicaid, and (ii) to the extent Bernecky is Medicaid-eligible and the service is Medicaid-covered, the condition may not be enforced in a manner that would require him to pay more than Medicaid's permitted cost-sharing amount, consistent with 42 C.F.R. § 447.15. **Flag for counsel:** whether Bernecky is actually Medicaid-eligible or Medicaid-enrolled is a factual question outside this research track's scope — this argument's practical force depends on that fact being true or at least plausible enough to warrant prophylactic clarifying language.

## TASK 4 — Nexus requirement for Special Condition (d) and reasonable-suspicion gating as a narrowing device

### 4(a). General nexus/tailoring standard (statutory + Second Circuit framing)

18 U.S.C. § 3583(d) requires discretionary conditions to be reasonably related to the nature and circumstances of the offense and the defendant's history and characteristics, and to involve no greater deprivation of liberty than reasonably necessary. Applied to Special Condition (d) ("In order to monitor the defendant's compliance with not buying or subscribing to online services that provide child pornography..."), the condition's own stated purpose (monitoring a *purchasing* behavior) has **no textual or factual anchor in the offense of conviction**, which — per the conditions-list intake — was **possession** of child pornography following a prior conviction for **possession and distribution** (18 U.S.C. § 2252A(a)(5)(B)), not purchase, subscription, or any transaction-based/commercial acquisition offense. A blanket, standing grant of access to "any requested personal and/or business financial information," untethered to any suspicion or event, is broader than the condition's own stated justification supports.

**United States v. Browder, 866 F.3d 504 (2d Cir. 2017)** — flagged in the case-file intake as likely central W.D.N.Y.-origin Second Circuit precedent on supervised-release condition scope. Based on search-summary corroboration (not independently opened — **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING]**), the case articulates a standard along these lines for monitoring-type conditions:

> A monitoring condition "must be narrowly tailored, and not sweep so broadly as to draw a wide swath of extraneous material into its net," and must bear "a close and substantial relation" to the government's legitimate interest — though it need not employ "the least intrusive means" available.

**Important nuance for this track:** A related search result (Federal Defenders of New York Second Circuit Blog, title: "Circuit affirms supervised-release condition authorizing suspicionless monitoring of defendant's internet use" — **[VERIFY — could not confirm which case this describes; may be Browder itself or a related computer-monitoring case]**) indicates the **Second Circuit has upheld suspicionless computer/internet monitoring conditions** in at least one instance. This cuts against arguing that reasonable suspicion is *constitutionally compelled* as a gating mechanism for Special Condition (d). **The honest framing for this motion, consistent with the client's own stated posture, is that reasonable-suspicion gating is a proposed narrowing/compromise the defense is offering as an acceptable middle ground — not a outcome the case law affirmatively requires.** That said, the "close and substantial relation to the government's interest" standard is still doing real work here: because the offense conduct was possession, not purchase, a financial-monitoring condition whose *stated purpose* is detecting *purchase* activity is not closely and substantially related to anything in this defendant's actual record — the government interest the condition invokes (preventing CP purchases) has no factual predicate as to Bernecky specifically. That gap is the strongest textual hook, independent of any suspicion-gating case law.

**Persuasive out-of-circuit support (use with caution — citation confidence is low):** A search result described **"United States v. Sterling"** as an Eighth Circuit case (cited inconsistently across searches — one synthesis gave "959 F.3d 855 (8th Cir. 2020)," which I could **not** independently corroborate in a follow-up targeted search, and other searches returned entirely different "Sterling" cases from the Fourth, Fifth, and Eleventh Circuits with unrelated subject matter) allegedly holding that a condition requiring disclosure of "any" financial information, where there was no evidence the defendant committed or was at risk of committing monetary crimes, was vague and overbroad under § 3583(d)(2) and U.S.S.G. § 5F1.5(b). **I cannot confirm this case exists with this citation or holding — treat as unconfirmed and do not cite without independent Westlaw/Lexis verification. [VERIFY BEFORE FILING — high risk this citation is a search-engine hallucination; if it cannot be independently located with matching facts, drop it entirely rather than risk citing a fabricated case.]** If verified, it would be strong out-of-circuit persuasive authority directly on point for both Special Condition (d) and Special Condition (i) (both involve "any" financial information without a demonstrated financial-crime risk specific to this defendant — although note Bernecky *does* have a documented predicate offense involving CP, just not a *purchase-based* one, so the analogy is imperfect and should be argued carefully, not as an exact factual match).

### 4(b). Reasonable-suspicion gating as a recognized narrowing device

The **strongest available support for reasonable-suspicion gating is not external case law but the Judgment's own internal structure**: Special Condition (a) (CIMP) already uses a suspicion-triggered escalation model for a closely related monitoring purpose — computer searches are triggered "as triggered by impermissible/suspicious activity," with continuous monitoring as the baseline but the more intrusive step (unannounced examination/seizure) gated by suspicion. This shows that **the same Judgment already accepts a suspicion-gated structure as an adequate and workable model for exactly this kind of compliance monitoring** in an analogous context. That internal consistency argument — "the court already built a reasonable-suspicion gate into the sister condition governing the same underlying concern (compliance monitoring for CP-related conduct); Special Condition (d) should use the same structure rather than blanket standing access" — is a clean, self-contained argument that does not depend on any external, unverified case citation, and I recommend leading with it.

**Relief supported:** Modify Special Condition (d) to require a reasonable-suspicion (or similar articulable-basis) trigger before Probation may request financial information, rather than open-ended standing access, mirroring the suspicion-gated structure already used in Special Condition (a) for computer examinations.

## TASK 5 — Standard for VACATING a moot/unnecessary condition under § 3583(e)(2) (Special Condition (i))

### United States v. Lussier, 104 F.3d 32 (2d Cir. 1997) — BINDING SECOND CIRCUIT PRECEDENT

This is the best-corroborated case citation in this entire research track — its citation, court, date, and holding were consistent across multiple independent search results (a law-firm blog title match, a case-summary aggregator, and a secondary legal-blog discussion), and the fact pattern (district court modifying a **restitution-related** condition of supervised release) is directly on point for Special Condition (i). **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — pull the actual opinion text to confirm pinpoint quotes below before using them in the motion; the quotes here are drawn from secondary paraphrase, not a directly-read opinion.]**

**Holding, per multiple corroborating sources:**
> Section 3583(e) "sets out the four ways that a district court — after considering the section 3553(a) factors — can subsequently alter the term or conditions of supervised release after a defendant has been initially sentenced." The court "recognizes that the 'sentencing court must be able to respond to changes in the [defendant's] circumstances as well as new ideas and methods of rehabilitation,'" but **new or changed circumstances are not a prerequisite to a district court's decision to modify the conditions of release** — changed circumstances "may in some instances justify a modification," but are not independently required.

**Binding/persuasive:** Binding Second Circuit authority (if the citation is confirmed as stated).

**Application to Bernecky:** Lussier establishes that the § 3583(e)(2) standard for modifying (and, by direct extension, for eliminating/vacating) a condition does **not** require the defendant to show some threshold, extraordinary change in circumstances — the court need only consider the enumerated § 3553(a) factors and decide the modification is warranted. This is actually a **more favorable standard for Bernecky's vacatur request than a "changed circumstances" framing would be** — counsel does not need to frame the request for Special Condition (i) as "circumstances have changed since 2018" (a framing that invites debate over whether the alleged full payment counts as a qualifying "change"), but can instead argue straightforwardly that, applying the § 3553(a) factors now, **continued imposition of an asset-restraint/disclosure condition whose sole stated purpose is "collection of any unpaid fine or restitution" is not reasonably related to any of the § 3553(a) purposes once there is no unpaid fine or restitution to collect** — the condition's own textual predicate is gone. Vacatur, not mere narrowing, is the correct remedy because the condition's stated purpose is fully satisfied/moot rather than merely excessive in scope (contrast with Special Condition (d), which should be *narrowed* because its purpose remains partially legitimate, just poorly tailored).

**Distinguishing vacatur from narrowing (an analytical framework, not itself a cited holding):** The two families of relief in this track are analytically different and should be argued as such:
- **Vacatur (Special Condition (i)):** appropriate where the condition's factual predicate (an unpaid balance) has ceased to exist — the condition has nothing left to attach to.
- **Narrowing (Special Condition (d), and to a lesser extent the four fee-bearing conditions):** appropriate where the condition's underlying purpose remains at least partially legitimate but the condition as written sweeps more broadly than that purpose justifies (no nexus to purchase-type conduct; no ability-to-pay/rate-setting mechanism).

**Critical outstanding factual gap (flagged, not resolved by this research track per the task instructions):** The premise for vacating Special Condition (i) — that the $500 restitution balance is paid in full — is a **factual question requiring independent confirmation** (BOP Inmate Financial Responsibility Program payment history, court financial/CM-ECF docket records showing satisfaction, or a probation confirmation letter). This research track does not attempt to resolve that fact; it is flagged in `client-directives.md` as an outstanding action item and must be nailed down with documentary proof before the vacatur argument is filed, since Lussier's forgiving standard for *modification* does not relieve counsel of the need for an accurate factual predicate when representing to the court that the restitution has, in fact, been paid.

## TASK 6 — U.S. Sentencing Commission's evidence-based supervision policy shift and supervision fees

I checked ussc.gov directly (via search, since direct page-fetch was unavailable) for the Commission's April 2025 supervised-release guideline amendments (the most significant recent USSC action on supervised release generally).

**Confirmed (via ussc.gov's own amendment/primer pages, corroborated across multiple listings — moderate-high confidence):**
- U.S.S.G. § 5D1.1 was amended to move away from automatic imposition of supervised release, directing courts to make an **individualized assessment** of whether further supervision is warranted at all, with reasons stated on the record.
- U.S.S.G. § 5D1.3 was amended so that discretionary conditions are **no longer treated as a standard package** — courts must **tailor** conditions to the individual defendant's offense, history, and the specific purposes of supervision, rather than imposing a boilerplate set.
- Early-termination provisions were also strengthened, encouraging courts to terminate supervision after one year of compliance where consistent with public safety.

**What I did NOT find:** Despite specifically searching for it, **I found no indication that the 2025 (or earlier) Sentencing Commission guideline amendments, primers, or policy statements specifically address supervision fees, cost-shifting of monitoring/treatment/polygraph costs to defendants, or financial "contribute to cost" conditions as a distinct topic.** The Commission's recent reform push is framed around the *scope, duration, and individualization* of supervision and its conditions generally, not the *financial burden* of complying with fee-bearing conditions specifically. **Do not overclaim a Sentencing Commission position on fees that does not exist in the current guidance** — the most this track can responsibly support is a general argument that the 2025 amendments reflect a broader institutional judgment that supervision conditions (including, by natural extension, fee-bearing ones) should be **individually tailored rather than imposed as undifferentiated boilerplate**, which is consistent with and reinforces the tailoring/ability-to-pay arguments above, but the Commission has not spoken to fees as such.

**Relief supported:** Usable as framing/context in the motion's introduction (the case-wide climate favoring individualized, non-boilerplate conditions) but should not be cited as if the Commission had issued a fee-specific policy statement, because it has not (so far as this search could determine).
