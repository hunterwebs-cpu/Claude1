# DEFENDANT'S EMERGENCY MOTION FOR A TEMPORARY RESTRAINING ORDER AND ORDER TO SHOW CAUSE WHY A PRELIMINARY INJUNCTION SHOULD NOT ISSUE PROHIBITING DISCLOSURE OF THE PRESENTENCE REPORT

Defendant Jeffrey Bernecky, proceeding *pro se*, respectfully moves this Court for a temporary restraining order, and for an order to show cause why a preliminary injunction should not issue, prohibiting the United States Probation Office from disclosing or transmitting Mr. Bernecky's presentence report ("the Report") to the New York State Board of Examiners of Sex Offenders, any county court, or any other third party, pending this Court's resolution of Mr. Bernecky's motion to modify Special Condition (e) of the Judgment. In support of this motion, Mr. Bernecky states as follows.


## INTRODUCTION AND STATEMENT OF EMERGENCY

Mr. Bernecky has just learned that a New York State risk-level classification hearing under the Sex Offender Registration Act is approaching. Special Condition (e) of the Judgment entered June 12, 2018 (Dkt. 28) purports to authorize the Probation Office to disclose Mr. Bernecky's presentence report to the New York State Board of Examiners of Sex Offenders, and further "to the county court and to the parties involved in the determination of the defendant's final classification level." Probation currently understands itself to be authorized, under that condition, to make that disclosure in connection with the approaching hearing.

To Mr. Bernecky's knowledge, the Report has not yet been disclosed to the Board, the county court, or any party to that proceeding. Mr. Bernecky is separately moving this Court, under 18 U.S.C. § 3583(e)(2) and Fed. R. Crim. P. 32.1(c), to vacate Special Condition (e)'s blanket disclosure authorization as inconsistent with the controlling standard this Circuit has already applied to exactly this kind of disclosure. *See United States v. Charmer Indus., Inc.*, 711 F.2d 1164 (2d Cir. 1983). That motion requires briefing and a decision this Court has not yet had the opportunity to make. What Mr. Bernecky asks for now is narrower and immediate: an order preserving the status quo — non-disclosure — until this Court can decide it.

This relief follows the same procedural path this Circuit's own controlling authority on presentence report disclosure took. In *Charmer Industries* itself, the defendant obtained an order to show cause and a temporary restraining order from the district court restraining the Probation Service and a third-party recipient from publishing, discussing, or using a presentence report, precisely so that the question of disclosure could be resolved before, not after, the report was used. 711 F.2d at 1168. Mr. Bernecky asks for nothing more than that same, well-established sequence.


## ARGUMENT

### I. This Court retains authority over its own presentence report, independent of any state proceeding.

A presentence report is a court document, prepared by the Probation Service as "an arm of th[e] [sentencing] court," and is "to be used by nonjudicial federal agencies and others only with the permission of the court." *Charmer Indus.*, 711 F.2d at 1169-70. The relief Mr. Bernecky seeks does not ask this Court to intervene in, restrain, or enjoin any proceeding of the state court or the Board of Examiners. It asks this Court to restrain its own Probation Office from transmitting a document that belongs to this Court, that this Court alone controls, and that this Court has not yet finished deciding whether to release. That authority does not depend on, and is not diminished by, the pendency of a state proceeding that may wish to receive the Report; it is this Court's own supervisory authority over its own record and its own officer.

### II. The requested relief satisfies the standard for a temporary restraining order and preliminary injunction.

The standard in this Circuit for injunctive relief requires a showing of (a) irreparable harm and (b) either (1) likelihood of success on the merits or (2) sufficiently serious questions going to the merits to make them a fair ground for litigation, together with a balance of hardships tipping decidedly toward the party requesting relief. *Jackson Dairy, Inc. v. H.P. Hood & Sons, Inc.*, 596 F.2d 70, 72 (2d Cir. 1979) (per curiam); *accord Charmer Indus.*, 711 F.2d at 1169 n.2 (applying the identical standard to a motion to enjoin presentence report disclosure). Each element is met here.

**A. Irreparable harm.** Disclosure of a presentence report's contents, once made, cannot be undone. In *Charmer Industries* itself, the district court found that release of a presentence report could cause irreparable injury to reputation and good will — a finding the reversal on appeal left undisturbed. 711 F.2d at 1169 & n.3. The same is true here, and more acutely so: a presentence report "may well contain hearsay statements that are not subject to cross-examination," *id.* at 1171, information provided in confidence by family members, treatment providers, and others, and details of "the defendant's character and personality" never tested by the rules of evidence that would apply at trial, *id.* at 1170-71. Once disclosed to a state Board, a county court, and "the parties involved" in a classification proceeding — a group Mr. Bernecky has no way to identify or limit in advance — that information cannot be recalled. No later ruling on the pending motion to modify Special Condition (e) can restore confidentiality already lost.

**B. Likelihood of success, or at minimum a serious question going to the merits.** A presentence report may be disclosed to a third party only on "the most compelling reasons," established through a "particularized showing" made at the time disclosure is actually sought — a standard the Second Circuit has described as approaching that required for the release of grand jury materials. *Charmer Indus.*, 711 F.2d at 1174-75. Special Condition (e) supplies no such showing. It is a blanket authorization entered at sentencing in 2018, years before any classification proceeding existed, addressed to no specific request and supported by no case-specific finding of compelling need. A standing authorization entered in advance cannot satisfy a standard that, by its own terms, requires an individualized showing made at the moment disclosure is sought. Mr. Bernecky's motion to modify Special Condition (e) is, at an absolute minimum, a serious question going to the merits — squarely presented by controlling Second Circuit authority that has never been reconciled with a condition like this one.

**C. Balance of hardships tips decidedly toward Mr. Bernecky.** A brief delay in transmitting a document that has already waited years costs the government nothing. Nothing about the state classification proceeding requires the Report specifically, as opposed to whatever other information the Board and county court already use to make risk-level determinations in cases without a disclosed federal presentence report. Mr. Bernecky, by contrast, loses the only chance he will ever have to prevent disclosure if the Report is transmitted before this Court rules.

**D. This is a prohibitory injunction preserving the status quo, not a mandatory one altering it — so the ordinary standard, not a heightened one, applies.** A "mandatory" injunction that alters the status quo by commanding an affirmative act "should issue only upon a clear showing that the moving party is entitled to the relief requested, or where extreme or very serious damage will result from a denial of preliminary relief." *Tom Doherty Assocs., Inc. v. Saban Entm't, Inc.*, 60 F.3d 27, 33-34 (2d Cir. 1995). The "typical" preliminary injunction, by contrast, is prohibitory and seeks only to maintain the status quo. *Id.* To Mr. Bernecky's knowledge, the Report has not yet been disclosed to the Board, the county court, or any party to the classification proceeding. An order maintaining that state of affairs until this Court rules on the pending motion is the paradigm prohibitory injunction — it changes nothing that has not already changed on its own. Notably, *Charmer Industries* itself had to satisfy the *heightened* mandatory-injunction showing, because the report there had already been disclosed and the requested relief required its return. 711 F.2d at 1168. Mr. Bernecky asks for less: an order that simply keeps things as they now stand.

**E. The public interest favors the requested order.** Rule 32's confidentiality framework, and the demanding standard *Charmer Industries* applies to any departure from it, exist precisely to prevent disclosure from outrunning judicial review. Enforcing that standard, rather than allowing Probation's own understanding of its authority to control before this Court has ruled, serves rather than disserves the public interest.


## CONCLUSION AND REQUESTED RELIEF

For the foregoing reasons, Mr. Bernecky respectfully requests that the Court:

1. Enter a temporary restraining order enjoining the United States Probation Office, and any other federal officer or agency acting on its behalf, from disclosing or transmitting Mr. Bernecky's presentence report to the New York State Board of Examiners of Sex Offenders, any county court, or any party to the pending classification proceeding, pending further order of this Court;

2. Enter an order to show cause why a preliminary injunction should not issue continuing that relief pending this Court's resolution of Mr. Bernecky's motion to modify Special Condition (e); and

3. Grant such other and further relief as the Court deems just and proper.
