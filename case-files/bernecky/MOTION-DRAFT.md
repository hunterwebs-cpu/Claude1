Defendant Jeffrey Bernecky, proceeding *pro se*, respectfully moves this Court, pursuant to 18 U.S.C. § 3583(e)(2) and Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 32.1(c), to modify the conditions of supervised release imposed by the Judgment entered June 12, 2018 (Dkt. 28). ## PRELIMINARY STATEMENT

One dispute in this motion already has a face, a name, and a closing window. A contact lens manufacturer is deciding, right now, whether Jeffrey Bernecky can keep a job he was hired to do — and it is not deciding based on anything he has done. It is deciding based on eleven words in a special condition of supervised release, "owned or operated by the defendant," which the employer read on its own initiative, reasonably understood might reach its own business computers, and has been unable to get anyone at the United States Probation Office to rule out. Mr. Bernecky called his probation officer to ask. The answer: it "should be fine so long as they have a way to monitor him" — an off-the-cuff reassurance, not a clarification of record. Probation has not called the employer since, and has not answered Mr. Bernecky's own emails. The job remains unresolved, and his ten-year term of supervision begins on or about July 27, 2026 — meaning he may walk out of Residential Reentry Center placement and directly into unemployment, in the same week, for a reason that has nothing to do with anything he has done and everything to do with a condition no one at Probation will actually construe. That is not the second chance the Second Chance Act and supervised release itself are meant to provide. It is very nearly the opposite of one.

This motion asks the Court to do one thing above every other: say, in writing, what the condition's own silence on employers already establishes — that Special Condition (a) confers no authority over an employer's systems at all, not an unclear or debatable authority, none. That single sentence is the difference between a job that survives the transition into supervision and one that does not. The remainder of this motion addresses ten further conditions where the same problem recurs in smaller form: language written broadly enough, or left administratively undefined enough, that Mr. Bernecky's liberty — and, in several instances, his family's ordinary life — depends on an assurance rather than an order.

This motion does not ask the Court to declare the June 2018 Judgment invalid, and it does not ask the Court to revisit the legality of any finding the sentencing court made, or did not make, eight years ago. It asks the Court to exercise the forward-looking modification authority 18 U.S.C. § 3583(e)(2) has always given it, informed by developments in the governing law since 2018 and by the U.S. Sentencing Commission's own November 2025 instruction that the commencement of supervision is precisely the moment a court should reassess whether its conditions still fit. *See* U.S.S.G. § 5D1.4(a) (2025). A condition's scope should not be worked out through informal, shifting assurances after a job is already in jeopardy. It should be settled now, in writing, while settling it still means something — for Probation's benefit, for any employer's benefit, and for Mr. Bernecky's own.

The motion proceeds in descending order of urgency. It leads with the employer dispute, because it is the most urgent, the most fully developed, and the one already causing concrete harm; it closes with several narrower requests, each capable of independent resolution. Throughout, the question is the same one the Sentencing Commission itself has now told courts to ask: not whether the 2018 Judgment was right when entered, but whether these conditions, read as they are actually being administered today, still do the job the law asks them to do.


## JURISDICTION AND STANDARD OF REVIEW

Title 18, United States Code, Section 3583(e)(2) provides that the Court "may . . . modify, reduce, or enlarge the conditions of supervised release, at any time prior to the expiration or termination of the term of supervised release, pursuant to the provisions of the Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure relating to the modification of probation." 18 U.S.C. § 3583(e)(2). The statute's own cross-reference to "the Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure relating to the modification of probation" identifies Rule 32.1(c) as the governing procedural vehicle.

The Second Circuit has confirmed that § 3583(e)(2) is a general, forward-looking tool, not one gated by a threshold showing of changed circumstances. *United States v. Lussier*, 104 F.3d 32, 36 (2d Cir. 1997) (quoting the Rule 32.1 Advisory Committee's Notes for the proposition that "the sentencing court must be able to respond to changes in the [defendant's] circumstances as well as new ideas and methods of rehabilitation"). At the same time, *Lussier* holds that § 3583(e)(2) does not reach the legality or validity of a condition as originally imposed — that is the office of direct appeal or a motion under 28 U.S.C. § 2255. *Id.* at 33. This motion is framed accordingly, throughout, as a request for present-tense clarification and reassessment of the scope of existing conditions — not as a claim that the 2018 Judgment was invalid when entered.

The Sentencing Commission's November 2025 amendment to U.S.S.G. § 5D1.4 confirms this is precisely the right moment to ask. Subsection (a) provides that "[a]t any time prior to the expiration or termination of the term of supervised release, the court may modify, reduce, or enlarge the conditions of supervised release whenever warranted by an individualized assessment of the appropriateness of existing conditions," *see* 18 U.S.C. § 3583(e)(2), and that "[t]he court is encouraged to conduct such an assessment in consultation with the probation officer after the defendant's release from imprisonment." U.S.S.G. § 5D1.4(a) (2025). Mr. Bernecky is, right now, in exactly that position.

Congress itself — not merely the Sentencing Commission — drew a sharp line between modification under § 3583(e)(2) and early termination under § 3583(e)(1). Early termination is available only "after the expiration of one year of supervised release." 18 U.S.C. § 3583(e)(1). Modification carries no such floor: it is available "at any time prior to the expiration or termination of the term of supervised release." *Id.* § 3583(e)(2). The Sentencing Commission's own guideline text tracks, rather than creates, that distinction — § 5D1.4(b)'s one-year figure expressly cross-cites § 3583(e)(1), while § 5D1.4(a)'s absence of any time-trigger cross-cites § 3583(e)(2). There is no procedural obstacle, and no reason, to wait: the harm described in Part A is already occurring before supervision has even begun, and a condition's meaning should not be left to informal, shifting reassurances worked out after the fact when it can be settled now, on the current record, for Probation's benefit, any employer's benefit, and Mr. Bernecky's own.


## STATEMENT OF FACTS

Mr. Bernecky was sentenced on June 12, 2018, by the Honorable David G. Larimer to 120 months' imprisonment followed by ten years of supervised release, following a guilty plea to possession of child pornography involving prepubescent minors, an offense that is, on the face of the Judgment, at least Mr. Bernecky's second child-pornography-related federal conviction. Since that conversation, Probation has done nothing to resolve the uncertainty it left in place. Mr. Bernecky has separately contacted both the employer's human resources department and Probation; human resources confirms that Probation has never contacted them, and the probation officer has not responded to any of Mr. Bernecky's own emails. Mr. Bernecky does not believe the employer will hold the position much longer, and he may become unemployed at the precise moment he transitions from Residential Reentry Center placement into supervision. Mr. Bernecky has signed a lease in reliance on stable income and is now in a financially precarious position. The problem is not confined to this one job: absent a clarification of the condition's scope, the identical silence will invite the identical concern from any future employer, for as long as Mr. Bernecky remains subject to the mandatory full-time-employment requirement of Standard Condition 7 — a requirement that this same silence is simultaneously capable of frustrating.

Two further factual developments, each concerning a different condition and postdating the 2018 sentencing, are addressed separately below because they raise a distinct set of issues from the employment dispute above. The first concerns Special Condition (c), the contact-with-minors condition addressed in Part E: while Mr. Bernecky was incarcerated, his sister had children, so that the specific nieces and nephews who are part of his family today did not exist — and could not have been the subject of any individualized finding — at the time of sentencing. The second concerns Special Condition (i), addressed in Part I: Mr. Bernecky believes the $500 restitution obligation imposed by the Judgment has been paid in full, a fact that, if confirmed by the record, moots the continuing need for that condition.


## ARGUMENT

### A. No Condition of This Judgment Authorizes an Occupational Restriction, and None Should Be Read to Impose One

**1. The word "employer" does not appear anywhere in the CIMP condition. That silence means there is no employer-facing restriction — not an ambiguous one, none at all — and a written order confirming that is all the relief this part of the motion seeks.**

Special Condition (a) says what it says: Mr. Bernecky must participate in CIMP monitoring, and Probation "is authorized to install any application as necessary to surveil all activity on computer(s) or connected device(s) owned or operated by the defendant." Nowhere in that sentence, or anywhere else in the condition, does the word "employer," "employment," or "workplace" appear. That silence is not incidental. The very same Judgment uses "employ-" language twice elsewhere — Standard Condition 7's full-time-employment requirement, and Special Condition (e)'s sex-offender-registration clause, which reaches any state "where the defendant . . . is employed" — showing the drafters knew exactly how to write "employer" into a condition when they meant to. They did not write it into Special Condition (a). No finding under U.S.S.G. § 5F1.5 appears anywhere in the Judgment, because none was ever made.

This is the ordinary, well-established interpretive canon of *expressio unius est exclusio alterius* — the expression of one thing is the exclusion of another — applied to the Judgment's own text, not merely asserted against it. The Supreme Court applies exactly this reasoning to a supervised-release statute in *Esteras v. United States*, 145 S. Ct. 2031 (2025): because Congress enumerated eight of the ten § 3553(a) factors a court may consider on revocation while omitting retribution, that omission was "[c]onspicuously missing" and compelled the conclusion that Congress meant to exclude it, not merely to leave it unaddressed. *Id.* The same logic controls here with even greater force, because it operates on a single, self-contained document rather than a cross-referenced statutory list: the Judgment itemizes, with real specificity, what Special Condition (a) reaches — "any computer, data storage device, or any internet capable device," and, separately, "computer(s) or connected device(s) owned or operated by the defendant" — and nowhere within that itemization does "employer" or any synonym for it appear, even though the same document reaches for that word without hesitation twice elsewhere. An itemized list that speaks with this much specificity, and that omits a category the drafters plainly knew how to include, does not leave that category's status unclear. It excludes it.

This canon has a real limit, and Mr. Bernecky's condition falls comfortably on the correct side of it. In *Chevron U.S.A. Inc. v. Echazabal*, 536 U.S. 73, 80-81 (2002), the Supreme Court held the canon "just fails to work" where the statutory list at issue used illustrative language — "may include" — rather than a genuinely closed enumeration, because an illustrative list does not purport to be exhaustive in the first place. Special Condition (a) contains no such illustrative language. It does not say devices "such as" or "including" those owned or operated by the defendant; it says "owned or operated by the defendant," full stop, and nothing more. Where *Echazabal*'s statute expressly disclaimed being a closed list, Bernecky's condition is one. The canon *Echazabal* found inapplicable on its own facts applies here precisely because this condition lacks the illustrative language that defeated it there.

This is precisely the confirmation the employer itself is asking for. The employer's concern is not that the condition clearly reaches its systems; it is that no one at Probation will say, one way or the other, that it does not. Mr. Bernecky is not asking this Court to resolve a genuinely open question of interpretation. He is asking the Court to put in writing what the condition's own silence already establishes: that Special Condition (a) confers no authority over the employer's systems, imposes no obligation on the employer to demonstrate anything about its own monitoring capability, and was never intended to reach this far. Once that is in writing, the employer has what it needs, and the job can proceed.

The authority for saying so plainly does not rest on the formalities governing how a new condition is created — it rests on the settled principle that the power to impose special conditions of supervised release "is vested exclusively in the district court." *United States v. Matta*, 777 F.3d 116, 122 (2d Cir. 2015). When a probation officer's administration of a condition — or, as here, an employer's understandable uncertainty about that administration — threatens to reach further than the condition the court actually imposed, the Second Circuit has not hesitated to say so: "[b]ecause such a requirement . . . would contradict, rather than execute, [the defendant's] sentence, the Probation Office was plainly without power to impose it." *United States v. Browder*, 866 F.3d 504, 514 (2d Cir. 2017). *Kunz* reaffirms the identical principle for the very CIMP boilerplate at issue here, construing an identical condition's privilege-protection clause as a real constraint on Probation, "not surplusage." *United States v. Kunz*, 68 F.4th 748, 766-67 (2d Cir. 2023). Because no provision of this Judgment authorizes reaching an employer's own systems, no one — not Probation, not an examiner, not an anxious but well-meaning employer — may treat the condition as if it did.[^1]

**2. Even if an occupational restriction were found to exist, no U.S.S.G. § 5F1.5 finding supports it, and Mr. Bernecky's own facts make the disproportion plain.**

Section 5F1.5(a) requires "a reasonably direct relationship . . . between the defendant's occupation, business, or profession and the conduct relevant to the offense of conviction," together with a necessity finding. This district's own recent case law treats even a mandatory employer-*notification* requirement as a *de facto* occupational restriction demanding that finding. *See United States v. Love*, No. 24-1706-cr, 2025 WL 1077176 (2d Cir. Apr. 8, 2025); *United States v. Jenkins*, 854 F.3d 181 (2d Cir. 2017); *United States v. Ruff*, No. 18-3247-cr (2d Cir. Nov. 14, 2019) (vacating outright); *United States v. Lombardi*, No. 17-2352-cr (2d Cir. Mar. 22, 2018) (remanding); *United States v. Piper*, No. 19-1058-cr (2d Cir. Mar. 30, 2020) (remanding).

If a mandatory *notification* clause is enough to trigger § 5F1.5 in that line of cases, a condition that has already produced, in practice, a considerably more burdensome and textually unsupported capability question is at least as strong a candidate for the same treatment — particularly because Mr. Bernecky's condition contains no notification requirement at all; he disclosed voluntarily, going further than the condition required.

*United States v. Brown*, 173 F.4th 45 (2d Cir. 2026), does not compel a different result, and the differences are not cosmetic. Brown's claim was that the condition functioned as a *compelled* notification requirement; Mr. Bernecky does not object to notification — he already made it, voluntarily, and it was accepted. Brown's condition also contained its own "as permitted by the employer" clause — a built-in consent gate Mr. Bernecky's condition lacks entirely — and Brown's occupational-restriction holding arose on plain-error review, a considerably more forgiving posture than the de novo consideration this motion invites. The disproportion is sharpened by this specific job: the position requires no public internet access at all, and the employer has already offered an internet-disabled internal account. CIMP's stated purpose — monitoring internet-facilitated risk — has essentially no application here, yet Mr. Bernecky remains in employment limbo while separately required, under Standard Condition 7, to maintain full-time work.

**3. Nothing in the Judgment authorizes reaching a non-party's systems, and Mr. Bernecky may say so in his own right.**

Leaving this to an employer's good-faith guesswork and a probation officer's off-the-cuff reassurance is not a fix; only a written order is. A materially identical condition produced identical confusion in *United States v. Mike*, 632 F.3d 686, 696-97 (10th Cir. 2011), where the government itself argued the condition "cannot be read fairly" to reach an employer's computers, and the Tenth Circuit's answer was not to leave the question open but to remand with instructions that the condition be clarified so no one would have to guess — expressly directing that "[i]f on remand the court decides that it intends for the computer monitoring conditions to apply to computers owned by [the defendant's] employers, it must make the necessary findings" under the same occupational-restriction framework discussed in Part A.2, above. *Id.*; *see also United States v. Matteson*, 327 F. App'x 791 (10th Cir. 2009). That is exactly the order Mr. Bernecky requests here.

Probation's own conduct since the employer's concern first surfaced has made a written order more necessary, not less: an informal comment that things "should be fine so long as they have a way to monitor him" is not a clarification of record and has not functioned as one — Probation has not followed up with the employer, and has not responded to Mr. Bernecky's own inquiries. Whatever authority the CIMP condition confers, it does not extend to an employer's own systems, and Mr. Bernecky may say so in his own right without needing to assert his employer's separate interests. His own injury — jeopardized employment, a live risk of a violation finding premised on an unwritten and shifting understanding of his own condition — "seeks to vindicate [his] own constitutional interests," not the employer's. *Bond v. United States*, 564 U.S. 211, 220 (2011). Should the Court view the matter differently, ordinary third-party standing doctrine independently supports this claim, given the close relationship between his own compliance and the employer's cooperation and the genuine hindrance an employer faces litigating its own interests inside someone else's federal criminal case. *See Powers v. Ohio*, 499 U.S. 400 (1991); *Kowalski v. Tesmer*, 543 U.S. 125 (2004); *Singleton v. Wulff*, 428 U.S. 106 (1976); *Warth v. Seldin*, 422 U.S. 490 (1975).

The same third-party-premises problem recurs elsewhere in the Judgment. Standard Condition 5 requires Probation's approval of Mr. Bernecky's residence; to the extent that is a standardless veto rather than simple verification of where he lives, it raises the same delegation concern. Standard Condition 6 permits Probation to visit Mr. Bernecky "at any time at [his] home or elsewhere" and to seize any prohibited item in plain view; at a workplace he does not own, lease, or control, he has no authority to "permit" a search of property that is not his to permit, and the condition does not address an employer's own security or contractual constraints. Both should be clarified consistently with this Part: neither confers any authority over property belonging to someone other than Mr. Bernecky.

**4. *Kunz* already supplies the interpretive method that resolves this dispute; a CPAP machine and a family car prove why.**

The Second Circuit has already told this district how to read CIMP conditions like this one. Construing the identical Western District of New York boilerplate at issue here, the Second Circuit refused a literal reading of the condition's "use . . . encryption" language, holding it "would be absurd to interpret" Probation as requiring permission every time a supervisee visits an ordinary "https" website or uses an encrypted messaging service. *Kunz*, 68 F.4th at 764-65. The court explained that "a sensible reading of the restrictions neutralizes the most troubling of those concerns." *Id.* at 754-55. This is not a novel interpretive tool — courts do not read plain condition language to compel "the impossible," *id.* at 763 (citing *United States v. Johnson*, 446 F.3d 272, 281 (2d Cir. 2006)), and the same absurdity-avoidance principle runs through the general canon of construction. *See Public Citizen v. U.S. Dep't of Justice*, 491 U.S. 440, 454-55 (1989); *Griffin v. Oceanic Contractors, Inc.*, 458 U.S. 564, 571 (1982); *Green v. Bock Laundry Mach. Co.*, 490 U.S. 504, 509 (1989).

Apply that same tool here, and the absurdity of the broadest possible literal reading is not hard to find. Consider Mr. Bernecky's CPAP machine. Like most sleep-apnea therapy devices sold today, a modern CPAP unit contains a built-in cellular or Wi-Fi modem whose sole function is to upload therapy-compliance data to the manufacturer and treating physician while Mr. Bernecky sleeps. It is, in every literal sense, an "internet capable device." It is also a single-purpose medical instrument with no web browser, no application store, and no capacity whatsoever to locate, store, or display an image of any kind. No one — not the government, not Probation, not this Court — would seriously suggest that a machine whose only "online" activity is reporting a patient's breathing pattern to his pulmonologist must be enrolled in the Computer and Internet Monitoring Program. Yet nothing in the condition's text says otherwise.

Consider, too, the paradigmatic thing a person "operates": not a laptop, not a phone — a vehicle. One uses a laptop; one operates a car. *See State v. Henderson*, 907 N.W.2d 623 (Minn. 2018) (construing "operate" a motor vehicle to mean "any act that causes a motor vehicle to function or controls the functioning of the motor vehicle"). A modern car is, by any ordinary understanding, an "internet capable device": today's vehicles routinely ship with built-in cellular telematics — OnStar, FordPass Connect, Tesla's own connectivity systems — keeping the car in constant contact with a manufacturer's network. If the condition's broadest reading controls, the very word the district court chose — "operated" — reaches its most natural referent first: Mr. Bernecky's car. Nobody believes that either. The government cannot have it both ways. Either it accepts the absurd consequence that a CPAP machine and a family sedan must be surveilled like a personal computer, or it concedes what *Kunz* already establishes: the condition's real, operative boundary is functional capability and nexus to the conduct that brought Mr. Bernecky here — the capacity to access, store, or display images — not literal internet-connectedness or literal operation.

That boundary is not foreign to the condition's own text; it is already there, applied inconsistently. The CIMP condition opens with a capability qualifier — "any internet capable device" — but the separate monitoring-authorization clause drops that qualifier and instead reaches any device "owned or operated by the defendant." Restoring internal consistency to the condition's own drafting logic, calibrated to the capability actually implicated by this offense, is not a request to import a foreign concept; it is a request that the condition mean, throughout, what its own opening sentence already says. Multiple circuits have accepted device-capability thresholds of exactly this kind in place of undifferentiated "any device" language. *E.g.*, *United States v. Rath*, No. 14-51059, 2015 WL 3559160 (5th Cir. June 8, 2015) (device permitted for school or work "as long as the computer is not capable of Internet access"); *United States v. Ullman*, No. 14-1414, 2015 WL 3559221 (10th Cir. June 8, 2015) ("devices without Internet access, such as gaming systems, are not restricted").

Nearly all offenses of this kind occur in one place — a private residence — on devices capable of locating, retrieving, and displaying image content. The crime is not committed at a coffee shop, or while walking through a grocery store, or on a coworker's telephone. Monitoring authority should track that reality. It is entirely sufficient, and considerably less intrusive, to prohibit Mr. Bernecky outright from using a device he does not own, rather than to authorize monitoring, inspection, or capability-parity demands directed at a third party's property. A flat prohibition on using another person's device achieves the government's interest at zero cost to any non-party, and avoids the *ultra vires* and standing problems discussed above entirely.

**5. Requested relief.**

The Court should enter an order clarifying and, to the extent necessary, modifying Special Condition (a) to provide: (i) that Special Condition (a) confers no authority over an employer's own systems and imposes no obligation on any employer to demonstrate anything about its own monitoring capability — there is no employer monitoring under this condition, full stop, and Mr. Bernecky's voluntary disclosure of his conviction to the employer, which he has already made, is more than the condition could ever require of him with respect to an employer; (ii) that the devices actually subject to monitoring are limited to those Mr. Bernecky owns and keeps or brings to his residence that are capable of accessing, storing, or displaying image content, consistent with the condition's own "internet capable device" language; and (iii) that Mr. Bernecky is prohibited from using another person's personal device, in lieu of any monitoring or capability-parity demand directed at that person's property. The clarification requested in (i) is the relief that matters most, and it is the only relief this motion seeks on this point: Mr. Bernecky should never have to choose between disclosing his conviction to an employer and asking that employer to permit monitoring of its own systems, because neither is owed at all.


### B. The Court Should Narrow CIMP's Monitoring Method to Exclude Indiscriminate Keystroke-Level Logging

The CIMP condition already contains a privilege-protection clause: monitoring "shall be designed to avoid, as much as possible, reading any privileged information or any private material that is not illegal." *Kunz* construed this identical clause and held it a real, judicially enforceable constraint on Probation — it "constrains the supervisors in their surveillance . . . by requiring them not to abuse their power and access," not surplusage. 68 F.4th at 766-67. Keystroke-level logging, which by definition captures every character typed — including privileged attorney-client communications, before any determination of privilege is even possible — cannot be reconciled with a monitoring method the condition's own text requires to avoid reading such material. The point is not that no privilege protection exists; it is that keystroke-level capture, as a category, is incapable of honoring the protection the Judgment already promises.

This Circuit's own case law concerning this exact offense type supports the narrowing directly. In *United States v. Sofsky*, 287 F.3d 122, 126-27 (2d Cir. 2002), this Circuit held that "a more focused restriction, limited to pornography sites and images," is an adequate, narrower alternative for an image-based offense. And in *United States v. Lifshitz*, 369 F.3d 173, 192 (2d Cir. 2004), this Circuit itself observed that constant, content-blind monitoring "might be more like searching [a defendant's] diary . . . than . . . the highly targeted diagnosis accomplished by drug testing," and — on remand — specifically approved keyword- or phrase-triggered monitoring as a narrower alternative to "recording all varieties of computer-related activity." *Id.* at 193 n.11. Mr. Bernecky's offense is entirely pictorial; unlike a computer-fraud or communications-based offense, in which the content of what was typed is itself the legally significant conduct, nothing about image-based possession justifies capturing the content of everything he writes.

The Court should modify Special Condition (a) to require that CIMP be implemented through a method — domain- or file-type review, image-hash scanning, or keyword-triggered flagging of the kind *Lifshitz* itself approved — that does not indiscriminately capture privileged or non-image content, rather than through full keystroke capture.


### C. The Treatment Provider's Own Rules Cannot Exceed What the Court Itself Ordered

Special Condition (b) requires Mr. Bernecky to "follow the rules and regulations" of a sex-offense-specific treatment program, without any court-set limit on what those rules may contain. Only the sentencing court may impose restrictions on a defendant's liberty as a condition of supervised release; that authority cannot be delegated wholesale to a private provider. *Matta*, 777 F.3d at 122. The controlling illustration is squarely on point: in *Browder*, a treatment provider's own participant agreement required advance permission before *any* contact with minors, including the defendant's own children — exceeding the sentence the court had actually imposed, which expressly excluded his own children from that restriction. The Second Circuit held the provider's rule unenforceable: "the Probation Office was plainly without power to impose it." 866 F.3d at 514. Mr. Bernecky's concern is the same in kind: a program category the court did order, whose specific, unreviewable content is set entirely by the provider.

The Court should enter clarifying language establishing that Mr. Bernecky cannot be found in violation of supervised release for declining to comply with a provider rule that exceeds the scope of what this Court itself ordered.

One further point belongs here, offered as principle rather than as an independent legal claim. Cognitive-behavioral therapy is a legitimate and valuable treatment modality. Using continuous CIMP surveillance data as raw material for a provider's effort to reshape a specific thought pattern is a different thing, and it is not what CBT is meant to be used for. The Constitution has long recognized that "no official . . . can prescribe what shall be orthodox . . . in matters of opinion." *West Virginia State Bd. of Educ. v. Barnette*, 319 U.S. 624, 642 (1943). Mr. Bernecky does not ask the Court to revive a possessory right to obscene material of the kind *Stanley v. Georgia*, 394 U.S. 557 (1969), addressed and *Osborne v. Ohio*, 495 U.S. 103 (1990), correctly declined to extend to child pornography, given the harm to real children and the government's interest in destroying the market for such material. His concern is different: no court has ever authorized this specific surveillance-to-treatment mechanism, and that lack of authorization — not a claim about the content of his thoughts — is what the delegation principle set out above already reaches.


### D. The Alcohol-Abstinence Requirement Should Be Physician-Gated, Not a Blanket Rule

Special Condition (h) requires Mr. Bernecky to participate in mental health treatment and, "while in treatment or taking psychotropic medication," to abstain from alcohol entirely. Mr. Bernecky does not object to mental health treatment. He objects to a blanket abstinence rule that applies regardless of whether any physician has ever determined that alcohol actually interacts with a medication or treatment he is receiving.

This Circuit has vacated a comparable blanket alcohol restriction for lack of the individualized nexus § 3583(d) requires. *United States v. Betts*, 886 F.3d 198 (2d Cir. 2018) (vacating a total alcohol ban as "not reasonably related to any of the factors outlined in Section 5D1.3(b)," where neither the offense nor any violation conduct involved alcohol and no evidence of alcohol abuse was in the record, and remanding "for further consideration of whether it is necessary to impose any special conditions related to alcohol use"). The Second Circuit has since applied the identical rule to a materially indistinguishable "abstain from alcohol during treatment" clause, rejecting the government's argument that the provision could simply be reimposed without an individualized assessment. *See United States v. Rodriguez*, No. 22-1820-cr (2d Cir. Dec. 7, 2023) (summary order).

The clause's own disjunctive structure sharpens the problem independently of *Betts* and *Rodriguez*: it applies "while in treatment **or** taking psychotropic medication," which means abstinence is triggered by talk therapy alone, with no medication involved at all — a circumstance in which no possible medication-interaction rationale for an alcohol restriction exists, even in principle. And even within the medication branch, the clinical premise is overbroad: alcohol-interaction risk varies substantially by drug class (some, such as MAOIs, carry a specific and serious risk; many others carry no comparable contraindication), so treating "psychotropic medication" as a single, uniform risk category does not track clinical reality. Notably, this exact condition template — including the identical alcohol clause — is the district's own standard-form mental-health-treatment condition, adopted in the wake of the Western District of New York's own remand in *Browder*; it was not the product of any individualized finding specific to Mr. Bernecky.

The Court should modify Special Condition (h) to provide that the alcohol-abstinence requirement applies only when, and for as long as, a treating physician determines that alcohol use is contraindicated by a specific medication or treatment Mr. Bernecky is then receiving — not as a categorical rule imposed independent of any such medical determination. This tracks existing federal supervision practice, under which medication-management judgment calls are already routed to the prescribing physician rather than decided by Probation or the Court.


### E. The Family-Contact Condition, as Written, Functions as a *De Facto* Ban on Ordinary Family Life

Special Condition (c) prohibits "deliberate contact with any child under 18 years of age, excluding his biological or adopted children." Mr. Bernecky has no biological or adopted children; the only carve-out the condition provides has always been, and remains, a null set.

The practical effect of that null carve-out is not narrow. It is not simply that Mr. Bernecky cannot see a specific niece or nephew without advance approval; it is that he cannot attend an ordinary family gathering at all, because he cannot know in advance whether a minor family member will be present, and the condition gives him no way to find out safely. If Mr. Bernecky stops by his mother's house for Christmas and his sister arrives with her children, he is, on the condition's literal terms, already in violation unless he leaves immediately — not because anyone has ever found him a danger to those children, but because the condition does not distinguish an unplanned family visit from the conduct the sentencing court was actually trying to prevent. The same is true if grandchildren stop by unannounced. No finding anywhere in this Judgment addresses whether Mr. Bernecky poses a physical danger to any specific family member, or whether ordinary, incidental presence at family events — as opposed to some other kind of contact — presents any risk at all. In practice, the condition forces Mr. Bernecky to choose between avoiding his own family's ordinary life altogether and risking a violation for conduct no one has ever evaluated.

This is precisely the kind of intrusion into familial association the Constitution protects against absent an individualized basis. Familial-association rights are not confined to the nuclear family; they extend to the extended-family relationships — grandparent, sibling, cousin — that give this condition's illusory carve-out its bite. *See Moore v. City of East Cleveland*, 431 U.S. 494 (1977) (protecting the choice to live with extended family, and requiring "careful scrutiny" of any government intrusion on it). This district's own case law requires exactly that individualized basis before a restriction of this breadth may stand. *United States v. Myers*, 426 F.3d 117, 129-30 (2d Cir. 2005) (heightened scrutiny and an individualized record required where a condition implicates a fundamental familial-association interest; vacating a contact-with-own-child condition imposed on the identical record-silence this Judgment shares). *United States v. Bleau*, 930 F.3d 35, 42-43 (2d Cir. 2019), confirms a court must explain the necessity of a contact-with-minors condition unless obvious from the record, and vacated one where the offense did not involve inappropriate contact with minors. Candidly, Mr. Bernecky's record is not *Bleau*'s clean-record case — this is at least his second child-pornography-related conviction, and this motion does not ask the Court to overlook that. It asks instead for a structure that accounts for it: contact with minor family members permitted in the presence of a knowledgeable adult — his mother, father, or sister, all of whom already know of his conviction — so an ordinary family gathering does not require case-by-case advance approval, or an unplanned departure, every time a minor family member happens to be there. Other circuits recognize this "knowledgeable adult" structure as an accepted, workable condition that reads restrictions on contact with minors "to exclude incidental encounters." *United States v. DaSilva*, 844 F.3d 8 (1st Cir. 2016).

Mr. Bernecky's sister having children during his incarceration is not, by itself, the whole of this argument, but it is a genuine changed circumstance under *Lussier*'s own terms: the specific family members whose presence at ordinary gatherings gives rise to this problem did not exist, and could not have been the subject of any individualized finding, at the time of sentencing.

Finally, this condition should be clarified, consistent with the theme of Part A above, to state expressly that it creates no occupational restriction: ordinary, incidental workplace contact with a minor — a coworker who is seventeen, a customer in the course of Mr. Bernecky's duties — is not "deliberate contact" requiring advance approval or removal from the situation.


### F. The Polygraph Condition Requires Real Procedural Protection, Not Merely a Later Opportunity to Object

**1. The Judgment's model does not resolve Mr. Bernecky's actual objection.** The Judgment lets Mr. Bernecky challenge, after the fact, the *use* of a polygraph statement as a Fifth Amendment violation; it does not let him decline to answer in the first instance. *United States v. Johnson*, 446 F.3d 272 (2d Cir. 2006), upheld exactly that structure — but only against the narrow immunity/waiver theory Johnson raised, on facts specific to his own documented history. Johnson never addressed either of Mr. Bernecky's actual objections: that whether a specific question is privileged is a pure question of law a layperson cannot reliably apply under pressure in real time, or that a blanket, prospective refusal to answer *any* question absent protections is materially different from answering and later challenging use of the answer. Properly understood, *Minnesota v. Murphy*'s "classic penalty situation" doctrine does the real work: if the State, "either expressly or by implication, asserts that invocation of the privilege would lead to revocation," the resulting statement is compelled. 465 U.S. 420, 435 (1984). A condition requiring Mr. Bernecky to "answer the questions posed" — full stop, before any privilege determination — asserts exactly that. *United States v. Von Behren*, 822 F.3d 1139, 1150-51 (10th Cir. 2016), applies this doctrine to a materially similar sex-offense polygraph and holds a threat of program termination and revocation for refusing incriminating questions unconstitutional compulsion. The privilege reaches not only direct confessions but any answer furnishing "a link in the chain of evidence" needed to prosecute. *Hoffman v. United States*, 341 U.S. 479, 486 (1951). This is at least his second child-pornography-related conviction, so any further compelled disclosure carries materially higher stakes than for a first-time offender — weighing in favor of the requested protections, not against them.

**2. The requested protections.** Mr. Bernecky requests that, until and unless each of the following is in place, he is entitled to decline to answer any polygraph or other quasi-scientific examination question:

  (i) appointed counsel present before, during, and after any such examination;

  (ii) counsel afforded adequate time, per question, to assess whether invocation is warranted — a determination of law, not one a layperson can be expected to make correctly under pressure;

  (iii) the examiner's credentials established to a degree sufficient to support a *Daubert* challenge, reaching separately the reliability of any computerized voice stress analyzer examination; and

  (iv) no disclosure of any examination-derived information to any person or agency without leave of court.

No federal court appears to have addressed a request of precisely this kind, and that should be said plainly rather than disguised as settled law. This is best presented as a discretionary safeguard this Court has authority to craft under 18 U.S.C. § 3583(d) — not as a claim that the polygraph room is custodial and requires Miranda-style protection, a framing that would invite an unfavorable comparison to authority rejecting a counsel-presence claim on custodial-interrogation grounds. *See J.B. v. New Jersey State Parole Bd.*, 229 N.J. 21 (2017). It is enough that whether a question is privileged is a legal judgment, and that existing federal supervision policy already treats this determination as too delicate to leave to a probation officer alone, directing that uncertain invocations be referred to the court rather than resolved unilaterally. Guide to Judiciary Policy, Vol. 8E, § 190.45. If the Court is unwilling to order counsel's presence throughout, Mr. Bernecky requests, in descending order, consultation access at any point during the exam, modeled on the grand jury practice of permitting a witness to step outside to consult counsel, *see United States v. Mandujano*, 425 U.S. 564 (1976); or, at minimum, advance written review of the question list by counsel, with any disputed question referred to the Court rather than resolved by the examiner.

**3. This same-case continuation is different from a request to extend the right to a new offense, and this Court should say so before the government does.** The Sixth Amendment's critical-stage doctrine is two-step: attachment, then critical-stage analysis. *Rothgery v. Gillespie County*, 554 U.S. 191 (2008). Attachment is offense-specific, *see Texas v. Cobb*, 532 U.S. 162 (2001); *McNeil v. Wisconsin*, 501 U.S. 171 (1991); *Kirby v. Illinois*, 406 U.S. 682 (1972), which forecloses this argument only as to a wholly new, uncharged offense — where the right has never attached at all. It does not answer the different question here: whether the right persists through the ongoing supervision phase of the *same* case in which it already attached at indictment. Supervision is a continuing consequence of the same conviction, not a new offense. The harder authority on that question is *Pennsylvania v. Finley*, 481 U.S. 551 (1987), and the *Gagnon v. Scarpelli*, 411 U.S. 778 (1973), and *Morrissey v. Brewer*, 408 U.S. 471 (1972), line, which treats post-conviction proceedings as due process rather than Sixth Amendment territory, and must be confronted directly rather than left for the government to raise first. *Estelle v. Smith*, 451 U.S. 454 (1981), is the best affirmative analogy: a compelled psychiatric examination was a critical stage because it was tied to a still-pending sentencing determination. *Finley*'s finality concern — no Sixth Amendment right survives "exhaustion of the appellate process" — never arose in *Estelle*, because that proceeding, unlike an ordinary closed conviction, was still pending. Mr. Bernecky's supervision is different in kind for the same reason: the sentencing court retains live, ongoing jurisdiction over it, which is the entire premise of this motion.

**4. A separate, independent basis supports appointing counsel for this motion.** 18 U.S.C. § 3006A(a)(1)(E) lists a person who "faces modification . . . of a condition . . . of a term of supervised release" among the categories for which representation "shall be provided" to a financially eligible person. Mr. Bernecky requests that the Court appoint counsel under this provision for purposes of this motion, independent of how the polygraph-specific request above is resolved.

**5. There is, independently, no factual predicate for the polygraph condition as applied to Mr. Bernecky.** He has never been found dishonest in treatment or supervision. A condition premised on presumed dishonesty, in the absence of any actual instance of it, lacks the nexus § 3583(d)'s reasonably-related standard requires.


### G. The Judgment's Pre-Authorization of Presentence Report Disclosure Violates *Charmer Industries* and Should Be Vacated

Special Condition (e) authorizes release of Mr. Bernecky's presentence report to the New York State Board of Examiners of Sex Offenders, and further disclosure "to the county court and to the parties involved in the determination of the defendant's final classification level." This Circuit places the burden of demonstrating a "compelling" and "particularized" need for disclosure of a presentence report on the party seeking it, at the time disclosure is sought — not on a standing, Judgment-level authorization entered years in advance with no opportunity for the defendant to be heard on the question when disclosure actually occurs. *See United States v. Charmer Indus., Inc.*, 711 F.2d 1164 (2d Cir. 1983). A blanket advance waiver of that particularized-need showing is difficult to reconcile with a standard that expressly places the burden on the party requesting disclosure and requires a case-specific showing at the time disclosure is sought.

The remaining registration language in this same condition is, independently, surplusage: Mandatory Condition 6 already requires registration under the Sex Offender Registration and Notification Act, 34 U.S.C. § 20901 *et seq.* The Court should strike the blanket PSR pre-authorization and the duplicative registration language, without prejudice to any case-specific disclosure request properly made, with notice to Mr. Bernecky, at the time such a request actually arises.


### H. The Fee-Bearing Conditions Lack Any Payment Methodology or Ability-to-Pay Mechanism

Four separate conditions — CIMP monitoring, sex-offense treatment, polygraph testing, and mental health treatment — require Mr. Bernecky to pay or "contribute to the cost of services rendered," without a dollar figure, a rate-setting procedure, or any mechanism accounting for ability to pay. The defect is structural, not a question of amount: nothing tells Mr. Bernecky, or Probation, how much is expected, whether a sliding scale applies, or what happens if he genuinely cannot pay. Judicial Conference policy itself directs that fee language "should not inadvertently delegate the establishment of the amount of the co-payment to the probation officer," and encourages a "court-approved sliding scale . . . responsive to changes in a defendant's financial circumstances." No such scale, schedule, or procedure appears anywhere in this record.

*Bearden v. Georgia*, 461 U.S. 660 (1983), requires a willfulness and bona-fide-efforts inquiry before nonpayment of a financial obligation may be treated as a basis for revocation; nothing in the record suggests that principle has been extended, in this Judgment, to these four "contribute to the cost" conditions. To the extent Mr. Bernecky is or becomes Medicaid-eligible and any of these services are Medicaid-covered, a "contribute to the cost" obligation risks conflicting outright with the federal rule that a Medicaid provider may not bill a beneficiary beyond the plan's permitted cost-sharing amount. 42 C.F.R. § 447.15.

The Court should modify each of the four conditions to state expressly that (i) any fee obligation is subject to Mr. Bernecky's demonstrated ability to pay, pursuant to a court-approved sliding scale, (ii) insurance or Medicaid coverage, where applicable, applies before any personal contribution is assessed, and (iii) inability to pay, standing alone, is not a basis for a violation finding absent the willfulness inquiry *Bearden* requires.


### I. Two Financial-Disclosure Conditions Should Be Narrowed or Vacated as No Longer Necessary

Special Condition (d) grants Probation blanket access to Mr. Bernecky's personal and business financial information "to monitor . . . compliance with not buying or subscribing to online services that provide child pornography." Mr. Bernecky has never been alleged to have purchased child pornography; his offense of conviction is possession. A financial-monitoring condition whose stated purpose is detecting purchase-type conduct has no factual anchor in a record that involves none. The condition should be modified to require a reasonable-suspicion predicate before financial information is accessed — a structure the Judgment already uses for the closely related purpose of computer monitoring itself, where examinations are triggered "as triggered by impermissible/suspicious activity" rather than conducted on a standing basis. Special Condition (d) should track that same, internally consistent model.

Special Condition (i) grants similarly broad financial-disclosure and asset-restraint authority, expressly tied to "the collection of any unpaid fine or restitution." Mr. Bernecky believes the $500 restitution obligation has been paid in full. If the record confirms this, the condition's predicate no longer exists, and *Lussier* confirms no separate showing of changed circumstances is required to vacate a condition whose stated purpose has been fully satisfied. 104 F.3d at 36.


### J. The No-Communication-With-Felons Condition Should Be Narrowed to Avoid Sweeping in Ordinary, Protected Conduct

Standard Condition 8 bars Mr. Bernecky from "communicat[ing] or interact[ing]" with anyone known to have a felony conviction, absent advance permission. As written, this is a communication ban, not merely an association restriction — the condition uses both words disjunctively, and "communicate" reaches a single, momentary exchange in a way "associate" does not — and it reaches core protected conduct with no incidental-contact or knowledge-timing exception: requesting an autograph from a public figure with a felony conviction, attending a rally advocating for felon voting rights, or exchanging ordinary pleasantries with a coworker or neighbor whose record becomes known only after the fact. It also places Mr. Bernecky in direct conflict with Special Condition (b)'s own requirement that he participate in group treatment alongside other convicted individuals, and with the practical reality that housing available to registered sex offenders routinely places them in proximity to other felons — a proximity the government's own residency and registration regime helped create.

*United States v. Reeves*, 591 F.3d 77, 80-81 (2d Cir. 2010), confirms the general vagueness/overbreadth principle applicable here — "[a] condition is too vague if it requires a reasonable person to speculate as to its meaning" — and confirms that this Circuit recognizes intimate personal association as a protected liberty interest against which a condition of this kind must be measured, even though *Reeves* itself concerned a romantic-disclosure requirement rather than a felon-communication ban. No case squarely narrows a "communicate or interact" felon condition to require a sustained relationship, and this motion says so candidly rather than overstating existing authority; the argument proceeds from first principles and from the condition's own disjunctive text. The Court should narrow this condition to a restriction on cohabitation with, or harboring of, a known felon, or, at minimum, add an incidental-contact and knowledge-timing exception consistent with ordinary civic and workplace life.


### K. Standard Condition 12 (Risk Notification)

Probation's own pre-release materials describe this condition in its pre-*Boles* form, unmodified to reflect the district's own corrective standing order. *See United States v. Boles*, 914 F.3d 95 (2d Cir. 2019) (holding the standard "risk" condition vague and remanding for the condition's scope to be clarified). Mr. Bernecky respectfully requests that the Court direct that the existing WDNY standing order be applied to him, as it already applies district-wide. No facial challenge to the standing order's mechanism is made here.[^2]


## CONCLUSION AND REQUESTED RELIEF

The dispute this motion leads with is not abstract, and it is not waiting for a future violation to make it real. It is happening now, to a real employer, over a real job, in the weeks before Mr. Bernecky's supervision begins. Every other request in this motion follows the same pattern in smaller scale: language broad enough, or left administratively undefined enough, that Mr. Bernecky's compliance depends on someone's informal assurance rather than the Court's own order. An assurance is not a clarification, and a shrug is not a standard. What this motion asks for, condition by condition, is simply that the Judgment say plainly what its own text, read carefully, already provides — no more authority than was actually granted, and no less protection than the law already requires.

For the foregoing reasons, Mr. Bernecky respectfully requests that the Court:

1. Confirm that Special Condition (a) confers no authority over an employer's own systems and imposes no employer monitoring obligation of any kind; and, to the extent necessary, modify the condition to limit monitored devices to those Mr. Bernecky owns that are capable of accessing image content, prohibit rather than authorize monitoring of third-party devices, and clarify that Standard Conditions 5 and 6 confer no authority over property or premises Mr. Bernecky does not own, lease, or control — all as set out in Part A;
2. Modify Special Condition (a) to exclude indiscriminate keystroke-level logging, as set out in Part B;
3. Clarify Special Condition (b) to establish that a treatment provider's rules exceeding this Court's own conditions cannot support a violation finding, as set out in Part C;
4. Modify Special Condition (h) so that the alcohol-abstinence requirement applies only upon a treating physician's determination that alcohol is contraindicated, as set out in Part D;
5. Modify Special Condition (c) to adopt a knowledgeable-adult family-contact structure and clarify that it imposes no occupational restriction, as set out in Part E;
6. Grant the procedural protections requested for Special Condition (g), or in the alternative the narrower relief described, and appoint counsel under 18 U.S.C. § 3006A(a)(1)(E) for purposes of this motion, as set out in Part F;
7. Vacate the blanket presentence-report disclosure authorization in Special Condition (e) and strike its duplicative registration language, as set out in Part G;
8. Modify Special Conditions (a), (b), (g), and (h) to include an ability-to-pay mechanism, as set out in Part H;
9. Modify Special Condition (d) to require a reasonable-suspicion predicate, and vacate Special Condition (i) upon confirmation that restitution has been paid in full, as set out in Part I;
10. Narrow Standard Condition 8 as set out in Part J; and
11. Direct that the district's existing risk-notification standing order be applied to Mr. Bernecky, as set out in Part K.

Mr. Bernecky's supervision begins in a matter of weeks. The Court should resolve these questions while resolving them can still keep a job intact, a family gathering ordinary, and a condition's meaning something other than a guess.
