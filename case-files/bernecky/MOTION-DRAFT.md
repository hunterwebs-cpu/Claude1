# DEFENDANT'S MOTION TO MODIFY CONDITIONS OF SUPERVISED RELEASE
## PURSUANT TO 18 U.S.C. § 3583(e)(2) AND FED. R. CRIM. P. 32.1(c)

Defendant Jeffrey Bernecky, proceeding *pro se*, respectfully moves this Court, pursuant to 18 U.S.C. § 3583(e)(2) and Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 32.1(c), to modify the conditions of supervised release imposed by the Judgment entered June 12, 2018 (Dkt. 28). In support of this motion, Mr. Bernecky states as follows.


## PRELIMINARY STATEMENT

A contact lens manufacturer offered Jeffrey Bernecky a job. He took it, told the employer about his conviction without being asked to, and has spent weeks since then waiting for someone at the United States Probation Office to tell that employer, in writing, what a monitoring condition actually requires of a company that is not on supervised release. What he has instead is a probation officer's remark that things "should be fine so long as they have a way to monitor him," and, as of this week, word that Probation has finally placed a call to the employer's human resources office. He still does not know whether he has a job. His ten-year term of supervision starts on or about July 27, 2026, whether or not that question is answered first.

The same defect shows up nine more times in this Judgment, in different forms. Special Condition (g) requires Mr. Bernecky to sit for a polygraph or voice-stress examination and answer whatever he is asked, with no lawyer in the room and no ruling in advance on what the privilege actually protects, under the identical revocation threat that backs every other condition here — and he will not do that. Special Condition (c) bars him from "deliberate contact" with any child, with a carve-out written for children he does not have, which as a practical matter means he cannot walk into his mother's living room at Christmas without knowing whether his sister's kids are already there. Special Condition (h) requires total abstinence from alcohol without any physician ever having said a specific medication of his interacts with it. In each instance, and in the others addressed below, the common thread is the same: nothing in the record shows that the finding the law requires before imposing this particular restriction was ever sought, or ever made.

This motion asks the Court to use the authority 18 U.S.C. § 3583(e)(2) has always given it, at the moment the U.S. Sentencing Commission's own November 2025 amendment identifies as the right one to use it — the start of supervision. *See* U.S.S.G. § 5D1.4(a) (2025). It is not an attack on the 2018 sentence, and it does not ask the Court to reopen anything the sentencing judge actually decided. It asks for ten specific corrections, taken up one condition at a time in the order the Judgment lists them, so that Mr. Bernecky begins supervision knowing what each condition requires of him rather than finding out through an informal phone call, a violation proceeding, or a guess.


## JURISDICTION AND STANDARD OF REVIEW

Section 3583(e)(2) authorizes the Court to "modify, reduce, or enlarge the conditions of supervised release, at any time prior to the expiration or termination of the term of supervised release, pursuant to the provisions of the Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure relating to the modification of probation." 18 U.S.C. § 3583(e)(2). That cross-reference makes Rule 32.1(c) the governing procedural vehicle for this motion.

The statute is a general, forward-looking tool; it does not require a threshold showing of changed circumstances before the Court may act. *United States v. Lussier*, 104 F.3d 32, 36 (2d Cir. 1997) (the Rule 32.1 Advisory Committee's Notes explain that "the sentencing court must be able to respond to changes in the [defendant's] circumstances as well as new ideas and methods of rehabilitation"). *Lussier* draws one boundary relevant here: § 3583(e)(2) reaches the scope and administration of a condition going forward, not a claim that a condition was illegal when the sentencing court imposed it — that is the office of direct appeal or 28 U.S.C. § 2255. *Id.* at 33. Every request below is framed accordingly, as a request to clarify or reassess what a condition means and how it should operate now, not as a challenge to the 2018 Judgment's validity.

Nor does timing stand in the way. Section 3583(e)(2) contains no waiting period, and the Sentencing Commission's most recent guidance confirms the point rather than creating it: courts are now expressly encouraged to reassess the fit of existing conditions "in consultation with the probation officer after the defendant's release from imprisonment" — the position Mr. Bernecky occupies today. U.S.S.G. § 5D1.4(a) (2025). There is no reason to leave any of the questions below for a later proceeding when they can be resolved now, before they cause the kind of harm already described above.


## STATEMENT OF FACTS

The Honorable David G. Larimer sentenced Mr. Bernecky on June 12, 2018 to 120 months' imprisonment and ten years of supervised release, following a guilty plea to possessing child pornography involving prepubescent minors — a conviction that followed an earlier one for possessing and distributing child pornography. He is scheduled to complete his prison term and begin supervision on or about July 27, 2026.

Near the end of his Bureau of Prisons term, a temporary-service placement turned into a real job offer from a contact lens manufacturer. Mr. Bernecky told the employer about his conviction and supervision status before anyone asked him to — Special Condition (a) imposes no such requirement on him at all. The employer read Special Condition (a)'s own language and, on its own, became concerned that the condition's computer-monitoring provisions might extend to the company's business systems. Mr. Bernecky called his probation officer to get a straight answer; what he received was an offhand assurance that things "should be fine so long as they have a way to monitor him," which resolved nothing for the employer and was never put in writing. He had already told the employer he would not need its shared computers to do the job, and the employer offered its own workaround — an internal account with no internet access at all.

Probation has since made contact with the employer's human resources office, but only this week, and only after the concern had already sat unaddressed for weeks. Mr. Bernecky still does not have confirmation that the job is his. A phone call is not the same thing as an answer to the question the employer actually asked, and the underlying uncertainty about what Special Condition (a) reaches remains exactly where it started. Mr. Bernecky has already signed a lease on the strength of steady income, and every week this goes unresolved is a week he cannot plan around. Nor is the exposure limited to this one employer: the same silence that produced this concern will produce it again with the next one, for as long as Mr. Bernecky is separately required, under Standard Condition 7, to hold full-time work.

Two more facts bear on conditions addressed later in this motion. Mr. Bernecky's sister had children while he was incarcerated; the nieces and nephews he would otherwise see at family gatherings today were not alive when he was sentenced, and no finding about them could have been made then. Separately, Mr. Bernecky believes the $500 restitution obligation in the Judgment has been paid in full — a fact that, once confirmed, removes the only stated purpose behind Special Condition (i).


## ARGUMENT

### I. Special Condition (a) — The Computer and Internet Monitoring Program

Special Condition (a) conditions Mr. Bernecky's use of any "internet capable device" on participation in CIMP, authorizes Probation to install monitoring software on any device "owned or operated by the defendant," and requires that any monitoring "be designed to avoid, as much as possible, reading any privileged information." Four distinct problems live inside this one condition.

**A. No finding under U.S.S.G. § 5F1.5 appears anywhere in the record, and without one, this condition cannot be read to reach an employer's systems.** An occupational restriction requires the sentencing court to find "a reasonably direct relationship . . . between the defendant's occupation, business, or profession and the conduct relevant to the offense of conviction," plus a finding that no lesser restriction would do. U.S.S.G. § 5F1.5(a); *see United States v. Peterson*, 248 F.3d 79, 85-86 (2d Cir. 2001) (per curiam). Nothing available in this case — not the Judgment, not any Statement of Reasons this motion's preparation was able to locate, not any filing by either party — shows that finding was ever requested or ever made. That gap matters because § 5F1.5's finding is not a formality layered on top of an occupational restriction; it is what makes one lawful in the first place. A court that was never asked to weigh, and never did weigh, whether Mr. Bernecky's occupation bears the required relationship to his offense has not authorized a condition to function as an occupational restriction, because it never did the analysis that authorization requires.

To be clear about what this argument does not ask: it does not ask the Court to make that finding now, belatedly, to justify a restriction already assumed to exist. It asks the Court to recognize that because the finding was never made, there is no occupational restriction to begin with. The point has teeth in this district already — even a bare employer-*notification* clause, considerably lighter than a monitoring-capability demand, has been treated as an occupational restriction requiring exactly this showing. *See United States v. Love*, No. 24-1706-cr, 2025 WL 1077176 (2d Cir. Apr. 8, 2025); *United States v. Jenkins*, 854 F.3d 181 (2d Cir. 2017); *United States v. Ruff*, No. 18-3247-cr (2d Cir. Nov. 14, 2019) (vacating outright); *United States v. Lombardi*, No. 17-2352-cr (2d Cir. Mar. 22, 2018) (remanding); *United States v. Piper*, No. 19-1058-cr (2d Cir. Mar. 30, 2020) (remanding). Mr. Bernecky's condition contains no notification requirement at all — he volunteered one anyway — yet it has produced, in practice, something more burdensome than notification: a live capability dispute with a prospective employer.

*United States v. Brown*, 173 F.4th 45 (2d Cir. 2026), points the same direction rather than away from it. Brown argued his condition operated as a *compelled* notification requirement; Mr. Bernecky makes no such claim, since his disclosure was voluntary and already accepted. Brown's condition also carried its own "as permitted by the employer" consent gate, which this condition lacks outright, and the Brown panel's occupational-restriction ruling came on plain-error review — a standard built for appeals correcting a lower court's error, which has no purchase here, where this Court is being asked to exercise its own § 3583(e)(2) discretion in the first instance. The mismatch between this condition's breadth and this job is stark on its own facts: the position requires no public internet access, the employer has already offered a disabled-internet internal account, and CIMP's entire purpose — policing internet-facilitated risk — has nothing to bite on here.

None of this rests on the mechanics of how a condition gets created in the first place; it rests on the more basic rule that only the sentencing court may impose a special condition of supervised release. *United States v. Matta*, 777 F.3d 116, 122 (2d Cir. 2015). When a probation officer's administration of a condition threatens to reach past what the court actually ordered, the Second Circuit has said so without hesitation: a requirement that would "contradict, rather than execute" the sentence is one "the Probation Office was plainly without power to impose." *United States v. Browder*, 866 F.3d 504, 514 (2d Cir. 2017). *United States v. Kunz*, 68 F.4th 748, 766-67 (2d Cir. 2023), applies that same principle to this identical CIMP language.

An informal reassurance to a worried employer does not fix any of this; a written order does. The Tenth Circuit faced the identical confusion over materially the same language in *United States v. Mike*, 632 F.3d 686, 696-97 (10th Cir. 2011) — where even the government conceded the condition "cannot be read fairly" to reach an employer's computers — and did not leave the question open. It remanded with instructions that the condition be clarified, directing that if the sentencing court really meant to reach an employer's devices, it would have to make the § 5F1.5 findings first. *Id.*; *see also United States v. Matteson*, 327 F. App'x 791 (10th Cir. 2009). That is what Mr. Bernecky asks for here: a declaration that, absent any § 5F1.5 finding in this record, Special Condition (a) confers no authority over an employer's own systems.

Mr. Bernecky raises this in his own right, not the employer's. The threat to his own job, and the risk of a violation finding built on an unwritten and shifting understanding of his own condition, are injuries to him — the kind of interest a defendant may vindicate directly. *Bond v. United States*, 564 U.S. 211, 220 (2011). If the Court sees it otherwise, ordinary third-party standing would reach the same result anyway, given how closely his compliance depends on the employer's cooperation and how impractical it would be for the employer to litigate its own interests inside someone else's criminal case. *See Powers v. Ohio*, 499 U.S. 400 (1991); *Kowalski v. Tesmer*, 543 U.S. 125 (2004); *Singleton v. Wulff*, 428 U.S. 106 (1976); *Warth v. Seldin*, 422 U.S. 490 (1975).

Two related conditions share this same flaw. Standard Condition 5 lets Probation approve or reject where Mr. Bernecky lives; if applied as a standardless veto rather than simple verification, it raises the identical delegation problem. Standard Condition 6 lets Probation search anywhere Mr. Bernecky is and seize what it finds in plain view; at a workplace he does not own or control, he has no authority to consent to a search of someone else's property in the first place. Both should be clarified alongside Special Condition (a): none of these conditions reaches property that belongs to somebody other than Mr. Bernecky.

**B. Unrestricted keystroke capture cannot coexist with the privilege protection the condition already promises.** The condition itself says monitoring "shall be designed to avoid, as much as possible, reading any privileged information or any private material that is not illegal." *Kunz* read this exact clause as a real, enforceable limit on Probation — language that "constrains the supervisors in their surveillance . . . by requiring them not to abuse their power and access," not window dressing. 68 F.4th at 766-67. Logging every keystroke means capturing privileged attorney-client communications along with everything else, before anyone has had the chance to sort privileged material from anything else. That is not a monitoring method that avoids reading privileged material "as much as possible" — it is one that cannot avoid it at all.

This Circuit's own precedent involving the same category of offense supports narrowing the method rather than the promise. *United States v. Sofsky*, 287 F.3d 122, 126-27 (2d Cir. 2002), approved "a more focused restriction, limited to pornography sites and images," as an adequate substitute for a blanket ban in an image-based case. *United States v. Lifshitz*, 369 F.3d 173, 192 (2d Cir. 2004), likened unfiltered, content-blind monitoring to "searching [a defendant's] diary" rather than performing "the highly targeted diagnosis accomplished by drug testing," and on remand specifically endorsed keyword- or phrase-triggered monitoring over "recording all varieties of computer-related activity." *Id.* at 193 n.11. Mr. Bernecky's offense involves images, not written communications; nothing about that offense justifies logging every word he types.

The Court should modify Special Condition (a) so that CIMP is implemented through a method that does not indiscriminately sweep in privileged or non-image material — domain review, file-type filtering, image-hash scanning, or the keyword-triggered approach *Lifshitz* itself blessed — rather than through full keystroke capture.

**C. "Internet capable device" should mean a device capable of handling images, not merely a device with a Wi-Fi chip.** The Second Circuit has already shown this district how to read boilerplate like this without absurd results. Facing the identical CIMP language, it refused to read the condition's encryption-related terms literally, holding it "would be absurd to interpret" Probation as requiring pre-approval every time a supervisee visits an ordinary "https" site or sends an encrypted message. *Kunz*, 68 F.4th at 764-65. The court's own description of its method was that "a sensible reading of the restrictions neutralizes the most troubling of those concerns." *Id.* at 754-55. Nothing about this is a novel interpretive move — plain condition language is not read to compel "the impossible," *id.* at 763 (citing *United States v. Johnson*, 446 F.3d 272, 281 (2d Cir. 2006)) — and the same instinct against absurd literal readings runs through general statutory construction as well. *See Public Citizen v. U.S. Dep't of Justice*, 491 U.S. 440, 454-55 (1989); *Griffin v. Oceanic Contractors, Inc.*, 458 U.S. 564, 571 (1982); *Green v. Bock Laundry Mach. Co.*, 490 U.S. 504, 509 (1989).

The absurdity of the broadest possible reading is not difficult to find once looked for. Ordinary households today run on devices that talk to the internet for one narrow purpose that has nothing to do with pictures: a refrigerator that reorders groceries, a doorbell camera that pings a phone when a package arrives, a fitness band that uploads a nightly heart rate, a CPAP machine reporting a sleep-apnea patient's breathing to his doctor. Each one is, taken at face value, an "internet capable device." None of them has a browser, an app store, or any way to find, save, or show an image. Nobody would seriously argue that a refrigerator belongs in CIMP because it connects to Wi-Fi. The word "operates" fares no better read literally: the thing people most naturally *operate* is not a phone or a laptop but a car, and a modern car — with OnStar, FordPass Connect, Tesla's own telematics, or the equivalent — is exactly as "internet capable" as a refrigerator is. *See State v. Henderson*, 907 N.W.2d 623 (Minn. 2018) (defining "operate" a motor vehicle as "any act that causes a motor vehicle to function or controls the functioning of the motor vehicle"). Either the government accepts that a refrigerator, a doorbell, and a car all belong under CIMP surveillance, or it accepts what *Kunz* already holds: the real boundary here is what a device can actually do with an image, not whether it can reach the internet at all.

That boundary is already written into the condition — just inconsistently. The opening sentence limits itself to "any internet capable device"; the very next monitoring-authorization clause drops that qualifier and reaches anything "owned or operated by the defendant." Reading the condition to mean, consistently, what its own first sentence says is not an import from outside the document — it is enforcement of the document as written. Other circuits already draw the line this way. *E.g.*, *United States v. Ullman*, No. 14-1414, 2015 WL 3559221 (10th Cir. June 8, 2015) ("devices without Internet access, such as gaming systems, are not restricted"). None of this bears on which devices belong to an employer or any other third party — that question is answered independently in Part I.A, above, on the ground that no § 5F1.5 finding exists at all.

A simpler, less intrusive alternative exists for anything Mr. Bernecky does not own: bar him from using it, rather than authorize Probation to inspect or demand monitoring capability from whoever does own it. That protects the government's interest without imposing anything on a person who is not before this Court.

**D. The fee obligation has no floor, no schedule, and no ability-to-pay check.** Special Condition (a) requires Mr. Bernecky to "pay the cost of monitoring services" without ever saying how much, how it is calculated, or what happens if he cannot afford it. Judicial Conference guidance warns against exactly this kind of open-ended delegation, favoring "a court-approved sliding scale . . . responsive to changes in a defendant's financial circumstances" — nothing resembling that appears here. Before nonpayment of a financial condition can support revocation, there must be an inquiry into willfulness and good-faith effort. *Bearden v. Georgia*, 461 U.S. 660 (1983). And if Mr. Bernecky ever qualifies for Medicaid and CIMP counts as a covered service, an open-ended contribution requirement risks colliding with the federal rule against billing a Medicaid beneficiary past the plan's own cost-sharing limits. 42 C.F.R. § 447.15. The identical defect recurs in Special Conditions (b), (g), and (h), addressed in Parts II, VI, and VII below — rather than making the same argument four separate times, this motion asks for the same fix each time it appears: an actual ability-to-pay mechanism.

**E. Requested relief.** The Court should order: (i) a declaration that, absent a § 5F1.5 finding in the record, Special Condition (a) grants no authority over an employer's own systems and places no burden on any employer to prove anything about its own capabilities; (ii) a monitoring-method modification excluding indiscriminate keystroke capture; (iii) a clarification that "internet capable device" means a device capable of accessing, storing, or displaying images, coupled with a prohibition on using another person's device rather than a mandate to monitor it; (iv) a clarification that Standard Conditions 5 and 6 grant no authority over property Mr. Bernecky does not own, lease, or control; and (v) a court-approved, ability-to-pay-adjusted fee schedule.


### II. Special Condition (b) — Sex-Offense-Specific Treatment

Special Condition (b) requires Mr. Bernecky to "follow the rules and regulations" of a sex-offense-specific treatment program without any court-imposed ceiling on what those rules may say, and to contribute to the cost of the program.

**A. A private provider cannot write rules that go beyond what the sentencing court itself ordered.** Only the court may restrict a defendant's liberty as a condition of supervision; it cannot hand that pen to a private provider. *Matta*, 777 F.3d at 122. *Browder* is precisely this fact pattern: a treatment provider's own agreement barred contact with *any* minor, including the defendant's own children, even though the court's actual sentence carved his children out. The Second Circuit struck the provider's rule down as something "the Probation Office was plainly without power to impose." 866 F.3d at 514. Mr. Bernecky faces the same structure — a program the court did order, whose actual content the provider alone controls. The Court should make clear that a provider rule exceeding what this Court itself ordered cannot support a violation.

One additional point belongs here as background rather than as a separate legal claim. Cognitive-behavioral therapy is legitimate treatment. Feeding a provider continuous CIMP surveillance data to reshape a specific pattern of thought is something else, and it is worth naming even though it is not the basis of relief requested. The government "can[not] prescribe what shall be orthodox . . . in matters of opinion." *West Virginia State Bd. of Educ. v. Barnette*, 319 U.S. 624, 642 (1943). Mr. Bernecky is not asking the Court to revisit *Stanley v. Georgia*, 394 U.S. 557 (1969), or the reasons *Osborne v. Ohio*, 495 U.S. 103 (1990), correctly declined to extend it to child pornography, given the real harm to real children. His point is narrower: no court has ever authorized turning surveillance into a treatment tool this specific way, and that missing authorization — not any claim about his thoughts — is what Part II.A already reaches.

**B. Fee obligation.** Same defect, same fix, as Part I.D above: an actual ability-to-pay mechanism consistent with *Bearden*.


### III. Special Condition (c) — Contact with Minors and Loitering

Special Condition (c) forbids "deliberate contact with any child under 18 years of age, excluding his biological or adopted children." Mr. Bernecky has neither, so the condition's only carve-out has never applied to him and never will.

**A. Written this way, the condition does not merely limit contact with one relative — it removes Mr. Bernecky from ordinary family life entirely.** He cannot show up to a family gathering without first knowing, with certainty, that no minor relative will be there, and the condition gives him no way to find that out in advance. Stop by his mother's house on Christmas and find his sister's kids already there, and he is in violation the moment he does not leave — not because anyone has ever found him dangerous to those children, but because the condition draws no line between an unplanned family visit and whatever the sentencing court actually meant to prevent. The same is true if grandchildren happen to stop by unannounced. Nothing in this record addresses whether Mr. Bernecky poses any danger to any particular relative, or whether merely being present at a family gathering — as distinct from some other kind of contact — poses any risk at all.

The right to maintain relationships with extended family — not just one's own children — is constitutionally protected, and this condition's illusory carve-out sits directly on top of that right. *See Moore v. City of East Cleveland*, 431 U.S. 494 (1977) (protecting the choice to live among extended family and demanding "careful scrutiny" of government intrusion on it). This district requires an individualized basis before restricting that right this broadly. *United States v. Myers*, 426 F.3d 117, 129-30 (2d Cir. 2005) (vacating a contact-with-own-child condition for the identical lack of an individualized record present here, and requiring heightened scrutiny wherever a condition touches familial association). *United States v. Bleau*, 930 F.3d 35, 42-43 (2d Cir. 2019), likewise required the court to explain the necessity of a minors-contact condition absent an obvious basis in the record, and vacated one where the underlying offense involved no actual contact with minors. Mr. Bernecky is not *Bleau*'s defendant — this is at least his second child-pornography-related conviction, and nothing in this motion asks the Court to pretend otherwise. What it asks for is a structure that accounts for that record honestly: contact with minor relatives permitted in the presence of a knowledgeable adult already aware of his conviction — his mother, father, or sister — so that an ordinary family gathering does not require advance clearance, or a sudden exit, every time a child happens to be there. Other circuits already use exactly this kind of carve-out, reading minors-contact conditions "to exclude incidental encounters." *United States v. DaSilva*, 844 F.3d 8 (1st Cir. 2016).

His sister's children being born during his incarceration is, on its own, a genuine change in circumstance under *Lussier*: the people whose presence at family gatherings creates this problem did not exist, and could not have been the subject of any finding, when he was sentenced.

**B. The condition should also be clarified to impose no restriction on Mr. Bernecky's occupation.** A seventeen-year-old coworker, or a minor customer encountered in the course of doing his job, should not trigger "deliberate contact" requiring advance permission or an abrupt exit — for the same reason given in Part I.A: no § 5F1.5 finding supports reading any condition in this Judgment as an occupational restriction.


### IV. Special Condition (d) — Financial Monitoring

Special Condition (d) gives Probation open access to Mr. Bernecky's personal and business financial records in order to monitor "compliance with not buying or subscribing to online services that provide child pornography." Nobody has ever alleged that Mr. Bernecky purchased child pornography — his conviction is for possession. A financial-monitoring condition aimed at detecting a purchase has nothing to detect in a case with no purchase in it. The fix is to require a reasonable-suspicion trigger before financial records may be accessed, matching the structure the Judgment already uses for computer monitoring itself, which activates "as triggered by impermissible/suspicious activity" rather than running on an open-ended basis.


### V. Special Condition (e) — Sex Offender Registration and Presentence Report Disclosure

Special Condition (e) authorizes disclosure of Mr. Bernecky's presentence report to the New York State Board of Examiners of Sex Offenders, and further disclosure "to the county court and to the parties involved in the determination of the defendant's final classification level." A presentence report may be disclosed to a third party only on a "compelling" and "particularized" showing made at the time disclosure is actually sought — not through a blanket authorization entered years earlier, with no chance for the defendant to be heard when the moment actually arrives. *See United States v. Charmer Indus., Inc.*, 711 F.2d 1164 (2d Cir. 1983). A standing waiver of that case-specific showing is hard to square with a rule that puts the burden squarely on whoever is asking for disclosure.

The registration language in the same condition adds nothing: Mandatory Condition 6 already requires registration under 34 U.S.C. § 20901 *et seq.* The Court should strike the advance PSR authorization and the duplicate registration language, without closing the door on a properly noticed, case-specific disclosure request if one is ever actually made.


### VI. Special Condition (g) — Polygraph and Computerized Voice Stress Analyzer Testing

Special Condition (g) requires Mr. Bernecky to submit to polygraph and CVSA examinations and answer whatever he is asked, reserving only a later chance to argue his answers cannot be used against him. Every other condition in this motion concerns a practical harm. This one concerns a constitutional one, and it should be read with that difference in mind.

**A. Answering first and arguing later does not solve the problem — Mr. Bernecky will not answer any polygraph or CVSA question, on any topic, without a lawyer present to advise him as he answers.** That is the position, not a negotiating opener. The reason is straightforward: deciding whether a specific question calls for a privileged answer is a legal judgment, not an instinct, and nothing about an examination room equips someone to make that judgment correctly under pressure. *United States v. Johnson*, 446 F.3d 272 (2d Cir. 2006), upheld an after-the-fact challenge structure — but only against the narrow immunity theory Johnson raised on his own facts, and it never confronted either point Mr. Bernecky raises here. *Minnesota v. Murphy* supplies the governing rule: a statement is compelled whenever the state, "either expressly or by implication, asserts that invocation of the privilege would lead to revocation." 465 U.S. 420, 435 (1984). A condition that requires Mr. Bernecky to "answer the questions posed" before any privilege determination is made does exactly that. *United States v. Von Behren*, 822 F.3d 1139, 1150-51 (10th Cir. 2016), applied *Murphy* to a nearly identical sex-offense polygraph and held that threatening program termination and revocation for refusing an incriminating question is unconstitutional compulsion. The privilege reaches more than a direct confession — it covers anything that supplies "a link in the chain of evidence" that could be used to prosecute. *Hoffman v. United States*, 341 U.S. 479, 486 (1951). Given that this is at least Mr. Bernecky's second child-pornography-related conviction, any further compelled disclosure carries real stakes — a fact that argues for the protections requested here, not against them. And nothing in his history justifies treating him as though he needs this scrutiny in the first place: he has never once been found dishonest in treatment or supervision, and a condition premised on presumed dishonesty that has never actually occurred cannot meet § 3583(d)'s reasonably-related standard regardless of how the privilege question comes out.

**B. Requested protections.** Until each of the following is in place, Mr. Bernecky is entitled to decline any polygraph or similar examination question: (i) counsel present before, during, and after the examination; (ii) enough time, per question, for counsel to determine whether invocation is warranted, since that determination is legal rather than instinctive; (iii) examiner credentials sufficient to withstand a *Daubert* challenge, with CVSA reliability addressed separately; and (iv) no disclosure of anything the examination produces without leave of court.

No federal court appears to have granted a request quite like this one, and that is worth saying plainly rather than dressing the request up as settled law. It fits most naturally as a discretionary safeguard this Court may craft under 18 U.S.C. § 3583(d) — not as an argument that the exam room is custodial in the Miranda sense, a framing that would invite comparison to authority already rejecting a counsel-presence claim on custodial grounds. *See J.B. v. New Jersey State Parole Bd.*, 229 N.J. 21 (2017). It is enough that the privilege question is a legal one, and that federal supervision policy already treats it as too delicate for a probation officer to resolve alone, directing that uncertain invocations go to the court instead. Guide to Judiciary Policy, Vol. 8E, § 190.45. If the Court will not order counsel's presence throughout, Mr. Bernecky asks, in descending order, for consultation access at any point during the exam — the same accommodation grand jury witnesses receive to step out and consult counsel, *see United States v. Mandujano*, 425 U.S. 564 (1976) — or, failing that, advance written review of the question list by counsel, with disputed questions sent to the Court rather than decided by the examiner.

**C. This is the same case continuing, not a new one starting, and that distinction should not wait for the government to raise it.** The Sixth Amendment right to counsel attaches offense by offense, and only later becomes a "critical stage" analysis. *Rothgery v. Gillespie County*, 554 U.S. 191 (2008); *see Texas v. Cobb*, 532 U.S. 162 (2001); *McNeil v. Wisconsin*, 501 U.S. 171 (1991); *Kirby v. Illinois*, 406 U.S. 682 (1972). That rule closes the door only on extending the right to a brand-new, uncharged offense — it says nothing about whether the right persists through ongoing supervision in the *same* case where it already attached at indictment, and supervision is a continuation of that same conviction, not a new one. The harder line of authority is *Pennsylvania v. Finley*, 481 U.S. 551 (1987), together with *Gagnon v. Scarpelli*, 411 U.S. 778 (1973), and *Morrissey v. Brewer*, 408 U.S. 471 (1972) — all treating post-conviction proceedings as due process matters rather than Sixth Amendment ones, and this motion confronts that line directly rather than leaving it for reply. *Estelle v. Smith*, 451 U.S. 454 (1981), is the closer analogy: a compelled psychiatric exam counted as a critical stage precisely because it was tied to a sentencing decision still pending. *Finley*'s concern — that no Sixth Amendment right outlasts "exhaustion of the appellate process" — never arose in *Estelle*, because that proceeding, unlike a closed conviction, was still open. Mr. Bernecky's supervision is open in the same sense: the sentencing court's jurisdiction over it continues, which is the entire reason this motion can be brought at all.

**D. Counsel should be appointed to help with these examinations, and there is no softer version of this request.** 18 U.S.C. § 3006A(a)(1)(E) already requires appointed counsel for a financially eligible person who "faces modification . . . of a condition . . . of a term of supervised release" — a description that fits this request exactly, since resolving the polygraph condition's procedures is what this Part asks the Court to do. The interests of justice reach the same conclusion from a different direction: appointing counsel to help someone choose between answering under compulsion and risking revocation for silence is not a courtesy extended for paperwork — it is what actually protecting the right requires. So the choice is this, stated as it is rather than softened: either the Court appoints counsel to assist with these examinations as described in Part VI.B, or Mr. Bernecky will invoke the Fifth Amendment categorically as to every future examination of this kind, rather than try to make real-time privilege calls alone.

**E. Fee obligation.** Same defect, same fix, as Part I.D above.


### VII. Special Condition (h) — Mental Health Treatment and Alcohol Abstinence

Special Condition (h) requires participation in mental health treatment and, "while in treatment or taking psychotropic medication," complete abstinence from alcohol, along with a contribution toward the cost of the program.

**A. Alcohol abstinence should follow a physician's determination, not apply automatically regardless of one.** Mr. Bernecky has no objection to the treatment itself. His objection is to a blanket abstinence rule triggered whether or not any physician has ever said a specific medication he takes actually interacts with alcohol. This Circuit has already vacated a comparable blanket restriction for lacking the individualized nexus § 3583(d) demands. *United States v. Betts*, 886 F.3d 198 (2d Cir. 2018) (striking a total alcohol ban as "not reasonably related to any of the factors outlined in Section 5D1.3(b)" where nothing in the record tied the offense, any violation, or any history to alcohol). It has since applied the same rule to a nearly identical "abstain from alcohol during treatment" clause, rejecting the government's position that the clause could simply be reimposed without any individualized look. *See United States v. Rodriguez*, No. 22-1820-cr (2d Cir. Dec. 7, 2023) (summary order).

The clause's own wording makes the problem worse on its own terms: it triggers on "treatment **or** medication," meaning talk therapy alone — with no medication involved — is enough to require abstinence, even though no interaction risk could possibly exist without a medication in the picture. And even the medication branch overgeneralizes, since alcohol-interaction risk differs sharply across drug classes rather than functioning as a single, uniform hazard. This precise clause, word for word, is this district's standard template for mental-health-treatment conditions, adopted after this district's own remand in *Browder* — not a finding made specifically about Mr. Bernecky.

The fix: the alcohol-abstinence requirement should apply only while, and only because, a treating physician has determined that alcohol is contraindicated for a specific medication or treatment Mr. Bernecky is actually receiving.

**B. Fee obligation.** Same defect, same fix, as Part I.D above.


### VIII. Special Condition (i) — General Financial Disclosure and Asset Restraint

Special Condition (i) gives Probation broad access to Mr. Bernecky's finances and control over his assets, tied by its own terms to "the collection of any unpaid fine or restitution." Mr. Bernecky believes the $500 restitution has already been paid in full. If the record bears that out, the condition has nothing left to collect, and *Lussier* confirms no separate showing of changed circumstances is needed to vacate a condition whose entire purpose has already been fulfilled. 104 F.3d at 36.


### IX. Standard Condition 8 — No Communication with Felons

Standard Condition 8 forbids Mr. Bernecky from "communicat[ing] or interact[ing]" with anyone he knows has a felony conviction, absent advance permission. That reaches further than it may first appear: by some estimates, roughly five percent of American adults have a felony conviction, which means a blanket communication ban with no exception for incidental contact or after-the-fact discovery of someone's record is likely to be triggered by ordinary life, not by any deliberate association with crime. The condition bans *communication*, not merely association — the two words appear separately and mean different things, and "communicate" covers a single exchange in a way "associate" does not — and it sweeps in conduct plainly worth protecting: asking a public figure with a felony record for an autograph, attending a rally for felon voting rights, or trading pleasantries with a coworker or neighbor whose record only becomes known later. It also conflicts with Special Condition (b)'s own requirement that Mr. Bernecky attend group treatment with other convicted people, and with the ordinary reality that sex-offender housing tends to sit near other people with felony records — proximity the government's own registration and residency rules helped create.

The general vagueness principle applies directly: "[a] condition is too vague if it requires a reasonable person to speculate as to its meaning." *United States v. Reeves*, 591 F.3d 77, 80-81 (2d Cir. 2010) (also recognizing intimate personal association as a protected interest, even though *Reeves* itself concerned a different disclosure requirement). No case squarely limits a "communicate or interact" felon condition to sustained relationships, and this motion says so rather than overstating what exists; the argument rests on first principles and the condition's own text. The fix is to narrow the condition to cohabitation with, or harboring of, a known felon, or, at minimum, to add exceptions for incidental contact and after-the-fact discovery consistent with ordinary civic and working life.


### X. Standard Condition 12 — Risk Notification

The version of this condition in Probation's pre-release materials predates the Second Circuit's own correction of it and the district's standing order implementing that correction. *See United States v. Boles*, 914 F.3d 95 (2d Cir. 2019) (holding the standard risk-notification condition vague and ordering its scope clarified). Mr. Bernecky asks the Court to apply the existing WDNY standing order to him, exactly as it already applies to everyone else in this district. This motion does not challenge the standing order's mechanism on its face.[^1]
