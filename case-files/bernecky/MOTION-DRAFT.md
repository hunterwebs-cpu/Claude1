## PRELIMINARY STATEMENT

This motion raises questions about several separate conditions of supervised release, addressed one at a time below. None of them is presented here as more important than the others, because they are not competing for the Court's attention — they are different symptoms of the same underlying problem. Time and again, the record contains no indication that the analysis the law requires before a particular restriction may be imposed was ever requested, or ever performed. No party asked the sentencing court to find, under U.S.S.G. § 5F1.5, that Mr. Bernecky's occupation bears the necessary relationship to his offense before authorizing a monitoring condition broad enough to reach an employer's own systems. No individualized finding addresses whether Mr. Bernecky poses any danger to the specific family members he cannot see today, because most of them were not yet born when he was sentenced. No finding addresses whether alcohol interacts with any medication he is actually prescribed, or whether he has ever once been dishonest in treatment or supervision, before requiring him to submit to abstinence and polygraph conditions built as though he had. None of these gaps is a drafting error to be waved away. Each one leaves Mr. Bernecky's liberty, his employment, and in one case his Fifth Amendment privilege, resting on an assumption that was never actually tested.

Two of these problems are especially concrete right now. First: a contact lens manufacturer offered Mr. Bernecky a job. The employer, reading the text of Special Condition (a) on its own, grew concerned that computer monitoring might reach its own business systems, and Mr. Bernecky's probation officer gave only an informal, non-committal answer rather than a clarification of record. As of this filing, Probation has finally contacted the employer's human resources department — but weeks after the concern first arose, and Mr. Bernecky still has no confirmation that the position will be honored. The delay itself is the harm: no one has yet told this employer, in writing, what the condition does and does not require of it, and Mr. Bernecky's ten-year term of supervision begins on or about July 27, 2026, whether or not that happens first. Second, and no less serious for being less time-pressured: Special Condition (g) requires Mr. Bernecky to answer polygraph and computerized voice stress analyzer questions with no counsel present and no advance determination of what the privilege actually covers, backed by the same revocation consequence that attaches to every other condition in this Judgment. A condition that can cost a person his liberty for declining to answer a question, decided in the moment, without assistance, is at least as serious as one that can cost him a job — if anything, more so, because the right at stake is constitutional rather than practical.

This motion does not ask the Court to declare the June 2018 Judgment invalid, and it does not ask the Court to revisit the legality of any finding the sentencing court made, or did not make, eight years ago. It asks the Court to exercise the forward-looking modification authority 18 U.S.C. § 3583(e)(2) has always given it, informed by developments in the governing law since 2018 and by the U.S. Sentencing Commission's own November 2025 instruction that the commencement of supervision is precisely the moment a court should reassess whether its conditions still fit. *See* U.S.S.G. § 5D1.4(a) (2025). Mr. Bernecky is, right now, in exactly that position, and the questions below should be resolved before supervision begins rather than worked out later through revocation proceedings, informal assurances, and guesswork.

The motion proceeds condition by condition, in the order the conditions appear in the Judgment, addressing every challenge raised to a given condition together rather than scattering related objections to the same condition across separate sections of this brief.


## JURISDICTION AND STANDARD OF REVIEW

Title 18, United States Code, Section 3583(e)(2) provides that the Court "may . . . modify, reduce, or enlarge the conditions of supervised release, at any time prior to the expiration or termination of the term of supervised release, pursuant to the provisions of the Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure relating to the modification of probation." 18 U.S.C. § 3583(e)(2). The statute's own cross-reference to "the Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure relating to the modification of probation" identifies Rule 32.1(c) as the governing procedural vehicle.

The Second Circuit has confirmed that § 3583(e)(2) is a general, forward-looking tool, not one gated by a threshold showing of changed circumstances. *United States v. Lussier*, 104 F.3d 32, 36 (2d Cir. 1997) (quoting the Rule 32.1 Advisory Committee's Notes for the proposition that "the sentencing court must be able to respond to changes in the [defendant's] circumstances as well as new ideas and methods of rehabilitation"). At the same time, *Lussier* holds that § 3583(e)(2) does not reach the legality or validity of a condition as originally imposed — that is the office of direct appeal or a motion under 28 U.S.C. § 2255. *Id.* at 33. This motion is framed accordingly, throughout, as a request for present-tense clarification and reassessment of the scope of existing conditions — not as a claim that the 2018 Judgment was invalid when entered.

The Sentencing Commission's November 2025 amendment to U.S.S.G. § 5D1.4 confirms this is precisely the right moment to ask. Subsection (a) provides that "[a]t any time prior to the expiration or termination of the term of supervised release, the court may modify, reduce, or enlarge the conditions of supervised release whenever warranted by an individualized assessment of the appropriateness of existing conditions," *see* 18 U.S.C. § 3583(e)(2), and that "[t]he court is encouraged to conduct such an assessment in consultation with the probation officer after the defendant's release from imprisonment." U.S.S.G. § 5D1.4(a) (2025). Mr. Bernecky is, right now, in exactly that position.

Congress itself — not merely the Sentencing Commission — drew a sharp line between modification under § 3583(e)(2) and early termination under § 3583(e)(1). Early termination is available only "after the expiration of one year of supervised release." 18 U.S.C. § 3583(e)(1). Modification carries no such floor: it is available "at any time prior to the expiration or termination of the term of supervised release." *Id.* § 3583(e)(2). The Sentencing Commission's own guideline text tracks, rather than creates, that distinction — § 5D1.4(b)'s one-year figure expressly cross-cites § 3583(e)(1), while § 5D1.4(a)'s absence of any time-trigger cross-cites § 3583(e)(2). There is no procedural obstacle, and no reason, to wait.

## ARGUMENT

### I. Special Condition (a) — The Computer and Internet Monitoring Program ("CIMP") Condition

Special Condition (a) requires Mr. Bernecky to participate in CIMP and authorizes Probation "to install any application as necessary to surveil all activity on computer(s) or connected device(s) owned or operated by the defendant," subject to a clause requiring that any monitoring or examination "be designed to avoid, as much as possible, reading any privileged information." This single condition presents four independent problems.

**A. Nothing in the record shows the court-ordered analysis for an occupational restriction was ever requested or performed, and this condition should not be read, or administered, as though it were.**

An occupational restriction may be imposed only where the sentencing court finds "a reasonably direct relationship . . . between the defendant's occupation, business, or profession and the conduct relevant to the offense of conviction," together with a necessity finding that no lesser restriction would suffice. U.S.S.G. § 5F1.5(a); *see United States v. Peterson*, 248 F.3d 79, 85-86 (2d Cir. 2001) (per curiam). Nothing in the record before this Court — not the Judgment, not any Statement of Reasons this motion's preparation has been able to locate, not any filing by either party — reflects that anyone ever requested this finding, or that the sentencing court ever made it. That is not a technical gap. Section 5F1.5's finding is a precondition to authority, not a formality: a court that was never asked to, and never did, weigh whether Mr. Bernecky's occupation bears the required relationship to his offense cannot have intended a condition of this Judgment to function as an occupational restriction, because it never undertook the analysis the law requires before imposing one.

This is not a request that the Court now supply, after the fact, the finding that was never made. It is the opposite: because the finding was never made, no occupational restriction exists, and Special Condition (a) should not be read or administered as though one does. This district's own recent case law confirms that even an employer-*notification* requirement — considerably milder than a monitoring-capability demand — is treated as an occupational restriction requiring exactly this finding. *See United States v. Love*, No. 24-1706-cr, 2025 WL 1077176 (2d Cir. Apr. 8, 2025); *United States v. Jenkins*, 854 F.3d 181 (2d Cir. 2017); *United States v. Ruff*, No. 18-3247-cr (2d Cir. Nov. 14, 2019) (vacating outright); *United States v. Lombardi*, No. 17-2352-cr (2d Cir. Mar. 22, 2018) (remanding); *United States v. Piper*, No. 19-1058-cr (2d Cir. Mar. 30, 2020) (remanding). If a mere notification clause requires a § 5F1.5 finding, a condition administered, in practice, as a capability demand on an employer's own systems requires one at least as much — and here there is no notification requirement in the condition's text at all; Mr. Bernecky disclosed voluntarily, going further than the condition required.

*United States v. Brown*, 173 F.4th 45 (2d Cir. 2026), does not compel a different result. Brown's claim was that his condition functioned as a *compelled* notification requirement; Mr. Bernecky does not object to notification — he already made it, voluntarily, and it was accepted. Brown's condition also contained its own "as permitted by the employer" clause — a built-in consent gate Mr. Bernecky's condition lacks entirely — and Brown's occupational-restriction holding arose on plain-error appellate review, a standard that has no application here, since this is an original motion asking this Court to exercise its own § 3583(e)(2) discretion in the first instance, not an appeal reviewing a prior ruling for error. The disproportion is sharpened by this specific job: the position requires no public internet access at all, and the employer has already offered an internet-disabled internal account. CIMP's stated purpose — monitoring internet-facilitated risk — has essentially no application here, yet Mr. Bernecky remains in employment limbo while separately required, under Standard Condition 7, to maintain full-time work.

The authority for saying so does not depend on the formalities governing how a new condition is created; it rests on the settled principle that the power to impose special conditions of supervised release "is vested exclusively in the district court." *United States v. Matta*, 777 F.3d 116, 122 (2d Cir. 2015). Where a probation officer's administration of a condition threatens to reach further than what the court actually authorized, the Second Circuit has not hesitated to say so: "[b]ecause such a requirement . . . would contradict, rather than execute, [the defendant's] sentence, the Probation Office was plainly without power to impose it." *United States v. Browder*, 866 F.3d 504, 514 (2d Cir. 2017). *United States v. Kunz*, 68 F.4th 748, 766-67 (2d Cir. 2023), reaffirms the identical principle for the very CIMP boilerplate at issue here.

Leaving this to an employer's own guesswork and a probation officer's off-the-cuff reassurance is not a fix; only a written order is. A materially identical condition produced identical confusion in *United States v. Mike*, 632 F.3d 686, 696-97 (10th Cir. 2011), where the government itself argued the condition "cannot be read fairly" to reach an employer's computers, and the Tenth Circuit's answer was to remand with instructions that the condition be clarified so no one would have to guess — expressly directing that if the district court actually intended the condition to reach an employer's devices, "it must make the necessary findings" under the same § 5F1.5 framework discussed above. *Id.*; *see also United States v. Matteson*, 327 F. App'x 791 (10th Cir. 2009). That is the order Mr. Bernecky requests here: a declaration that, absent the § 5F1.5 finding the record does not contain, Special Condition (a) confers no authority over an employer's own systems and imposes no obligation on any employer to demonstrate anything about its own monitoring capability.

Mr. Bernecky may raise this in his own right. His own injury — jeopardized employment, and a live risk of a violation finding premised on an unwritten and shifting understanding of his own condition — "seeks to vindicate [his] own constitutional interests," not the employer's. *Bond v. United States*, 564 U.S. 211, 220 (2011). Should the Court view the matter differently, ordinary third-party standing doctrine independently supports this claim given the close relationship between his own compliance and the employer's cooperation, and the genuine hindrance an employer faces litigating its own interests inside someone else's federal criminal case. *See Powers v. Ohio*, 499 U.S. 400 (1991); *Kowalski v. Tesmer*, 543 U.S. 125 (2004); *Singleton v. Wulff*, 428 U.S. 106 (1976); *Warth v. Seldin*, 422 U.S. 490 (1975).

The same third-party-premises problem recurs in Standard Conditions 5 and 6. Standard Condition 5 requires Probation's approval of Mr. Bernecky's residence; to the extent that functions as a standardless veto rather than simple verification of where he lives, it raises the same delegation concern addressed above. Standard Condition 6 permits Probation to visit Mr. Bernecky "at any time at [his] home or elsewhere" and to seize any prohibited item in plain view; at a workplace he does not own, lease, or control, he has no authority to "permit" a search of property that is not his to permit. Both conditions should be clarified consistently with this Part: neither confers any authority over property belonging to someone other than Mr. Bernecky.

**B. The condition's monitoring method — unqualified capture of all computer activity — cannot honor the privilege protection the condition's own text promises, and should be narrowed to exclude indiscriminate keystroke-level logging.**

The condition already contains a privilege-protection clause: monitoring "shall be designed to avoid, as much as possible, reading any privileged information or any private material that is not illegal." *Kunz* construed this identical clause and held it a real, judicially enforceable constraint on Probation — it "constrains the supervisors in their surveillance . . . by requiring them not to abuse their power and access," not surplusage. 68 F.4th at 766-67. Keystroke-level logging, which by definition captures every character typed — including privileged attorney-client communications, before any determination of privilege is even possible — cannot be reconciled with a monitoring method the condition's own text requires to avoid reading such material. The point is not that no privilege protection exists in the text; it is that keystroke-level capture, as a category, is incapable of honoring the protection the Judgment already promises.

This Circuit's own case law concerning this exact offense type supports the narrowing directly. In *United States v. Sofsky*, 287 F.3d 122, 126-27 (2d Cir. 2002), this Circuit held that "a more focused restriction, limited to pornography sites and images," is an adequate, narrower alternative for an image-based offense. And in *United States v. Lifshitz*, 369 F.3d 173, 192 (2d Cir. 2004), this Circuit itself observed that constant, content-blind monitoring "might be more like searching [a defendant's] diary . . . than . . . the highly targeted diagnosis accomplished by drug testing," and — on remand — specifically approved keyword- or phrase-triggered monitoring as a narrower alternative to "recording all varieties of computer-related activity." *Id.* at 193 n.11. Mr. Bernecky's offense is entirely pictorial; nothing about image-based possession justifies capturing the content of everything he writes.

The Court should modify Special Condition (a) to require that CIMP be implemented through a method — domain- or file-type review, image-hash scanning, or keyword-triggered flagging of the kind *Lifshitz* itself approved — that does not indiscriminately capture privileged or non-image content, rather than through full keystroke capture.

**C. "Internet capable device" should mean what its own words say — a device capable of accessing, storing, or displaying image content — not any device that happens to connect to the internet for an unrelated purpose.**

The Second Circuit has already told this district how to read CIMP conditions like this one. Construing the identical Western District of New York boilerplate at issue here, the Second Circuit refused a literal reading of the condition's "use . . . encryption" language, holding it "would be absurd to interpret" Probation as requiring permission every time a supervisee visits an ordinary "https" website or uses an encrypted messaging service. *Kunz*, 68 F.4th at 764-65. The court explained that "a sensible reading of the restrictions neutralizes the most troubling of those concerns." *Id.* at 754-55. This is not a novel interpretive tool — courts do not read plain condition language to compel "the impossible," *id.* at 763 (citing *United States v. Johnson*, 446 F.3d 272, 281 (2d Cir. 2006)) — and the same absurdity-avoidance principle runs through the general canon of construction. *See Public Citizen v. U.S. Dep't of Justice*, 491 U.S. 440, 454-55 (1989); *Griffin v. Oceanic Contractors, Inc.*, 458 U.S. 564, 571 (1982); *Green v. Bock Laundry Mach. Co.*, 490 U.S. 504, 509 (1989).

Apply that same tool here, and the absurdity of the broadest possible literal reading is not hard to find. The ordinary household today is filled with devices that connect to the internet for a single, narrow purpose entirely unrelated to images: a refrigerator that orders groceries when supplies run low; a video doorbell that streams a delivery notification to a phone; a fitness tracker that uploads a heart rate overnight; a CPAP machine that reports a sleep-apnea patient's breathing pattern to his physician while he sleeps. Every one of these is, in the most literal sense, an "internet capable device." Not one of them has a web browser, an application store, or any capacity whatsoever to locate, store, or display an image of any kind. No one — not the government, not Probation, not this Court — would seriously suggest that a fitness tracker or a sleep-therapy machine must be enrolled in CIMP simply because it has a Wi-Fi chip. Consider, too, the paradigmatic thing a person "operates": not a laptop, not a phone — a vehicle. One uses a laptop; one operates a car. *See State v. Henderson*, 907 N.W.2d 623 (Minn. 2018) (construing "operate" a motor vehicle to mean "any act that causes a motor vehicle to function or controls the functioning of the motor vehicle"). An ordinary car today ships with built-in cellular telematics — OnStar, FordPass Connect, Tesla's own connectivity systems — making it, too, an "internet capable device" by any literal measure. Nobody believes a refrigerator, a doorbell, a fitness tracker, or a family car must be surveilled like a personal computer. The government cannot have it both ways: either it accepts that absurd consequence, or it concedes what *Kunz* already establishes — the condition's real, operative boundary is functional capability and nexus to the conduct at issue in this case, the capacity to access, store, or display images, not literal internet-connectedness.

That boundary is not foreign to the condition's own text; it is already there, applied inconsistently. The condition opens with a capability qualifier — "any internet capable device" — but the separate monitoring-authorization clause drops that qualifier and instead reaches any device "owned or operated by the defendant." Restoring internal consistency to the condition's own drafting logic is not a request to import a foreign concept; it is a request that the condition mean, throughout, what its own opening sentence already says. Multiple circuits have accepted device-capability thresholds of exactly this kind — distinguishing devices by what they can access, not by who owns them or where they are kept. *E.g.*, *United States v. Ullman*, No. 14-1414, 2015 WL 3559221 (10th Cir. June 8, 2015) ("devices without Internet access, such as gaming systems, are not restricted"). This threshold governs which of Mr. Bernecky's own devices are subject to monitoring; it has no bearing on, and concedes nothing about, devices belonging to an employer or any other third party, which Part I.A, above, places outside the condition's reach for the independent reason that no § 5F1.5 finding was ever made.

It is entirely sufficient, and considerably less intrusive, to prohibit Mr. Bernecky outright from using a device he does not own, rather than to authorize monitoring, inspection, or capability-parity demands directed at a third party's property. A flat prohibition on using another person's device achieves the government's interest at zero cost to any non-party.

**D. The condition's fee requirement lacks any payment methodology or ability-to-pay mechanism.**

Special Condition (a) requires Mr. Bernecky to "pay the cost of monitoring services" without a dollar figure, a rate-setting procedure, or any mechanism accounting for ability to pay. Judicial Conference policy directs that fee language "should not inadvertently delegate the establishment of the amount of the co-payment to the probation officer," and encourages a "court-approved sliding scale . . . responsive to changes in a defendant's financial circumstances." No such scale, schedule, or procedure appears anywhere in this record. *Bearden v. Georgia*, 461 U.S. 660 (1983), requires a willfulness and bona-fide-efforts inquiry before nonpayment of a financial obligation may be treated as a basis for revocation; nothing in the record extends that principle to this condition. To the extent Mr. Bernecky is or becomes Medicaid-eligible and CIMP services are Medicaid-covered, a payment obligation risks conflicting with the federal rule that a Medicaid provider may not bill a beneficiary beyond the plan's permitted cost-sharing amount. 42 C.F.R. § 447.15. This same defect recurs in Special Conditions (b), (g), and (h), addressed in Parts II, VI, and VII, below, and the remedy requested is the same throughout: an express ability-to-pay mechanism, not a separate ability-to-pay doctrine reargued four times.

**E. Requested relief.** The Court should enter an order: (i) declaring that, absent any § 5F1.5 finding in the record, Special Condition (a) confers no authority over an employer's own systems and imposes no obligation on any employer to demonstrate anything about its own monitoring capability; (ii) modifying the condition's monitoring method to exclude indiscriminate keystroke-level capture; (iii) clarifying that "internet capable device" means a device capable of accessing, storing, or displaying image content, and that Mr. Bernecky is prohibited from, rather than required to permit monitoring of, another person's device; (iv) clarifying that Standard Conditions 5 and 6 confer no authority over property Mr. Bernecky does not own, lease, or control; and (v) requiring that any fee obligation under this condition be subject to a court-approved, ability-to-pay-adjusted sliding scale.


### II. Special Condition (b) — Sex-Offense-Specific Treatment

Special Condition (b) requires Mr. Bernecky to "follow the rules and regulations" of a sex-offense-specific treatment program, with no court-set limit on what those rules may contain, and separately requires him to "contribute to the cost of services rendered."

**A. A treatment provider's own rules cannot exceed what this Court itself ordered.** Only the sentencing court may impose restrictions on a defendant's liberty as a condition of supervised release; that authority cannot be delegated wholesale to a private provider. *Matta*, 777 F.3d at 122. In *Browder*, a treatment provider's own participant agreement required advance permission before *any* contact with minors, including the defendant's own children — exceeding the sentence the court had actually imposed, which expressly excluded his own children from that restriction. The Second Circuit held the provider's rule unenforceable: "the Probation Office was plainly without power to impose it." 866 F.3d at 514. Mr. Bernecky's concern is the same in kind: a program category the court did order, whose specific, unreviewable content is set entirely by the provider. The Court should enter clarifying language establishing that Mr. Bernecky cannot be found in violation for declining to comply with a provider rule that exceeds the scope of what this Court itself ordered.

One further point belongs here, offered as principle rather than as an independent legal claim. Cognitive-behavioral therapy is a legitimate and valuable treatment modality. Using continuous CIMP surveillance data as raw material for a provider's effort to reshape a specific thought pattern is a different thing, and it is not what CBT is meant to be used for. The Constitution has long recognized that "no official . . . can prescribe what shall be orthodox . . . in matters of opinion." *West Virginia State Bd. of Educ. v. Barnette*, 319 U.S. 624, 642 (1943). Mr. Bernecky does not ask the Court to revive a possessory right to obscene material of the kind *Stanley v. Georgia*, 394 U.S. 557 (1969), addressed and *Osborne v. Ohio*, 495 U.S. 103 (1990), correctly declined to extend to child pornography, given the harm to real children and the government's interest in destroying the market for such material. His concern is different: no court has ever authorized this specific surveillance-to-treatment mechanism, and that lack of authorization — not a claim about the content of his thoughts — is what the delegation principle above already reaches.

**B. Fee-bearing requirement.** As in Part I.D, above, nothing sets a payment methodology or accounts for ability to pay. The Court should apply the same relief requested there: an express ability-to-pay mechanism consistent with *Bearden*.


### III. Special Condition (c) — Contact with Minors and Loitering

Special Condition (c) prohibits "deliberate contact with any child under 18 years of age, excluding his biological or adopted children." Mr. Bernecky has no biological or adopted children; the only carve-out the condition provides has always been, and remains, a null set.

**A. As written, the condition functions as a *de facto* ban on ordinary family life, not merely a limit on contact with a specific niece or nephew.** The practical effect of the null carve-out is not narrow. Mr. Bernecky cannot attend an ordinary family gathering at all, because he cannot know in advance whether a minor family member will be present, and the condition gives him no way to find out safely. If he stops by his mother's house for Christmas and his sister arrives with her children, he is, on the condition's literal terms, already in violation unless he leaves immediately — not because anyone has ever found him a danger to those children, but because the condition does not distinguish an unplanned family visit from the conduct the sentencing court was actually trying to prevent. The same is true if grandchildren stop by unannounced. Nothing in the record addresses whether Mr. Bernecky poses a physical danger to any specific family member, or whether ordinary, incidental presence at family events — as opposed to some other kind of contact — presents any risk at all.

Familial-association rights are not confined to the nuclear family; they extend to the extended-family relationships — grandparent, sibling, cousin — that give this condition's illusory carve-out its bite. *See Moore v. City of East Cleveland*, 431 U.S. 494 (1977) (protecting the choice to live with extended family, and requiring "careful scrutiny" of any government intrusion on it). This district's own case law requires an individualized basis before a restriction of this breadth may stand. *United States v. Myers*, 426 F.3d 117, 129-30 (2d Cir. 2005) (heightened scrutiny and an individualized record required where a condition implicates a fundamental familial-association interest; vacating a contact-with-own-child condition imposed on the identical record-silence present here). *United States v. Bleau*, 930 F.3d 35, 42-43 (2d Cir. 2019), confirms a court must explain the necessity of a contact-with-minors condition unless obvious from the record, and vacated one where the offense did not involve inappropriate contact with minors. Candidly, Mr. Bernecky's record is not *Bleau*'s clean-record case — this is at least his second child-pornography-related conviction, and this motion does not ask the Court to overlook that. It asks instead for a structure that accounts for it: contact with minor family members permitted in the presence of a knowledgeable adult — his mother, father, or sister, all of whom already know of his conviction — so an ordinary family gathering does not require case-by-case advance approval, or an unplanned departure, every time a minor family member happens to be there. Other circuits recognize this "knowledgeable adult" structure as an accepted, workable condition that reads restrictions on contact with minors "to exclude incidental encounters." *United States v. DaSilva*, 844 F.3d 8 (1st Cir. 2016).

Mr. Bernecky's sister having children during his incarceration is, independently, a genuine changed circumstance under *Lussier*'s own terms: the specific family members whose presence at ordinary gatherings gives rise to this problem did not exist, and could not have been the subject of any individualized finding, at the time of sentencing.

**B. The condition should also be clarified to impose no occupational restriction.** Ordinary, incidental workplace contact with a minor — a coworker who is seventeen, a customer in the course of Mr. Bernecky's duties — is not "deliberate contact" requiring advance approval or removal from the situation, for the same reason set out in Part I.A, above: no § 5F1.5 finding exists to support treating this condition as an occupational restriction either.


### IV. Special Condition (d) — Financial Monitoring

Special Condition (d) grants Probation blanket access to Mr. Bernecky's personal and business financial information "to monitor . . . compliance with not buying or subscribing to online services that provide child pornography." Mr. Bernecky has never been alleged to have purchased child pornography; his offense of conviction is possession. A financial-monitoring condition whose stated purpose is detecting purchase-type conduct has no factual anchor in a record that involves none. The condition should be modified to require a reasonable-suspicion predicate before financial information is accessed — the same structure the Judgment already uses for computer monitoring itself, where examinations are triggered "as triggered by impermissible/suspicious activity" rather than conducted on a standing basis. Special Condition (d) should track that same, internally consistent model.


### V. Special Condition (e) — Sex Offender Registration and Presentence Report Disclosure

Special Condition (e) authorizes release of Mr. Bernecky's presentence report to the New York State Board of Examiners of Sex Offenders, and further disclosure "to the county court and to the parties involved in the determination of the defendant's final classification level." This Circuit places the burden of demonstrating a "compelling" and "particularized" need for disclosure of a presentence report on the party seeking it, at the time disclosure is sought — not on a standing, Judgment-level authorization entered years in advance with no opportunity for the defendant to be heard on the question when disclosure actually occurs. *See United States v. Charmer Indus., Inc.*, 711 F.2d 1164 (2d Cir. 1983). A blanket advance waiver of that particularized-need showing is difficult to reconcile with a standard that expressly places the burden on the party requesting disclosure and requires a case-specific showing at the time disclosure is sought.

The remaining registration language in this same condition is, independently, surplusage: Mandatory Condition 6 already requires registration under the Sex Offender Registration and Notification Act, 34 U.S.C. § 20901 *et seq.* The Court should strike the blanket PSR pre-authorization and the duplicative registration language, without prejudice to any case-specific disclosure request properly made, with notice to Mr. Bernecky, at the time such a request actually arises.


### VI. Special Condition (g) — Polygraph and Computerized Voice Stress Analyzer Testing

Special Condition (g) requires Mr. Bernecky to submit to polygraph and CVSA examinations and to "answer the questions posed," preserving only a right to challenge the *use* of his statements after the fact. Of every condition in this Judgment, this is the one where the stakes are constitutional rather than practical, and it deserves to be treated that way.

**A. The condition's after-the-fact model does not resolve the actual problem: Mr. Bernecky will not answer any polygraph or CVSA question, on any subject, without counsel present to advise him in real time.** That is his position, stated plainly, because the choice the condition currently gives him is not a real choice: answer first, under an unassisted, real-time guess about what the privilege covers, and litigate the guess later if it turns out to matter. *United States v. Johnson*, 446 F.3d 272 (2d Cir. 2006), upheld the after-the-fact structure — but only against the narrow immunity/waiver theory Johnson raised, on facts specific to his own documented history. Johnson never addressed either of Mr. Bernecky's actual objections: that whether a specific question is privileged is a pure question of law a layperson cannot reliably apply under pressure in real time, or that a blanket, prospective refusal to answer *any* question absent protections is materially different from answering and later challenging use of the answer. Properly understood, *Minnesota v. Murphy*'s "classic penalty situation" doctrine does the real work: if the State, "either expressly or by implication, asserts that invocation of the privilege would lead to revocation," the resulting statement is compelled. 465 U.S. 420, 435 (1984). A condition requiring Mr. Bernecky to "answer the questions posed," with no privilege determination made before he must do so, asserts exactly that. *United States v. Von Behren*, 822 F.3d 1139, 1150-51 (10th Cir. 2016), applies this doctrine to a materially similar sex-offense polygraph and holds a threat of program termination and revocation for refusing incriminating questions unconstitutional compulsion. The privilege reaches not only direct confessions but any answer furnishing "a link in the chain of evidence" needed to prosecute. *Hoffman v. United States*, 341 U.S. 479, 486 (1951). This is at least his second child-pornography-related conviction, so any further compelled disclosure carries materially higher stakes than for a first-time offender — weighing in favor of the requested protections, not against them. And there is no factual predicate to weigh on the other side of that scale: Mr. Bernecky has never been found dishonest in treatment or supervision, and a condition premised on presumed dishonesty, without a single actual instance of it in the record, cannot satisfy § 3583(d)'s reasonably-related standard regardless of how the privilege question is resolved.

**B. The requested protections.** Mr. Bernecky requests that, until and unless each of the following is in place, he is entitled to decline to answer any polygraph or other quasi-scientific examination question: (i) appointed counsel present before, during, and after any such examination; (ii) counsel afforded adequate time, per question, to assess whether invocation is warranted — a determination of law, not one a layperson can be expected to make correctly under pressure; (iii) the examiner's credentials established to a degree sufficient to support a *Daubert* challenge, reaching separately the reliability of any CVSA examination; and (iv) no disclosure of any examination-derived information to any person or agency without leave of court.

No federal court appears to have addressed a request of precisely this kind, and that should be said plainly rather than disguised as settled law. This is best presented as a discretionary safeguard this Court has authority to craft under 18 U.S.C. § 3583(d) — not as a claim that the polygraph room is custodial and requires Miranda-style protection, a framing that would invite an unfavorable comparison to authority rejecting a counsel-presence claim on custodial-interrogation grounds. *See J.B. v. New Jersey State Parole Bd.*, 229 N.J. 21 (2017). It is enough that whether a question is privileged is a legal judgment, and that existing federal supervision policy already treats this determination as too delicate to leave to a probation officer alone, directing that uncertain invocations be referred to the court rather than resolved unilaterally. Guide to Judiciary Policy, Vol. 8E, § 190.45. If the Court is unwilling to order counsel's presence throughout, Mr. Bernecky requests, in descending order, consultation access at any point during the exam, modeled on the grand jury practice of permitting a witness to step outside to consult counsel, *see United States v. Mandujano*, 425 U.S. 564 (1976); or, at minimum, advance written review of the question list by counsel, with any disputed question referred to the Court rather than resolved by the examiner.

**C. This same-case continuation is different from a request to extend the right to a new offense, and this Court should say so before the government does.** The Sixth Amendment's critical-stage doctrine is two-step: attachment, then critical-stage analysis. *Rothgery v. Gillespie County*, 554 U.S. 191 (2008). Attachment is offense-specific, *see Texas v. Cobb*, 532 U.S. 162 (2001); *McNeil v. Wisconsin*, 501 U.S. 171 (1991); *Kirby v. Illinois*, 406 U.S. 682 (1972), which forecloses this argument only as to a wholly new, uncharged offense — where the right has never attached at all. It does not answer the different question here: whether the right persists through the ongoing supervision phase of the *same* case in which it already attached at indictment. Supervision is a continuing consequence of the same conviction, not a new offense. The harder authority on that question is *Pennsylvania v. Finley*, 481 U.S. 551 (1987), and the *Gagnon v. Scarpelli*, 411 U.S. 778 (1973), and *Morrissey v. Brewer*, 408 U.S. 471 (1972), line, which treats post-conviction proceedings as due process rather than Sixth Amendment territory, and must be confronted directly rather than left for the government to raise first. *Estelle v. Smith*, 451 U.S. 454 (1981), is the best affirmative analogy: a compelled psychiatric examination was a critical stage because it was tied to a still-pending sentencing determination. *Finley*'s finality concern — no Sixth Amendment right survives "exhaustion of the appellate process" — never arose in *Estelle*, because that proceeding, unlike an ordinary closed conviction, was still pending. Mr. Bernecky's supervision is different in kind for the same reason: the sentencing court retains live, ongoing jurisdiction over it, which is the entire premise of this motion.

**D. Counsel should be appointed to assist with these examinations directly, and this request is not offered as a hedge.** 18 U.S.C. § 3006A(a)(1)(E) already requires that representation "shall be provided" to a financially eligible person who "faces modification . . . of a condition . . . of a term of supervised release" — a category the polygraph relief sought throughout this Part falls squarely within, since resolving that condition's procedural protections is precisely what this motion asks the Court to do. The interests of justice independently point the same direction: appointing counsel to help a financially eligible person navigate a real, present choice between answering under compulsion and risking revocation for silence is not a convenience extended for this motion's paperwork — it is what protecting the constitutional right actually at stake requires. Mr. Bernecky presents the choice as it actually is, not as an alternative to be softened: either the Court appoints counsel to assist him with these examinations as described in Part VI.B, above, or, absent that appointment, Mr. Bernecky will invoke his Fifth Amendment privilege categorically as to any and all such examinations going forward, rather than attempt to make real-time legal privilege determinations unassisted and under pressure.

**E. Fee-bearing requirement.** As in Part I.D, above, the same relief is requested: an express ability-to-pay mechanism.


### VII. Special Condition (h) — Mental Health Treatment and Alcohol Abstinence

Special Condition (h) requires Mr. Bernecky to participate in mental health treatment and, "while in treatment or taking psychotropic medication," to abstain from alcohol entirely, and separately requires him to "contribute to the cost of services rendered."

**A. The alcohol-abstinence requirement should be physician-gated, not a blanket rule.** Mr. Bernecky does not object to mental health treatment. He objects to a blanket abstinence rule that applies regardless of whether any physician has ever determined that alcohol actually interacts with a medication or treatment he is receiving. This Circuit has vacated a comparable blanket alcohol restriction for lack of the individualized nexus § 3583(d) requires. *United States v. Betts*, 886 F.3d 198 (2d Cir. 2018) (vacating a total alcohol ban as "not reasonably related to any of the factors outlined in Section 5D1.3(b)," where neither the offense nor any violation conduct involved alcohol and no evidence of alcohol abuse was in the record). The Second Circuit has since applied the identical rule to a materially indistinguishable "abstain from alcohol during treatment" clause, rejecting the government's argument that the provision could simply be reimposed without an individualized assessment. *See United States v. Rodriguez*, No. 22-1820-cr (2d Cir. Dec. 7, 2023) (summary order).

The clause's own disjunctive structure sharpens the problem independently: it applies "while in treatment **or** taking psychotropic medication," which means abstinence is triggered by talk therapy alone, with no medication involved at all — a circumstance in which no possible medication-interaction rationale exists, even in principle. And even within the medication branch, treating "psychotropic medication" as a single, uniform risk category does not track clinical reality, since alcohol-interaction risk varies substantially by drug class. This exact condition template — including the identical alcohol clause — is this district's own standard-form mental-health-treatment condition, adopted in the wake of the Western District of New York's own remand in *Browder*; it was not the product of any individualized finding specific to Mr. Bernecky.

The Court should modify Special Condition (h) to provide that the alcohol-abstinence requirement applies only when, and for as long as, a treating physician determines that alcohol use is contraindicated by a specific medication or treatment Mr. Bernecky is then receiving.

**B. Fee-bearing requirement.** As in Part I.D, above, the same relief is requested.


### VIII. Special Condition (i) — General Financial Disclosure and Asset Restraint

Special Condition (i) grants Probation broad financial-disclosure and asset-restraint authority, expressly tied to "the collection of any unpaid fine or restitution." Mr. Bernecky believes the $500 restitution obligation has been paid in full. If the record confirms this, the condition's predicate no longer exists, and *Lussier* confirms no separate showing of changed circumstances is required to vacate a condition whose stated purpose has been fully satisfied. 104 F.3d at 36.


### IX. Standard Condition 8 — No Communication with Felons

Standard Condition 8 bars Mr. Bernecky from "communicat[ing] or interact[ing]" with anyone known to have a felony conviction, absent advance permission. The population this condition reaches is not a small one: by some estimates, approximately five percent of adults in the United States have been convicted of a felony at some point in their lives — a substantial enough share of the general public that a blanket communication ban, without an incidental-contact or knowledge-timing exception, is all but guaranteed to be triggered by ordinary daily life rather than by anything resembling a deliberate association with crime. As written, this is a communication ban, not merely an association restriction — the condition uses both words disjunctively, and "communicate" reaches a single, momentary exchange in a way "associate" does not — and it reaches core protected conduct with no incidental-contact or knowledge-timing exception: requesting an autograph from a public figure with a felony conviction, attending a rally advocating for felon voting rights, or exchanging ordinary pleasantries with a coworker or neighbor whose record becomes known only after the fact. It also places Mr. Bernecky in direct conflict with Special Condition (b)'s own requirement that he participate in group treatment alongside other convicted individuals, and with the practical reality that housing available to registered sex offenders routinely places them in proximity to other felons — a proximity the government's own residency and registration regime helped create.

*United States v. Reeves*, 591 F.3d 77, 80-81 (2d Cir. 2010), confirms the general vagueness/overbreadth principle applicable here — "[a] condition is too vague if it requires a reasonable person to speculate as to its meaning" — and confirms that this Circuit recognizes intimate personal association as a protected liberty interest against which a condition of this kind must be measured, even though *Reeves* itself concerned a romantic-disclosure requirement rather than a felon-communication ban. No case squarely narrows a "communicate or interact" felon condition to require a sustained relationship, and this motion says so candidly rather than overstating existing authority; the argument proceeds from first principles and from the condition's own disjunctive text. The Court should narrow this condition to a restriction on cohabitation with, or harboring of, a known felon, or, at minimum, add an incidental-contact and knowledge-timing exception consistent with ordinary civic and workplace life.


### X. Standard Condition 12 — Risk Notification

Probation's own pre-release materials describe this condition in its pre-*Boles* form, unmodified to reflect the district's own corrective standing order. *See United States v. Boles*, 914 F.3d 95 (2d Cir. 2019) (holding the standard "risk" condition vague and remanding for the condition's scope to be clarified). Mr. Bernecky respectfully requests that the Court direct that the existing WDNY standing order be applied to him, as it already applies district-wide. No facial challenge to the standing order's mechanism is made here.[^1]
