## 4. JURISDICTION AND STANDARD OF REVIEW

1. **18 U.S.C. § 3583(e)(2)**: the Court "may... modify, reduce, or enlarge
   the conditions of supervised release, at any time prior to the expiration
   or termination of the term of supervised release, pursuant to the
   provisions of the Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure relating to the
   modification of probation." Note the express cross-reference to
   Fed. R. Crim. P. 32.1(c) as the governing procedural vehicle.
2. ***United States v. Lussier***, 104 F.3d 32, 36 (2d Cir. 1997): § 3583(e)(2)
   is a forward-looking, general punishment-policy tool; a threshold showing
   of changed circumstances is not a required element. **Important limiting
   principle to state candidly, not concede as a weakness but frame
   correctly:** § 3583(e)(2) does not reach the legality/validity of a
   condition — that is what direct appeal or § 2255 is for. *Id.* at 33.
   **This is exactly why the motion is framed as present-tense
   clarification/reassessment throughout, not as a claim that the 2018
   Judgment was invalid when entered** — state this expressly in this
   section so the framing is doctrinally grounded from the first page, not
   just a rhetorical choice.
3. **U.S.S.G. § 5D1.4(a)** (2025) (Policy Statement): "At any time prior to
   the expiration or termination of the term of supervised release, the
   court may modify, reduce, or enlarge the conditions of supervised release
   whenever warranted by an individualized assessment of the appropriateness
   of existing conditions. See 18 U.S.C. § 3583(e)(2). The court is
   encouraged to conduct such an assessment in consultation with the
   probation officer after the defendant's release from imprisonment."
   **This is the single best "why now" authority in the entire case** —
   cite it prominently here.
4. **Statutory expressio unius, correctly framed (per the corrected
   analysis — see client-directives.md correction):** 18 U.S.C.
   § 3583(e)(1) (early termination) contains an explicit one-year floor
   ("at any time after the expiration of one year of supervised release");
   § 3583(e)(2) (modification, the provision at issue here) contains no
   analogous time-trigger at all. **Frame this as Congress's own choice, not
   an inference about the Sentencing Commission's drafting** — U.S.S.G.
   § 5D1.4(b) simply cross-cites and implements the statute's own (e)(1)
   one-year rule ("See 18 U.S.C. § 3583(e)(1)"), while § 5D1.4(a)
   implements (e)(2)'s no-time-trigger rule. Confirms there is no procedural
   defect in bringing this motion now, before supervision has even begun.
5. **Post-2018 legal developments as "changed circumstances" (controlling
   framing directive):** every argument below that rests on a legal
   development postdating the June 2018 sentencing (*Boles* (2019), *Betts*
   (2018), *Carlineo* (2021), *Kunz* (2023), *Burnash*/*Gorychka* (2024),
   *Love* (2025), *Brown* (2026), the November 2025 § 5D1.3/5D1.4
   amendments) should note that development as an independent
   changed-circumstances hook under *Lussier*'s own framing — even though,
   per *Lussier*, no such showing is strictly required.

## 6. ARGUMENT SECTION A — NO CONDITION IN THIS JUDGMENT IMPOSES AN
OCCUPATIONAL RESTRICTION, BECAUSE NONE WAS EVER AUTHORIZED (flagship
section — ~7 pages)

### A.1 Lead framing: the Judgment's silence on employment is the best
evidence of what the sentencing court actually intended

**Lead authority (corrected per client pushback — do NOT lead with
*Maiorana*):** the delegation-doctrine line —
- ***United States v. Matta***, 777 F.3d 116, 122 (2d Cir. 2015) (power to
  impose special conditions of supervised release "is vested exclusively in
  the district court").
- ***United States v. Browder***, 866 F.3d 504, 514 (2d Cir. 2017)
  (treatment provider's own rule exceeding the court's actual sentence:
  "Because such a requirement... would contradict, rather than execute,
  Browder's sentence, the Probation Office was plainly without power to
  impose it").
- ***United States v. Kunz***, 68 F.4th 748, 766-67 (2d Cir. 2023)
  (construing identical WDNY CIMP boilerplate; privilege clause is a real,
  enforceable constraint on Probation, not surplusage).
- ***United States v. Carlineo***, 998 F.3d 533 (2d Cir. 2021) (cite as
  supporting, not leading, authority — distinguished on its own facts from
  Bernecky's condition, which names a real recognized program category,
  unlike Carlineo's wholly undefined program).

Argument: Probation's administration of the CIMP condition to reach an
employer's own systems, and to impose a capability-parity demand with no
textual basis, is not something the sentencing court authorized — it is
Probation exercising authority "vested exclusively in the district court."
The remedy requested is a clarifying order, not a ruling that the 2018
Judgment was invalid.

**Footnote only, not body-text lead (per corrected framing):** *Maiorana*,
153 F.4th 306 (2d Cir. 2025) (en banc), overruling *Truscello*'s
oral-pronouncement doctrine, may be cited in a narrow a fortiori footnote:
if an unpronounced condition cannot bind a defendant at all, still less can
a validly pronounced condition be stretched by silent inference to cover
territory (employment) it never addressed. **Do not present this as a
direct-fit lead authority — it governs formation of new conditions, not
interpretation of an existing one's scope.**

### A.2 § 5F1.5's two-part nexus/necessity test was never satisfied

- **U.S.S.G. § 5F1.5(a)**: requires "a reasonably direct relationship
  existed between the defendant's occupation, business, or profession and
  the conduct relevant to the offense of conviction" plus a necessity
  finding — quoted verbatim within *Brown*, 173 F.4th 45 (2d Cir. 2026).
- The Love/Jenkins/Ruff/Lombardi/Piper line treats a mandatory
  employer-**notification** condition as a de facto occupational
  restriction requiring § 5F1.5 findings:
  - ***United States v. Love***, No. 24-1706-cr, 2025 WL 1077176 (2d Cir.
    Apr. 8, 2025).
  - ***United States v. Jenkins***, 854 F.3d 181 (2d Cir. 2017).
  - ***United States v. Lombardi***, No. [VERIFY docket] (2d Cir. Mar. 22,
    2018) (summary order).
  - ***United States v. Ruff***, No. [VERIFY docket] (2d Cir. Nov. 14,
    2019) (summary order) (vacated outright).
  - ***United States v. Piper***, No. [VERIFY docket] (2d Cir. Mar. 30,
    2020) (summary order) (remanded).
- **A fortiori point:** if a mere mandatory *notification* clause triggers
  § 5F1.5 in this line of cases, the PO's considerably more burdensome,
  textually unsupported capability-parity *demand* here is at least as
  strong a candidate — especially since Bernecky's own condition contains
  no notification requirement at all; he voluntarily notified the employer,
  going further than the condition required.
- **Distinguish *Brown*, 173 F.4th 45 (2d Cir. 2026), carefully and
  affirmatively (per client's corrected pushback — do not concede it as
  controlling):**
  1. Plain-error posture: Brown did not object at sentencing; research the
     general doctrine on whether a plain-error affirmance's statement of
     the governing rule carries full precedential weight versus a narrower
     "not plainly wrong" holding, and apply it to Brown's Part III.B
     specifically (part10 already did this — condense into 1-2 sentences
     with a cite to the doctrine).
  2. Brown's actual theory of harm was different in kind: Brown argued the
     condition functioned as a **compelled** employer-notification
     requirement; Bernecky does not object to notification at all — he
     already did it voluntarily. Brown's holding, keyed to the compelled-
     notification theory, may not even reach a claim that isn't about
     compelled disclosure.
  3. Brown's own condition contained an **"as permitted by the employer"**
     veto clause — a built-in employer consent-gate Bernecky's condition
     lacks entirely. This is an affirmative textual distinction, not just a
     factual one.
- **Proportionality/no-nexus point specific to this job:** the contact-lens
  manufacturer position requires no public internet access; the employer
  already offered an internet-disabled internal account. CIMP's stated
  purpose (monitoring internet-facilitated risk) has minimal application
  here, yet Bernecky remains suspended anyway.

### A.3 Ultra vires / no authority over the non-party employer; standing

- No textual basis anywhere in the condition for reaching employer systems
  or demanding third-party capability proof.
- **Standing (client directive — needs to read as ironclad, not assumed):**
  lead with ***Bond v. United States***, 564 U.S. 211 (2011) ("Bond seeks to
  vindicate her own constitutional interests") — Bernecky's injury (lost
  employment, personal exposure to a violation/revocation risk from an
  unauthorized demand) is direct and personal, not a derivative assertion of
  the employer's own Fourth Amendment rights. Backstop, if the court frames
  it differently: ***Warth v. Seldin***, 422 U.S. 490 (1975); ***Powers v.
  Ohio***, 499 U.S. 400 (1991); ***Kowalski v. Tesmer***, 543 U.S. 125
  (2004); ***Singleton v. Wulff***, 428 U.S. 106 (1976).
- **New primary support, directly parallel fact pattern:** ***United States
  v. Mike***, 632 F.3d 686, 696-97 (10th Cir. 2011), applying ***United
  States v. Matteson***, 327 F. App'x 791 (10th Cir. 2009) — held a
  computer-monitoring condition unconstitutionally vague where it could be
  read to reach devices the defendant did not own, expressly including his
  employer's computers, and rejected the government's argument that the
  condition "cannot fairly be read" that broadly. Persuasive only
  (out-of-circuit), but squarely on point, read in full. **Note for
  drafting: Mike's own remand instructions expressly tied the
  employer-reach question to the occupational-restriction findings
  requirement** ("If on remand the court decides that it intends for the
  computer monitoring conditions to apply to computers owned by Mike's
  employers, it must make the necessary findings"), reinforcing the direct
  link between this sub-section and A.2 above — cite it in both places,
  cross-referenced.

### A.4 Place/device-capability tailoring (Track 19)

- Bernecky's own condition already opens with an "internet capable device"
  threshold qualifier — but the separate monitoring-authorization clause
  ("owned or operated by the defendant") drops that qualifier entirely.
  **Frame as restoring internal consistency to the condition's own drafting
  logic, recalibrated to image-capability given this offense — not as
  importing a foreign concept.**
- Capability-based device scoping (vs. "any connected device") is an
  accepted, multi-circuit drafting convention: ***United States v.
  Granger***, 117 F. App'x 247 (4th Cir. 2004) (unpublished) [VERIFY];
  ***United States v. Rath***, 2015 WL 3559160 (5th Cir. 2015) [VERIFY];
  ***United States v. Ullman***, 2015 WL 3559221 (10th Cir. 2015) [VERIFY];
  ***United States v. Fletcher***, 435 F. App'x 578 (8th Cir. 2011)
  (unpublished) [VERIFY]; ***United States v. Phillips***, 370 F. App'x 610
  (6th Cir. 2010) (unpublished) [VERIFY].
- **Forbid-rather-than-monitor structure for third-party/borrowed devices:**
  a clean prohibition on using another person's personal device achieves
  the government's interest with zero reach into non-party property —
  cleaner than even the employer-veto "Path 2" language (see A.5), since it
  needs no third-party cooperation at all. First-impression synthesis of

## 7. ARGUMENT SECTION B — THE COURT SHOULD NARROW CIMP'S MONITORING METHOD
TO EXCLUDE KEYSTROKE-LEVEL LOGGING (Track 18 — ~3 pages after trim)

1. **Existing privilege clause + Kunz's construction of it, correctly
   framed (per the corrected "existing carve-out cannot be honored" framing
   — do not claim no privilege language exists):** the Judgment's own CIMP
   condition already contains a privilege-avoidance clause ("designed to
   avoid, as much as possible, reading any privileged information..."). See
   *Kunz*, 68 F.4th at 766-67 (construing the identical clause as a real,
   judicially enforceable constraint — "constrains the supervisors in their
   surveillance... by requiring them not to abuse their power and access").
   Keystroke-level logging, which by definition captures every character
   typed — including privileged attorney-client communications before any
   privilege determination is possible — cannot be reconciled with a clause
   requiring Probation to avoid reading such material.
2. **Pictorial-nexus/narrow-tailoring:** ***United States v. Sofsky***, 287
   F.3d 122, 126-27 (2d Cir. 2002) (quoted in *Lifshitz*, 369 F.3d at 189) —
   "a more focused restriction, limited to pornography sites and images" is
   an adequate narrower alternative for this exact offense type.
   ***United States v. Lifshitz***, 369 F.3d 173, 191-93 & n.11 (2d Cir.
   2004) — the "searching his diary" comparison for constant, content-blind
   capture; footnote 11's own suggestion of keyword/phrase-triggered
   monitoring as a narrower alternative to "recording all varieties of
   computer-related activity."
3. **Honest limits to state, not omit:** no case uses the phrase
   "attorney-client privilege" in this exact context; no case decides
   whether automated capture/storage (before human review) violates a
   clause phrased in terms of avoiding "reading" privileged material — a
   genuinely open question, favorable in that nothing forecloses it.
4. **Requested relief:** a clarifying/modifying order that the existing
   privilege clause requires Probation to implement CIMP using a method
   that does not indiscriminately capture privileged content (e.g.,
   domain/image-hash scanning, or keyword-triggered flagging per *Lifshitz*
   footnote 11) rather than full keystroke capture.
