## 2. Cases Matching Our Tracked [VERIFY] List — Primary-Source Confirmation

Of the tracked list (Johnson, Von Behren, Murphy, Balon, Carlineo, Boles, Charmer Industries, Lussier, Brown, Betts, Matta, Kunz, Reeves, Moore, Troxel, Peterson, Mingo, Hoffman, Lifshitz, Leone), the following were **independently confirmed via an actual opinion I read in full** (not via an AI memo's citation):

### *United States v. Browder*, 866 F.3d 504 (2d Cir. 2017) — read in full, primary source
- **Holding on narrow tailoring (quoting Lifshitz verbatim):** "[A] monitoring condition must be narrowly tailored, and not sweep so broadly as to draw a wide swath of extraneous material into its net." And: "[A] computer monitoring condition 'must bear "a close and substantial relation" to the government's interest in pursuing the search,' but it need not employ 'the least intrusive means.'" (866 F.3d at 511, quoting *Lifshitz*, 369 F.3d at 190, 192).
- **"Common sense reading" language: CONFIRMED ABSENT.** I read the entire opinion; the phrase "common sense" does not appear anywhere in it. **The prior research pass's flag is resolved: Browder does NOT contain "common sense reading" interpretive language.** (That phrase does appear in a *different* case in this folder — the 10th Circuit's *Mike* opinion, quoting the 5th Circuit's *Brigham* — not in Browder, and not in the Second Circuit at all on this record.)
- **Matta quoted directly:** "'The power to impose special conditions of supervised release ... is vested exclusively in the district court.' *Matta*, 777 F.3d at 122." — genuine primary-source confirmation of *Matta*'s holding language.
- Held Browder's CIMP-violation finding reasonable but remanded for the district court to "re-state the terms of Browder's computer monitoring condition with greater specificity" — leading directly to document #9 below.

### *United States v. Browder*, W.D.N.Y. Decision and Order, March 14, 2018 (Vilardo, J.) — read in full, primary source
**This is the single most important document found in the entire review.** On remand, Judge Vilardo re-drafted the CIMP condition with the following language:

> "The defendant shall not use or possess any computer, data storage device, or any internet capable device unless the defendant participates in the Computer and Internet Monitoring Program (CIMP), or unless authorized by the Court or the U.S. Probation Office. The defendant must provide the U.S. Probation Office advance notification of any computer(s), automated service(s), or connected device(s) that will be used during the term of supervision. The U.S. Probation Office is authorized to install any application as necessary to surveil all activity on computer(s) or connected device(s) owned or **used** by the defendant. The defendant will be required to pay the cost of monitoring services..."

Compare Bernecky's actual Special Condition (a): identical, verbatim, **except Bernecky's Judgment says "owned or operated"** where Browder's 2018 template says "owned or **used**." This is essentially the exact source template for WDNY's current CIMP condition. Implications:
- This confirms the condition's WDNY provenance and gives a direct textual history to argue from.
- The court in this same order **expressly rejected the keystroke-logging challenge**: "Browder argues that capturing every keystroke and recording all activity is overbroad... But the Second Circuit found to the contrary in this very case, approving the practice." The court analogized computer monitoring to blood-alcohol testing (the tester may only look for what they're authorized to look for) and held six-month data retention reasonable. **This is adverse authority on the client's keystroke-logging theory** — it should be confronted directly, not ignored, in the motion.
- The court also held the defendant must provide notice only of devices he "will use," not all devices to which he has "access" — relevant to the client's "operated" vs. broader verbs argument, though it cuts the other way in the "used" vs. "operated" comparison (Bernecky's word is actually *narrower* than "used," not broader — worth flagging to the client since this differs from the assumption that "operated" was the more expansive term).
