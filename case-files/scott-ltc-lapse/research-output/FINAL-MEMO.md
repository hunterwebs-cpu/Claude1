## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**The policy is alive, and there is a real, contract-based path to more than what the client
currently has.** This is not a "dead policy" problem. Three things are true simultaneously:

1. **A real asset already exists and should be confirmed/preserved now.** The 2022 nonpayment
   converted the policy to paid-up status with a $70,845.71 benefit pool at $525/day — confirmed
   in force as recently as January 2026. This should be affirmatively claimed regardless of
   anything else below.
2. **A stronger claim may exist to restore full coverage** (originally a $1,149,750 lifetime
   pool on a 6-year benefit period) under the policy's own §5.4 "Added Protection Against
   Lapse" clause, which allows reinstatement to full status — not just the reduced paid-up
   amount — if Pamela was already benefit-eligible (chronically ill, cognitively impaired, or
   ADL-dependent) on the date of termination (~April 2022). This is a contractual right,
   reinforced by an identical NJ regulatory provision (N.J.A.C. 11:4-34.5(b)).
3. **The insurer likely failed to follow its own required lapse process.** No third-party
   designee was ever on file, despite the policy and NJ regulation requiring John Hancock to
   have obtained a signed designee-or-waiver at issuance in 2000 and to have sent her reminders
   of that right (annually, once she passed age 62½). Nothing in the client's files shows John
   Hancock actually sent the required 30-day written pre-termination notice. Under binding NJ
   Supreme Court authority (*Meier v. New Jersey Life Ins. Co.*), the burden is on **John
   Hancock**, not the client, to prove that notice was properly given before a policy can be
   treated as validly converted/terminated.

**The recommended path is advocacy first, litigation second — not because litigation is weak,
but because the two threshold facts that make or break every legal theory (Pamela's cognitive/
functional condition in early-to-mid 2022, and whether John Hancock actually sent the required
notices) are not yet known, and a document demand + regulatory complaint is the fastest, lowest-
cost way to get them.**

## PART I: WHAT WE KNOW VS. WHAT WE NEED

### Established from the client's files
- Original policy: $525/day, 6-year benefit period, 5% simple inflation, $1,149,750 lifetime
  limit, issued 1/26/2000.
- Premium rose to $6,052.96/year effective 1/26/2022 via a DOBI-approved phased rate increase.
- Premium went unpaid; policy lapsed roughly 65 days after the due date (~early April 2022).
- The policy's Contingent Nonforfeiture Protection Endorsement automatically converted it to
  paid-up status: **Policy Limit $70,845.71, $525/day, $0 premium** — confirmed in every annual
  statement from January 2023 through January 2026.
- No third-party designee was ever on file ("NO DESIGNEE SELECTED" on every notice, 2020–2022).
- The policy's §5.4 clause and NJ's N.J.A.C. 11:4-34.5(b) both provide a right to full
  reinstatement (not just the reduced paid-up amount) if Pamela was already benefit-eligible
  due to cognitive impairment or loss of functional capacity at the relevant time — a 5-month
  window from termination under both the regulation and (by its own terms) the policy.

### The two facts that determine everything — not yet in file
1. **Pamela's medical/functional condition between roughly January and September 2022.** Was
   she already meeting the policy's own benefit-eligibility definition (chronic illness,
   cognitive impairment per §2.2, or ADL dependency) during that window? This is the single
   most important fact in the entire matter — it is the gating question for the §5.4/11:4-34.5(b)
   full-reinstatement claim, and (per *Pickett v. Lloyd's*) a prerequisite to any bad-faith claim.
2. **What John Hancock actually did in early-to-mid 2022.** No document in the client's
   possession shows the actual 30-day written lapse/termination notice, proof it was mailed to
   Pamela, any waiver-of-designee form from 2000, or any record of whether John Hancock ever
   informed her (or anyone) of the reinstatement alternative before defaulting to the reduced
   nonforfeiture conversion. Under *Meier v. New Jersey Life Ins. Co.*, 101 N.J. 597 (1986)
   (binding), **the burden of proving proper notice is on John Hancock, not the client** — so
   this gap in the file is not fatal, it is the leverage point.

## PART II: LEGAL THEORIES, RANKED

| # | Theory | Basis | Strength | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Contract claim — full reinstatement under §5.4** | Policy's own "Added Protection Against Lapse" clause; reinforced by N.J.A.C. 11:4-34.5(b) | **Strongest** | Contingent entirely on Fact #1 (medical condition in 2022). If proven, restores the original $1,149,750/6-year benefit, not just $70,845.71. |
| 2 | **Contract claim — defective lapse notice voids the 2022 termination** | Policy §5.2 Grace Period clause; N.J.A.C. 11:4-34.5(a)(1)-(2); *Meier v. N.J. Life Ins. Co.*, 101 N.J. 597 (1986) (binding — burden on insurer) | **Strong** | Independent of Fact #1. If John Hancock cannot produce proof of the required notice and designee-reminder mailings, there is a real argument the "conversion" was never validly effected in the first place. |
| 3 | **Common-law bad faith / breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing** | *Pickett v. Lloyd's*, 131 N.J. 457 (1993) (binding) | **Solid, but gated** | Requires Facts #1 and #2 to both cut favorably first (*Pickett*'s threshold bar: no bad-faith claim without an underlying right to the benefit). Damages measured by the value gap ($1,149,750 vs. $70,845.71) plus foreseeable consequential losses; punitive damages unlikely absent evidence of a broader company pattern. |
| 4 | **NJ Consumer Fraud Act** — framed around deception/nondisclosure of rights, not benefit amount | *Lemelledo*, 150 N.J. 255 (1997); *Weiss v. First Unum*, 482 F.3d 254 (3d Cir. 2007); *Alpizar-Fallas v. Favero*, 908 F.3d 910 (3d Cir. 2018) (all binding/persuasive as noted) | **Plausible, higher payoff, higher risk** | Treble damages + mandatory fees if it survives dismissal. Must be pled as "insurer never properly disclosed the designee mechanism or the reinstatement alternative" — NOT as "insurer paid too little," which NJ courts reject (*Myska*, *Pierzga*, *Nikiper*). |
| 5 | **NJ DOBI regulatory complaint** | N.J.A.C. 11:4-34.5; N.J.S.A. 17B:30-13.1; N.J.S.A. 17:29B-4(9) | **No private right of action, but zero-cost and procedurally favored** | *R.J. Gaydos Ins. Agency v. National Consumer Ins. Co.*, 168 N.J. 255 (2001) (binding) affirmatively directs exactly this sequencing — DOBI referral before/alongside civil suit. Use to force document production and, potentially, trigger a market-conduct exam (John Hancock already had a $26.3M NY DFS consent order in Aug. 2022 for wrongfully terminating 156 LTC policies 2001–2019 — a different population, not this client, but real pattern-of-practice leverage). |

**Foreclosed / not viable, confirmed by research — do not pursue:**
- A standalone civil suit for "violation of the NJ lapse-notice regulation" itself (no private
  right of action — *Gaydos*; *Lemelledo*; *In re Comm'r of Ins.'s March 24, 1992 Order*, 132
  N.J. 209 (1993), all binding).
- NJ Insurance Fair Conduct Act (2022) — confirmed limited to first-party auto UM/UIM claims;
  does not reach LTC.
- NJ Adult Protective Services Act / SAFE Act — neither reaches an insurance carrier by its
  own terms; no reporting or heightened-duty obligation runs from John Hancock to Pamela under
  either statute.
- Rate-increase-itself-was-improper theory — courts have rejected this where (as here) the
  increase was DOBI-approved and disclosed in bold/cap language (*Rakes v. Life Investors Ins.
  Co.*, 582 F.3d 886 (8th Cir. 2009), persuasive). Do not lead with this; lead with the notice/
  reinstatement defects instead.

## PART III: RECOMMENDED PLAN OF ATTACK

### Phase 1 — Fact development (run in parallel, start immediately)

1. **Medical/functional timeline.** Gather all medical records, care logs, family
   correspondence, prior LTC claim activity (if any), and any contemporaneous notes bearing on
   Pamela's cognitive and functional status from roughly **late 2021 through late 2022** —
   specifically whether she would have met the policy's own §2.2 Cognitive Impairment
   definition (SPMSQ score, ADL supervision needs) or otherwise qualified for benefits (but for
   the 100-day elimination period) during that window. This is the single highest-priority
   task — it determines whether Theory #1 (full reinstatement) is even available, and it has
   its own urgency independent of any notice/process argument.
2. **Document demand to John Hancock.** Send a written request (ideally from counsel) for the
   complete policy administration file: the 2000 underwriting file (designee designation or
   waiver form), all third-party-designee reminder notices sent 2000–2022, the actual 30-day
   pre-termination lapse notice purportedly sent in early-to-mid 2022 (and proof of mailing),
   any internal claims notes or correspondence from that period, and the specific calculation
   worksheet for the $70,845.71 conversion. Frame this as a precondition to evaluating
   reinstatement — not yet as a demand for relief — to keep the door open for a cooperative
   resolution.
3. **NJ DOBI complaint (Consumer Inquiry and Response Center).** File in parallel with the
   document demand, not after it. A DOBI complaint costs nothing, requires no private-suit
   standing, and DOBI's practice of requiring the carrier to "respond to all aspects" of a
   complaint functions as a second, independent channel to force production of the same
   materials — with the added credibility of a state regulator's request. Reference the August
   2022 NY DFS $26.3M consent order (156 wrongfully terminated LTC policies, 2001–2019) as
   pattern-of-practice context inviting DOBI to take the complaint seriously, while being clear
   this client's policy is not part of that settlement.

### Phase 2 — Contingent on Phase 1 results

- **If medical records show benefit-eligibility in the Jan–Sept 2022 window:** send a formal
  written demand invoking §5.4/N.J.A.C. 11:4-34.5(b) for full reinstatement, supported by the
  medical proof and payment (or tender) of the overdue premium. This is the strongest, most
  direct path and should be pursued whether or not the notice defect is ever resolved.
- **If John Hancock cannot produce proof of the required 30-day/designee notices:** pursue the
  contract-notice-defect argument under *Meier* regardless of the medical facts — this could
  support unwinding the 2022 lapse entirely (not merely qualifying for reinstatement) since
  the termination may never have been validly effected under the policy's own terms.
- **If both come back unfavorable or John Hancock stonewalls:** the $70,845.71 paid-up benefit
  remains real and should be formally claimed regardless — confirm the claims process and file
  now so it is not lost to any administrative lapse of its own.
- **Bad faith / CFA claims are the fallback if advocacy fails**, not the opening move — per
  *Pickett*'s threshold bar and the entire-controversy-doctrine timing risk flagged in *Wadeer
  v. N.J. Mfrs. Ins. Co.*, 220 N.J. 591 (2015), any such claim should be prepared to be filed
  together with (not after) any reinstatement/contract claim, once the Phase 1 facts are in hand.
