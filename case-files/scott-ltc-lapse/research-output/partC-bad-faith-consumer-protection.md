# TRACK C: Bad Faith / Unfair Claims Practices / Consumer Protection Theories Under New Jersey Law

**Matter:** Pamela Vandeveer Jeanes — John Hancock long-term care insurance policy (issued NJ, 2000); lapsed 2022 after premium rate increase; converted via contractual nonforfeiture provision to paid-up policy worth $70,845.71 (down from $1,149,750 lifetime benefit); no third-party designee on file.

## Executive Summary

1. **No private right of action exists under New Jersey's Unfair Claims Settlement Practices Act/regulations** for an LTC policyholder. Enforcement is exclusively by the Commissioner of Banking and Insurance (N.J.S.A. 17:29B-7). New Jersey's 2021 Insurance Fair Conduct Act (IFCA), which *does* create a private right of action, is **statutorily limited to first-party UM/UIM automobile claims** and does **not** reach life, health, or LTC insurance. This is a critical negative finding — it forecloses a direct statutory claim.
2. **Common-law bad faith / breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing** (*Pickett v. Lloyd's*, 131 N.J. 457 (1993)) remains the live private-law analogue, and the regulatory scheme (UCSPA/UCSPR, and especially the LTC-specific unintentional-lapse regulation, N.J.A.C. 11:4-34.5) is expressly usable as evidence of "state policy" under *Pickett* itself, even though it creates no direct cause of action.
3. **The New Jersey Consumer Fraud Act (N.J.S.A. 56:8-1 et seq.) does apply to insurance transactions** (*Lemelledo v. Beneficial Mgmt Corp.*, 150 N.J. 255 (1997)), but New Jersey courts sharply distinguish an insurer's "refusal to pay benefits" (not CFA-covered, per *Myska*/*Pierzga*/*Nikiper*) from fraud/misrepresentation in the "subsequent performance" of the policy, including discontinuation of previously authorized coverage (CFA-covered, per *Weiss v. First Unum* and *Alpizar-Fallas v. Favero*). How these facts are framed will make or break a CFA claim.
4. **No New Jersey elder-abuse or Adult Protective Services statute imposes a reporting or heightened-scrutiny duty on insurance carriers** toward cognitively impaired insureds. The APS Act's "caretaker" definition does not reach an insurer, and the mandatory-reporting "SAFE Act" is limited to broker-dealers/investment advisers regulated by the Bureau of Securities. The functional equivalent of a heightened elder-protective duty in this fact pattern comes not from elder-law statutes but from the LTC-specific nonforfeiture/reinstatement regulation itself (N.J.A.C. 11:4-34.5(b)), which is squarely on point.

## 1. New Jersey's Unfair Claims Settlement Practices Act and Regulations

### 1.1 The statutory/regulatory scheme

**N.J.S.A. 17:29B-4(9)** (part of the Insurance Trade Practices Act, N.J.S.A. 17:29B-1 et seq.)
- **Binding** (NJ statute)
- **Holding/text:** Declares it an unfair method of competition/unfair or deceptive act for an insurer to engage in "unfair claim settlement practices," defined (subsection (9)) to include — per secondary-source compilation, structure confirmed across multiple aggregators but exact subsection lettering **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING]** against official N.J.S.A. — misrepresenting policy provisions; failing to acknowledge and act reasonably promptly on communications; failing to adopt reasonable standards for prompt investigation of claims; refusing to pay claims without conducting a reasonable investigation; not attempting in good faith to effectuate prompt, fair, and equitable settlement of claims in which liability has become reasonably clear; and compelling claimants to institute litigation by offering substantially less than amounts ultimately recovered. Quoted directly in *Pickett*, 131 N.J. at 467-68: N.J.S.A. 17:29B-4(9)(f) prohibits "[n]ot attempting in good faith to effectuate prompt, fair and equitable settlements of claims in which liability has become reasonably clear."
- **Verification status:** Core prohibition and the (f) subsection language **VERIFIED** via direct quotation in *Pickett v. Lloyd's* opinion text (CourtListener, opinion id 1464124). Full lettered list of all subsections (a)-(n) **NOT independently verified against primary source** — official njleg.gov statutory text was not reachable by automated fetch (403/blocked). **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING]**
- **Application:** The statute requires the violative conduct occur "with such frequency as to indicate a general business practice" to constitute an actionable unfair practice for regulatory purposes — a single insured's mishandled lapse, standing alone, may not meet that threshold for a DOBI market-conduct action, but John Hancock's 2022 across-the-board LTC premium increase likely triggered a wave of similarly situated lapses/conversions, which is worth developing factually (via a public-records/DOBI complaint or discovery) to show a "general business practice" — this could support both a stronger *Pickett* bad-faith claim (evidence of knowing/reckless disregard) and a regulatory complaint to DOBI urging a market-conduct exam.

**N.J.S.A. 17B:30-13.1** (Unfair claim settlement practices — Title 17B, the Insurance Code provision most directly applicable to a life/health/LTC insurer such as John Hancock)
- **Binding**
- **Holding/text:** "No person shall engage in unfair claim settlement practices in this State." Then lists (per Justia's codification, structure cross-checked) categories substantially mirroring N.J.S.A. 17:29B-4(9): misrepresenting policy facts/coverage provisions; failing to acknowledge communications promptly; lacking reasonable standards for claim investigation; refusing payment without investigation; delaying affirmation of coverage after proof of loss; failing to settle fairly once liability is clear; compelling litigation via lowball offers; and failing to promptly provide a reasonable explanation of the basis for denial with reference to policy provisions and facts.
- **Verification status:** Existence and general prohibition **VERIFIED** (title/citation confirmed via Justia and multiple secondary sources, and this is the statute the NJ DOBI's own regulatory subchapter, N.J.A.C. 11:2-17, is promulgated to implement together with 17:29B-4). Full verbatim lettered text **NOT independently confirmed against official source** — attempted direct fetch of law.justia.com and findlaw.com returned 403 errors. **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING]**
- **Application:** Directly on point — failure to give required lapse notice, failure to notify a designee, and failure to explain the cognitive-impairment reinstatement alternative before defaulting to the reduced nonforfeiture conversion would, if proven, fall within several of these categories (failure to acknowledge/explain, failure to settle fairly, misrepresenting or omitting policy provisions regarding the reinstatement right). But see §1.3 below — this statute does **not** itself give Pamela's family a lawsuit; it operates only through DOBI enforcement or as evidence in a common-law claim.

**N.J.A.C. 11:2-17.1 to -17.15** (Unfair Claims Settlement Practices regulations, implementing 17:29B-4(9) and 17B:30-13.1)
- **Binding** (validly promulgated NJ agency regulation)
- **Holding/text:** Sets "minimum standards for the settlement of claims which, if violated with such frequency as to indicate a general business practice, would constitute unfair claims settlement practices." N.J.A.C. 11:2-17.9 (Rules for fair and equitable settlements applicable to **life and health insurance**) prohibits, among other things: marking a claim file "closed" or "final" when additional benefits may be due without informing the claimant in writing what additional proof is needed (11:2-17.9(a)-(b)); rescinding a policy after the contestable period based on undisclosed information not specifically requested on the application (11:2-17.9(j)); and issuing an explanation-of-benefits that includes reasons for denial/reduction that do not actually apply to the specific claim (11:2-17.9(l)). N.J.A.C. 11:2-17.4(b) requires that "[a]ll correspondence to a claimant required of an insurer pursuant to this subchapter shall be written in easy to read and understandable terms."

Favero*, 908 F.3d 910, 914 (3d Cir. 2018), as "the New Jersey Unfair Claims Settlement Practices Regulations").
- **Application:** Section 11:2-17.9's requirement of clear written communication about what is needed to preserve additional benefits, and the general "easy to read and understandable terms" mandate of 11:2-17.4(b), are directly relevant if John Hancock's lapse/conversion correspondence to Pamela (or, more pointedly, its failure to correspond with any third-party designee) obscured or omitted the cognitive-impairment reinstatement alternative under N.J.A.C. 11:4-34.5(b) (see §1.2 and §4 below).

**N.J.A.C. 11:4-34.5 (Unintentional lapse) — the LTC-specific regulation, the single most important regulatory provision in this case**
- **Binding**
- **Holding/text (verbatim structure, verified via direct Cornell LII fetch and cross-checked against Justia Regulations and a NJ DOBI rule-proposal notice):**
  - Subsection (a)(1): Carriers must obtain from the applicant, at or before issuance, either (i) a written designation of at least one person, in addition to the applicant, to receive notice of lapse or termination for nonpayment of premium, or (ii) a signed waiver declining such designation. The form must provide space for at least one designee's full name and home address. Carriers must notify the insured of the right to change the designation "no less often than once every two years" for insureds under age 62½, and "no less than annually" for insureds age 62½ and older.
  - Subsection (a)(2): "No individual long-term care policy or group certificate shall lapse or be terminated for nonpayment of premium or required contribution unless the carrier, at least 30 days before the effective date of the lapse or termination, has given written notice to the insured and to [any designated person]," transmitted by first-class United States mail, effective five days after mailing.
  - Subsection (b): Policies must include a provision for **restoration of coverage** in the event of lapse or termination if the carrier receives proof, within **five months after termination**, that the policyholder was **cognitively impaired or suffered a loss of functional capacity** before the premium was due and unpaid, permitting collection of past-due premiums as a condition of restoration. Critically: **the standard of proof for cognitive impairment/loss of functional capacity may be no more stringent than the benefit eligibility criteria contained in the policy itself.**
- **Verification status:** **VERIFIED** — obtained via direct fetch of Cornell LII regulatory text (law.cornell.edu/regulations/new-jersey/N-J-A-C-11-4-34-5) with subsection structure cross-confirmed by a second independent web search summary and by a NJ DOBI rule-proposal document reference (nj.gov/dobi/pn02_046.htm, discussing the third-party-designee lapse-notice rule for LTC). This regulation implements the NAIC Long-Term Care Insurance Model Act/Regulation provisions New Jersey adopted; exact numbering (a)(1)/(a)(2)/(b)) should still be cross-checked against the current N.J.A.C. text via Lexis/Westlaw before pleading, since administrative codes are renumbered periodically. **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — confirm current subsection numbers]**
- **Application — this is the crux of the case:** The facts state (1) no third-party designee was ever on file, and (2) the family is investigating whether required notice was given before termination. N.J.A.C. 11:4-34.5(a)(1) required John Hancock to have obtained either a designee or a *signed waiver* from Pamela at issuance in 2000, and to have reminded her of the right to name a designee at least annually (given her likely age bracket by 2022) or biennially for 22 years. If no waiver is in the underwriting file and no reminders were sent, that alone is a regulatory violation with obvious evidentiary value. Separately, subsection (b) means that **if Pamela was already cognitively impaired or functionally incapacitated at the time of the 2022 lapse — likely provable using the same benefit-eligibility standard the policy itself uses for LTC claims — she had an unconditional right to full reinstatement (not merely the reduced nonforfeiture conversion) if that proof was submitted within five months of termination.** Whether the family/insured were ever informed of this right, and whether John Hancock offered it before defaulting to the $70,845.71 nonforfeiture conversion, is the single most important factual question for both the contract-track claims and this bad-faith track.

### 1.2 The nonforfeiture regulation

**N.J.A.C. 11:4-34.24 (Nonforfeiture benefit requirement)**
- **Binding**
- **Holding/text:** Requires that LTC policies include a nonforfeiture benefit providing "paid-up long-term care insurance coverage after lapse," calculated at not less than 100% of the sum of all premiums paid less claims paid, subject to a floor of "30 times the daily nursing home benefit at the time of lapse," with coverage continuing at "the same benefits (amounts and frequency) in effect at the time of lapse but not increased thereafter," up to the nonforfeiture credit limit. Also separately establishes a "contingent benefit upon lapse" triggered by premium increases exceeding specified percentage thresholds tied to issue age — offering the insured the choice of reduced benefits, conversion to paid-up status, or (if no election is made) automatic conversion.
- **Verification status:** **VERIFIED** via direct Cornell LII fetch; exact dollar/percentage figures and effective dates (applies to policies issued after January 18, 2006, per the regulation's applicability clause) should be **[VERIFIED BEFORE FILING]** against the policy's 2000 issue date — a 2000-issued policy may be grandfathered under an earlier nonforfeiture standard or the specific NAIC Model provision incorporated by its own policy language, which is a contract-interpretation question for the other tracks, not this one.
- **Application:** Establishes the regulatory floor for the paid-up conversion amount ($70,845.71) as a baseline, but does not itself excuse noncompliance with the separate notice/reinstatement regulation (11:4-34.5). The interplay is important for the bad-faith analysis: an insurer that mechanically applies the contingent-benefit/nonforfeiture default without first exhausting its notice and reinstatement obligations under 11:4-34.5 has, at minimum, no "debatable" defense that both regulatory tracks were honored.

### 1.3 Is there a private right of action?

**No.** This is well established and was directly litigated (and lost) in *Alpizar-Fallas v. Favero*, 908 F.3d 910, 914 (3d Cir. 2018) (applying NJ law): "the District Court first dismissed [plaintiff's] class action claim to the extent it alleged a violation of the [Unfair Claims Settlement Practices Regulations] because that set of regulations does not provide a private right of action" — a holding the Third Circuit did not disturb on appeal (the plaintiff did not challenge it). *Pickett v. Lloyd's* itself confirms this at 131 N.J. 457, 482: "Although the regulatory framework does not create a private cause of action, it does declare state policy" — and the Court used that state policy as the foundation for recognizing the *common-law* bad-faith cause of action instead. Enforcement of N.J.S.A. 17:29B-4(9)/17B:30-13.1 is committed exclusively to the Commissioner of Banking and Insurance under **N.J.S.A. 17:29B-7**, which authorizes cease-and-desist orders and civil penalties of up to $1,000 per violation (up to $5,000 if the insurer "knew or reasonably should have known" of the violation), collected by the Commissioner in a summary enforcement proceeding. — **Citation/text VERIFIED for the *Pickett*/*Alpizar-Fallas* holdings (primary source, pinpoint quotes above); the specific $1,000/$5,000 penalty figures in 17:29B-7 are drawn from secondary-source summaries and should be [VERIFIED BEFORE FILING] against the official statutory text.**

The **New Jersey Insurance Fair Conduct Act (IFCA)**, N.J.S.A. 17:29BB-1 et seq. (P.L. 2021, c.388, effective January 18, 2022) — the one recent NJ statute that *does* create a private right of action against an insurer for UCSPA-type violations — is **expressly limited to "first-party claimants" defined as individuals "entitled to the uninsured or underinsured motorist coverage of an insurance policy."** It has no application to life, health, or long-term care insurance. **This is a negative but important finding: IFCA is not available here.** — **Binding statute; scope-limitation finding VERIFIED via the statutory text summary and multiple independent secondary sources (Lewis Brisbois, Chartwell Law, Scura) all describing IFCA as UM/UIM-only; the operative definitional language should still be pulled directly from N.J.S.A. 17:29BB-2 before filing.** **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING]**

**Practical upshot for Track C:** There is no direct statutory lawsuit here. The UCSPA/UCSPR and the LTC-specific regulations function as (a) the evidentiary backbone for a common-law bad-faith claim (§2), and (b) grounds for a parallel regulatory complaint to the NJ Department of Banking and Insurance, which could trigger a market-conduct examination given the apparent frequency of similar 2022 rate-increase-driven lapses across John Hancock's NJ LTC book — a complaint track worth pursuing regardless of the litigation track, since DOBI action requires no private standing showing and could generate discoverable findings.

## 2. Common-Law Bad Faith / Breach of the Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing

### *Pickett v. Lloyd's*, 131 N.J. 457, 621 A.2d 445 (1993)
- **Binding** (NJ Supreme Court; controlling statement of the first-party bad-faith standard)
- **Verbatim controlling text (pinpoint N.J. Reports cites):**
  - The "fairly debatable" standard: "If a claim is 'fairly debatable,' no liability in tort will arise." 131 N.J. at 487 (quoting *Bibeault v. Hanover Ins. Co.*, 417 A.2d 313, 319 (R.I. 1980)).
  - Elements: "To show a claim for bad faith, a plaintiff must show the absence of a reasonable basis for denying benefits of the policy and the defendant's knowledge or reckless disregard of the lack of a reasonable basis for denying the claim." 131 N.J. at 487.
  - The Court's final formulation (Conclusion): "The scope of that duty is not to be equated with simple negligence. In the case of denial of benefits, bad faith is established by showing that no debatable reasons existed for denial of the benefits. In the case of processing delay, bad faith is established by showing that no valid reasons existed to delay processing the claim and the insurance company knew or recklessly disregarded the fact that no valid reasons supported the delay." 131 N.J. at 495.
  - Threshold bar: "a claimant who could not have established as a matter of law a right to summary judgment on the substantive claim would not be entitled to assert a claim for an insurer's bad-faith refusal to pay." 131 N.J. at 487-88.
  - Delay-specific test (adopting Colorado's *Travelers Ins. Co. v. Savio* framework): bad faith in delay requires "(1) the insurer's conduct is unreasonable, and (2) the insurer knows that the conduct is unreasonable or recklessly disregards the fact that the conduct is unreasonable." 131 N.J. at 487-88.
  - Relationship to UCSPA: "Although the regulatory framework does not create a private cause of action, it does declare state policy and we do not think that finding a cause of action for the breach of the duty of good faith and fair dealing would conflict with that policy." 131 N.J. at 482.
  - Damages: "we believe that the familiar principles of contract law will suffice to measure the damages" (131 N.J. at 488); consequential/economic losses recoverable if "fairly within the contemplation of the insurance company" (131 N.J. at 495); but "absent egregious circumstances, no right to recover for emotional distress or punitive damages exists for an insurer's allegedly wrongful refusal to pay a first-party claim" (131 N.J. at 489-90) — punitive damages require "something other than a breach of the good-faith obligation as we have defined it" (131 N.J. at 489-90).
- **Verification status:** **VERIFIED** — full opinion text read via CourtListener (opinion id 1464124), all quotes pulled directly from the html_with_citations text with star-pagination.
- **Application to these facts:** *Pickett*'s framework maps directly onto the two available theories here:
  - **Denial/termination theory:** Did John Hancock have a "debatable reason" to treat the policy as validly and finally converted to the reduced nonforfeiture benefit, as opposed to (a) properly notifying a designee (none was on file — potentially because the insurer never obtained the mandatory waiver in 2000 or never sent the periodic reminders required by N.J.A.C. 11:4-34.5(a)(1)), and (b) processing a reinstatement under the cognitive-impairment provision of N.J.A.C. 11:4-34.5(b) if Pamela was already benefit-eligible/impaired at the time of lapse? If the underwriting file shows no designee waiver and no reminder mailings over 22 years, and if medical/claims records show Pamela met the policy's own benefit-eligibility (cognitive impairment) standard before the 2022 nonpayment, there may be **no debatable basis** for John Hancock's choice to apply the lesser remedy (nonforfeiture conversion) instead of the greater one (reinstatement) — precisely the *Pickett* "no debatable reasons" showing.
  - **Processing/delay theory:** If John Hancock's claims/administration unit had information suggesting cognitive impairment (e.g., from prior claim submissions, from the family, or from its own outreach attempts) and nonetheless processed the file on the assumption of straightforward nonpayment without investigating reinstatement eligibility, that is analyzable under the *Savio*/*Pickett* delay-bad-faith test — unreasonable conduct plus knowledge or reckless disregard of its unreasonableness.
  - **Threshold gate:** Under *Pickett*'s bar (131 N.J. at 487-88), the family must be able to show Pamela would have been entitled to summary judgment on the underlying substantive claim — i.e., that she in fact met the cognitive-impairment/benefit-eligibility standard at the time of lapse, and that proper notice was not given — before a bad-faith claim can proceed. This makes the medical-eligibility and notice-compliance factual record (likely developed in Tracks A/B) a prerequisite to a viable Track C bad-faith claim, not a separate showing.
  - **Damages exposure:** Under *Pickett*, damages would be measured under contract principles — the delta between the $1,149,750 original lifetime benefit (or the value of the reinstatement she should have received) and the $70,845.71 paid-up value actually received, plus any consequential losses (e.g., self-funded LTC costs) that were foreseeable to the insurer. Emotional distress and punitive damages are foreclosed absent "egregious circumstances" or independent tortious conduct — a high bar, though evidence of a knowing, company-wide pattern of shortchanging cognitively impaired NJ LTC policyholders during the 2022 rate-increase wave could potentially supply it.

### *Wadeer v. New Jersey Manufacturers Insurance Co.*, 220 N.J. 591, 110 A.3d 19 (2015)
- **Binding** (NJ Supreme Court)
- **Holding:** Reaffirms and refines the "fairly debatable" standard, holding that an insurer may rely on a fairly debatable position even in rejecting an arbitration award, and addresses procedural traps — the entire controversy doctrine can bar a bad-faith claim not raised together with the underlying benefits dispute, and the bad-faith claim's accrual is tied to the underlying claim's resolution, not to some later point.
- **Verification status:** Holding and procedural posture **substantially confirmed** via multiple independent secondary-source summaries (Justia case summary, Wilson Elser and Clark & Fox law-firm analyses) describing this as a companion decision to *Cynthia Badiali v. New Jersey Manufacturers Insurance Group* addressing NJ's bad-faith standard; full opinion was not independently read line-by-line for this memo. **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — read full opinion text before citing for its precise holding and quotable language.]**
- **Application:** Procedural warning, not substantive: if the family pursues an administrative/regulatory track (DOBI complaint) or a declaratory/reinstatement claim first, counsel must ensure any bad-faith claim is raised in the same action or joined promptly, to avoid an entire-controversy-doctrine bar analogous to what defeated the plaintiff in *Wadeer*.

### *Rova Farms Resort, Inc. v. Investors Insurance Co. of America*, 65 N.J. 474, 323 A.2d 495 (1974)
- **Binding** (NJ Supreme Court — foundational predecessor case)
- **Holding:** New Jersey's original recognition that an insurer's bad-faith mishandling of a claim (there, refusal to settle within policy limits, exposing the insured to an excess verdict) can give rise to liability beyond the policy's face value; cited by commentators as the doctrinal root from which *Pickett*'s first-party "fairly debatable" standard grew.
- **Verification status:** Citation and general holding **confirmed via case-law search** (CourtListener search results, 65 N.J. 474 (1974)) and via secondary-source discussion (Gfeller Laurie LLP article). Full text not independently read for this memo. **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING if cited for specific quoted language — otherwise safe as general background authority.]**
- **Application:** Useful as background/historical framing showing NJ's bad-faith doctrine has deep roots, but *Pickett* is the controlling articulation for first-party claims like this one and should be the primary citation.

## 3. New Jersey Consumer Fraud Act (N.J.S.A. 56:8-1 et seq.)

### Elements
A private CFA claim requires: (1) unlawful conduct (an "unconscionable commercial practice, deception, fraud, false pretense, false promise, misrepresentation, or the knowing concealment, suppression, or omission of any material fact" made "in connection with the sale ... or with the subsequent performance"); (2) an "ascertainable loss" ("either an out-of-pocket loss or a demonstration of loss in value that is quantifiable or measurable"); and (3) a causal relationship between the two. *D'Agostino v. Maldonado*, 216 N.J. 168, 78 A.3d 527, 536-37 (2013); *Marcus v. BMW of N. Am., LLC*, 687 F.3d 583, 606 (3d Cir. 2012) (quoting *Thiedemann v. Mercedes-Benz USA, LLC*, 183 N.J. 234 (2005)). — **Binding/Persuasive as noted; VERIFIED via direct quotation extracted from the *Alpizar-Fallas* opinion text (CourtListener opinion id 4341930), which itself quotes *D'Agostino* and *Marcus*/*Thiedemann* verbatim.**

### *Lemelledo v. Beneficial Management Corp. of America*, 150 N.J. 255, 696 A.2d 546 (1997)
- **Binding** (NJ Supreme Court — controlling on CFA/insurance-exemption question)
- **Verbatim controlling text (pinpoint N.J. Reports cites):**
  - Insurance is covered: "our reading of the CFA convinces us that the statute's language is ample enough to encompass the sale of insurance policies as goods and services that are marketed to consumers," and "[t]hat omission [of insurance from the CFA's text] ... is far from determinative." 150 N.J. at 265-66.
  - The displacement test: "In order to overcome the presumption that the CFA applies to a covered activity, a court must be satisfied ... that a direct and unavoidable conflict exists between application of the CFA and application of the other regulatory scheme or schemes. It must be convinced that the other source or sources of regulation deal specifically, concretely, and pervasively with the particular activity ... We stress that the conflict must be patent and sharp, and must not simply constitute a mere possibility of incompatibility." 150 N.J. at 270-71.
  - The mere existence of another regulator is not enough: "a regulated practice is [not] automatically covered by the CFA or automatically exempt from it based solely on the number of administrative agencies having regulatory jurisdiction over the practice." 150 N.J. at 267-68.
  - Remedial construction: "The language of the CFA evinces a clear legislative intent that its provisions be applied broadly in order to accomplish its remedial purpose, namely, to root out consumer fraud." 150 N.J. at 263-64. "[I]t should ordinarily be assumed that the CFA applies to the covered practice ... because of the strong and sweeping legislative remedial purpose apparent in the CFA." 150 N.J. at 268-69.
  - Outcome: reviewing four insurance/lending-related statutes (including the Insurance Trade Practices Act, i.e., the UCSPA discussed in §1 above), the Court held none created a disqualifying conflict, because "[a]ll four of the statutes have, at least in part, the same general goal as the CFA ... The CFA simply complements those statutes." 150 N.J. at 273 (analysis at 553-55 of the parallel A.2d cite). Disposition: "We affirm the judgment of the Appellate Division reinstating plaintiff's CFA claim." (6-0, Handler, J.)
  - Important limiting dictum: Lemelledo expressly noted, without deciding, that "several lower courts have held that the payment of insurance benefits is not subject to the CFA" (citing *Nikiper v. Motor Club of Am. Cos.*, 232 N.J. Super. 393, 557 A.2d 332, 336 (App. Div. 1989), and *Pierzga v. Ohio Cas. Grp. of Ins. Cos.*, 208 N.J. Super. 40, 504 A.2d 1200, 1205 (App. Div. 1986)) — leaving that specific question open.
- **Verification status:** **VERIFIED** — full opinion read via CourtListener (opinion id 1511116); all quotes pulled directly with pinpoint N.J. Reports pagination.
- **Application:** *Lemelledo* firmly establishes that insurance transactions generally, and LTC insurance administration specifically, are not categorically exempt from the CFA — the UCSPA's existence does not, by itself, knock out a CFA claim over the same conduct. This is an important affirmative finding for the family.

### The "denial/refusal-to-pay-benefits" carve-out — *Myska*, *Pierzga*, *Nikiper*

**Myska v. New Jersey Manufacturers Insurance Co.**, 440 N.J. Super. 458, 114 A.3d 761, 777 (App. Div. 2015)
- **Binding** (NJ Appellate Division — controlling intermediate authority on this point, expressly credited by the Third Circuit as authoritative)
- **Verbatim holding:** The CFA "was not intended as a vehicle to recover damages for an insurance company's refusal to pay benefits." Where "the essence of plaintiffs' causes of action involved whether they filed and supported a claim for a specified amount of benefits under their respective policies," the claims are "not cognizable under the CFA." 114 A.3d at 777.
- **Verification status:** **VERIFIED** — quoted verbatim within the *Alpizar-Fallas* opinion (CourtListener opinion id 4341930), which itself is a primary appellate source directly discussing and applying *Myska*.

### The "subsequent performance" / discontinuation exception — *Weiss* and *Alpizar-Fallas*

**Weiss v. First Unum Life Insurance Co.**, 482 F.3d 254 (3d Cir. 2007)
- **Persuasive** (Third Circuit predicting NJ law — not a NJ state-court decision, but treated as authoritative by NJ federal courts and cited approvingly within *Alpizar-Fallas*)
- **Holding:** The CFA covers "fraud both in the initial sale (where the seller never intends to pay), and fraud in the subsequent performance (where the seller at some point elects not to fulfill its obligations)," 482 F.3d at 266 — i.e., the CFA reaches an insurer's allegedly **fraudulent discontinuation of previously authorized benefit payments**, as distinct from an initial claim denial.
- **Verification status:** Holding language **VERIFIED** via direct quotation appearing in the *Alpizar-Fallas* opinion (CourtListener opinion id 4341930, quoting 482 F.3d at 266).

**Alpizar-Fallas v. Favero**, 908 F.3d 910 (3d Cir. 2018)
- **Persuasive** (Third Circuit applying NJ law — highly relevant, recent, and directly grapples with the UCSPA/CFA interaction)
- **Verbatim holding on UCSPA preclusion:** Rejecting the insurer's argument that the UCSPR (i.e., N.J.A.C. 11:2-17) precludes a CFA claim: "the New Jersey Supreme Court directly addressed any potential conflict between the CFA and the ITPA in Lemelledo and held that none exists ... the fact that a private right of action exists under the CFA but not the ITPA does not create a 'direct and unavoidable conflict' that would preclude application of the CFA here." 908 F.3d at 916-17.
- **Verbatim holding distinguishing *Myska*:** "Here, Alpizar-Fallas alleges neither that she filed an insurance claim nor that she was denied any benefits. Instead, the allegations in her complaint fall squarely within the language of the CFA and our holding in Weiss." 908 F.3d at 917-18. The complaint alleged the insurer's adjuster "falsely represented the nature of the documents" the insured was asked to sign, and that this "same pattern of unlawful conduct" was used against "other similarly situated individuals" — held to state "an allegation of fraud in connection with the subsequent performance of a consumer contract, a situation explicitly covered by the language of the CFA." 908 F.3d at 918.
- **Disposition:** Vacated dismissal and remanded, allowing the CFA claim to proceed.
- **Verification status:** **VERIFIED** — full opinion text read directly via CourtListener (opinion id 4341930), quotes pulled from primary source with pinpoint page cites.
- **Application — this is the key doctrinal fork for the family's CFA theory:** New Jersey/Third Circuit law draws a real, litigated line: (1) a claim that merely re-argues *how much* the insurer owed on a filed benefits claim (*Myska*, *Pierzga*, *Nikiper*) is **not** CFA-actionable; but (2) a claim that the insurer **deceived the insured about her rights or about what a document/process meant**, or **fraudulently discontinued previously authorized/existing coverage** (*Weiss*, *Alpizar-Fallas*), **is** CFA-actionable. On these facts, no LTC benefits claim was ever "filed and denied" in the *Myska* sense — this is not a dispute over the amount of a paid claim. It is a policy-administration event: a 22-year, fully-paid-up policy was unilaterally downgraded through a lapse-and-convert process in which (a) no third-party designee mechanism was ever properly established or maintained, and (b) the family alleges the insurer did not properly explain or offer the reinstatement alternative available for a cognitively impaired insured. If the facts show John Hancock's communications affirmatively represented the nonforfeiture conversion as the only or final outcome — without disclosing the cognitive-impairment reinstatement right under N.J.A.C. 11:4-34.5(b) — that is a materially similar "misrepresentation regarding rights in connection with subsequent performance" to what saved the claim in *Alpizar-Fallas*, and is far closer to *Weiss*'s "discontinuation of previously authorized benefits" than to *Myska*'s "refusal to pay a filed claim." **Ascertainable loss** is straightforward and well-documented: the quantifiable difference between the $1,149,750 original lifetime benefit value (or the value of a proper reinstatement) and the $70,845.71 paid-up conversion actually received satisfies the *Thiedemann*/*Marcus* "quantifiable or measurable" loss standard. A successful CFA claim carries **treble damages and mandatory attorney's fees** (N.J.S.A. 56:8-19) — materially better remedies than the *Pickett* bad-faith claim's plain contract-damages measure.
- **Litigation risk:** The insurer will predictably argue this case is really about a benefits-eligibility dispute (whether Pamela met the cognitive-impairment standard) dressed up as fraud, i.e., a *Myska*/*Pierzga* "refusal-to-pay" case in disguise. Framing the complaint around the *deception/notice/process failures* (no designee ever established; no clear disclosure of the reinstatement alternative; possible misrepresentation that conversion was the only option) rather than around the *substantive benefits determination* will be essential to surviving a motion to dismiss on this theory.

## 4. Heightened Duty of Care / Fiduciary-Like Obligations Toward Elderly Insureds

### New Jersey Adult Protective Services Act, N.J.S.A. 52:27D-406 et seq.
- **Binding** (NJ statute) as to its own terms; **not applicable** to this defendant by its terms
- **Verbatim text:** "Vulnerable adult" means "a person 18 years of age or older who resides in a community setting and who, because of a physical or mental illness, disability or deficiency, lacks sufficient understanding or capacity to make, communicate, or carry out decisions concerning his well-being and is the subject of abuse, neglect or exploitation." "Caretaker" means "a person who has assumed the responsibility for the care of a vulnerable adult as a result of family relationship or who has assumed responsibility for the care of a vulnerable adult voluntarily, by contract, or by order of a court of competent jurisdiction, whether or not they reside together." "Exploitation" means "the act or process of illegally or improperly using a person or his resources for another person's profit or advantage." (Definitions at N.J.S.A. 52:27D-407.) Reporting of suspected abuse/neglect/exploitation of a community-dwelling vulnerable adult to county APS is **voluntary** in New Jersey generally; only law enforcement and certain enumerated healthcare professionals were made mandatory reporters by a 2010 amendment.
- **Verification status:** Definitions **VERIFIED** via direct fetch/search of the statutory text (Justia codification of N.J.S.A. 52:27D-407, cross-checked across multiple independent secondary sources describing the 1994 enactment and 2010 mandatory-reporter amendment). - **Application — negative finding:** An insurance carrier does not fit the "caretaker" definition (it has not assumed responsibility for Pamela's *care* by family relationship, voluntary undertaking, contract, or court order — an insurance contract obligating premium payment and benefit administration is not a "care" relationship in the sense the Act contemplates). ### Safeguarding Against Financial Exploitation ("SAFE") Act, N.J.S.A. 49:3-84 et seq.

### No general fiduciary duty; the LTC nonforfeiture/reinstatement regulation is the closest functional analogue
- No New Jersey case law was located in this research imposing a fiduciary duty on an insurer toward an insured generally (the *Pickett* good-faith duty is contractual/quasi-fiduciary in character but is not framed as a true fiduciary relationship, and nothing in *Pickett* or its progeny singles out elderly or cognitively impaired insureds for a heightened standard).
- The practical, fact-specific analogue to a "heightened duty toward elderly/impaired insureds" in this case comes not from elder-law statutes but from **N.J.A.C. 11:4-34.5(b)** itself (discussed in §1.1 above) — the LTC-specific regulation that (i) requires an insurer-obtained designee-or-waiver mechanism precisely so that a third party can intervene if the insured herself cannot manage her own lapse risk, and (ii) creates a no-fault reinstatement right specifically for insureds who were cognitively impaired or functionally incapacitated at the time of lapse, judged against the *policy's own* benefit-eligibility standard. This regulation functions, in effect, as New Jersey's chosen mechanism for protecting cognitively vulnerable LTC policyholders — and it is squarely on these facts. **[Not a "VERIFY" item as to reasoning — this is original legal analysis synthesizing verified primary sources, not a claimed case holding.]**

## Strategic Assessment: Viable Causes of Action If Advocacy Fails

Ranked by strength, assuming the anticipated facts hold up (no designee ever on file/no waiver in underwriting file; deficient or absent 30-day lapse notice; Pamela cognitively impaired/benefit-eligible before the 2022 nonpayment; John Hancock's communications did not clearly disclose the reinstatement alternative):

1. **Common-law bad faith / breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing** (*Pickett v. Lloyd's*, 131 N.J. 457 (1993)) — the most doctrinally secure private cause of action. The LTC-specific reinstatement regulation (N.J.A.C. 11:4-34.5(b)) supplies exactly the kind of "state policy" *Pickett* said could ground a finding that no debatable reason existed for treating the nonforfeiture conversion as final. Contract-measure damages (the value gap between $1,149,750 and $70,845.71, or the value of proper reinstatement) are recoverable; punitive/emotional-distress damages are a stretch absent evidence of a knowing, company-wide pattern.

2. **NJ Consumer Fraud Act claim, framed around deceptive claims-administration/failure to disclose rights** (*Lemelledo*; *Weiss v. First Unum*; *Alpizar-Fallas v. Favero*) — potentially the most lucrative theory (treble damages + mandatory fees under N.J.S.A. 56:8-19), but also the riskiest to plead, because New Jersey courts will dismiss anything that looks like a repackaged "refusal to pay benefits" claim (*Myska*; *Pierzga*; *Nikiper*). Success depends on developing specific evidence of misrepresentation or omission regarding Pamela's rights (the designee mechanism, the reinstatement alternative) in the insurer's actual lapse/conversion correspondence — not merely on the fact that she ended up with a smaller benefit.

3. **Regulatory complaint to the NJ Department of Banking and Insurance** under N.J.S.A. 17B:30-13.1 / N.J.S.A. 17:29B-4(9) and N.J.A.C. 11:2-17 / 11:4-34.5 — no private right of action, but no standing bar either; costs nothing to file; could trigger a market-conduct examination if John Hancock mishandled the 2022 rate-increase lapse wave broadly enough to show a "general business practice," which would generate discoverable findings usable in the bad-faith and CFA claims above.

4. **NJ elder-abuse/APS Act or SAFE Act direct claim against the insurer** — **not viable**. Neither statute reaches an insurance carrier on these facts; this door is closed absent legislative change or an insurance-specific analog this research did not locate.
