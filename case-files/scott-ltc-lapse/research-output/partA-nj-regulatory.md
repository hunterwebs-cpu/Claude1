## Summary of Practical Implications

1. **NJ regulation independently *requires* the contingent nonforfeiture conversion the client already received.** N.J.A.C. 11:4-34.24, adopted under N.J.S.A. 17B:27E-8, is the regulatory engine behind the policy's own "Contingent Nonforfeiture Protection Endorsement." This confirms John Hancock's paid-up conversion to $70,845.71 was not a favor — it was mandatory once the premium increase crossed the age-based percentage threshold and the policy lapsed within 120 days of the increased-premium due date. This is good background but does not, by itself, give the client anything beyond what she already has.

2. **The third-party designee requirement is a state-mandated minimum, not merely a policy term — and NJ's version is *more protective* than the NAIC model on reminder frequency.** N.J.A.C. 11:4-34.5(a)(1)(i) required John Hancock to obtain a written designation or signed waiver *before the policy could even be issued*, and to remind the insured of her right to change that designation "no less often than once every two years for insureds who have not attained sixty-two and a half years and no less than annually for insureds who have attained age sixty-two and a half years of age" — i.e., **annual** reminders once Ms. Jeanes turned 62½, not merely biennial. If she was over 62½ during the relevant period (highly likely given a 2000 policy issue date) and John Hancock cannot produce annual reminder notices, that is a distinct regulatory violation independent of the grace-period notice question.

3. **The reinstatement window is genuinely 5 months everywhere — NAIC model, NJ regulation, and the private policy's §5.4 clause all agree — but the *timing benchmark* for the impairment differs, and the policy's own language is the most favorable to the client.** N.J.A.C. 11:4-34.5(b) (mirroring NAIC Model Regulation §7.B verbatim) requires proof of cognitive impairment/functional incapacity "before a premium or contribution was required to be paid" — i.e., before the Jan. 26, 2022 due date. The policy's own §5.4 clause instead asks whether she "would meet the eligibility for the payment of benefits... on the date of termination" — the later, more insured-favorable benchmark (~April 2022). Since regulatory minimums cannot be used to *narrow* a more generous contract term, **counsel should anchor the reinstatement argument on the policy's own §5.4 language, not the regulatory floor.**

4. **No notice = no valid termination, and this is independently a state regulatory violation, not just a breach of contract.** N.J.A.C. 11:4-34.5(a)(2) requires 30 days' written notice by first-class mail to *both* the insured and any designated third party before a lapse/termination for nonpayment can take effect — tracking the policy's own §5.2 Grace Period clause almost exactly. If John Hancock cannot produce proof of that mailing (to Ms. Jeanes and to a third-party designee, if one was on file, or proof of a valid waiver if none was designated), the file has both a contract defect and a regulatory compliance gap that DOBI's Consumer Inquiry and Response Center (CIRC) can be asked to investigate and that would support a complaint-driven document demand.

5. **COVID-era DOBI emergency grace periods do not reach this lapse.** All identified DOBI COVID emergency grace-period bulletins (life: 20-16; general A&H: 20-27; and the related 20-11 through 20-15, 20-17, 20-22) were expressly limited to policyholders "in good standing... on March 1, 2020" and premiums due beginning April/May 2020, with no bulletin located in the 2021 or 2022 bulletin index extending or reviving that relief. The Jan. 26, 2022 premium due date and ~April 2022 lapse fall entirely outside any COVID emergency grace period. **[VERIFIED — see Q3 below]**

6. **DOBI's practical remedy is investigative/complaint-driven, not a direct "order reinstatement" power.** DOBI can compel the carrier to respond and produce file materials via a CIRC complaint and, if warranted, a market-conduct examination (N.J.S.A. 17:23A-14, -15) — a strong tool for obtaining proof (or disproof) that the required §5.2/11:4-34.5 notices were sent. But DOBI's own consumer-complaint materials do not describe a power to unilaterally order reinstatement of an individual policy; its levers are cease-and-desist orders and administrative penalties (N.J.S.A. 17:29B-7) plus referral for civil/AG action. Actual reinstatement relief will likely need to be pursued civilly, using any DOBI investigative findings as evidence.

## Question 1: NJ Statutory & Regulatory Requirements

### (a) Grace periods and notice of impending lapse/termination

**Statutory grace period — N.J.S.A. 17B:26-6 ("Grace period")**

This is the general individual accident & health "standard provisions" statute (part of the Uniform Individual Accident and Sickness Policy Provisions Law codified at N.J.S.A. 17B:26-1 et seq.), made applicable to individual long-term care policies by N.J.A.C. 11:4-34.4(h) (quoted below). Per multiple corroborating sources (WebSearch summary of the FindLaw/Justia text, cross-checked twice):

> "A grace period of not less than 7 days for weekly premium policies, 10 days for monthly premium policies, and 31 days for all other policies will be granted for the payment of each premium falling due after the first premium, during which grace period the policy shall continue in force."

Ms. Jeanes's LTC-96 NJ 9/96 policy, billed on an annual/semi-annual basis (not weekly or monthly), falls in the "31 days for all other policies" category — consistent with the ~65-day gap between the Jan. 26, 2022 due date and the ~April 2022 lapse (30-day statutory/regulatory grace, once John Hancock's separate 30-day pre-termination notice period is layered on top, as described immediately below). **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — I was unable to independently pull the primary-source verbatim text of 17B:26-6 due to Justia/FindLaw blocking automated retrieval; the quoted language above is corroborated by two independent WebSearch summaries reading the same statute but should be confirmed against the official njleg.state.nj.us codification or a Westlaw/Lexis pull before it is quoted in a filing.]**

**Cross-reference confirming LTC policies must contain this provision — N.J.A.C. 11:4-34.4(h)** (verbatim, pulled directly from Cornell LII's regulation text):

> "(h) Any long-term care benefit that is provided other than through acceleration of an annuity or life insurance benefit shall contain the required provisions described at N.J.S.A. 17B:26-3. N.J.S.A. 17B:26-16 will also apply to provisions respecting the matters set forth therein that are contained in individual long-term care policies. Group long-term care insurance policies shall contain the standard provisions required by N.J.S.A. 17B:27-33."

**[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — I could not independently confirm the full text of N.J.S.A. 17B:26-3 itself (the "required provisions" statute that 11:4-34.4(h) points to) to see whether it explicitly enumerates the grace-period provision of 17B:26-6 among the provisions an individual A&H/LTC policy "shall contain." This link is standard practice under the Uniform Individual Accident and Sickness Policy Provisions Law model but should be pulled and confirmed directly from njleg.state.nj.us before relying on it.]**

**Regulatory notice-before-lapse requirement — N.J.A.C. 11:4-34.5(a)(2) ("Unintentional lapse")**, verbatim (pulled directly from Cornell LII):

> "Lapse or termination for non-payment of premium. No individual long-term care policy or group certificate shall lapse or be terminated for nonpayment of premium or required contribution unless the carrier, at least 30 days before the effective date of the lapse or termination, has given written notice to the insured and to those persons designated pursuant to (a)1 above, at the address provided by the insured for purposes of receiving notice of lapse or termination. Notice shall be given by first class United States mail, postage prepaid; and notice may not be given until 30 days after a premium or contribution is due and unpaid. Notice shall be deemed to have been given as of five days after the date of mailing."

This is a direct, near-verbatim state-regulatory adoption of NAIC Model Regulation Section 7.A(2) (confirmed against the official NAIC PDF; see Question 2 below). It tracks the policy's own §5.2 Grace Period clause (30-day notice, termination effective 30 days after notice given) — meaning this is *both* a contract term *and* an independent, state-mandated minimum. A failure to send this notice is therefore not merely a breach of contract; it is also a regulatory violation DOBI has jurisdiction to investigate.

### (b) Mandatory third-party designee notification — is this a state minimum, not just a contract term?

**Yes.** N.J.A.C. 11:4-34.5(a)(1)(i), verbatim (pulled directly from Cornell LII):

> "No individual long-term care policy or group certificate shall be issued until the carrier has received from the applicant either a written designation of at least one person, in addition to the applicant, who is to receive notice of lapse or termination of the policy or certificate for nonpayment of premium or required contribution, or a written waiver dated and signed by the applicant electing not to designate an additional person to receive notice. The applicant has the right to designate at least one person who is to receive the notice of termination, in addition to the insured. Designation shall not constitute acceptance of any liability by the third party for services provided to the insured. The form used for the written designation must provide space clearly earmarked for listing at least one person. The designation shall include each person's full name and home address. In the case of an applicant who elects not to designate an additional person, the waiver shall state: 'Protection against unintended lapse. I understand that I have the right to designate at least one person other than myself to receive notice of lapse or termination of this long-term care insurance for non-payment of premium or required contribution. I understand that notice will not be given until 30 days after a premium is due and unpaid. I elect NOT to designate any person to receive this notice.' **The carrier shall notify the insured in writing in a clear and conspicuous manner of the right to change this written designation, no less often than once every two years for insureds who have not attained sixty-two and a half years and no less than annually for insureds who have attained age sixty-two and a half years of age.**"

Two points of practical significance:

- **Issuance-conditioning requirement.** The regulation does not merely require that the *offer* be made; it prohibits the policy from being *issued at all* absent either a signed designation or a signed waiver. If John Hancock's underwriting file lacks either document from January 2000, that is itself an issuance-stage compliance defect — though after 26 years this is likely undiscoverable/immaterial as a practical matter, and a signed waiver is the far more probable explanation for "no third-party designee ever on file."
- **Age-tiered reminder frequency exceeding the NAIC model.** The NAIC Model Regulation's parallel provision (Section 7.A(1), see Question 2) only requires reminders "no less often than once every two (2) years" — flat, with no age escalation. **New Jersey's regulation is more protective than the model**: once an insured turns 62½, the carrier must remind her *annually*, not biennially. This is a affirmative, state-specific enhancement worth flagging: if Ms. Jeanes was 62½ or older at any point during her policyholder tenure and John Hancock's file shows only biennial (not annual) reminder notices, that is a distinct violation from the grace-period notice issue and independently supports a DOBI complaint.

### (c) Nonforfeiture / contingent benefit upon lapse requirements

**Statutory hook — N.J.S.A. 17B:27E-8** ("Conditions for delivery, issuance of policy"), the New Jersey statute expressly cross-referenced by N.J.A.C. 11:4-34.24(b)-(c). Per WebSearch-corroborated summary (**[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — verbatim text not independently pulled from a primary source; codes.findlaw.com and law.justia.com both blocked automated retrieval]**):

> "A long-term care insurance policy shall not be delivered or issued for delivery in this State unless the policyholder or certificate holder has been offered the option of purchasing a policy or certificate including a nonforfeiture benefit... If the policyholder or certificate holder declines the nonforfeiture benefit, the insurer shall provide a contingent benefit upon lapse."

**Implementing regulation — N.J.A.C. 11:4-34.24 ("Nonforfeiture benefit requirement")**, verbatim, pulled directly from Cornell LII (key excerpts; full section is longer):

> "(c) If the offer required to be made under N.J.S.A. 17B:27E-8 is rejected, individual and group policies may be issued without nonforfeiture benefits but must contain a contingent benefit upon lapse. ... 2. The contingent benefit on lapse for an individual policy or group certificate shall be triggered every time a carrier increases the premium rates to a level which results in a cumulative increase of the annual premium equal to or exceeding the percentage of the insured's initial annual premium set forth below based on the insured's issue age, and the policy or certificate lapses within 120 days of the due date of the premium so increased. Unless otherwise required, a policyholder or certificate holder shall be notified at least 30 days prior to the due date of the premium reflecting the rate increase.
>
> 3. On or before the effective date of a substantial premium increase as defined in (c)2 above, the carrier shall do all of the following: i. Offer to reduce policy benefits... ii. Offer to convert the coverage to a paid-up status with a shortened benefit period in accordance with the terms of (d) below. This option may be elected at any time during the 120-day period referenced in (c)2 above; and iii. Notify the policyholder or certificate holder that a default or lapse at any time during the 120-day period referenced in (c)2 above shall be deemed to be the election of the offer to convert in (c)3ii above.
>
> (d) ... 2. ... the nonforfeiture benefit shall be of a shortened benefit period providing paid-up long-term care insurance coverage after lapse. The same benefits (amounts and frequency in effect at the time of lapse but not increased thereafter) will be payable for a qualifying claim, but the lifetime maximum dollars or days of benefits shall be determined as specified in (d)3 below.
>
> 3. The standard nonforfeiture credit will be equal to 100 percent of the sum of all premiums paid, including the premiums paid prior to any changes in benefits, less any claims paid. ... However, the minimum nonforfeiture credit shall not be less than 30 times the daily nursing home benefit at the time of lapse. ...
>
> 4. The nonforfeiture benefit shall begin not later than the end of the third year following the policy or certificate issue date. The contingent benefit upon lapse shall be effective during the first three years as well as thereafter. ...
>
> (g) The provisions of this section apply to any long-term care policy issued in this State on or after January 18, 2006."

Two things worth flagging for the file:

- **(g) is a real gap.** N.J.A.C. 11:4-34.24 by its own terms applies "to any long-term care policy issued in this State on or after January 18, 2006." Ms. Jeanes's policy issued January 26, 2000 — **before** this regulation's effective date. That means the *state regulatory version* of the contingent nonforfeiture benefit does not, by its plain terms, apply to her policy; the Contingent Nonforfeiture Protection Endorsement she actually has is a **policy-form feature carried on the LTC-96 NJ 9/96 form itself** (likely required by an earlier version of the rule, a predecessor bulletin, or simply offered voluntarily/contractually by John Hancock), not something N.J.A.C. 11:4-34.24 compelled for *this* policy. **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — confirm whether an earlier NJ nonforfeiture rule (pre-2006) applied to policies issued in 2000, and whether the LTC-96 NJ 9/96 form's Contingent Nonforfeiture Protection Endorsement was itself mandated by that earlier rule or by the 2000 NAIC Model Act requirement referenced in DOBI Bulletin 08-08.]** This matters because if the current N.J.A.C. 11:4-34.24 terms (100% premium credit less claims, 30× daily nursing home benefit floor, 120-day trigger window) do not technically govern this policy, the controlling terms are whatever the LTC-96 NJ 9/96 endorsement itself says — which should be checked against the $70,845.71 calculation for consistency.
- **Confirms the mechanism, regardless of applicability.** Even if 11:4-34.24 does not directly govern by its effective-date clause, it independently confirms that NJ's regulatory design for this exact scenario — premium increase crossing an age-based percentage threshold, followed by lapse — is conversion to paid-up, reduced-benefit status, not termination. That is consistent with, and corroborates, the operation of the endorsement the client already has.

### (d) Reinstatement rights for insureds cognitively impaired/functionally incapacitated at time of lapse

**N.J.A.C. 11:4-34.5(b) ("Restoration of Coverage")**, verbatim, pulled directly from Cornell LII:

> "(b) Restoration of Coverage: In addition to the requirement in (a) above, a long-term care insurance policy or group certificate shall include a provision that provides for restoration of coverage in the event of lapse, if the carrier is provided proof that the policyholder or certificate holder was cognitively impaired or had a loss of functional capacity before a premium or contribution was required to be paid. This option shall be available to the insured if requested within five months after termination and shall allow for the collection of past due premium or contributions, where appropriate. The standard of proof of cognitive impairment or loss of functional capacity shall not be more stringent than the benefit eligibility criteria for cognitive impairment or the loss of functional capacity contained in the policy and certificate."

This is a **state-mandated minimum** — every individual LTC policy delivered in New Jersey must contain this provision, separate from and in addition to whatever the policy's own reinstatement clause (here, §5.4) says. Key features:

- **5-month window from termination**, matching the private policy's §5.4 clause exactly.
- **Standard of proof capped at the policy's own eligibility criteria** — the carrier cannot demand a *more* stringent showing of impairment than what the policy itself uses to trigger benefit eligibility (i.e., whatever ADL/cognitive-impairment benefit-trigger definition is in the LTC-96 NJ 9/96 form's "Eligibility for the Payment of Benefits" section). **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — the policy's actual benefit-trigger definition (including whether it folds in an elimination-period requirement) needs to be checked against this to confirm John Hancock cannot impose a higher bar for reinstatement purposes than for ordinary claims purposes.]**
- **Timing benchmark differs from the policy's own §5.4 clause — and the difference favors relying on the contract, not the regulation.** The regulation asks whether the insured was impaired "before a premium or contribution was required to be paid" (i.e., before the Jan. 26, 2022 due date). The policy's §5.4 clause instead asks whether she "would meet the eligibility for the payment of benefits... on the date of termination" (the later ~April 2022 date). Because a carrier may lawfully offer terms *more generous* than the regulatory floor (the floor is a minimum, not a ceiling), and because N.J.A.C. 11:4-34.5(b) is expressly "in addition to" — not a replacement for — other policy provisions, **the policy's own §5.4 "date of termination" benchmark should control and should be the primary hook**, with the regulatory provision cited as a fallback/reinforcing argument, not the lead.

## Question 2: Does the NAIC Model Regulation's Reinstatement Provision Offer More Than the Policy's Own §5.4 Clause?

**Short answer: No.** New Jersey adopted the NAIC Long-Term Care Insurance Model Regulation's unintentional-lapse/reinstatement provision (Section 7) essentially **verbatim** as N.J.A.C. 11:4-34.5. Both provide the same 5-month window and the same capped standard of proof; neither contains an open-ended or legal-representative-triggered reinstatement right beyond that 5-month period.

**NAIC Long-Term Care Insurance Model Regulation, Section 7 ("Unintentional Lapse")**, verbatim, extracted directly from the NAIC's official model-regulation PDF (`content.naic.org/sites/default/files/model-law-641.pdf`, "NAIC Model Laws, Regulations, Guidelines and Other Resources — 1st Quarter 2017" compilation, model no. MO-641):

> "Section 7. Unintentional Lapse
>
> Each insurer offering long-term care insurance shall, as a protection against unintentional lapse, comply with the following:
>
> A.
> (1) Notice before lapse or termination. No individual long-term care policy or certificate shall be issued until the insurer has received from the applicant either a written designation of at least one person, in addition to the applicant, who is to receive notice of lapse or termination of the policy or certificate for nonpayment of premium, or a written waiver dated and signed by the applicant electing not to designate additional persons to receive notice. ... The insurer shall notify the insured of the right to change this written designation, no less often than once every two (2) years.
>
> (2) Lapse or termination for nonpayment of premium. No individual long-term care policy or certificate shall lapse or be terminated for nonpayment of premium unless the insurer, at least thirty (30) days before the effective date of the lapse or termination, has given notice to the insured and to those persons designated pursuant to Subsection A(1)... Notice shall be given by first class United States mail, postage prepaid; and notice may not be given until thirty (30) days after a premium is due and unpaid. Notice shall be deemed to have been given as of five (5) days after the date of mailing.
>
> B. Reinstatement. In addition to the requirement in Subsection A, a long-term care insurance policy or certificate shall include a provision that provides for reinstatement of coverage, in the event of lapse if the insurer is provided proof that the policyholder or certificateholder was cognitively impaired or had a loss of functional capacity before the grace period contained in the policy expired. This option shall be available to the insured if requested within five (5) months after termination and shall allow for the collection of past due premium, where appropriate. The standard of proof of cognitive impairment or loss of functional capacity shall not be more stringent than the benefit eligibility criteria on cognitive impairment or the loss of functional capacity contained in the policy and certificate.
>
> Drafting Note: The language in Subsection B addressing the provision of proof of cognitive impairment or less of functional capacity has been amended to more precisely clarify the original intent in adopting the reinstatement provision."

Comparison of the three "timing benchmark" formulations for when the impairment must have existed:

| Source | Benchmark date |
|---|---|
| NAIC Model Reg. §7.B | "before the grace period contained in the policy expired" (mid-point) |
| N.J.A.C. 11:4-34.5(b) (as adopted by NJ) | "before a premium... was required to be paid" (earliest — the due date itself) |
| Policy §5.4 (private contract) | "on the date of termination" (latest — most generous to insured) |

New Jersey's codification is, if anything, **textually narrower** than the NAIC model on this one point (moving the cutoff earlier, to the due date, rather than to the end of the grace period) — not more generous. Neither the model nor the NJ regulation contains any provision for an open-ended reinstatement right keyed to when "a legal representative later comes forward," nor any window longer than 5 months. **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — I worked from the NAIC's 1st Quarter 2017 compilation of Model MO-641; if a materially later amendment to the model exists (post-2017) that changes Section 7, it should be checked against the NAIC's current model law library at content.naic.org/model-laws before ruling out a more generous provision entirely. I found no evidence of such an amendment in this research, and the 2017 compilation's own drafting note suggests Subsection B was already stable/mature by then.]** The correct strategic conclusion is the one already reached in the Q1(d) discussion above: **the client's best argument is the policy's own §5.4 language, not an appeal to a more generous state or NAIC floor, because no such more-generous floor exists.**

### COVID-era emergency grace period extensions (2020-2022) — do they affect this timeline?

**No — confirmed the 2020 COVID emergency grace-period relief does not reach the January 2022 due date / April 2022 lapse.** The relevant bulletins, pulled and read in full from their PDF text:

- **Bulletin No. 20-16** ("90-Day Grace Period for Life Insurance Premium Payments Due to the Disruption Caused by COVID-19," issued April 10, 2020) — applies only to "policyholders that were in good standing with their insurance carrier on March 1, 2020," with the 90-day grace period beginning "retroactively on April 1, 2020 or... - **2021 bulletins** (21-01, 21-03, 21-07) address COVID-19 immunization coverage and special health-insurance enrollment periods — **not** premium grace periods.
- **2022 bulletin index** contains no COVID-related grace-period or premium-relief bulletin; the only 2022 COVID-related bulletin located (22-03) concerns coverage for COVID-19 tests, unrelated to premium payment or lapse.

Because these emergency measures were expressly tied to a March 1, 2020 good-standing baseline and April/May 2020 retroactive start dates, and no bulletin extending or reviving them into 2021 or 2022 was located, **they have no application to Ms. Jeanes's January 26, 2022 premium due date or the resulting ~April 2022 lapse.** This should be treated as a dead end for this specific timeline (though it is useful precedent that DOBI has, in the past, exercised broad emergency authority to compel blanket grace-period extensions across an entire line of business by bulletin, which may be rhetorically useful in any advocacy to DOBI, even though it doesn't supply a legal hook here).

## Question 4: NJ DOBI Consumer Complaint Process and Investigative/Compulsory Authority

### The complaint process

Consumers file with DOBI's **Consumer Inquiry and Response Center (CIRC)**, reachable online, by phone (609-292-7272 or the Consumer Hotline 1-800-446-7467), by mail, or by fax (P.O. Box 471, Trenton, NJ 08625-0471). Per DOBI's own consumer-information page (nj.gov/dobi/consumer.htm) and enforcement/consumer-protection page (nj.gov/dobi/enfcon.htm):

> "Each inquiry or complaint is assigned to an investigator who reviews the case to ensure that the insurance company and/or producer involved in the matter has complied with applicable insurance statutes and regulations and that the consumer has been treated fairly." The investigator "will contact the insurer or producer and require that the licensee respond to all aspects of the complaint/inquiry," after which the consumer receives "a written report of our findings." "In appropriate circumstances, a case will be referred to the Department's Enforcement Unit for further investigation, which may result in the imposition of administrative penalties."

A complaint requires "the company name/agent's name, the policy/certificate number(s), any documentation that will substantiate your position, and a brief description of the problems," plus evidence of prior efforts to resolve the matter directly with the carrier. This is the correct, low-cost first step to formally demand that John Hancock produce proof of the §5.2/N.J.A.C. 11:4-34.5(a)(2) lapse notices and any third-party-designee reminder notices — DOBI's practice is to require the licensee to "respond to all aspects" of the complaint, which as a practical matter forces document production into the file even though DOBI is not itself acting as the client's private counsel.

### Investigative/compulsory authority

- **N.J.S.A. 17:23A-1 et seq. (Examination of Insurers Act)** gives the Commissioner power to "examine and investigate into the affairs of every insurance institution or agent doing business in the State to determine whether the insurance institution or agent has been or is complying with applicable laws," with **N.J.S.A. 17:23A-14** ("Power of Commissioner") and **N.J.S.A. 17:23A-15** ("Hearings, Witnesses, Appearances, Production of Books and Service of Process") providing the examination/subpoena/document-production framework. **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — I was not able to independently pull verbatim primary-source text of §§17:23A-14 and -15 (Cornell LII does not host NJSA, and Justia/FindLaw blocked automated retrieval); this summary is based on a WebSearch-level characterization only and the exact scope of the production-compelling power (e.g., whether it extends to individual policyholder files outside a formal market conduct exam, versus only company-wide examinations) should be confirmed directly from njleg.state.nj.us or Westlaw/Lexis before relying on it in a filing.]**
- **N.J.S.A. 17:29B-1 et seq. (Unfair Trade Practices Act)**, specifically **N.J.S.A. 17:29B-7** ("Cease and desist orders; penalties; modifications"): if the Commissioner determines a practice violates the Act, "the commissioner shall issue an order requiring the person to cease and desist," with penalties "not to exceed $1,000 for each violation" (or up to $5,000 per violation for knowing violations). This is enforcement/deterrence authority, not a mechanism for ordering an individual policy reinstated.
- **N.J.S.A. 17B:30-13.1 (Unfair Claims Settlement Practices Act)**: prohibits practices including "failing to acknowledge and act reasonably promptly upon communications with respect to claims" and "failing to adopt and implement reasonable standards for the prompt investigation of claims." Notably, **this Act does not create a private right of action** — but a documented violation can serve as evidence of bad faith in a separate civil action against the carrier. **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — confirm current codification and no-private-right-of-action holding against a primary source/case citation; this was sourced from a WebSearch summary referencing secondary commentary, not a verified case citation.]**
- **Market Conduct Examinations**: DOBI's published process (nj.gov/dobi/division_consumers/insurance/mcesteps.htm) confirms examiners review "claim and policyholder files, company systems and billing" as part of an 11-step examination process typically triggered by "an increase in complaint volume, an increase in the frequency of complaints on a particular issue," findings from a prior exam, or a change in the company's market presence. This is the more powerful tool for compelling *company-wide* production of lapse-notice practices and could be worth flagging to DOBI if there is reason to believe John Hancock's failure to send Grace Period/§5.2 notices to elderly NJ LTC policyholders is a pattern rather than a one-off — but it is not typically triggered by, or available as a remedy in, a single individual complaint.

### Does DOBI have authority to order reinstatement?

**No such authority was identified in DOBI's own published consumer-complaint materials.** DOBI's complaint process describes investigation, a written findings report, and referral to the Enforcement Unit for "administrative penalties" — not a power to directly order a specific policy reinstated or to compel specific performance of a contract term. The practical path is: (1) file a CIRC complaint to force John Hancock to produce (or admit it cannot produce) proof of the required notices; (2) use DOBI's investigator findings — favorable or not — as an evidentiary building block; and (3) pursue actual reinstatement relief through a civil action (breach of contract / declaratory judgment on the §5.2 notice failure and §5.4 reinstatement right), where the DOBI complaint file and any market-conduct findings can be offered as supporting evidence of a pattern or of the specific compliance failure in this file. **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — this conclusion is based on DOBI's own public-facing consumer materials, not an exhaustive review of the Commissioner's full statutory toolkit (e.g., N.J.S.A. 17:29B-4, 17B:30-13.1 in their entirety, or any consent-order precedent in which DOBI secured individual policyholder relief, including reinstatement, as part of an enforcement settlement — such settlements do exist industry-wide for LTC insurers nationally and a search of NJ-specific consent orders against John Hancock or its affiliates at nj.gov/dobi/division_insurance/enforcement/ is recommended as a follow-up.]**
