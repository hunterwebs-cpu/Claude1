### 1(a) — Class actions / individual suits over LTC rate increases, notice adequacy, and nonforfeiture administration

**No John Hancock–specific LTC class action or MDL concerning rate-increase notice, grace-period notice, or Contingent Nonforfeiture Benefit (CNFB) administration was located in CourtListener's opinion or docket databases.** CourtListener's RECAP/docket index returned dozens of John Hancock federal cases (ERISA disputes over group LTD/life plans it administers, interpleader and diversity contract suits, agent-termination suits, a 2026-filed premium-finance dispute (*John Hancock Life & Health Ins. This is likely because most John Hancock LTC rate-increase disputes were resolved through the regulatory channel described in 1(b) below (state DOI rate hearings and the NY DFS enforcement action) rather than through class litigation, and/or through confidential settlement.

The closest analogous **litigated** LTC rate-increase class action against a different carrier is:

- **Case Name:** Rakes v. Life Investors Insurance Company of America
- **Citation:** 582 F.3d 886 (8th Cir. 2009); related district opinion at 622 F. Supp. 2d 755 (N.D. Iowa 2008)
- **Binding or Persuasive:** Persuasive only (8th Circuit; not John Hancock; applies Iowa/Missouri/Virginia law)
- **Verbatim holding quote with pinpoint cite:** "Plaintiffs have not shown that their LTC insurance policies contained a fraudulent representation... Life Investors disclosed its right to change premium rates on the first page of its policies, in boldface, capital letters... there is no genuine issue of material fact as to whether the policies' guaranteed renewable language constituted a fraudulent representation." 582 F.3d at 894 (slip pagination per CourtListener chunk 3).
- **Verification status:** CONFIRMED via CourtListener (opinion IDs 3047531 (8th Cir.) — full text read).
- **How it helps/hurts:** Factually notable because the named plaintiff Rakes, like Pamela Jeanes, **"opted to convert the policy to a contingent nonforfeiture benefit"** after a rate hike (582 F.3d at 891 n.4, confirmed via CourtListener) — confirming CNFB conversion is a recognized, litigated feature of the LTC market, not an obscure clause. But the holding **hurts** any theory sounding in fraud/bad-faith about the rate increase itself: the Eighth Circuit affirmed summary judgment for the insurer because the policy's "limited right to change premiums" was disclosed in bold/cap text and the insureds admitted awareness that rates could rise. **Strategic takeaway: do not frame this case around "the rate increase was improper" — courts routinely reject that theory where the increase was DOBI-approved and disclosed. The stronger, more defensible theory is the notice-of-lapse/third-party-designee defect and the incapacity-based reinstatement clause, not the rate increase itself.**

- **Case Name:** Newman v. Metropolitan Life Insurance Co.
- **Citation:** 240 F. Supp. 3d 761 (N.D. Ill. 2017)
- **Binding or Persuasive:** Persuasive (N.D. Ill.; different carrier — MetLife, not John Hancock)
- **Holding (per CourtListener snippet):** Case "arises from a long-term care insurance policy that Plaintiff Margery Newman purchased from Defendant Metropolitan Life Insurance Company. In connection with the policy, Plaintiff purchased a premium-payment option titled the 'Reduced-Pay at 65 Option.' As set forth more fully below, the option didn't function a[s advertised]..."
- **Verification status:** CONFIRMED via CourtListener (case metadata and snippet; full opinion text not separately pulled — pinpoint page for full holding should be verified before filing). **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — pinpoint quote beyond the snippet]**

Not a strong precedent standing alone; cite sparingly and only after full-text verification.

## PART 2: GENERAL CASE LAW ON NOTICE-OF-LAPSE, EQUITABLE RELIEF, AND NONFORFEITURE INTERPRETATION

### 2(a) — Duty to provide adequate notice of lapse/termination, especially to an elderly/potentially impaired insured

- **Case Name:** Meier v. New Jersey Life Insurance Co.
- **Citation:** 101 N.J. 597, 503 A.2d 862 (1986)
- **Binding or Persuasive:** **BINDING** — New Jersey Supreme Court, construing New Jersey insurance law directly.
- **Verbatim holding quote with pinpoint cite:** "[U]nless the company receives written notice that is in conformity with the insurance contract prior to expiration of the grace period... it must use the [premium loan provision] to pay the unpaid premiums due no later than the last day of the grace period... An insured cannot be penalized for an insurance company's internal procedures." 101 N.J. at 610–611 (per CourtListener html_with_citations, chunk 1–2, opinion ID 9693991). Also: "Courts apply the adhesion doctrine because of the unequal bargaining power of the parties... in interpreting insurance contracts any ambiguities should be construed against the insurer and in favor of the insured." 101 N.J. at 612–613.
- **Verification status:** CONFIRMED via CourtListener (full opinion text read directly, cluster 1904507 / opinion 9693991).
- **How it helps:** This is the single most useful **binding** NJ Supreme Court case found in this research. Facts are strikingly close: the insurer (New Jersey Life) placed a policy on **reduced paid-up (RPU) status** — the same species of nonforfeiture conversion at issue here — based on internal processing timelines and ambiguous customer communications, without sending the insured a clear notice that the policy had actually been placed on RPU. The NJ Supreme Court held for the insured's estate, emphasizing (1) the insurer bears the burden of proving a policy was properly converted/terminated, not the insured; (2) an insurer **cannot use its own internal administrative delay or procedure to the insured's disadvantage**; and (3) LTC/life policies are contracts of adhesion construed liberally in favor of the insured's **objectively reasonable expectations** — citing the "reasonable expectations" doctrine originating in **Gaunt v. John Hancock Mut. Life Ins. Co.**, 160 F.2d 599 (2d Cir. 1947) (Learned Hand, J.) ("A man must indeed read what he signs, and he is charged, if he does not; but insurers who seek to impose upon words of common speech an esoteric significance intelligible only to their craft, must bear the burden of any resulting confusion." 160 F.2d at 602). Note the doctrinal irony/leverage: the "reasonable expectations" doctrine that NJ courts apply against insurers on notice/nonforfeiture questions was itself born in a case *against John Hancock's corporate predecessor* (John Hancock Mutual Life). **This is a strong affirmative authority for the argument that John Hancock bears the burden of proving it gave adequate, unambiguous notice before the January 2022 lapse and CNFB conversion, and that any ambiguity in its Grace Period / third-party-designee notice procedure should be resolved in Ms. Jeanes's favor.**
- **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING]:** Confirm pinpoint page cites against the official NJ Reports/Atlantic Reporter (101 N.J. 597 / 503 A.2d 862) rather than relying solely on CourtListener's HTML pagination markers, which can occasionally drift from the print reporter by a page.

- **Case Name:** Boylan v. Jackson National Life Insurance Co.
- **Citation:** 353 F. App'x 708 (3d Cir. 2009) (unpublished)
- **Binding or Persuasive:** Persuasive (Third Circuit applying New Jersey substantive law; unpublished, so not binding precedent even within the 3d Cir., but directly on point and useful for its recitation of governing NJ statutes).
- **Verbatim holding quote with pinpoint cite:** "New Jersey law requires that all life insurance policies within the state contain a grace period of 30 days, during which an insured may submit payment without interruption of coverage. N.J. Stat. Ann. § 17B:25-3... it is beyond dispute that once a life insurance policy has lapsed for nonpayment, mere submission of an overdue premium — even if accepted by the insurer — is insufficient to reinstate coverage." 353 F. App'x at 711 (citing Glezerman v. Columbian Mut. Life Ins. Co., 944 F.2d 146, 154 (3d Cir. 1991), and Hogan v. John Hancock Mut. Life Ins. Co., 195 F.2d 834, 837 (3d Cir. 1952)).
- **Verification status:** CONFIRMED via CourtListener (full opinion text read, opinion ID 8475000).
- **How it helps/hurts:** **Mixed.** It hurts to the extent it shows the Third Circuit, applying NJ law, held that an insurer's month-long silent retention of an overdue premium did NOT constitute waiver of formal reinstatement requirements absent a "clear, unequivocal, and decisive act" of relinquishment — so a passive theory ("they should have known/done more") will not succeed on its own. It helps because (1) it confirms the governing NJ statutory framework — N.J. Stat. Ann. § 17B:25-3 (mandatory grace period) and § 17B:25-9 (reinstatement of lapsed **life** policies) — which the family's team should map precisely against the LTC-specific statute (N.J.S.A. 17B:27E, and the NAIC-model-derived NJ LTC regulations, discussed in Part 3 below), since Boylan's own footnote 8 makes clear that courts treat the **life** insurance reinstatement statute (§17B:25-9) as distinct from the **health** insurance reinstatement statute (§17B:26-7) — meaning the LTC-specific statute/regulation is what actually governs here, not the general life-insurance grace-period case law by direct analogy. (2) It affirmatively cites, and does not question, **Hogan v. John Hancock Mut. Life Ins. Co.**, 195 F.2d 834 (3d Cir. 1952) — another John-Hancock-predecessor case on reinstatement mechanics — as good law, which may be worth pulling directly given the same-family-of-companies angle.
- **Related, cited-within-Boylan case, also useful:**
  - **Glezerman v. Columbian Mutual Life Insurance Co.**, 944 F.2d 146 (3d Cir. 1991) — CONFIRMED via CourtListener citation chain in Boylan (full text not independently pulled in this session; **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING]** for pinpoint quote beyond what Boylan quotes). Holds that an insurer has "a reasonable amount of time" to evaluate a reinstatement application and that retaining an overdue premium for an extended period is *evidence* (not conclusive proof) of waiver/reinstatement — useful if John Hancock is shown to have sat on any post-lapse communications or a benefit-eligibility determination without timely action.

### 2(b) — Equitable tolling / waiver / estoppel to reinstate a lapsed policy where notice was defective or incapacity prevented a timely response

- **General framework (persuasive, multi-jurisdictional, from secondary-source-confirmed case law — [VERIFY BEFORE FILING] each specific case before citing):** Courts applying general insurance-law equitable-estoppel doctrine require: (1) the insurer knew the relevant facts; (2) it intended its conduct to be relied upon (or should reasonably have expected reliance); (3) the insured was ignorant of the true facts; and (4) the insured detrimentally relied. This four-part test is the standard formulation applied broadly across jurisdictions including New Jersey (see, e.g., the waiver/estoppel discussion embedded in *Boylan*, 353 F. App'x at 711–712, quoting *W. Jersey Title & Guar. Co. v. Indus. Trust Co.*, 27 N.J. 144, 141 A.2d 782, 786 (1958) — **CONFIRMED via CourtListener** as quoted directly in the Boylan opinion text).
- **On incapacity specifically:** No John Hancock-specific or NJ-specific *published* opinion squarely holding that an insured's cognitive incapacity excuses/tolls a premium-payment default was located via CourtListener search in the time available. Secondary sources (law-firm client-education articles, not verified court opinions) describe a general trend of courts "applying equitable doctrines to prevent insurers from lapsing policies when the insured's incapacity was itself the reason for non-payment," but no specific, citable holding was confirmed. **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING] — this is the single biggest gap in this research and should be the subject of a follow-up, more exhaustive search** (try terms like "premium," "incompetent," "de facto guardian," "waiver," combined with specific state courts; also check treatises — Couch on Insurance §32 (reinstatement) and §26A (agency) were cited approvingly by the NJ Supreme Court in *Meier*, and Couch's own case citations may lead to the missing incapacity-tolling authority).
- **More productive avenue — statutory, not common-law, incapacity protection:** The NAIC Long-Term Care Insurance Model Regulation (and its state-law implementations, including New Jersey's) **requires** LTC policies to include a **statutory right to reinstatement upon proof of cognitive impairment or loss of functional capacity existing before the grace period expired**, typically exercisable **within five months after lapse**. This is consistent with the "separate policy clause" the family describes as allowing full reinstatement if Ms. Jeanes was already chronically-ill/cognitively-impaired-eligible at the time of lapse — it is very likely not merely a discretionary policy feature but a **regulatory mandate** John Hancock was required to include in form LTC-96 NJ 9/96 to obtain NJ DOBI approval. **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING]** — confirm the exact NJ citation (likely N.J.A.C. 11:4-23.7 or 11:4-23.8, implementing N.J.S.A. 17B:27E) and confirm the specific five-month (or other) window and its interaction with the January 26, 2022 lapse date — if the clock is keyed to the DATE OF LAPSE rather than date of discovery, this may be a live deadline issue that should be flagged to the family's counsel immediately regardless of the notice-defect theory's ultimate strength.

### 2(c) — How courts interpret "contingent nonforfeiture" / "reduced paid-up" provisions when insureds contest the lapse

- **Meier v. New Jersey Life Insurance Co.**, 101 N.J. 597 (1986) — see full discussion at 2(a) above; this is the primary, binding authority on point. Its core teaching for RPU/CNFB disputes: (1) burden is on the insurer to prove the conversion to reduced/paid-up status was proper and adequately communicated; (2) internal processing delay/ambiguity is construed against the insurer; (3) the policy is read for the insured's objectively reasonable expectations, not hyper-technical insurer-favorable readings.
- **Rakes v. Life Investors Ins. Co. of Am.**, 582 F.3d 886 (8th Cir. 2009) — see full discussion at 1(a) above. Useful only to establish that CNFB/contingent-nonforfeiture conversion following a rate increase is an established, litigated LTC mechanism — not to establish any interpretive rule favorable to insureds (the substantive fraud holding there went against the insureds).
- **No case was located directly construing the specific "eligible for benefits at time of lapse => full reinstatement" clause** the family describes. This clause tracks the NAIC Model Regulation's "protection against unintentional lapse" / cognitive-impairment reinstatement provision discussed at 2(b) above, which is more often enforced through direct claims/DOBI complaints than through published appellate opinions (these disputes are typically resolved administratively or via confidential settlement, which is consistent with the dearth of published case law found here). **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING]** — recommend a targeted follow-up search of NJ Superior Court (Law Division/Appellate Division) unpublished opinions and NJ OAL (Office of Administrative Law) decisions specifically, since DOBI-track disputes often surface only there, not in CourtListener's federal-heavy index.

## PART 3: DOES NEW JERSEY RECOGNIZE A PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION FOR VIOLATION OF ITS INSURANCE NOTICE-OF-LAPSE REGULATIONS?

**Short answer: No — New Jersey courts have consistently declined to imply a private right of action for violations of insurance regulatory statutes (including notice/lapse-type provisions) where the statute itself provides an administrative enforcement mechanism through DOBI, and instead have required exhaustion of / referral to DOBI's primary jurisdiction before a court will act. This is a real procedural obstacle the family's counsel must plan around.**

- **Case Name:** R.J. Gaydos Insurance Agency, Inc. v. National Consumer Insurance Co.
- **Citation:** 168 N.J. 255, 773 A.2d 1132 (2001)
- **Binding or Persuasive:** **BINDING** — New Jersey Supreme Court.
- **Verbatim holding quote with pinpoint cite:** "[W]e hold that FAIRA does not confer a private right of action to Gaydos to pursue its FAIRA allegations against NCIC." 168 N.J. at 281 (per CourtListener html_with_citations, chunk 9, opinion ID 7326429). And, on the remedy: "Because we find that FAIRA's elaborate legislative and regulatory scheme suggests that the Legislature intended to invest the DOBI with primary authority to implement the Act and enforce its provisions... on remand, the Law Division should transfer this matter to the DOBI for an administrative determination..." 168 N.J. at 283–284.
- **Verification status:** CONFIRMED via CourtListener (full opinion text read directly, cluster 7407213 / opinion 7326429).
- **How it helps/hurts the client's position:** **Hurts** — directly forecloses a freestanding civil damages suit against John Hancock premised solely on an alleged violation of NJ's insurance notice/lapse regulations (whether the LTC Insurance Act, N.J.S.A. 17B:27E, or its implementing grace-period/third-party-designee regulations). The NJ Supreme Court's three-factor test for an implied private right of action (from *Cort v. Ash*, 422 U.S. 66 (1975), as adopted in *In re State Comm'n of Investigation*, 108 N.J. 35, 41 (1987)) — (1) is plaintiff within the class the statute was meant to protect; (2) is there legislative intent to create a private remedy; (3) is a private remedy consistent with the legislative scheme — will very likely cut against an implied right of action here for the same reason it did in Gaydos: NJ's insurance code contains its own civil-penalty/administrative-enforcement mechanism (see N.J.S.A. 17:33-2, 17B:21-2, cited in the LTC third-party-designee regulatory materials reviewed for this memo), and NJ courts read such built-in enforcement schemes as evidence the Legislature did NOT intend parallel private suits.
- **Companion authority, also binding and squarely on insurance statutes specifically (not just auto insurance):**
  - **Lemelledo v. Beneficial Management Corp. of America**, 150 N.J. 255, 264, 696 A.2d 546 (1997) — cited approvingly within Gaydos. Holds **no private right of action** under the NJ Insurance Trade Practices Act, the NJ Insurance Producer Licensing Act, or the Credit Life and Health Insurance Act — expressly distinguishing those statutes from the Consumer Fraud Act, which *does* expressly authorize private suits. **CONFIRMED via CourtListener** (quoted directly within the Gaydos opinion text at chunk 6, id. at 297).
  - **In re Commissioner of Insurance's March 24, 1992 Order**, 256 N.J. Super. 158, 176–78, 606 A.2d 851 (App. Div. 1992), aff'd, 132 N.J. 209, 624 A.2d 565 (1993) — "Whenever the Legislature intended to create civil penalties for violations of insurance statutes, regulations, and Department orders, it knew how to do so.... Implied remedies are unlikely to be intended by a Legislature that enacts a comprehensive legislative scheme including an integrated system of procedures for enforcement." **CONFIRMED via CourtListener** (quoted directly within Gaydos at chunk 6, id. at 176).
- **Practical implication / recommended path forward:** Do not plead a standalone private cause of action for "violation of NJ's LTC lapse-notice regulation." Instead: (1) frame the core theory as **contract-based** (breach of the policy's own Grace Period and reinstatement provisions, and/or the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, per Sons of Thunder, Inc. v. Borden, Inc., 148 N.J. 396, 421, 690 A.2d 575 (1997), cited within Gaydos) rather than as a statutory tort; (2) use the regulatory notice requirements (the third-party-designee rule, and the cognitive-impairment reinstatement rule) as **evidence of the standard of care / objectively reasonable expectations** under Meier, not as an independent cause of action; and (3) **file a parallel complaint with NJ DOBI** (Division of Insurance, Consumer Protection Services / Enforcement) alongside or before any litigation — Gaydos affirmatively contemplates and endorses this exact sequencing (referral to DOBI for an administrative determination, with the door left open to a common-law claim only after that process runs, see 168 N.J. at 284–285), and a DOBI finding of a regulatory violation would be powerful, low-cost leverage feeding directly into the contract/good-faith claims described above. Note also that DOBI's own market-conduct exam authority means a *family-initiated complaint* could itself trigger exactly the kind of exam that produced the NY DFS action against this same carrier — worth pursuing regardless of the private-litigation posture.
- **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING]:** Confirm whether the NJ Insurance Fair Conduct Act (effective January 2022) — which the research located as now creating a private right of action for unreasonable delay/denial of UM/UIM claims and Unfair Claims Settlement Practices Act violations — has been extended by subsequent amendment or case law beyond auto/UM context to LTC or first-party health-adjacent claims; if so, it could change this analysis materially. This statute is very recently amended relative to this memo's research window and deserves a dedicated, fresh search before final case strategy is set.
