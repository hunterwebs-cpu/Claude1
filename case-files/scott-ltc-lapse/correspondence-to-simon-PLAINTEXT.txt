THE MOST IMPORTANT THING FIRST: THE POLICY IS NOT CANCELED.

I know that's not how it was described to you, and I understand why - "your policy lapsed" is what people hear when a payment is missed, and John Hancock's own language to you probably used a word like that. But look at what their own Annual Statements have said every year since 2023, including the most recent one you sent me: the policy is "currently in a paid-up status with reduced benefits. Premiums are no longer due." That is not the same thing as canceled.

Here's what actually happened. Your mother's policy had a built-in feature - John Hancock calls it the "Contingent Nonforfeiture Benefit" - that a lot of long-term care policies from that era carry, precisely to prevent exactly what people fear will happen if a payment gets missed after years of paying in. When she didn't pay the January 2022 premium (after a string of rate increases John Hancock had been phasing in since 2020), instead of the policy simply evaporating, this provision automatically converted it to a paid-up policy - no more premiums ever due - worth $70,845.71 in total lifetime benefits, at the same $525/day benefit rate as before. That is real money that is sitting there right now, confirmed as recently as this January. Whatever else we do, that benefit should be formally claimed and preserved. It is not contingent on anything I describe below.

BUT THERE'S A SECOND, MUCH BIGGER QUESTION, AND IT'S THE ONE THAT MATTERS MOST.

The original policy - before the lapse - had a $1,149,750 lifetime benefit on a 6-year benefit period. The $70,845.71 she has now is about 6% of that. The policy itself contains a separate clause (I'll call it the "hardship reinstatement" clause) that lets a lapsed policy be restored to its full, original value - not just the reduced paid-up amount - if the policyholder was already sick enough, or impaired enough, to qualify for benefits on the date the policy actually terminated. New Jersey law requires every long-term care policy sold in the state to contain a version of this same protection, specifically for policyholders who missed a payment because of cognitive impairment or loss of functional capacity.

You told me your mother "forgot to pay her annual fee" and that her health - primarily her memory - has been declining. If that decline was already underway around the time of the missed payment in early 2022, that is not just an unfortunate detail. That is potentially the legal basis for restoring the full policy, not the reduced one. This is the central question the whole case turns on, and it's why I need your help gathering specific records (list below).

A SECOND PROBLEM, INDEPENDENT OF THE MEDICAL QUESTION: I DON'T THINK JOHN HANCOCK FOLLOWED ITS OWN RULES WHEN IT PROCESSED THE LAPSE.

Both the policy itself and New Jersey insurance regulations require the insurance company to do two specific things before a long-term care policy can be allowed to lapse for nonpayment:

1. Get your mother, at the time she bought the policy in 2000, to either name someone else (a "third-party designee") who would also be notified if a premium ever went unpaid, or sign a written waiver declining to do so - and then remind her of that right periodically (once she passed age 62 1/2, New Jersey requires that reminder every year, not just every two years).

2. Give both her and any designated third party 30 days' written notice, by mail, before the policy is actually allowed to lapse.

Every single billing notice in the file - going back to 2020 - shows "NO DESIGNEE SELECTED." I don't yet know whether that's because she declined in writing in 2000 (which is allowed) or because John Hancock simply never did what it was supposed to do. I also haven't seen a copy of the actual notice that would have had to go out in early-to-mid 2022 before the policy could lawfully lapse. Under a controlling 1986 New Jersey Supreme Court case, Meier v. New Jersey Life Insurance Co., the burden is on the insurance company - not on us - to prove it gave that notice properly. If they can't produce it, that is an independent problem for them, separate from and in addition to the medical question above.

WHY I'M ASKING YOU TO HOLD OFF ON CALLING JOHN HANCOCK AND SIMPLY OFFERING TO "PAY THE PAST FEES."

I understand the instinct - it feels like the direct, simple fix. But the policy actually has two different paths back to coverage, and they are not equivalent:

- Ordinary reinstatement (apply, pay back premiums, John Hancock approves or denies) - this is what "pay the past fees" describes. But the policy's own language says a policy reinstated this way "will cover only loss due to an injury sustained or physical or mental condition which begins after the date of reinstatement." If your mother's memory issues already existed before we go down this road, this path could get us a technically-reinstated policy that still won't pay for the exact condition we're trying to cover - because memory/cognitive decline that predates the reinstatement date would be excluded as a pre-existing condition.

- The hardship reinstatement path described above - no such exclusion, and restores the original full benefit, not just whatever's paid back.

If we call and simply ask to reinstate and pay back premiums, we risk locking her into the worse path before we've even established she qualifies for the better one. I want to gather the facts first, then approach John Hancock with a specific, deliberate request - not an open-ended "please reinstate us" that they get to interpret however is cheapest for them.
