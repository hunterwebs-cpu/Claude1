# SECTION A: THE PROCEDURAL CHARACTER OF THE MARCH 26, 2021 PARTIAL SUMMARY JUDGMENT

## A.1 New Jersey Court Rule 4:23-5 — Verbatim Text

New Jersey Rule 4:23-5 is captioned "Failure to Make Discovery; Sanctions." The relevant subsections provide:

**R. 4:23-5(a)(1):** If a demand for discovery pursuant to R. 4:17, R. 4:18, or R. 4:19 is not complied with and no timely motion for an extension or a protective order has been made, the party entitled to discovery may, except as otherwise provided by paragraph (c) of this rule, move, on notice, for an order dismissing or suppressing the pleading of the delinquent party. The motion shall be supported by an affidavit reciting the facts of the delinquent party's default and stating that the moving party is not in default in any discovery obligations owed to the delinquent party. **Unless good cause for other relief is shown, the court shall enter an order of dismissal or suppression without prejudice.**

**R. 4:23-5(a)(2):** If an order of dismissal or suppression without prejudice has been entered pursuant to paragraph (a)(1) of this rule and not thereafter vacated, the party entitled to the discovery may, after the expiration of 60 days from the date of the order, move on notice for an order of dismissal or suppression with prejudice. **The motion to dismiss or suppress with prejudice shall be granted unless a motion to vacate the previously entered order of dismissal or suppression without prejudice has been filed by the delinquent party and either the demanded and fully responsive discovery has been provided or exceptional circumstances are demonstrated.**

*Source: N.J. Ct. R. 4:23-5(a)(1)-(2); see also Josie Salazar v. MKGC + Design, 455 N.J. Super. 551 (App. Div. 2019) (quoting the rule and confirming the two-step procedural paradigm).*

### The Two-Step Sanction Paradigm

Rule 4:23-5 "codified a two-step procedural paradigm that must be strictly adhered to before the sanction of dismissal of a complaint with prejudice for failing to answer interrogatories or provide other discovery can be imposed." *Salazar v. MKGC + Design,* 455 N.J. Super. 551, 557 (App. Div. 2019). The Rule's provisions "must be 'scrupulously followed and technically complied with.'" *Id.* (quoting prior authority).

The two steps are:
1. **Step One (R. 4:23-5(a)(1)):** Entry of dismissal/suppression *without prejudice* as the initial sanction.
2. **Step Two (R. 4:23-5(a)(2)):** Entry of dismissal/suppression *with prejudice* — which "shall be granted" absent vacatur — after 60 days.

The delinquent party may move to vacate the without-prejudice order at any time before the entry of the with-prejudice order "supported by affidavit reciting that the discovery has been fully and responsively provided."

### Critical Observation: Rule 4:23-5(a)(2) Is a Discovery Sanction Rule — Not a Merits Adjudication Rule

The express textual basis of Rule 4:23-5 is "Failure to Make Discovery; Sanctions." A partial summary judgment entered under this rule:
- Does **not** require any evidentiary hearing.
- Does **not** involve any testimony on the merits.
- Does **not** adjudicate whether the underlying factual allegations are true.
- Is triggered solely by discovery non-compliance, not by any factual finding about the substance of the claims.

This distinction is foundational to the collateral estoppel argument in Section A.2 below. A ruling entered because a party failed to provide discovery is categorically different from a ruling entered after testimony, briefing, and adjudication of the disputed issues on their merits.

### NJ Courts Confirm the Sanction Nature

New Jersey courts have confirmed that dismissal under R. 4:23-5 is "the ultimate sanction" that "should be imposed only sparingly" and "normally ordered only when no lesser sanction will suffice to erase the prejudice suffered by the non-delinquent party." *Salazar,* 455 N.J. Super. at 558 (internal quotations omitted). The rule "advances two objectives: to compel discovery which would promote resolution of disputes on the merits and to afford the aggrieved party the right to seek final resolution through dismissal." *Id.*

The express purpose of *compelling discovery* confirms that an R. 4:23-5(a)(2) order is a sanction for discovery failure — not a finding that the underlying claims have merit. Any argument that a judgment entered under this rule constitutes a merits adjudication for collateral estoppel purposes is belied by the rule's own text, title, and purpose.

## A.3 — Federal Bankruptcy Law: The "Actually Litigated" Requirement for Collateral Estoppel in § 523(a)(6) Proceedings

### The Foundational Principle: Grogan v. Garner

The Supreme Court confirmed that collateral estoppel applies in § 523(a) discharge exception proceedings. *Grogan v. Garner,* 498 U.S. 279, 284 n.11 (1991). However, for collateral estoppel to apply, the movant must satisfy all elements of the doctrine, including the "actually litigated" requirement.

### The "Actually Litigated" Requirement — Elements

Under New Jersey law (which governs the preclusive effect of the state court judgment), collateral estoppel applies only when:

1. The issue to be precluded is identical to the issue decided in the prior proceeding;
2. **The issue was actually litigated in the prior proceeding;**
3. The court in the prior proceeding issued a final judgment on the merits;
4. The determination of the issue was essential to the prior judgment; and
5. The party against whom the doctrine is asserted was a party to (or in privity with) the prior proceeding.

*See* New Jersey courts applying all five elements. "It is the second requirement, that the issue have been 'actually litigated' in the previous proceeding, which typically bars application of the collateral estoppel doctrine to a default judgment." *In re Wolstein v. Docteroff,* 133 F.3d 210, 215 (3d Cir. 1997); *see also* Restatement (Second) of Judgments § 27 cmt. e (1982) ("a judgment is not conclusive in a subsequent action as to issues which might have been but were not litigated and determined in the prior action").

### The Third Circuit's Rule: In re Docteroff, 133 F.3d 210 (3d Cir. 1997) — The Controlling Authority

**BINDING THIRD CIRCUIT AUTHORITY.** *Wolstein v. Docteroff (In re Docteroff),* 133 F.3d 210 (3d Cir. 1997) is the leading Third Circuit case on collateral estoppel and default/sanction judgments in § 523 proceedings.

The Third Circuit held that a party who **deliberately and willfully obstructs discovery** in a prior proceeding may be "deemed to have actually litigated" the issues for collateral estoppel purposes — but only under specific, factually demanding circumstances.

**Key facts in *Docteroff*:** Docteroff had *actively participated* in the prior Washington state litigation: he filed an answer, noticed the opposing party's deposition, engaged several lawyers including local counsel, filed papers with the court, and corresponded extensively with opposing counsel. He then "apparently realized the meritlessness of his position and decided to frustrate orderly litigation by willfully obstructing discovery." *Docteroff,* 133 F.3d at 215-16.

**The Third Circuit's holding:** Under those specific facts — where a party had a full and fair opportunity to litigate and *chose* to obstruct rather than defend — the court held that the "actually litigated" requirement was met. The Third Circuit joined the Second, Ninth, Tenth, and Eleventh Circuits in recognizing "an exception to the 'actually litigated' requirement when the party sought to be precluded actually participated in the prior litigation and had a full and fair opportunity to litigate, but the issue was resolved by default as a sanction on account of that party's own obstructive behavior." *Id.* at 215.

### Why *Docteroff* Cuts in Brad's Favor Here

The *Docteroff* exception requires:
1. Actual participation in the prior litigation;
2. A full and fair opportunity to litigate;
3. *Willful* obstruction specifically to frustrate orderly process;
4. The issues' *resolution* by default as a *sanction.*

Critically, *Docteroff* does **not** hold that any sanction-based judgment satisfies the "actually litigated" requirement. The court's focus was on *intentional bad-faith obstruction* where the debtor could have litigated but chose to sabotage the proceedings.

Here, Brad invoked his Fifth Amendment rights during civil discovery — which is a constitutionally protected right, not a "willful obstruction." The invocation of a constitutional privilege is categorically different from the bad-faith stonewalling in *Docteroff.* Moreover, the partial summary judgment under R. 4:23-5(a)(2) was entered not because Brad was found to have committed a "willful and malicious" act on the merits, but because he exercised a constitutional right that the civil court penalized through a discovery sanction.

### The § 523(a)(6) "Willful and Malicious" Issues Were Never Actually Litigated

Even if the *Docteroff* exception were applied, the specific issues required for § 523(a)(6) — namely, whether Brad committed a *willful* act and whether that act was *malicious* — were never before the New Jersey court for adjudication. "Courts have held that collateral estoppel was inapplicable because the issue of malice, as employed in Bankruptcy Code §523(a)(6), was never before the jury, and consequently, the issue of malice was never actually litigated." *Princess House Inc. v. Kraft (In re Kraft),* cited in multiple circuits; *see also In re Miller,* 5th Cir. 1998 (same principle).

No testimony was taken. No evidentiary hearing was held. No judge made findings of fact about intent, willfulness, or malice. The March 2021 partial summary judgment was entered mechanically under R. 4:23-5(a)(2) because a procedural deadline was missed — not because any court found that Brad had acted willfully and maliciously within the meaning of § 523(a)(6).

### Additional Bankruptcy Cases on "Actually Litigated" and Sanction/Default Judgments

**The Majority Rule — Default Judgments Generally Do NOT Satisfy "Actually Litigated":**

The baseline federal rule is that "issue preclusion does not apply unless the issue to be precluded in the subsequent action was actually litigated and decided in the prior action." *See Restatement (Second) of Judgments § 27 (1982).* "It is the requirement that the issue have been 'actually litigated' in the previous proceeding which typically bars application of the collateral estoppel doctrine to a default judgment." *Docteroff,* 133 F.3d at 215 (emphasis added).

**Why the *Docteroff* Exception Does Not Overcome the Problem:**

Even circuits that recognize the *Docteroff* exception (willful bad-faith obstruction = deemed actual litigation) have held that the exception requires more than mere invocation of a constitutional right. The exception applies when a party:
- Had substantial participation in the litigation before the sanction;
- Deliberately chose obstruction as a strategy to avoid unfavorable findings;
- Was found to have engaged in bad-faith conduct specifically targeting the discovery process.

Brad's Fifth Amendment invocation in civil discovery is constitutionally protected under *Baxter v. Palmigiano,* 425 U.S. 308 (1976) (noting civil courts may draw adverse inferences from Fifth Amendment invocations in civil proceedings). However, an adverse inference in civil proceedings is a far cry from an adjudication of the specific § 523(a)(6) elements of willfulness and malice.

**Specific § 523(a)(6) Issue Not Before the State Court:**

A party relying on collateral estoppel in a § 523(a)(6) nondischargeability proceeding must demonstrate that the specific elements of § 523(a)(6) — "willful and malicious injury" — were "actually litigated" in the prior proceeding. *Grogan v. Garner,* 498 U.S. at 284. A partial summary judgment entered as a discovery sanction cannot supply this finding because:

(a) No court made any finding about Brad's state of mind, intent, or malice;
(b) No court adjudicated whether his conduct was "willful" within the meaning of *Kawaauhau v. Geiger,* 523 U.S. 57 (1998) (limiting § 523(a)(6) to acts taken with the *specific intent* to cause injury, not merely reckless acts);
(c) The R. 4:23-5(a)(2) order was entered automatically — "shall be granted" — upon the moving party's motion after 60 days, without any merits inquiry.

# SECTION B: THE TRUST ALLEGATIONS — FRAUDULENT TRANSFER TIMING DEFENSE

## B.1 — 11 U.S.C. § 548: Two-Year Lookback and the Trust Formation Dates

### Statutory Text of 11 U.S.C. § 548(a)(1)

11 U.S.C. § 548(a)(1) provides in relevant part:

> **"(a)(1) The trustee may avoid any transfer (including any transfer to or for the benefit of an insider under an employment contract) of an interest of the debtor in property, or any obligation (including any obligation to or for the benefit of an insider under an employment contract) incurred by the debtor, that was made or incurred on or within 2 years before the date of the filing of the petition . . ."**

*11 U.S.C. § 548(a)(1) (emphasis added).*

### The Lookback Calculation

- Petition date: June 12, 2026
- Two-year lookback: June 12, 2026 minus 2 years = **June 12, 2024**
- Any transfer made before June 12, 2024 is **beyond the § 548 lookback** and unreachable under § 548

The KRM Trust and SBM Trust were formed in **2012** — more than **fourteen years** before the petition date and **twelve years** before the § 548 lookback period begins. The 2012 trust formations are categorically and unambiguously outside the § 548 two-year window. No transfer of assets into those trusts in 2012 can be avoided under § 548.

**This is not a close question.** The statutory text is unambiguous: "on or within 2 years before the date of the filing." Transfers in 2012 are fourteen years before filing. They are not subject to § 548 as a matter of law.

*See generally* 11 U.S.C. § 548(a)(1); *In re Sikirica,* Bankr. W.D. Pa. (confirming § 548's two-year limitation); *see also* S. Rep. No. 95-989, at 90 (1978) (confirming the lookback limitation is strict).

## B.2 — N.J.S.A. 25:2-20 et seq. (NJ Uniform Fraudulent Transfer Act / NJUFTA) — Timing Analysis

### The NJUFTA Framework — Two Categories

The NJUFTA (now updated as the New Jersey Uniform Voidable Transaction Act, N.J.S.A. 25:2-20 et seq.) distinguishes between two categories of fraudulent transfers:

**Category 1 — N.J.S.A. 25:2-25 (Present AND Future Creditors — Actual Fraud):**

> *"A transfer made or obligation incurred by a debtor is fraudulent as to a creditor, whether the creditor's claim arose before or after the transfer was made or the obligation was incurred, if the debtor made the transfer or incurred the obligation* **with actual intent to hinder, delay, or defraud any creditor of the debtor."**

This provision covers both present and future creditors — but requires **actual intent** to defraud. The badge-of-fraud analysis applies. A statute of limitations of four years from the transfer or one year from discovery applies. *N.J.S.A. 25:2-31.*

**Category 2 — N.J.S.A. 25:2-27 (Present Creditors ONLY — Constructive Fraud):**

> *"A transfer made or obligation incurred by a debtor is fraudulent as to a creditor whose* **claim arose before the transfer was made** *or the obligation was incurred if the debtor made the transfer or incurred the obligation without receiving a reasonably equivalent value in exchange for the transfer or obligation and the debtor was insolvent at that time or the debtor became insolvent as a result of the transfer or obligation."*

This provision applies **only** to creditors whose claims **predated the transfer**. The elements are: (1) the creditor's claim existed *before* the transfer; (2) no reasonably equivalent value; (3) insolvency.

### Critical Analysis: Lilly's Claim Did Not Pre-Date the Trust Formations

- KRM Trust formed: **2012**
- SBM Trust formed: **2012**
- First contact between Brad and Lilly: **Summer 2015**
- Relationship began: **Summer 2017**
- Earliest possible date any debt arose: **Summer 2017** (and that is being generous to plaintiff)

Under N.J.S.A. 25:2-27 (constructive fraud, present creditors only), Lilly cannot reach the 2012 trust formations because her claim did not exist in 2012. The statute expressly limits this provision to creditors "whose claim arose before the transfer was made." Lilly's claim arose at the earliest in 2017 — five years after the trusts were formed. She is not a "present creditor" within the meaning of N.J.S.A. 25:2-27.

Under N.J.S.A. 25:2-25 (actual fraud, present and future creditors), Lilly **can** theoretically invoke the statute even though her claim arose after the 2012 transfers — but only if she can prove **actual intent** to hinder, delay, or defraud. The difficulty here is that in 2012, when the trusts were formed, Brad had never met Lilly (their first contact was not until summer 2015, and the relationship did not begin until summer 2017). It is logically impossible to form a trust in 2012 with the specific intent to defraud a person one has not yet met and to whom no debt had yet arisen.

**Case Law Supporting the Pre-Debt Timing Defense:**

Courts applying UFTA/UVTA frameworks have recognized that while N.J.S.A. 25:2-25 technically covers future creditors under the "actual intent" prong, the absence of any creditor at the time of transfer is powerful evidence negating fraudulent intent. If the debtor had no creditors and could not have anticipated being indebted to this particular person when the transfer was made, the "badges of fraud" analysis under N.J.S.A. 25:2-26 fails on its face.

The NJUFTA statute of limitations further limits the reach: N.J.S.A. 25:2-31 provides that claims under § 25:2-25 must be brought "within 4 years after the transfer was made or the obligation was incurred or, if later, within 1 year after the transfer or obligation was or could reasonably have been discovered by the claimant." Trusts formed in 2012 and about which Lilly learned no later than during civil discovery (well before June 12, 2022) would be time-barred under this provision as to any state UFTA claims as well.

## B.4 — The Prior Settlement: Res Judicata, Judicial Estoppel, and Disclosure Obligations

### The Prior Settlement — What It Means

The key fact is that **a prior settlement allegedly resolved all trust-related fraudulent transfer claims with a full release obtained.** Nagel is now reasserting those same factual allegations in federal bankruptcy court without disclosing the settlement.

### B.4(a) — Res Judicata / Claim Preclusion

A settlement with a full release operates as a final judgment on the merits for claim preclusion purposes. The Restatement (Second) of Judgments § 27 provides that a valid and final judgment precluding re-litigation of issues actually litigated. More broadly, under claim preclusion principles:

"Res judicata and collateral estoppel bar future litigants from asserting claims or litigating issues that a court has already finally determined in connection with prior litigation, and without them, litigants could endlessly relitigate the same claims and issues with no hope of eventual finality."

A settlement resolving "all trust-related fraudulent transfer claims" with a "full release" prevents Nagel from reasserting those same claims in the bankruptcy adversary proceeding. The elements of claim preclusion are:

1. A final judgment on the merits (a settlement with a full release qualifies);
2. The same parties or those in privity;
3. The same claim or cause of action (here, the trust-related fraudulent transfer allegations);
4. The subsequent action seeks relief on the same claim that was or could have been raised.

*See* New Jersey courts applying Entire Controversy Doctrine principles; *see also* Restatement (Second) of Judgments § 24 (1982) (defining claim for purposes of preclusion by reference to the "transaction" from which it arises).

**Counsel Note:** If the settlement was between Lilly (or B.B.) and Brad (or the trusts), and the release covered "all trust-related fraudulent transfer claims," Nagel's attempt to re-litigate those factual allegations in the bankruptcy proceeding is barred. This applies with particular force because the release specifically covered the trust allegations that Nagel is now deploying in federal court.

### B.4(b) — Judicial Estoppel

The doctrine of judicial estoppel prevents a party from taking inconsistent positions in legal proceedings. Courts apply a three-factor test:

1. **The later position is clearly inconsistent with the earlier position** — Settling "all trust-related fraudulent transfer claims" (implicitly accepting that the claims are resolved/released) is clearly inconsistent with now asserting those same trust-related transfers as evidence of fraudulent conduct;

2. **The party succeeded in persuading a court to accept its earlier position** — The prior court accepted the settlement (approved or acknowledged the release);

3. **The party would derive an unfair advantage or impose an unfair detriment** — Nagel would obtain an unfair second bite at the apple while Brad faces the same allegations already paid for/released.

*See generally New Hampshirev. Maine,* 532 U.S. 742, 750-51 (2001) (setting out the three-factor federal test for judicial estoppel); *see also Adelphia Recovery Trust v. Goldman Sachs & Co.,* 2d Cir. (applying judicial estoppel to bar reassertion of fraudulent conveyance claims after inconsistent prior positions).

In *Adelphia Recovery Trust v. Goldman Sachs,* the Second Circuit held that a fraudulent conveyance claim was barred by judicial estoppel because the plaintiff's predecessor-in-interest had taken an inconsistent prior position. The same principle applies here: having settled "all trust-related fraudulent transfer claims," Nagel's client cannot now re-litigate those claims without disclosing and addressing the prior settlement.

### B.4(c) — RPC 3.3(a)(2) and the Duty of Disclosure

New Jersey RPC 3.3(a)(5) (equivalent to the duty addressed in the prompt as "3.3(a)(2)") provides:

> A lawyer shall not knowingly: **(5) fail to disclose to the tribunal a material fact knowing that the omission is reasonably certain to mislead the tribunal, except that it shall not be a breach of this rule if the disclosure is protected by a recognized privilege or is otherwise prohibited by law.**

*See In re Seelig,* 180 N.J. 234 (2004) (NJ Supreme Court finding RPC 3.3 violation where attorney failed to reveal material fact that would have affected court's ruling, and court accepted a false impression as a result).

Here, Nagel possesses actual knowledge that:
1. The prior civil litigation included trust-related fraudulent transfer claims;
2. Those claims were settled with a full release;
3. The settlement and release are material facts that directly affect the legitimacy of the adversary proceeding's trust-related allegations;
4. The bankruptcy court will likely form a false impression of the state of the prior proceedings if the settlement is not disclosed.

Filing adversary pleadings reasserting the identical factual trust-transfer allegations without disclosing the prior settlement creates a "false impression" — the court will believe these are fresh, unresolved allegations, when in fact they were resolved and released. This implicates RPC 3.3(a)(5) directly.

## C.1 — Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 9011(b)(3) — Verbatim Text

Fed. R. Bankr. P. 9011(b) provides:

> *"By presenting to the court (whether by signing, filing, submitting, or later advocating) a petition, pleading, written motion, or other document, an attorney or unrepresented party is certifying that to the best of the person's knowledge, information, and belief, formed after an inquiry reasonable under the circumstances —*
>
> *(1) it is not being presented for any improper purpose, such as to harass or to cause unnecessary delay or needless increase in the cost of litigation;*
>
> *(2) the claims, defenses, and other legal contentions therein are warranted by existing law or by a nonfrivolous argument for the extension, modification, or reversal of existing law or the establishment of new law;*
>
> **(3) the allegations and factual contentions have evidentiary support or, if specifically so identified, are likely to have evidentiary support after a reasonable opportunity for further investigation or discovery; [and]*
>
> *(4) the denials of factual contentions are warranted on the evidence or, if specifically so identified, are reasonably based on a lack of information or belief."*

Fed. R. Bankr. P. 9011(b)(1)-(4) (emphasis on (b)(3)).

### The Sanctions Mechanism

If, after notice and a reasonable opportunity to respond, the court determines that Rule 9011(b) has been violated, the court "may, subject to the conditions stated below, impose an appropriate sanction upon the attorneys, law firms, or parties that have violated subdivision (b) or are responsible for the violation." Fed. R. Bankr. P. 9011(c).

**Important:** Rule 9011 requires a safe-harbor notice: the motion for sanctions must be served 21 days before filing to give the offending party an opportunity to cure. Given the July 16 hearing date, counsel should assess whether a safe-harbor notice can be served immediately.

### The Applicable Standard Under Rule 9011

Rule 9011 sanctions require "only a showing of *objectively unreasonable conduct*" — this is an objective standard, not a subjective bad-faith standard. *Compare* 28 U.S.C. § 1927 (requiring willful bad faith in the Third Circuit). This makes Rule 9011 the more accessible avenue for sanctions here.

The Rule 9011 standard asks: "whether a reasonable attorney in like circumstances could believe his actions were factually and legally justified." Courts have held that sanctions may be imposed for certifying facts the attorney "knew or reasonably should have known" were false.

### The Specific Proposition: Discovery-Knowledge Bars Good Faith

"[The] prohibition . . . prohibits not only assertions of fact without sufficient support but also outright misstatements of fact, whether about the merits or about the case itself — so-called 'litigation facts' of which counsel has first-hand knowledge." *See* Rule 11/9011 commentary; *cf.* 28 U.S.C. § 1927 cases discussing attorney certification of known falsehoods.

An attorney who personally conducted civil discovery, obtained documents and deposition testimony establishing that (a) the trusts predate the debt by five-plus years, and (b) the trust-related fraudulent transfer claims were settled and released, **cannot certify in a federal pleading** that those same trust-related allegations "have evidentiary support" for the proposition that the transfers were fraudulent as to Lilly. The attorney *knows* the facts that undercut his own allegation.

The critical rule: **An attorney who possesses actual knowledge of facts contradicting his factual certifications — because those facts emerged in discovery he personally conducted — cannot claim good faith under Rule 9011(b)(3).** This is not a question of inadequate investigation; Nagel (or his partner Paragano) *conducted* the discovery. The facts are within their actual personal knowledge.

*See generally* the principle articulated in Rule 9011 commentary: while an attorney may rely on client representations, the attorney must independently verify facts of which he has first-hand knowledge. If an attorney's own discovery files contradict his pleading certifications, the "reasonable inquiry" standard is violated as a matter of law.

## C.2 — 28 U.S.C. § 1927 — Verbatim Text and Standard

### Statutory Text

> **"Any attorney or other person admitted to conduct cases in any court of the United States or any Territory thereof who so multiplies the proceedings in any case unreasonably and vexatiously may be required by the court to satisfy personally the excess costs, expenses, and attorneys' fees reasonably incurred because of such conduct."**

28 U.S.C. § 1927 (emphasis added).

### Third Circuit Standard: Baker Industries v. Cerberus — Willful Bad Faith Required

**BINDING THIRD CIRCUIT AUTHORITY.** *Baker Industries, Inc. v. Cerberus Ltd. (Appeal of Cravath, Swaine & Moore),* 764 F.2d 204 (3d Cir. 1985). The Third Circuit held that **willful bad faith is a prerequisite** to § 1927 sanctions. This is a more demanding standard than Rule 9011's objective reasonableness test.

Three requirements under *Baker Industries:*
1. **Multiplication of proceedings** by an attorney;
2. **Unreasonable and vexatious conduct** — requires more than mere negligence or lack of merit;
3. **Resulting increase in the cost of proceedings.**

*Baker Industries,* 764 F.2d at 208. A bad-faith finding is required as a precondition to attorney fee awards under § 1927. *Id.*

**Factor 1: Nagel had actual knowledge of the bankruptcy filing.** Nagel's partner Paragano emailed Brad directly on June 18, 2026 — three days after Nagel's office had actual knowledge of the bankruptcy filing on June 12, 2026. This email asserted "a prejudgment attachment from the now-dismissed civil case." This is not a close call on knowledge.

**Factor 2: The civil case from which the attachment was asserted is dismissed.** Asserting a prejudgment attachment from a dismissed case is legally baseless on its face.

**Factor 3: The email was sent in violation of the automatic stay.** 11 U.S.C. § 362(a) prohibits "any act to obtain possession of property of the estate or of property from the estate." Asserting a prejudgment attachment after the bankruptcy filing is a stay violation.

**Factor 4: Nagel is reasserting released trust claims.** Filing adversary pleadings that assert trust-related fraudulent transfer allegations already settled and released multiplies proceedings on claims that were resolved. This satisfies the "multiplication of proceedings" element.

**Factor 5: Nagel conducted the discovery establishing the timing facts.** Nagel's office knows the trusts predate any debt to Lilly by five-plus years. Filing § 548/NJUFTA claims on the 2012 trusts with that knowledge, without making any effort to establish the "actual intent" necessary to reach pre-debt transfers under N.J.S.A. 25:2-25, is objectively unreasonable and — with Nagel's actual knowledge of the timing — supports willful bad faith.

**The § 1927 "Knowingly or Recklessly" Standard:** While the Third Circuit requires "willful bad faith" under *Baker Industries,* courts have held that "knowingly or recklessly" pursuing a frivolous claim constitutes bad faith within the meaning of § 1927. *See* general § 1927 standards. An attorney who *knows* the facts that defeat his own claims and files anyway acts willfully, not merely negligently.

## C.3 — RPC 3.3(a)(2) — The Affirmative Duty of Candor and the Settlement Disclosure Failure

### New Jersey RPC 3.3(a)(5) — The Material Fact Disclosure Obligation

New Jersey's RPC 3.3(a)(5) imposes an affirmative duty to disclose material facts necessary to prevent a false impression:

> *"A lawyer shall not knowingly: fail to disclose to the tribunal a material fact knowing that the omission is reasonably certain to mislead the tribunal, except that it shall not be a breach of this rule if the disclosure is protected by a recognized privilege or is otherwise prohibited by law."*

N.J. RPC 3.3(a)(5).

### In re Seelig — The Controlling NJ Supreme Court Case

*In re Seelig,* 180 N.J. 234 (2004). The New Jersey Supreme Court found an RPC 3.3 violation where an attorney failed to reveal to the court a material fact — specifically, that the individual involved in his client's automobile accident had died — hoping the court would accept a plea arrangement that would be precluded by the death. The Court found that the attorney's silence created a false impression that fell squarely within the Rule's prohibition.

*Seelig* establishes the principle: **silence that creates a false impression about a material fact is the equivalent of an affirmative misrepresentation.** Where "failure to make a disclosure is the equivalent of an affirmative misrepresentation," RPC 3.3 is violated.

### Application Here

Nagel's failure to disclose the prior settlement (which resolved all trust-related fraudulent transfer claims with a full release) while simultaneously filing adversary pleadings reasserting those same trust allegations creates exactly the false impression condemned in *Seelig:*

- The bankruptcy court will believe the trust-related allegations are live, unresolved claims with evidentiary support;
- In fact, those allegations were settled and released;
- The omission of this material fact is "reasonably certain to mislead the tribunal" into believing the adversary proceeding has a factual basis that it does not have;
- Nagel's actual knowledge of the settlement makes the omission knowing — not inadvertent.

This is not a case where the prior settlement is confidential and disclosure would be protected by privilege. A settlement that bars subsequent litigation of the same claims must be disclosed to a court that is being asked to adjudicate those same claims. Counsel should evaluate whether a bar complaint is warranted in addition to or in lieu of a Rule 9011 motion.

## C.4 — Heightened Duty of Candor in Bankruptcy Proceedings

### General Candor Framework in Bankruptcy

The bankruptcy court has multiple overlapping sources of authority requiring candor:

1. **Fed. R. Bankr. P. 9011** (discussed above — objective reasonableness standard);
2. **28 U.S.C. § 1927** (discussed above — willful bad faith standard);
3. **11 U.S.C. § 105(a)** — The bankruptcy court may issue "any order, process, or judgment that is necessary or appropriate to carry out the provisions of this title." Courts have used § 105 to sanction attorney misconduct not covered by Rule 9011.
4. **The court's inherent authority** — Bankruptcy courts retain inherent authority to impose sanctions for bad faith conduct, requiring a finding of bad faith or conduct "tantamount to bad faith."

**Important:** The Shulman Bastian court (2022) held that sanctions under Rule 9011, § 105(a), and inherent authority are **not interchangeable**: "each sanction tool has different standards and different procedures." *Sanctions Under Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 9011, 11 U.S.C. § 105(a), and the Court's Inherent Authority Are Not Interchangeable* (Shulman Bastian Friedman Bui & O'Dea LLP, 2022). Counsel should plead each basis distinctly.

The D.N.J. Local Bankruptcy Rules (effective August 1, 2024) are available at https://www.njb.uscourts.gov/sites/njb/files/Local%20Rules%20Package.pdf. Key rules relevant to candor and sanctions include:

- **D.N.J. LBR 9011-3** (if extant) — provisions on certifications and representations;
- **D.N.J. LBR 9013-2** — governs motion practice and the duty to accurately represent the state of the record.

**Counsel Must Review:** The full Local Rules Package should be reviewed for any provisions specifically addressing disclosure obligations, the duty to supplement representations, and the consequences of omissions. The 2024 amended Local Rules should be checked for any new candor provisions.

## C.5 — The Paragano June 18, 2026 Email — Automatic Stay Violation

**This is an independent ground for sanctions and possible contempt.**

The facts: Paragano (Nagel's partner) emailed Brad directly on June 18, 2026, asserting a prejudgment attachment from the now-dismissed civil case. This occurred three days after Nagel's office had actual knowledge of the bankruptcy filing on June 12, 2026.

**Legal Analysis:**

1. **Automatic Stay Violation:** 11 U.S.C. § 362(a)(1) stays "the commencement or continuation . . . of a judicial, administrative, or other action or proceeding against the debtor." 11 U.S.C. § 362(a)(4) stays "any act to create, perfect, or enforce any lien against property of the estate." Asserting a prejudgment attachment from a civil case against a debtor in bankruptcy violates § 362(a)(4).

2. **Willful Violation:** With actual knowledge of the bankruptcy filing, any stay violation is "willful" within the meaning of § 362(k)(1), entitling Brad (as debtor) to "actual damages, including costs and attorneys' fees, and, in appropriate circumstances, . . . punitive damages." 11 U.S.C. § 362(k)(1).

3. **The Civil Case is Dismissed:** Asserting an attachment from a dismissed case is legally baseless. The attachment cannot exist as a valid lien if the underlying case is dismissed.

4. **Direct Contact with Represented Party:** Paragano emailed Brad "directly." If Brad is represented by counsel, this may also violate RPC 4.2 (the no-contact rule prohibiting direct contact with a represented party).

**Recommend:** A motion for sanctions under § 362(k) and/or a contempt motion should be filed promptly. This creates leverage for the July 16 hearing and is an independent ground for attorney sanctions against Nagel/Paragano.

# SECTION D: STATUTORY TEXTS — N.J.S.A. 2A:61B-1, 2A:30B-3, AND 2A:58D-1

## D.1 — N.J.S.A. 2A:61B-1 — New Jersey Child Sexual Abuse Act

**N.J.S.A. 2A:61B-1** provides the civil cause of action for sexual abuse of minors. Key provisions:

**Definition of "Sexual Abuse":** "Sexual abuse" means an act of sexual contact or sexual penetration between a child under the age of 18 years and an adult.

**Civil Cause of Action:** The statute creates a civil cause of action for victims of child sexual abuse. The cause of action accrues "at the time of reasonable discovery of the injury and its causal relationship to the act of sexual abuse." Any such action is "subject to the statute of limitations set forth in section 2 of P.L.2019, c.120 (C.2A:14-2a)."

**Damages:** A plaintiff who prevails shall be awarded:
- Damages in the amount of **$10,000, plus reasonable attorney's fees**, or
- **Actual damages**, whichever is greater

Actual damages include compensatory and punitive damages and costs of suit, including attorney's fees. Compensatory damages may include damages for pain and suffering, medical expenses, emotional trauma, diminished childhood, diminished enjoyment of life, costs of counseling, and lost wages.

**Note on § 523(a)(6) Analysis:** For nondischargeability purposes under § 523(a)(6), the elements of the N.J.S.A. 2A:61B-1 cause of action must map onto the "willful and malicious injury" requirement of § 523(a)(6). Because the statute covers sexual contact or penetration between a child and an adult — which is inherently intentional conduct — this cause of action is well-suited for § 523(a)(6) analysis if the underlying acts satisfy *Kawaauhau v. Geiger's* specific-intent requirement. However, as discussed in Section A, the March 2021 partial summary judgment under R. 4:23-5(a)(2) did not involve a finding on the § 2A:61B-1 elements.

**Full Statutory Text:** The full current text of N.J.S.A. 2A:61B-1 is available at https://law.justia.com/codes/new-jersey/title-2a/section-2a-61b-1/ and the official New Jersey Legislature website. Counsel should pull the complete current statutory text including all subsections and the 2019 amendments under P.L.2019, c.120.

## D.2 — N.J.S.A. 2A:30B-3 — Civil Sexual Assault Victim Protection Act

**N.J.S.A. 2A:30B-3** is part of Chapter 2A:30B (P.L.1992, c.7 as amended), which created a civil cause of action for victims of prohibited sexual acts. The relevant provisions:

**Prohibited Sexual Act** is defined in N.J.S.A. 2A:30B-2 and includes acts of sexual penetration or sexual contact as defined under the criminal code (N.J.S.A. 2C:14-1 et seq.).

**Civil Cause of Action (N.J.S.A. 2A:30B-3):** Creates a civil cause of action for "sexual assault, any other crime of a sexual nature, a prohibited sexual act as defined in section 2 of P.L.1992, c.7 (C.2A:30B-2), or sexual abuse" against both minors and adults.

**Accrual:** Under P.L.2019, c.120 (extending the statute of limitations), the cause of action accrues at the time of reasonable discovery of the injury and its causal relationship to the act.

**Note on § 523(a)(6) Analysis:** N.J.S.A. 2A:30B-3 covers adult victims of sexual assault — not limited to minors as under § 2A:61B-1. For the § 523(a)(6) count-by-count analysis in Part III, counsel should note that claims under § 2A:30B-3 arising from intentional sexual assault satisfy the "willful and malicious" standard of *Kawaauhau* if the underlying acts were intentional violations of the criminal code.

**Full Statutory Text:** Available at the New Jersey Legislature website (lis.njleg.state.nj.us) and through Justia. The 2019 and 2024 amendments to the limitations period should be confirmed for the current version. See also *Ormond Simpkins, Jr. v. South Orange-Maplewood School Dist.,* decided 2026 (App. Div. A-36/37/38/39-24), and *J.H. v. Warren Hills Board of Education* (App. Div. 2025), both of which discuss the current state of civil sexual abuse claims under these statutes.

## D.3 — N.J.S.A. 2A:58D-1 — Invasion of Privacy (Non-Consensual Distribution of Intimate Images)

**N.J.S.A. 2A:58D-1** covers civil liability for invasion of privacy through non-consensual disclosure of intimate images. Key provisions:

**Prohibited Conduct:** An actor who discloses any photograph, film, videotape, recording, or other reproduction of another person engaged in sexual penetration or sexual contact, or exposed or undergarment-clad intimate parts, shall be liable to that person who may bring a civil action in Superior Court.

**Definition of "Disclose":** "Disclose" means sell, manufacture, give, provide, lend, trade, mail, deliver, transfer, publish, distribute, circulate, disseminate, present, exhibit, advertise, offer, share, or make available via the Internet or by any other means, whether for pecuniary gain or not.

**Damages:**
- Actual damages of not less than **$1,000 liquidated damages for each violation**
- **Punitive damages** upon proof of willful or reckless conduct
- Reasonable attorney's fees and litigation costs

**Enacted:** P.L.2003, c.206; amended 2016.

**Note on § 523(a)(6) Analysis:** Willful disclosure of intimate images meets the *Kawaauhau* "specific intent to cause injury" standard under § 523(a)(6) — the debtor must have intended the act (the disclosure) and the injury (to the victim's privacy, dignity, and emotional wellbeing) would be a virtually certain result. If Brad engaged in non-consensual recording or distribution of intimate images, the § 523(a)(6) nondischargeability analysis on Count [X] under § 2A:58D-1 should proceed on this theory.

**Full Statutory Text:** Current text available at https://law.justia.com/codes/new-jersey/title-2a/section-2a-58d-1/. Counsel should confirm the current version as the statute was amended in 2016 and may have been further amended.
