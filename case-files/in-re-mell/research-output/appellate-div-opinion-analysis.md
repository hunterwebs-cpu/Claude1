## 1. FULL HOLDING — WHAT DID THE APPELLATE DIVISION DECIDE?

The Appellate Division resolved an interlocutory appeal on the scope of deposition questioning permitted defendants at plaintiff B.B.'s deposition.

**The precise holding:**

> "We affirm the trial court's order denying plaintiff's request to bar defendants from asking questions at deposition relating to her consent to sexual activity, **modified** to remove the age restriction imposed therein."

In other words:
- The trial court had entered an order "precluding deposition questioning on the issue of consent as to facts that occurred before plaintiff was 16 years old" while noting the order did not limit questions on damages.
- The Appellate Division **affirmed** the denial of the broad protective order B.B. sought but **modified** the order to remove the age-16 cutoff entirely — defendants may ask consent-related questions about acts both before and after B.B.'s sixteenth birthday.

The court's central holding on the scope of discovery:

> "Defendants are entitled to full factual discovery on all issues relevant to the claims put forth in B.B.'s complaint and any available defenses, including the nature and extent of her sexual activity with Mell, without limitation under Rule 4:10-2."

And:

> "plaintiff's conduct, including whether she 'consented' or actively participated in sexual acts with Mell whether she was fifteen or sixteen is relevant, at this stage of the proceeding, to liability on all of her common law claims as well as to all of her damages claims."

**Nature of the proceeding:** "This appeal is here on our leave to resolve whether a plaintiff, eighteen years old when she filed her complaint, and who is suing to recover damages for injuries resulting from child sexual abuse can block defendants, including the abuser, from questioning her at deposition regarding the sexual acts she alleges in her complaint that occurred before the age in which she could legally consent under the State's criminal law."

**Key limiting language:** "All we've done is establish the proper scope of discovery — not the admissibility of evidence at trial — by conducting the analysis Rule 4:10-2(a) demands..."

## 2. EVERY PASSAGE CHARACTERIZING THE MARCH 26, 2021 PARTIAL SUMMARY JUDGMENT ORDER (R. 4:23-5(A)(2))

**CRITICAL NOTE TO DRAFTING COUNSEL:** The opinion contains NO reference to R. 4:23-5(a)(2). The March 26, 2021 partial summary judgment order is referenced, but it is characterized as a merits ruling on liability — not as a sanction order under R. 4:23-5. The text of footnote 2 is the primary characterization passage:

### Passage 1 — Main Body (describing procedural posture):

> "The trial court also granted B.B. partial summary judgment on liability on her claims under the Child Sexual Abuse Act and the child exploitation statute, as well as for assault and battery and both counts of invasion of privacy but denied summary judgment on her claim for false imprisonment."

*Characterization: A merits ruling on liability on specific counts.*

### Passage 2 — Footnote 2 (the critical interlocutory/procedural characterization):

> "[2] Although B.B.'s counsel repeatedly notes B.B. has already obtained judgment for liability on several of her claims, **the order for partial summary judgment is interlocutory and not a final judgment.** It is not clear from the record whether Mell responded to the summary judgment motion."

*Characterization: Interlocutory, not final. The court expressly declines to treat it as a final judgment. The court also notes uncertainty as to whether Mell even responded to the motion.*

### Passage 3 — Relevance to discovery dispute:

> "Acknowledging he'd awarded B.B. summary judgment as to liability on several counts of her complaint, the judge found the questions 'go to damages,' and defendants are 'certainly allowed' to probe the basis of the damages claim."

*Characterization: The trial court itself treated the partial summary judgment as resolving only liability, with damages remaining open for discovery and trial.*

### Passage 4 — Scope limitation by footnote 4:

> "[4] Contrary to our concurring colleague's view, our analysis does not in any way address what evidence may be admissible at trial. The muddle in the trial court resulted from plaintiff's insistence that the criminal age of consent dictated the scope of discovery in this civil case... The trial court's failure to address that issue head on led to its ruling 'preclud[ing] deposition questioning on the issue of consent as to facts that occurred before... plaintiff was 16 years old' while allowing 'questions on any and all damages that plaintiff is claiming in this case [including those flowing from acts that occurred before her sixteenth birthday] and is expected to testify to at the time of trial.' In our view, that ruling provided the parties with too little guidance about the scope of discovery, and would, in any event, prove unworkable in practice, leading to more depositions like the first one."

*This passage illustrates that even after the partial summary judgment on liability, the scope of discovery on damages remained fully contested and the court treated damages as entirely unresolved.*

### Passage 5 — B.B.'s own framing (accepted by court):

> "B.B. moved to quash those subpoenas and to limit the questioning of third parties 'to the facts related to the counts of the amended complaint which have not yet been decided by the court on partial summary judgment.'"

*This confirms the partial PSJ was treated by all parties as covering only liability on specific counts, leaving all else open.*

**SUMMARY FOR BANKRUPTCY PURPOSES:** The Appellate Division characterized the March 26, 2021 order as (a) interlocutory, (b) not a final judgment, (c) a partial ruling on liability only on specific counts, (d) leaving damages entirely unresolved, and (e) not even certain whether Mell had responded to the motion. There is **no characterization** of this order as a sanction under R. 4:23-5(a)(2) anywhere in the opinion. The court treats it as a merits determination on liability — but an incomplete, interlocutory one.

## 4. PASSAGES ABOUT THE NATURE OF THE UNDERLYING LIABILITY DETERMINATION

### Passage 1 — Scope of counts covered:

> "The trial court also granted B.B. partial summary judgment on liability on her claims under the Child Sexual Abuse Act and the child exploitation statute, as well as for assault and battery and both counts of invasion of privacy but **denied** summary judgment on her claim for false imprisonment."

### Passage 2 — Discovery continues on full record:

> "Even if B.B. could eventually establish that Mell should be precluded from apportioning liability to her, not because of any criminal statute, but, for instance, because his civil 'duty of care encompasse[d] the obligation to prevent the specific misconduct that caused [her] injury,' see, e.g., Martin v. Prime Hosp. Corp., 345 N.J. Super. 278, 287 (App. Div. 2001), she could not do so on less than a full record..."

### Passage 3 — Comparative fault remains open:

> "Thus, a jury finding liability on the part of a defendant in an action brought under the Child Sexual Abuse Act for actual damages is required, if there are sufficient facts in the record to permit a reasonable jury to conclude the plaintiff's conduct contributed to the injuries she has allegedly sustained, to assess whether the plaintiff was also negligent, and, if so, whether plaintiff's negligence was a proximate cause of the harm suffered."

### Passage 4 — Discovery scope confirms the PSJ is not final on all issues:

> "plaintiff's conduct, including whether she 'consented' or actively participated in sexual acts with Mell whether she was fifteen or sixteen is relevant, at this stage of the proceeding, to liability on all of her common law claims as well as to all of her damages claims."

### Passage 5 — Not yet deposed:

> "Although Mell has yet to be deposed and B.B.'s deposition has not been completed, certain significant events have already occurred in the case."

**KEY FINDING:** The liability determination, while granting partial summary judgment on certain counts, left open: (a) all damage questions, (b) comparative fault, (c) liability on the false imprisonment count, (d) liability of the non-Mell defendants (Gulfstream entities, Aero Care, Ruggles Mell, the trusts, and Mountainside Securities), and (e) Mell was never deposed as of the opinion date.

## 5. EXACT QUOTES FROM THE CONCURRENCE BY JUDGE NATALI

The concurrence is set off beginning: "NATALI, J.A.D., concurring. Having received the majority's opinion, I write separately to detail the bases for my concurrence and to state I cannot join in the majority's opinion except as expressly noted."

### On Agreement With the Result (Discovery Is Proper):

> "First, I agree, based on the specific allegations of plaintiff's complaint and her attendant claims for punitive and compensatory damage, that appropriate discovery into those allegations and claims is proper. I do so because our Supreme Court has said, time and again, that '[o]ur court system has long been committed to the view that essential justice is better achieved when there has been full disclosure so that the parties [may become] conversant with all the available facts,' Capital Health Sys. v. Horizon Healthcare Servs., 230 N.J. 73, 80 (2017) (quoting Jenkins v. Rainner, 69 N.J. 50, 56 (1976)), and, to serve that purpose, the Court has further instructed that our discovery rules 'are to be construed liberally in favor of broad pretrial discovery,' ibid. (quoting Payton v. N.J. Tpk. Auth., 148 N.J. 524, 535 (1997)). But I cannot, and do not, join in the rest of the opinion's analysis for the following reasons."

### On Scope of Discovery:

> "There is much in the majority's opinion that purports to determine whether or how plaintiff's answers to such questions may be used at trial. I agree, as plaintiff's counsel noted at oral argument, that defendants may inquire about those factual allegations as well as to damages. On this point all parties agree, discovery is broad; in my view, plaintiff — who is now an adult — may be asked about her claims to the extent those questions may be relevant or might lead to the discovery of relevant evidence.[8] How or whether the defense may use her words or acts when she was a minor at trial poses other and different questions not presently before us."

### On Sensitive Nature of Questioning and Concern About the Majority's Approach:

> "Second, I agree with the majority's penultimate paragraph in which they recognize the 'sensitive nature of the questioning at issue' and they 'remain committed to the belief that no witness — particularly one suing under the Child Sexual Abuse Act — should be unnecessarily embarrassed by questions posed at a deposition.' But, the approach taken by the majority, in my view, may have the unintended consequence of denying those very protections. On this point, let us not lose sight of the facts underlying this appeal. Plaintiff has alleged, in excruciating detail, that Mell — who pled guilty to serious charges — engaged in criminal conduct by, among other aberrant behavior, grooming her and sexually assaulting her when she was but a fifteen-year old child."

### On the Limits of Discovery:

> "While defendants are certainly entitled to inquire about those facts reasonably, discovery is not a free for all. And I am convinced that the majority's legal conclusion that 'plaintiff's conduct, including, whether she 'consented' or actively participated in a sexual relationship with Mell whether she was fifteen or sixteen is relevant, at this stage of the proceeding, to liability on all of her common law claims as well as to all of her damages claims',[9] is unnecessary to resolve whether the trial court properly exercised its considerable discretion."

### On Risk of Abuse:

> "I also believe those comments may actually result in an abuse of the discovery process, particularly in a case involving a fifteen and sixteen-year old abused by Mell, as alleged. My concerns are based not on an imaginary parade of horribles, but on the series of questions that led to this appeal. As detailed in the majority's opinion, plaintiff's counsel did not preclude questioning as to what effect Mell's conduct and sexual assaults had on her emotional well-being, or how it harmed her personally, educationally or professionally. Instead, after an earlier teleconference with the court, defendants proceeded to ask plaintiff, over four times, about anal sex, and what she said to Mell. When plaintiff responded she didn't like it and it 'really hurt', and counsel continued in the manner described by the majority, plaintiff's counsel objected.[10] How will the court realistically be able to enter a protective order if, hypothetically, defendants, on remand, ask cumulative, harassing and oppressive questions of B.B., in light of the language in the majority's decision detailed above?"

### On Judicial Restraint and the Proper Approach:

> "I would permit discovery as to the factual allegations of the complaint and damages and say no more. As the trial court noted, certain questions that appear to involve 'consent' may address damages and are likely appropriate. Other questions, that address the factual allegations but are repetitive and harassing could be determined by the trial judge to be beyond the scope of proper discovery or warranting a protective order. The trial court is in the best position to make those rulings which are at their core specific. In my view, they cannot be decided in the abstract and without a full record and as the majority correctly notes, the trial court has an 'arsenal of tools and sanctions at its disposal to ensure the deposition of a witness is conducted fairly and professionally'."

### On Temporal Restraint:

> "Finally, there is another reason I would let the trial court resolve any discovery disputes on a more detailed record and that is because I believe judicial restraint compels such an approach. I choose not to predict what questions will be asked and in what context. Nor do I prefer to suggest how or whether such information may be used at trial. We can understand and appreciate whether this information should be given to the factfinder only once it is offered at trial when its relevance, if any, can be appreciated in the context of the other evidence. At present, we neither know the answers to any future questions nor how any party may attempt to use those answers — if at all — at trial; for these reasons, the majority's expansive views are precipitous."

### Footnote 8 (Natali):

> "[8] My view might be different if plaintiff were still a minor."

### Footnote 9 (Natali — expressly rejecting majority language):

> "[9] I expressly reject and do not support any language in the majority's opinion that characterizes plaintiff's allegations as if she 'partipat[ed]' in Mell's sexual assaults or describes her claims in any other way than how plaintiff detailed them in her complaint. I similarly reject any comments that suggest discovery is necessary as it may be relevant to any hypothetical 'fault' that the majority believes may, at trial, be ascribed to plaintiff. The current record provides no support for such speculation."

### Footnote 10 (Natali):

> "[10] As my colleague recounts, the court addressed the issue of plaintiff's counsel's improper speaking objections. If anything, that evidences the court's ability to control the discovery process."

## 7. LANGUAGE USABLE IN BANKRUPTCY OPPOSITION BRIEF — PROCEDURAL/NON-MERITS NATURE OF THE PARTIAL SUMMARY JUDGMENT

### Primary Language:

**Footnote 2 (verbatim):**
> "Although B.B.'s counsel repeatedly notes B.B. has already obtained judgment for liability on several of her claims, **the order for partial summary judgment is interlocutory and not a final judgment.** It is not clear from the record whether Mell responded to the summary judgment motion."

This passage is the most powerful. The Appellate Division — in this very case — expressly stated the March 26, 2021 partial summary judgment is **interlocutory and not a final judgment**. It also notes factual uncertainty as to whether Mell even responded to the motion.

### Supporting Language on Incompleteness of Liability Determination:

1. The partial summary judgment covered only Counts I, II, III, VII, and VIII of the First Amended Complaint. The trial court **denied** summary judgment on the false imprisonment count. Multiple defendants (Ruggles Mell, the trusts, Gulfstream entities, Aero Care, Mountainside Securities, and the children) were not subject to the partial summary judgment.

2. > "Although Mell has yet to be deposed and B.B.'s deposition has not been completed, certain significant events have already occurred in the case."

   As of the decision date (December 31, 2024), Mell had not been deposed. The record was explicitly incomplete.

3. > "Even if B.B. could eventually establish that Mell should be precluded from apportioning liability to her... she could not do so on less than a full record..."

4. > "Thus, a jury finding liability on the part of a defendant in an action brought under the Child Sexual Abuse Act for actual damages is required, if there are sufficient facts in the record to permit a reasonable jury to conclude the plaintiff's conduct contributed to the injuries she has allegedly sustained, to assess whether the plaintiff was also negligent..."

   The court's comparative fault analysis confirms that a jury — not the trial court on partial summary judgment — must resolve the full liability picture.

5. The majority explicitly limited its holding to discovery scope, not trial admissibility:
   > "All we've done is establish the proper scope of discovery — not the admissibility of evidence at trial..."

### Nagel's Own Characterization in the MTD (to be used against him):

In the bankruptcy Motion to Dismiss (Doc. 8), Nagel himself describes the March 26, 2021 order as granting "partial summary judgment against Mell on Count I (Violation of the Child Sex Abuse Act), Count II (Child Pornographer's Liability to Victim), Count III (Assault and Battery), Count VII (Invasion of Privacy) and Count VIII (Invasion of Privacy Pursuant to N.J.S.A. 21:58D-1) of B.B.'s Complaint." He also states: "B.B. has obtained a judgment of liability against Mell." He does not anywhere in the MTD characterize the order as a final judgment — it remains a judgment on liability on specific counts, with damages (and full comparative fault analysis) entirely pending.

### Concurrence Supporting Procedural View:

Judge Natali expressly declines to endorse any language suggesting fault could be ascribed to plaintiff — underscoring that the majority's comparative fault discussion was dicta regarding future trial proceedings, not a merits ruling:

> "[9] I expressly reject and do not support any language in the majority's opinion that characterizes plaintiff's allegations as if she 'partipat[ed]' in Mell's sexual assaults... I similarly reject any comments that suggest discovery is necessary as it may be relevant to any hypothetical 'fault' that the majority believes may, at trial, be ascribed to plaintiff. The current record provides no support for such speculation."

## COUNSEL NOTES

1. **No R. 4:23-5(a)(2) reference anywhere in this opinion.** If the partial summary judgment was entered under that rule (sanctions for non-participation in discovery), that characterization must come from the March 26, 2021 order itself or from the trial court's statements at the time of entry — not from this Appellate Division opinion, which treats the order on its face as a merits liability ruling.

2. **The opinion is unpublished (R. 1:36-3)** and expressly not precedential. Its value in bankruptcy proceedings is as evidence of the state of the litigation, not as binding authority.

3. **The two-year gap between argument (November 10, 2022) and decision (December 31, 2024)** is notable. The trial was eventually scheduled for June 2026 — nearly four years after this appeal was argued.

4. The appellate opinion **nowhere addresses** the Fifth Amendment, asset transfers, the trusts, the divorce/MSA, or the fraudulent transfer claims. Those are distinct claims against different defendants.
