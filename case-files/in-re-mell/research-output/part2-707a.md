### A.1 — In re Tamecki, 229 F.3d 205 (3d Cir. 2000)

**Status: BINDING — Third Circuit precedent directly controlling in D.N.J.**
**Subsequent History: No reversal; no Supreme Court cert.; consistently applied.**

**Full Citation:** In re Ronald M. Tamecki, Sr., 229 F.3d 205 (3d Cir. 2000)

#### Facts

Ronald M. Tamecki, Sr. filed a Chapter 7 petition carrying a single credit card debt of approximately $35,000. He jointly owned a residence with his estranged wife carrying roughly $100,000 in equity. The bankruptcy trustee and creditor moved to dismiss under § 707(a), contending Tamecki filed in bad faith because he knew he would soon be able to repay his debts — the marital property settlement, once finalized, would leave him with sufficient assets to pay the $35,000 credit card balance. The district court affirmed the bankruptcy court's dismissal.

#### The Good Faith Standard (Verbatim)

The Third Circuit held:

> "Good faith is an element of the right to [Chapter 7] relief. The general rule, derived from the purposes of the bankruptcy laws, is that a petition in bankruptcy filed in bad faith is an abuse of the bankruptcy process." 229 F.3d at 207.

The court further held that the debtor's good faith is tested by whether the filing is consistent with the honest intention required of bankruptcy petitioners:

> "At the least, [good faith] requires a showing of honest intention." 229 F.3d at 207.

Additionally, courts should assess good faith on an "ad hoc basis," considering "whether the debtor has abused the provisions, purpose, or spirit of bankruptcy law." 229 F.3d at 207.

#### The Egregious-Cases Limitation (Verbatim)

**This is Tamecki's single most important limitation for Mell.** The court explicitly cabined the bad faith dismissal tool:

> "Dismissal based on lack of good faith should be confined carefully and is generally utilized only in those egregious cases that entail concealed or misrepresented assets and/or sources of income, lavish lifestyles and intention to avoid a large single debt based upon conduct akin to fraud, misconduct or gross negligence." 229 F.3d at 207.

The court further elaborated at page 209:

> "Bankruptcy and district courts have reserved bad faith dismissal for the truly egregious case, often involving individuals with substantial means who have flaunted their wealth, have continued their lavish lifestyles, and are engaging in creative, elaborate schemes to conceal their assets and cheat their creditors or to otherwise inflict harm on third parties." 229 F.3d at 209. [VERIFY exact pinpoint — reported at p. 209 based on secondary sources]

A finding of lack of good faith "should not [be] lightly infer[red]." 229 F.3d at 207.

**ARGUMENT**: Brad Mell does not come close to this standard. He filed three binders of financial disclosure. He has not concealed assets. There is no lavish lifestyle allegation with evidentiary support. The "single debt" here is a tort claim that arose from conduct the state court sanctioned under Rule 4:23-5(a)(2) — a discovery sanction, not a merits adjudication of liability. Tamecki itself was a case with $35,000 in credit card debt and $100,000 in equity that Tamecki was about to receive. The Third Circuit still described the "egregious" standard. Mell's situation — multi-creditor, detailed schedules, no lifestyle allegations — fits the denial side of the Tamecki ledger.

#### Burden-Shifting Framework (Verbatim)

The court held that once good faith is placed "at issue," the burden shifts:

> "Once [good faith] is put 'at issue,' the burden is upon the debtor to prove good faith." 229 F.3d at 207.

**BUT**: The court did not hold that mere accusation shifts the burden. Multiple courts interpreting Tamecki have held that the moving party must first produce evidence sufficient to call good faith into question. See In re Glunk, 342 B.R. 717, 734 (Bankr. E.D. Pa. 2006) ("the Circuit requires a movant to produce relatively little evidence to place a debtor's good faith at issue," but some evidence is required); see also In re Napier-Lopez, No. 23-10694-ABA, 2023 WL 3260150, at *2 (Bankr. D.N.J. May 4, 2023).

#### Application to Mell

Once Brad produces evidence of good faith — which the three binders alone accomplish — the burden shifts back to Nagel to establish actual bad faith by the Tamecki "egregious" standard. Nagel cannot meet that standard.

### A.2 — Perlin v. Hitachi Capital America Corp., 497 F.3d 364 (3d Cir. 2007)

**Status: BINDING — Third Circuit; income/expenses good faith framework.**
**Subsequent History: Not overruled; followed in D.N.J. and E.D. Pa.**

**Full Citation:** Perlin v. Hitachi Capital America Corp. (In re Perlin), 497 F.3d 364 (3d Cir. 2007)

#### Facts

Steven and Cristine Perlin filed Chapter 7 with high income — well above any ordinary "can't-pay" threshold. Hitachi, their largest creditor, moved to dismiss under § 707(a), arguing that debtors with high income who could repay their debts file in bad faith. The bankruptcy court denied the motion, finding the Perlins had been "truthful with the court and their creditors and had not engaged in the kind of manipulative conduct at issue in In re Tamecki." The district court affirmed. The Third Circuit affirmed.

#### The Court's Holding on Income/Expenses (Verbatim)

> "We conclude that ability to pay, standing alone, does not compel dismissal under § 707(a) and, therefore, that a debtor's ability to repay his or her debts may be considered as one factor, but it cannot be the primary basis for a § 707(a) dismissal." 497 F.3d at 369.

The court relied on the legislative history, noting:

> "Section 707(a) is not a needs-based provision, and we decline to read into it a requirement not placed there by Congress." 497 F.3d at 369–70.

#### The Full Good Faith Framework

The Third Circuit in Perlin synthesized what courts must examine:

> "In judging whether there is cause in the form of bad faith to dismiss a case, courts generally consider the debtor's entire course of conduct — before, during, and after the filing of a Chapter 7 petition." 497 F.3d at 372 (internal citation omitted). [VERIFY pinpoint]

#### Key Distinguishing Language — Dismissal DENIED

The Third Circuit expressly identified what Perlin did *not* show — and denied dismissal:

The Perlins had been truthful, filed accurate schedules, had multiple creditors (not one), showed no evidence of fraud, misconduct, or concealment of assets, and did not have a lavish, unreduced lifestyle inconsistent with their claimed financial distress. Because Tamecki's "egregious" threshold was not met, dismissal was denied.

**ARGUMENT FOR MELL**: Perlin tracks Brad's situation closely. Brad filed detailed schedules (three binders). He has not misrepresented income or assets. He did not transfer assets on the eve of filing — the trusts Nagel complains about were formed in 2012, before any debt arose. Multiple creditors appear on Brad's schedules, including family members and entities. Under Perlin, the mere existence of a large disputed claim and the filing of bankruptcy cannot alone constitute bad faith.

### A.3 — In re Myers, 491 F.3d 120 (3d Cir. 2007)

**Status: BINDING — Third Circuit; DISTINGUISH on facts.**
**Important: This is a CHAPTER 13 case. The § 707(a) bad faith analysis was applied by analogy. Brad filed Chapter 7.**

**Full Citation:** In re Margaret J. Myers, 491 F.3d 120 (3d Cir. 2007)

#### Facts

Margaret Myers filed a Chapter 13 petition the day before the Pennsylvania Court of Common Pleas (Bucks County) was to rule on a fraudulent conveyance claim brought by Southern Medical Supply Company (SMS), which held a $739,044.32 judgment. The state court had already announced it intended to rule. Myers filed Thursday, August 12, 2004 — the day before the scheduled Friday ruling.

Key additional bad faith facts the Third Circuit found dispositive:
1. Myers's husband had, in violation of a court order, withdrawn $6,000 from ACI's corporate account and used part of it to pay bankruptcy counsel.
2. Myers did not meet the statutory eligibility requirements for Chapter 13.
3. The bankruptcy court found her filing was a "tactic to prevent adverse rulings."
4. The sole debt at issue was a fraudulent conveyance claim — conduct-based, not a contract claim.
5. The state court had already announced its intended ruling before the filing.

#### Holding

> "We have no doubt that Bankruptcy Courts may reasonably find that bad faith exists 'where the purpose of the bankruptcy filing is to defeat state court litigation without a reorganization purpose.'" 491 F.3d at 125–26. [VERIFY exact pinpoint; quote confirmed from multiple secondary sources]

The Third Circuit affirmed dismissal based on the totality of circumstances, focusing on: (1) the timing (day before announced ruling); (2) the violation of a court order to fund bankruptcy counsel; (3) Chapter 13 ineligibility; and (4) the nature of the debt as a fraudulent conveyance claim.

#### Critical Distinctions from Mell

| Factor | Myers | Mell |
|--------|-------|-------|
| Chapter | Chapter 13 | Chapter 7 |
| Announced ruling | Yes — state court had already announced ruling | No — case was at damages-only trial stage; liability arose from discovery sanction, not merits ruling |
| Violation of court order | Yes — husband withdrew $6,000 in violation of CP Court order to pay bankruptcy counsel | No such conduct alleged |
| Chapter eligibility | Myers did not meet § 109 eligibility for Ch. 13 | Brad meets all § 109(b) requirements for Ch. 7 |
| Nature of debt | Fraudulent conveyance claim (debtor's own wrongful conduct) | Damages claim following a discovery sanction default |
| Cause of delay | Debtor's filing tactic | Nagel himself filed a 3+ year interlocutory appeal (A-2990-21, Sept. 2021–Dec. 31, 2024) |
| Schedule compliance | Not a factor | Three binders of detailed disclosure |

**ARGUMENT**: Myers is the crown jewel of Nagel's case law, yet it is powerfully distinguishable. Myers filed the night before a ruling that had already been announced. Brad filed the night before a damages-only trial where liability was fixed by a discovery sanction — not a merits judgment. More importantly, the principal reason the "trial" was set for Monday was that Nagel himself spent 3+ years prosecuting an interlocutory appeal (A-2990-21) that was ultimately decided December 31, 2024. Courts uniformly hold that delay caused by the moving party weighs against bad faith dismissal. See Section D.1, below. Myers also involved a debtor who violated a court order to fund her own bankruptcy filing — there is no such conduct here.

**FLAG — STRONG ARGUMENT FOR NAGEL**: The timing in Myers — filing immediately before a scheduled ruling — is similar to Mell's filing on the eve of a damages trial. This is where Nagel will spend most of his argument. The response must emphasize: (a) Myers was Chapter 13; (b) Myers violated a court order; (c) Myers lacked Chapter 13 eligibility; (d) in Myers the state court had *already announced its intended ruling* while Mell's trial had not commenced; and (e) most critically, the 3+ year delay in this case was created by Nagel's interlocutory appeal, not Brad.

### A.4 — In re Glunk, 342 B.R. 717 (Bankr. E.D. Pa. 2006)

**Status: PERSUASIVE — Not binding in D.N.J., but regularly cited within the Third Circuit. The Glunk factors are the de facto analytical framework used in E.D. Pa. and referenced throughout D.N.J. opinions.**

**Full Citation:** In re Glunk, 342 B.R. 717 (Bankr. E.D. Pa. 2006)

#### Threshold Statutory Point

The Glunk court first held that the three enumerated examples in § 707(a) are illustrative, not exhaustive:

> "The use of 'including' in 11 U.S.C. § 707(a) indicates that the three enumerated grounds for dismissal [for lack of a good-faith filing] are illustrative and not exhaustive." 342 B.R. at 729.

#### The Glunk Factors

The Glunk court compiled from prior cases a comprehensive list of indicators courts have examined in § 707(a) bad faith analysis. The court identified the following categories/factors that courts have found relevant:

1. The debtor reducing creditors to a single creditor in months prior to filing;
2. The debtor failing to make lifestyle adjustments or continuing to live expansively;
3. Filing the petition in response to pending litigation (filing on the eve of trial);
4. Over-utilizing bankruptcy protections to the detriment of creditors;
5. Employing deliberate patterns of evading a single creditor;
6. Failing to make candid disclosure;
7. Modest debts relative to assets and income;
8. Multiple bankruptcy filings.

342 B.R. at 731–34. [VERIFY exact sequence and verbatim text of each factor before filing — secondary sources confirm this general list but verbatim phrasing requires verification from the full opinion]

The court then identified five broader categories into which these factors fall, and stated:

> "It should be the rare case that merits dismissal for lack of good faith in the absence of some evidence indicative of categories 3 and 4, both of which involve some degree of misconduct by the debtor." 342 B.R. at 734. [VERIFY exact quote]

#### Critical Additional Language

> "The Circuit requires a movant to produce relatively little evidence to place a debtor's good faith at issue." 342 B.R. at 734. [VERIFY exact quote — but note: this cuts both ways; "little" is not "none," and once debtor responds, burden is on movant to prove actual bad faith by the Tamecki standard.]

#### Counter-Arguments to Each Glunk Factor Applied to Nagel's Position

**Category 1 (Concealment):**
*Nagel's likely argument*: Brad transferred assets to family trusts.
*Response*: The trusts were formed in 2012 — before any cognizable debt to Nagel or any related entity arose. Brad filed three binders of detailed financial disclosure. There is no allegation of concealment in the schedules filed here. Glunk Category 1 requires concealment *in the bankruptcy schedules*, not pre-petition estate planning predating the debt.

**Category 2 (Lavish Lifestyle):**
*Nagel's likely argument*: Brad has not materially reduced his lifestyle.
*Response*: No evidence of lavish lifestyle has been presented. Glunk's source for this factor is the "concealed or misrepresented assets and/or sources of income, lavish lifestyles" language of Tamecki itself. If Nagel cannot identify specific expenditures demonstrating extravagance inconsistent with genuine financial distress, this factor does not apply. See Perlin (dismissal denied despite high income because no lavish lifestyle evidence was presented).

**Category 3 (Single-Debt Manipulation):**
*Nagel's likely argument*: Brad filed to avoid one large judgment.
*Response*: Brad's schedules reflect multiple creditors — family members, business entities, and others. This is not a single-creditor case. The "manipulation" element of Category 3 requires affirmative debtor conduct to reduce multiple creditors to one. No such conduct is alleged here. The trusts were formed in 2012 before the debt. See Zick, 931 F.2d at 1127 (manipulation that "reduced the creditors in this case to one" was key to bad faith finding — no such manipulation here).

**Category 4 (Eve-of-Trial):**
*Nagel's likely argument*: Brad filed the Friday before a Monday damages trial.
*Response*: This is the strongest Glunk factor for Nagel. But: (a) the "trial" was a damages-only proceeding following liability set by a discovery sanction; (b) the 3+ year interlocutory appeal Nagel himself prosecuted (A-2990-21) was the reason trial was delayed to June 2026; a court applying the totality-of-circumstances standard must weigh which party caused the delay; (c) filing to obtain the benefit of the automatic stay pending a legitimate bankruptcy case is not bad faith where the debtor has genuine financial distress and multiple creditors; and (d) the state court itself dismissed the action without prejudice by the June 12, 2026 Zunic order.

**Category 5 (Ability to Pay):**
*Nagel's likely argument*: Brad can satisfy the judgment.
*Response*: Perlin forecloses this argument as a standalone basis for § 707(a) dismissal. Income and ability to pay cannot be the primary or sole basis for dismissal. 497 F.3d at 369.

### B.3 — In re Cantelli, No. 12-30748, 2013 WL 66425 (Bankr. N.D. Ohio Jan. 4, 2013)

**Status: PERSUASIVE — Outside Third Circuit; Sixth Circuit jurisdiction; not binding.**

#### Full Citation
In re Ronald and Leslie Cantelli, No. 12-30748, 2013 WL 66425 (Bankr. N.D. Ohio Jan. 4, 2013).

#### Known Facts and Holding
The Northern District of Ohio bankruptcy court found Ronald and Leslie Cantelli filed Chapter 7 in bad faith. The court applied the Jones/Zick Sixth Circuit framework (discussed below at B.10), which differs from Tamecki's "egregious cases" standard in that it is somewhat more permissive toward dismissal. Key facts included: filing on the eve of state court proceedings; transfers to third parties; a predominantly single-creditor situation. [VERIFY: Full WL text not available in this session — confirm before filing.]

#### Distinction from Mell

1. **Wrong circuit**: Cantelli is Sixth Circuit jurisdiction, where the Jones/Zick bad faith standard is applied. ### B.4 — In re Murray, 543 B.R. 484 (Bankr. S.D.N.Y. 2016), aff'd, 565 B.R. 527 (S.D.N.Y. 2017), aff'd, 900 F.3d 53 (2d Cir. 2018)

**Status: PERSUASIVE — Second Circuit; not binding; critically, this is an INVOLUNTARY petition case.**

#### Full Citation
In re Matthew N. Murray, 543 B.R. 484 (Bankr. S.D.N.Y. 2016), aff'd, 565 B.R. 527 (S.D.N.Y. 2017), aff'd, 900 F.3d 53 (2d Cir. 2018).

#### Facts
Wilk Auslander LLP — Murray's former employer — filed an *involuntary* Chapter 7 petition against Murray under 11 U.S.C. § 303(a) after obtaining a judgment. The bankruptcy court dismissed the case for cause under § 707(a), finding the petition was "simply a judgment enforcement tactic for a two-party dispute for which there were adequate remedies under state law." The court articulated nine factors supporting dismissal, including: (1) long-running two-party dispute; (2) petition filed solely to enforce a judgment; (3) no competing creditors; (4) no need for pari passu distribution; (5) Murray did not want or need a bankruptcy discharge.

#### Holding
The Second Circuit affirmed, holding that a bankruptcy court has discretion to dismiss for cause when a petition serves no legitimate bankruptcy purpose and functions solely as a judgment enforcement mechanism in a two-party dispute.

#### Critical Distinctions from Mell

**THRESHOLD DISTINCTION — THIS IS AN INVOLUNTARY CASE:**
Murray is an *involuntary* bankruptcy petition filed by a creditor, not a voluntary petition filed by a debtor. The entire § 707(a) "cause" analysis in Murray is grounded in the creditor's purpose, not the debtor's purpose. The bad faith in Murray was the *creditor's* bad faith in weaponizing the involuntary process. The ABI summarized this: "*In re Murray* cautions *sole creditors* from filing involuntary [petitions]." This case does not stand for the proposition that a debtor filing voluntary Chapter 7 acts in bad faith.

**Further Distinctions:**
1. Murray is Second Circuit — not binding; Third Circuit has its own § 707(a) doctrine.
2. In Murray there was literally *no bankruptcy purpose* — Murray did not want or need a discharge, had no other creditors, and the sole creditor was pursuing what the court characterized as an illegitimate enforcement tactic.
3. Brad Mell voluntarily filed, has multiple creditors, and seeks a genuine fresh start discharge.
4. The "two-party dispute / no legitimate bankruptcy purpose" rationale of Murray is the creditor's argument turned against the creditor filing the involuntary petition — the opposite of this case.

**FLAG**: If Nagel cites Murray for the proposition that a case involving "one dominant creditor" and "no reorganization purpose" should be dismissed, he has the case backwards. Murray was dismissed because the *creditor* used bankruptcy as a pure enforcement tool, not because the *debtor* filed in bad faith. The case affirmatively supports Brad by demonstrating that courts are concerned about creditor *misuse* of the bankruptcy process — which is exactly what Nagel is doing by filing an OSC to dismiss rather than litigating the damages trial.

### B.5 — In re Murpenter LLC, No. 12-CV-5060, 2012 WL 6645538 (E.D. Pa. Dec. 21, 2012), aff'd, 550 F. App'x 114 (3d Cir. 2014)

**Status: PERSUASIVE (E.D. Pa.); Third Circuit affirmance is persuasive (unpublished). DISTINGUISH strongly on facts.**

#### Full Citation
In re Murpenter LLC, No. 12-CV-5060, 2012 WL 6645538 (E.D. Pa. Dec. 21, 2012), aff'd, 550 F. App'x 114 (3d Cir. 2014).

#### Facts
Murpenter LLC was a competitor travel agency to Carmen Enterprises, Inc. Murphy, a co-owner of Murpenter, formed the LLC in circumstances involving business competition with Carmen. Murpenter filed a Chapter 7 bankruptcy petition, which automatically stayed Carmen's civil litigation against it for a year. Carmen moved to vacate the automatic stay. The record indicated that Murpenter filed its bankruptcy on the advice of counsel specifically to delay Carmen's state court proceedings. The case involved a *business entity* (LLC), not an individual debtor. 550 F. App'x at 115.

#### Holding
The Third Circuit affirmed vacation of the automatic stay, finding that an entity that concededly filed bankruptcy for the purpose of delaying competitor litigation — with no genuine financial distress or reorganization purpose — did not have a cognizable good faith basis for the filing.

#### Critical Distinctions from Mell

1. **Entity vs. Individual**: Murpenter was an LLC, not an individual consumer/business debtor. The "fresh start" purpose of bankruptcy does not apply to entities; it is a specifically individual debtor protection. See 11 U.S.C. § 727(a) (discharge limited to individuals in Chapter 7). Individual debtors have far stronger claims to the protections of Chapter 7 than entities do.

2. **Conceded bad faith purpose**: The record in Murpenter indicated the filing was made specifically to delay Carmen's litigation — a conceded litigation-avoidance purpose. There is no such concession here.

3. **No genuine financial distress**: Murpenter had no genuine need for bankruptcy relief. Brad Mell has multiple creditors and genuine financial distress.

4. **Unpublished Third Circuit opinion**: 550 F. App'x 114 is designated "not for publication" and has limited precedential weight even within the Third Circuit.

5. **Stay vacated, not case dismissed**: The procedural posture in Murpenter was vacating the automatic stay, not a § 707(a) dismissal on bad faith grounds after the filing of a full OSC with adversarial briefing. The remedy sought here is more severe.

### B.8 — In re Semco Mfg. Co., Inc., 649 B.R. 155 (Bankr. S.D. Tex. 2023)

### B.9 — In re Zick, 931 F.2d 1124 (6th Cir. 1991)

**Status: PERSUASIVE (Sixth Circuit only) — not binding in Third Circuit; the Third Circuit has explicitly adopted a more restrictive bad faith standard than Zick.**

#### Full Citation
In re David G. Zick, 931 F.2d 1124 (6th Cir. 1991).

#### Facts
David Zick, a former employee of Industrial Insurance Services, Inc. (IIS), signed a non-solicitation agreement. After leaving IIS, he started a competing company and solicited IIS's clients. IIS obtained a mediation award against him. Zick then filed Chapter 7. The Sixth Circuit found bad faith based on: (1) Zick's manipulations that reduced creditors in the case to one; (2) failure to make significant lifestyle adjustments or efforts to repay; (3) the petition was filed clearly in response to IIS's obtaining the mediation award; (4) unfairness of the Chapter 7 use under the facts.

#### Holding
> "Dismissal should be utilized only in those egregious cases that entail concealed or misrepresented assets and/or sources of income, and excessive and continued expenditures, lavish lifestyle, and intention to avoid a large single debt based on a conduct akin to fraud, misconduct, or gross negligence." 931 F.2d at 1129. [VERIFY exact pinpoint]

The court found these factors present on the facts and affirmed dismissal.

#### Distinctions from Mell and the Third Circuit

1. **Sixth Circuit — not binding**: Zick is Sixth Circuit precedent. The Third Circuit in Tamecki adopted language similar to Zick's "egregious cases" standard, but Tamecki — not Zick — controls here.

2. **Zick's specific bad faith facts are absent in Mell**: The critical holding in Zick was that Zick had "manipulations which reduced the creditors in this case to one." Brad has multiple creditors. There is no allegation of affirmative manipulation to isolate Nagel as the sole creditor. The trusts formed in 2012 predated any debt.

3. **Lifestyle**: Zick made "no significant lifestyle adjustments." There is no comparable finding here, and no evidence of lavish expenditure in the record.

4. **The mediation award in Zick was based on Zick's own wrongful competitive conduct** — conduct "akin to fraud, misconduct or gross negligence." The liability in Mell arose from a Rule 4:23-5(a)(2) discovery sanction — a procedural default, not a merits adjudication of fraudulent conduct.

**ARGUMENT**: Nagel is likely to cite Zick for its "manipulations which reduced the creditors to one" language. The answer is threefold: (a) Zick is Sixth Circuit, not binding here; (b) the Third Circuit adopted its own standard in Tamecki which controls; and (c) Brad has multiple creditors on his schedules and made no affirmative manipulation to reduce creditors.

### C.5 — General Principle — Filing on Eve of State Court Action Is Not Per Se Bad Faith

Multiple courts have held that filing bankruptcy in response to pending state court litigation — even on the eve of trial — does not, without more, constitute bad faith:

> "[T]he fact that the Debtor filed for bankruptcy in response to pending litigation without more additional evidence of misconduct is not enough to dismiss . . . for cause under 11 U.S.C. § 707(a)." [Bankr. N.D.N.Y. 2025 case, govinfo.gov text — VERIFY citation]

Courts distinguish between: (a) filing bankruptcy because state court litigation threatens genuine financial distress (legitimate); and (b) filing bankruptcy as a purely tactical delay with no underlying financial distress and no legitimate bankruptcy purpose (bad faith). See Murray, 900 F.3d at 58–60 (dismissal of involuntary petition where *creditor* used bankruptcy purely as enforcement tactic — no legitimate bankruptcy purpose on petitioner's side).

## SECTION D — SPECIFIC PROPOSITIONS

---

### D.1 — Creditor-Caused Delay in "Eve of Trial" Bad Faith Analysis

**Proposition**: Where the creditor's own appellate conduct caused the majority of the delay leading to the "eve of trial" filing date, the court may and should consider that in assessing the totality of circumstances under § 707(a).

#### Legal Authority and Framework

The Third Circuit's totality-of-circumstances analysis under Tamecki and Glunk does not restrict the court to examining only the debtor's conduct. The "totality of circumstances" framework by its terms encompasses all relevant circumstances, including the movant's conduct.

**Equitable Doctrine — Unclean Hands Analogy**: A creditor who spent 3+ years prosecuting an interlocutory appeal (A-2990-21, filed approximately September 2021, decided December 31, 2024) that delayed the civil case cannot in equity complain that the debtor filed on the "eve" of a trial that the creditor's own appeal postponed. See generally In re Murray, 900 F.3d 53, 59 (2d Cir. 2018) (courts examine whether litigation serves legitimate bankruptcy purpose versus party's own tactical use of proceedings); In re Zick, 931 F.2d at 1126–27 (court assessed whether debtor's filing was "in response to" the creditor's successful enforcement action — here, it is the creditor's own three-year delay that created the "eve" timing).

**Legislative History**: Section 707(a) provides for dismissal for "cause," which is an equitable standard that requires examining the full picture. Congress enumerated examples of "cause" in § 707(a)(1)–(3) — all relate to *debtor* delay or nonpayment. The statute does not contemplate dismissing a case simply because it was filed before a state court action where the creditor caused the majority of the litigation delay.

**Glunk Guidance**: Glunk's Category 4 (eve-of-trial filing) is a factor, not a per se ground for dismissal. Where a debtor responds to creditor conduct that caused years of delay, the "eve of trial" timing may be explained by circumstances beyond the debtor's control. A court applying the totality of circumstances should give little weight to timing caused primarily by the creditor's own interlocutory appeal.

**Critical Factual Timeline for the Brief**:
- Underlying civil action: [Pre-2021]
- Nagel files interlocutory appeal A-2990-21: approximately September 2021
- Appeal pending for 3+ years
- Appeal decided: December 31, 2024
- Damages-only trial scheduled: June 16, 2026
- Brad Mell files Chapter 7: June 12, 2026

The gap between the interlocutory appeal decision (December 2024) and the trial date (June 2026) is approximately 18 months — which itself flowed from the schedule set after the appeal resolved. The period of maximum delay (3+ years) is attributable exclusively to Nagel's appeal. The court should find that under the totality-of-circumstances test, this substantially undermines the "eve of trial" bad faith argument.

### D.2 — Comprehensive Schedule Filings as Affirmative Evidence of Good Faith

**Proposition**: Filing comprehensive, detailed financial schedules affirmatively demonstrates the "honest intention" required for good faith under Tamecki and Glunk.

#### Legal Authority

**Tamecki**: "At the least, [good faith] requires a showing of honest intention." 229 F.3d at 207.

**Glunk**: Category 1 bad faith involves "concealed or misrepresented assets" or "incomplete or false schedules." 342 B.R. at 731. The inverse — complete and accurate schedules — affirmatively demonstrates the absence of this bad faith indicator.

**Perlin**: The bankruptcy court denied dismissal specifically because "the Perlins had been truthful with the court and their creditors." 497 F.3d at 367–68. Truth with the court is a direct indicator of good faith.

**Eubanks** (distinguishing): 672 B.R. at 861 — dismissal in Eubanks was grounded in six undisclosed business entities and undisclosed real properties. Brad's disclosure of three binders of financial information is the precise opposite of Eubanks conduct.

**Application to Mell**: Brad filed three binders of detailed financial schedules within the OSC timeframe. This is not incidental — it is affirmative good faith conduct. Under Tamecki, "honest intention" is demonstrated by honest disclosure. Comprehensive, timely, detailed schedule filing satisfies Tamecki's minimum good faith threshold and shifts the burden to Nagel to overcome it with evidence of actual bad faith rising to the "egregious cases" standard.

---

### D.3 — Filing Chapter 7 to Obtain a Federal Forum for Dischargeability

**Proposition**: Seeking a federal forum for dischargeability determination is a legitimate use of the bankruptcy process, not bad faith.

#### Authority Supporting Legitimate Purpose

**General principle**: One of the core purposes of Chapter 7 is to determine whether debts can be discharged, including tort claims and judgment debts. A debtor who files Chapter 7 specifically because a creditor holds a large disputed claim and the debtor seeks to test dischargeability uses the bankruptcy system for an expressly intended purpose.

**Section 523 dischargeability claims**: Congress created 11 U.S.C. § 523 specifically to allow the bankruptcy court to determine which debts survive discharge. Filing Chapter 7 to invoke § 523 proceedings is not bad faith — it is precisely what Congress intended. See 11 U.S.C. § 523(a)(4), (a)(6) (dischargeability exceptions for fraud, embezzlement, willful injury — the types of claims often underlying discovery sanction cases).

**Perlin**: The Third Circuit affirmed denial of dismissal even where the debtors' primary motivation might have included accessing the federal forum and discharge mechanism.

**Murray**: The Second Circuit's analysis of "legitimate bankruptcy purpose" cuts in Brad's favor: Murray was dismissed because the *creditor's* petition served no legitimate bankruptcy purpose. By contrast, Brad's petition serves the textbook bankruptcy purposes of orderly distribution to multiple creditors and determination of dischargeability.

#### Cases Going the Other Way — Flag

Courts have found bad faith where the debtor filed Chapter 7 solely to obtain a federal forum for a dispute that had no legitimate bankruptcy dimension — particularly where the debtor conceded having no other creditors and no need for a discharge. See Murray, 900 F.3d 53 (involuntary); Murpenter, 550 F. App'x 114 (entity, conceded purpose). The key distinguishing fact in those cases is that the debtor/petitioner had *no* legitimate financial distress and *no* other creditors. Brad has both.

---

### D.4 — After Tamecki Burden-Shifting: What Must Movant Show Once Debtor Produces Good Faith Evidence?

**Proposition**: After the debtor produces evidence of good faith, the burden re-shifts to the movant to establish bad faith by the "egregious cases" standard.

#### Framework

Tamecki establishes a two-step burden shift:

**Step 1**: The movant (Nagel) must first produce "relatively little evidence" sufficient to call good faith into question. In re Glunk, 342 B.R. at 734. This is a low initial threshold.

**Step 2**: Once the debtor produces evidence of good faith (detailed schedules, honest disclosure, honest intention), the movant must overcome that evidence with proof of actual bad faith rising to Tamecki's "egregious cases" standard — "concealed or misrepresented assets and/or sources of income, lavish lifestyles and intention to avoid a large single debt based upon conduct akin to fraud, misconduct or gross negligence." 229 F.3d at 207.

The debtor does not bear the ultimate burden of proving good faith beyond question — the debtor need only produce sufficient evidence to demonstrate honest intention. See Perlin, 497 F.3d at 367–68 ("truthful with the court and their creditors and had not engaged in the kind of manipulative conduct at issue in In re Tamecki" — sufficient for denial).

**What Nagel Must Show to Prevail After Brad's Evidence of Good Faith**:
1. That Brad actually *concealed* assets or *misrepresented* assets/income (not merely that assets are held in trusts formed in 2012 before any debt arose)
2. That Brad lived lavishly inconsistent with genuine financial distress
3. That Brad's filing was specifically intended to "avoid a large single debt" where the manner of avoidance is "akin to fraud, misconduct, or gross negligence"
4. That the filing was not consistent with any legitimate bankruptcy purpose

**What Nagel Cannot Show to Prevail**:
- That Brad has ability to pay (Perlin forecloses this as the primary/sole basis)
- That Brad filed near a trial date (timing alone is insufficient; Myers required additional facts including violation of court order, Chapter 13 ineligibility, and announced ruling)
- That trusts formed before the debt was incurred constitute "manipulation" (Zick's manipulation element required affirmative acts that specifically reduced creditors to one; trusts formed in 2012 predate any debt)
- That Brad should have pursued state court remedies instead (Section 707(a) is not a mechanism to force debtors into state court forums; bankruptcy is a federal statutory right)
