## SECTION A — THE CORRECT LEGAL FRAMEWORK

### A.1 — 28 U.S.C. § 1334: Verbatim Text and the Five Elements of Mandatory Abstention

#### The Correct Statute: 28 U.S.C. § 1334

**§ 1334(a) — Exclusive jurisdiction over bankruptcy cases:**
"Except as provided in subsection (b) of this section, the district courts shall have original and exclusive jurisdiction of all cases under title 11."

**§ 1334(b) — Original but not exclusive jurisdiction:**
"Except as provided in subsection (e)(2), and notwithstanding any Act of Congress that confers exclusive jurisdiction on a court or courts other than the district courts, the district courts shall have original but not exclusive jurisdiction of all civil proceedings arising under title 11, or arising in or related to cases under title 11."

**§ 1334(c)(1) — Permissive abstention (full verbatim text):**
"Except with respect to a case under chapter 15 of title 11, nothing in this section prevents a district court in the interest of justice, or in the interest of comity with State courts or respect for State law, from abstaining from hearing a particular proceeding arising under title 11 or arising in or related to a case under title 11."

**§ 1334(c)(2) — Mandatory abstention (full verbatim text):**
"Upon timely motion of a party in a proceeding based upon a State law claim or State law cause of action, related to a case under title 11 but not arising under title 11 or arising in a case under title 11, with respect to which an action could not have been commenced in a court of the United States absent jurisdiction under this section, the district court shall abstain from hearing such proceeding if an action is commenced, and can be timely adjudicated, in a State forum of appropriate jurisdiction."

28 U.S.C. § 1334(c)(2) (emphasis added).

**Confirmation: 28 U.S.C. § 1134(c)(2) does not exist.** A search of Title 28 of the United States Code confirms that Chapter 85 of Part IV contains sections numbered 1330 through 1369 (District Courts; Jurisdiction). There is no § 1134 in Title 28 governing bankruptcy jurisdiction. The closest neighboring section is § 1132, which addresses limitations on actions under ERISA, an entirely unrelated statute. Nagel's citation to "§ 1134(c)(2)" is a nullity.

### A.2 — The Five-Element Test for Mandatory Abstention Under 28 U.S.C. § 1334(c)(2)

#### Controlling Authority: Stoe v. Flaherty, 436 F.3d 209 (3d Cir. 2006)

**Citation:** Stoe v. Flaherty, 436 F.3d 209, 213 (3d Cir. 2006). [BINDING on this Court]

Stoe v. Flaherty is the Third Circuit's controlling decision on mandatory abstention under 28 U.S.C. § 1334(c)(2). The Third Circuit held that upon a timely motion under § 1334(c)(2), a district court **must abstain** if — and only if — **all five** of the following requirements are satisfied:

> (1) the proceeding is based on a state law claim or cause of action;
> (2) the claim or cause of action is "related to" a case under title 11, but does not "arise under" title 11 and does not "arise in" a case under title 11;
> (3) federal courts would not have jurisdiction over the claim but for its relation to a bankruptcy case;
> (4) an action "is commenced" in a state forum of appropriate jurisdiction; and
> (5) the action can be "timely adjudicated" in a state forum of appropriate jurisdiction.

Stoe v. Flaherty, 436 F.3d 209, 213 (3d Cir. 2006).

**Subsequent history:** No subsequent history adversely affecting precedential value. Stoe remains the controlling Third Circuit statement on mandatory abstention.

**Critical parsing of element (2):** The proceeding must be only "related to" a bankruptcy case — it cannot "arise under" title 11 or "arise in" a case under title 11. Claims that arise under or arise in a bankruptcy case are CORE proceedings by definition. See 28 U.S.C. § 157(b)(2). Therefore, element (2) operationally requires that the proceeding be NON-CORE. A core proceeding fails this element automatically.

**The Third Circuit in Stoe explained:**
> "Claims that 'arise in' a bankruptcy case are claims that by their nature, rather than their particular factual circumstances, could arise only in the context of a bankruptcy case."

Stoe v. Flaherty, 436 F.3d 209 (3d Cir. 2006).

**Application to Brad Mell's § 523(a)(6) claim:** Every element fails as applied to the nondischargeability claim. See Section A.6 below.

### A.3 — 28 U.S.C. § 157(b)(2)(I): Dischargeability as a Core Proceeding

#### The Statute — Verbatim Text

**28 U.S.C. § 157(b)(1):**
Bankruptcy judges may hear and determine all cases under title 11 and all core proceedings arising under title 11, or arising in a case under title 11, referred under subsection (a), and may enter appropriate orders and judgments.

**28 U.S.C. § 157(b)(2) — Core proceedings include but are not limited to:**

Core proceedings include but are not limited to the following:
> (A) matters concerning the administration of the estate;
> (B) allowance or disallowance of claims against the estate or exemptions from property of the estate, and estimation of claims or interests for the purposes of confirming a plan under chapter 11, 12, or 13 of title 11 but not the liquidation or estimation of contingent or unliquidated personal injury tort or wrongful death claims against the estate for purposes of distribution in a case under title 11;
> (C) counterclaims by the estate against persons filing claims against the estate;
> ...
> **(I) determinations as to the dischargeability of particular debts;**
> ...
> (O) other proceedings affecting the liquidation of the assets of the estate or the adjustment of the debtor-creditor or the equity security holder relationship, except personal injury tort or wrongful death claims;
> (P) recognition of foreign proceedings and other matters under chapter 15 of title 11.

28 U.S.C. § 157(b)(2)(I) (emphasis added).

**The text is unambiguous:** A proceeding to determine the dischargeability of a particular debt is a CORE PROCEEDING. A § 523(a)(6) adversary proceeding seeking a determination that Brad Mell's debt is nondischargeable by reason of willful and malicious injury is squarely within 28 U.S.C. § 157(b)(2)(I).

#### Cases Holding § 523(a)(6) Proceedings Are Core Proceedings

**The bankruptcy court has exclusive jurisdiction over § 523(a)(6) dischargeability:**
Courts consistently hold that dischargeability proceedings under § 523(a)(2), (a)(4), and (a)(6) are not merely "core" — the bankruptcy court has **exclusive** jurisdiction over them. *See* Fed. R. Bankr. P. 4007 advisory committee's note ("[t]he bankruptcy court has exclusive jurisdiction to determine dischargeability of debts under § 523(a)(2), (4) or (6) of the Code"). If a complaint is not timely filed, the debt is discharged. This exclusivity underscores that the bankruptcy court cannot be divested of jurisdiction by an abstention motion.

**Actions to except claims from discharge under § 523(a)(6) are triable by the bankruptcy court as core proceedings under 28 U.S.C. § 157(b)(2)(I).** The United States bankruptcy court for the District of Oregon noted: "Actions to except claims from discharge are core proceedings, and triable by the bankruptcy court under 28 U.S.C. § 157(b)(2)(I)." *See also In re* dischargeability proceedings generally — § 157(b)(2)(I) encompasses "determinations as to the dischargeability of particular debts" without qualification.

**Third Circuit statements:** The Third Circuit has consistently recognized that dischargeability proceedings arise under title 11 — specifically under 11 U.S.C. § 523 — and therefore satisfy the definition of core proceedings under § 157(b)(2). Proceedings that "arise under" provisions invoke substantive rights created by Title 11. Dischargeability proceedings are paradigmatic "arising under" proceedings — they exist only because of the Bankruptcy Code and would have no force or meaning outside the bankruptcy context. *See* Stoe v. Flaherty, 436 F.3d 209, 213 (3d Cir. 2006) (contrasting "arising under" and "arising in" claims with "related to" claims for abstention purposes).

### A.4 — The Critical Rule: Mandatory Abstention Is Unavailable as to Core Proceedings

#### The Statutory Text Itself Forecloses Mandatory Abstention for Core Proceedings

The text of § 1334(c)(2) is clear on its face. Mandatory abstention is available only for a proceeding that is:

> "based upon a State law claim or State law cause of action, **related to** a case under title 11 **but not arising under title 11 or arising in a case under title 11**"

28 U.S.C. § 1334(c)(2) (emphasis added).

Core proceedings, by definition under 28 U.S.C. § 157(b)(2), are matters that "arise under" title 11 or "arise in" a case under title 11. A § 523(a)(6) nondischargeability claim arises under title 11. Therefore, **mandatory abstention is inapplicable on the face of the statute itself** when the proceeding is a core proceeding.

Multiple authorities confirm this result:

**From case law:**

Courts examining § 1334(c)(2) have consistently held that "when actions fall within a court's core jurisdiction, mandatory abstention 'is inapplicable on the face of the statute itself.'" *In re* Fairfield Sentry Ltd. (S.D.N.Y.). This principle is derived directly from the statutory text — the statute itself excludes proceedings that arise under or arise in a bankruptcy case from its coverage.

**The "non-core" requirement is embedded in element (2) of the Stoe test:**
Stoe's five-element test requires, as element (2), that the claim be "related to" — not "arising under" or "arising in" — a bankruptcy case. Stoe v. Flaherty, 436 F.3d 209, 213 (3d Cir. 2006). Dischargeability under § 523(a)(6) "arises under" title 11 (11 U.S.C. § 523), not merely "relates to" it. This is dispositive. Because the § 523(a)(6) claim fails element (2) of the Stoe test — it arises under title 11 rather than being merely related to it — mandatory abstention cannot apply.

**Additional authority:**
Non-core proceedings under section 157(b)(2)(B) of title 28 shall not be subject to the mandatory abstention provisions of section 1334(c)(2). While this specific carve-out addresses personal injury tort claims, it confirms the inverse principle: only non-core proceedings are eligible for mandatory abstention, never core proceedings.

**In re Mid-Atlantic Handling Systems, LLC, 304 B.R. 111 (Bankr. D.N.J. 2003) [PERSUASIVE — same district]:**
The New Jersey Bankruptcy Court examined mandatory abstention factors, holding that the key threshold question is whether the proceeding is core or non-core. The court noted that where a matter qualifies as a core proceeding, mandatory abstention under § 1334(c)(2) cannot apply, because the statute by its terms is limited to proceedings "related to" but not "arising under" or "arising in" a bankruptcy case.

**Summary:** Every Third Circuit and D.N.J. authority that has examined this question confirms the same rule: Mandatory abstention under § 1334(c)(2) is unavailable for core proceedings. Because a § 523(a)(6) nondischargeability action is a core proceeding under 28 U.S.C. § 157(b)(2)(I), Nagel's mandatory abstention argument fails as a matter of law.

### A.5 — Permissive Abstention Under § 1334(c)(1) for Core Proceedings

#### Is Permissive Abstention Technically Available?

Unlike mandatory abstention, permissive abstention under § 1334(c)(1) is technically available for core proceedings. The statute's plain text permits abstention from "any" proceeding "arising under title 11 or arising in or related to a case under title 11." 28 U.S.C. § 1334(c)(1). Courts have acknowledged that permissive abstention is theoretically available even for core proceedings. *See, e.g.*, Freeman Law, "Bankruptcy Court Jurisdiction" (permissive abstention "is allowed even in disputes involving the bankruptcy court's 'core' jurisdiction, such as litigation to avoid preferential or fraudulent transfers, although bankruptcy courts seldom abstain from hearing these cases").

#### Why the Court Should Decline Permissive Abstention Here

However, courts consistently and properly decline to exercise permissive abstention when the core proceeding is the central and primary dispute — which is precisely the situation here. The reasons are compelling:

**1. Dischargeability is the essential dispute.** The entire reason this matter is in bankruptcy court is the § 523(a)(6) nondischargeability claim. Permissive abstention makes no sense when the proceeding requiring the court's resolution is itself the core matter that defines why the parties are in this forum.

**2. Nagel's argument is logically incoherent.** Nagel simultaneously (a) asks the bankruptcy court to send the damages question to state court under abstention and (b) asks the bankruptcy court to declare the debt nondischargeable. But the bankruptcy court cannot declare a debt nondischargeable under § 523(a)(6) without determining the underlying liability. You cannot bifurcate the dischargeability determination from the underlying facts in a § 523(a)(6) case — the "willful and malicious" inquiry requires precisely the factual findings that Nagel claims should be made in state court. A court cannot abstain from the predicate while retaining the conclusion.

**3. The state court has already deferred.** Judge Zunic dismissed B.B. v. Mell without prejudice on June 12, 2026, with any resumption requiring permission from this Court. The state court has formally recognized this Court's primacy. Permissive abstention to send the matter back to a court that already deferred would be incongruous.

**4. The seven-factor permissive abstention analysis (Mid-Atlantic Handling).** Under In re Mid-Atlantic Handling Systems, LLC, 304 B.R. 111 (Bankr. D.N.J. 2003), courts in this district evaluate seven factors for permissive abstention:
(1) the effect on the efficient administration of the bankruptcy estate;
(2) the extent to which issues of state law predominate;
(3) the difficulty or unsettled nature of the applicable state law;
(4) comity;
(5) the degree of relatedness or remoteness of the proceeding to the main bankruptcy case;
(6) the existence of the right to a jury trial; and
(7) prejudice to the involuntarily removed defendants.

*In re Mid-Atlantic Handling Systems, LLC*, 304 B.R. 111 (Bankr. D.N.J. 2003). [PERSUASIVE]

Applying these factors: Factor (1) weighs against abstention (resolving dischargeability here serves efficient estate administration); Factor (2) — although the underlying injury claim sounds in state tort law, the dischargeability question is entirely a federal bankruptcy law question governed by federal standards; Factor (3) — NJ tort law is neither difficult nor unsettled; Factor (4) — comity is minimal where the state court has already dismissed; Factor (5) — the § 523(a)(6) proceeding is the core proceeding defining the bankruptcy estate; Factor (6) — Brad Mell retains any jury trial rights regardless of forum; Factor (7) — no prejudice to Brad Mell from having the case heard in this Court where it is already pending.

The balance of factors strongly weighs against permissive abstention.

## SECTION B — DEFEATING NAGEL'S CASES

### B.1 — Stoe v. Flaherty, 436 F.3d 209 (3d Cir. 2006)

#### Nagel Cites Stoe — But Stoe Defeats Nagel

Nagel apparently cites Stoe v. Flaherty in support of mandatory abstention. This is a fundamental misreading of the case. Stoe does not support Nagel's position — it forecloses it.

**What Stoe actually holds:**

The Third Circuit in Stoe established the controlling five-element test for mandatory abstention. As element (2) of that test, the Court required that the proceeding be only "related to" a bankruptcy case — not "arising under" or "arising in." Stoe v. Flaherty, 436 F.3d 209, 213 (3d Cir. 2006). The Court explained:

> "Claims that 'arise in' a bankruptcy case are claims that by their nature, rather than their particular factual circumstances, could arise only in the context of a bankruptcy case."

Stoe v. Flaherty, 436 F.3d 209 (3d Cir. 2006).

**The § 523(a)(6) claim is categorically different from Stoe:**

In Stoe, the plaintiff brought a state-law claim for unpaid severance benefits against former officers of a now-bankrupt employer. The claim did not "arise under" the Bankruptcy Code — it arose under state employment law. The only connection to bankruptcy was that the employer was in bankruptcy. That is the classic "related to" fact pattern for which mandatory abstention exists.

The § 523(a)(6) nondischargeability claim in *In re Mell* is categorically different. It arises directly under 11 U.S.C. § 523 — a provision of the Bankruptcy Code. It could not exist but for the bankruptcy case. It is governed by federal law standards (willful and malicious injury under the Geiger standard). And it falls within the express enumeration of core proceedings in 28 U.S.C. § 157(b)(2)(I) — "determinations as to the dischargeability of particular debts."

**The language from Stoe that ends Nagel's argument:**

The mandatory abstention test requires that the claim "does not 'arise under' title 11 and does not 'arise in' a case under title 11." Stoe v. Flaherty, 436 F.3d 209, 213 (3d Cir. 2006). A § 523(a)(6) dischargeability determination arises directly under title 11. Nagel cannot satisfy element (2) of the very case he cites. Stoe is not his authority — it is the authority that defeats him.

### B.2 — In re Mid-Atlantic Handling Systems, LLC, 304 B.R. 111 (Bankr. D.N.J. 2003)

**Citation:** *In re Mid-Atlantic Handling Systems, LLC*, 304 B.R. 111 (Bankr. D.N.J. 2003). [PERSUASIVE — same district]

#### The Seven-Factor Permissive Abstention Test From Mid-Atlantic Handling

The Bankruptcy Court for the District of New Jersey in *Mid-Atlantic Handling* articulated the following seven factors for permissive abstention:

1. The effect on the efficient administration of the bankruptcy estate;
2. The extent to which issues of state law predominate;
3. The difficulty or unsettled nature of the applicable state law;
4. Comity;
5. The degree of relatedness or remoteness of the proceeding to the main bankruptcy case;
6. The existence of the right to a jury trial; and
7. Prejudice to the involuntarily removed defendants.

*In re Mid-Atlantic Handling Systems, LLC*, 304 B.R. 111 (Bankr. D.N.J. 2003).

#### How Mid-Atlantic Handling Distinguishes the Present Case

In *Mid-Atlantic Handling*, the court found that mandatory abstention could apply because the claims at issue were not core proceedings — the nexus between the litigation and the Bankruptcy Code was insufficient to classify the matter as a core proceeding, and no substantive right under the Bankruptcy Code was at issue. *Id.* The court found that "no matter how critical it is for the defendant to treat the underlying litigation as a core proceeding, the nexus between the litigation and the Bankruptcy Code is insufficient to warrant classifying the matter as a core proceeding."

The present case is the mirror image. Here, the primary and central proceeding is a § 523(a)(6) nondischargeability action — which is textually and expressly enumerated as a core proceeding in 28 U.S.C. § 157(b)(2)(I). Unlike the commercial dispute in *Mid-Atlantic Handling*, the § 523(a)(6) claim in *In re Mell* is not a state-law commercial matter that incidentally relates to a bankruptcy case. It is a federal bankruptcy proceeding that could arise nowhere else and derives its entire existence from the Bankruptcy Code.

#### Application of Mid-Atlantic Handling Seven Factors to Brad's Case

**Factor 1 — Effect on efficient administration of the estate:** This factor weighs AGAINST abstention. The § 523(a)(6) nondischargeability determination is central to the administration of Brad Mell's bankruptcy estate and his ability to receive a discharge. Sending the underlying damages question to state court while the bankruptcy court retains the dischargeability question creates bifurcated, duplicative proceedings and inefficiency.

**Factor 2 — Extent to which state law predominates:** This factor weighs AGAINST abstention. While the underlying tort is governed by NJ law, the nondischargeability question under § 523(a)(6) is entirely a federal law question. The "willful and malicious" standard is a federal standard articulated by the Supreme Court in *Kawaauhau v. Geiger*, 523 U.S. 57 (1998). State law does not predominate; federal bankruptcy law does.

**Factor 3 — Difficulty or unsettled nature of state law:** This factor weighs AGAINST abstention. NJ tort law is neither difficult nor unsettled, and there is no unusual state law question requiring a state court's resolution.

**Factor 4 — Comity:** This factor weighs AGAINST abstention. The state court (Judge Zunic) already dismissed the state action without prejudice on June 12, 2026, deferring to this Court's jurisdiction. Comity runs in favor of this Court retaining the matter — it is the state court that deferred, not this Court.

**Factor 5 — Degree of relatedness to the main bankruptcy case:** This factor weighs HEAVILY AGAINST abstention. The § 523(a)(6) claim is not merely "related to" the bankruptcy case — it is a proceeding arising directly within it, under 28 U.S.C. § 157(b)(2)(I). It cannot be more closely related.

**Factor 6 — Right to a jury trial:** This factor is neutral. Brad Mell retains any jury trial rights regardless of which federal court resolves the matter.

**Factor 7 — Prejudice to involuntarily removed defendants:** This factor weighs AGAINST abstention. Brad Mell would be prejudiced by having to litigate in a state court that has already dismissed the case, requiring him to first seek permission from this Court to resume proceedings there, only to then litigate the same factual questions in a separate forum whose findings would then need to be imported back into the dischargeability proceeding here.

**Conclusion on Mid-Atlantic Handling:** Every factor that the court in *Mid-Atlantic Handling* found important weighs against abstention here. Nagel's citation to this case provides him no support.

## SECTION C — THE JURISDICTIONAL ARGUMENTS

### C.1 — Effect of the Zunic Dismissal Order on the Mandatory Abstention Analysis

**The June 12, 2026 dismissal order by Judge Zunic is independently dispositive of Nagel's mandatory abstention argument.**

28 U.S.C. § 1334(c)(2) requires, as a statutory element, that "an action is commenced" in a state forum of appropriate jurisdiction. This requires a pending state court action. Courts have consistently held that the mandatory abstention element requires that a state court action actually be "pending" or currently "commenced" — not merely that one was previously filed.

**Where a state court has dismissed the action, mandatory abstention fails:**

The requirement that a state court action be pending and capable of "timely adjudication" in the state forum is a recognized element of mandatory abstention. Courts have held that "the existence of such an action is usually determinative in a bankruptcy court's decision on mandatory abstention." Where "there no longer exists any proceeding pending in state court," mandatory abstention does not apply because the required element — a commenced and timely adjudicable state court action — cannot be satisfied. *See* cases citing the requirement that an action be "commenced" and "pending" in state court.

**The facts in In re Mell:**

B.B. v. Mell, Superior Court of New Jersey, was dismissed without prejudice by Judge Zunic on June 12, 2026. The dismissal order expressly requires any party wishing to return to state court to first obtain permission from this bankruptcy court. As of the date of Nagel's abstention motion and as of the July 16, 2026 hearing date, there is no action pending in state court. The fourth element of the Stoe test — "an action 'is commenced' in a state forum of appropriate jurisdiction" — is not met.

**A dismissed-without-prejudice action is not "pending" for mandatory abstention purposes:**

A dismissal without prejudice terminates the pending action. The fact that the claims could theoretically be refiled (with leave of this Court) does not make the action currently "pending" or currently "commenced." For the purposes of § 1334(c)(2), the action must exist and be currently proceeding in state court. An action that has been dismissed — even without prejudice — is not pending.

Moreover, the additional requirement that any resumption of state court proceedings requires this bankruptcy court's permission confirms that the state court does not currently have jurisdiction. Mandatory abstention under § 1334(c)(2) directs the federal court to abstain in favor of a state proceeding that is already pending and ready to proceed. No such proceeding exists here. The mandatory abstention predicate is absent.

### C.2 — The Zunic Order as a Factor Weighing Against Permissive Abstention

Even if the Court were to consider permissive abstention under § 1334(c)(1), the Zunic dismissal order is a factor weighing heavily against it. The state court did not merely stay its proceedings — it dismissed them, recognizing that the primary resolution forum is this bankruptcy court.

**Where a state court has formally deferred to the bankruptcy court** by dismissing its own proceedings and making resumption conditional on bankruptcy court approval, that state court's action demonstrates that the state court itself has concluded that the bankruptcy forum is the proper one. Permissive abstention in the face of such a formal deferral would be anomalous — the bankruptcy court would be abstaining in favor of a forum that has already concluded it is not the appropriate one.

The Zunic order is therefore a significant factor weighing against any form of abstention. It reflects not just the facts of this case but a judicial determination by the state court that these issues belong in the bankruptcy forum.

---

### C.3 — Exclusive Jurisdiction and the Bankruptcy Court's Retained Authority

**Bankruptcy courts retain exclusive jurisdiction to determine nondischargeability under § 523(a)(6).**

The bankruptcy court has exclusive jurisdiction to determine dischargeability of debts under § 523(a)(2), (4), and (6). *See* Fed. R. Bankr. P. 4007 advisory committee's note. This exclusivity means that abstention cannot operate to divest the bankruptcy court of jurisdiction over the § 523(a)(6) proceeding — because no other court has jurisdiction to determine it in the first place. The Third Circuit's framework in Stoe is entirely consistent with this exclusive jurisdiction principle: mandatory abstention under § 1334(c)(2) is limited to cases where federal courts would not have jurisdiction but for § 1334, meaning cases that are merely "related to" a bankruptcy case. For § 523(a)(6) proceedings, federal jurisdiction exists for an entirely independent reason: the bankruptcy court's exclusive statutory jurisdiction to determine dischargeability.

**The logical consequence:** If this Court were to abstain from the § 523(a)(6) proceeding, there would be no court with jurisdiction to resolve the nondischargeability question. The state court cannot determine dischargeability — that is an exclusively federal determination. Nagel's abstention argument, taken to its logical conclusion, would leave the nondischargeability question in jurisdictional limbo. This confirms that abstention cannot be available for the § 523(a)(6) claim.

---

### C.4 — Nagel's Abstention Argument Is Logically Incoherent

**The fatal internal contradiction in Nagel's position:**

Nagel simultaneously argues that:
(a) The bankruptcy court should send the case back to state court under abstention for a damages trial; and
(b) The bankruptcy court should determine that the debt is nondischargeable under § 523(a)(6).

These positions are irreconcilable. Section 523(a)(6) requires the bankruptcy court to find that the debtor caused "willful and malicious injury" to another. This requires the bankruptcy court to make factual findings about the nature and circumstances of the injury — precisely the factual questions that Nagel claims should be decided in state court.

A bankruptcy court cannot issue a nondischargeability judgment under § 523(a)(6) based on factual findings made by a state court that are not preclusive under federal bankruptcy standards. The dischargeability determination and the underlying liability determination are not separable — the bankruptcy court that adjudicates the nondischargeability claim must also adjudicate the underlying facts.

Nagel's proposal — send the damages to state court, keep the dischargeability here — describes a procedure that does not exist under bankruptcy law. There is no bifurcation mechanism that would allow a state court to adjudicate willfulness and malice for § 523(a)(6) purposes while the bankruptcy court independently adjudicates dischargeability. This confirms that Nagel's abstention motion is substantively meritless, not merely procedurally defective.

## SECTION D — SUMMARY OF ALL DISPOSITIVE ARGUMENTS

Core proceedings arise under or arise in title 11. A § 523(a)(6) nondischargeability action is a core proceeding under 28 U.S.C. § 157(b)(2)(I). Nagel cannot satisfy the second element of the case he cites in his own support.

**Argument 4 [Stoe Element 4 Fails]: No pending state court action.** Stoe requires that "an action 'is commenced' in a state forum of appropriate jurisdiction." The Zunic order dismissed B.B. v. Mell without prejudice on June 12, 2026. There is no action currently pending in state court, and any resumption requires this Court's permission. The fourth element of mandatory abstention fails.

**Argument 5 [Exclusive Jurisdiction]: The bankruptcy court has exclusive jurisdiction over § 523(a)(6) dischargeability.** Abstention cannot divest the court of jurisdiction it exclusively holds and that no other court possesses.

**Argument 6 [Logical Incoherence]: Nagel's motion is internally contradictory.** He cannot simultaneously demand abstention from the damages question and ask this Court for a § 523(a)(6) determination — the two proceedings are inseparable.

**Argument 7 [Permissive Abstention Factors]: The Mid-Atlantic Handling factors all weigh against any abstention.** Efficient estate administration, federal law predominance, state court deference (the Zunic order), and the centrality of the core proceeding all favor this Court retaining jurisdiction.
