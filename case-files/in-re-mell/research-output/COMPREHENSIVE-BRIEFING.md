## SECTION 2: STATEMENT OF FACTS

### A. Background: Steven Bradley Mell and the 2012 Family Trusts

Steven Bradley Mell is a fifty-two-year-old New Jersey resident and former principal of Gulfstream Capital Management LLC, a private investment and asset management firm operating out of Bedminster, New Jersey. Through Gulfstream and its related entities, Brad managed a portfolio of investments and operated a charter aviation business, Aero Care Services, LLC, which owned two aircraft used for business and personal travel. He and his then-wife Kimberly Ruggles Mell maintained a family home at 620 Black River Road, Bedminster, New Jersey, and together they raised three children — Emily, Eliza, and William Mell — who were born and raised in that community.

In December 2012, Brad and Kimberly executed estate planning instruments that would become the principal focus of the trust-fraud allegations in the state court litigation: the Steven Bradley Mell 2012 Family Trust ("SBM Trust") and the Kimberly Ruggles Mell 2012 Family Trust ("KRM Trust"). The timing of these instruments — December 18, 2012, specifically — is the most legally important fact in this case for § 707(a) purposes, and it cannot be overstated. The first sexual encounter between Brad and B.B. did not occur until June 20, 2017. (Appellate-div-opinion-analysis.md § 6; nagel-mtd-analysis.md § 5.) The trusts predate any cognizable debt to B.B. by approximately four years and eight months. At the moment those trust instruments were executed, Brad Mell had never met Lilly Cannon. She was ten years old.

The SBM Trust was established with Brad as the grantor and Kimberly Ruggles Mell as the trustee. The designated beneficiaries were Kimberly and the three children. The trust's express purpose, as set forth in Article 3(A) of the Trust Agreement, was to "provide for the health, education (including, but not limited to, elementary, secondary, undergraduate, graduate and postgraduate education), maintenance and support of KIMBERLY, the Children and their issue, as well as for any expenses incurred by or for them, or any of them, because of any illness, operation, infirmity, or other medical emergency." (SBM Trust SJ Motion Jan 15, 2026.pdf, Statement of Material Facts ¶¶ 10–12, citing Ex. 2 to Whalen Cert.) Brad himself was never a beneficiary of the SBM Trust. (Id. ¶ 46.) The KRM Trust was established with Kimberly Ruggles Mell as the grantor and three independent individuals as trustees — Michael Simon Scott among them — with Brad's three children (Emily, Eliza, and William) as the designated beneficiaries. (KRM SJ Motion Jan 15, 2026-7-45.pdf, Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶¶ 5–8.) The KRM Trust Agreement expressly limited the trustee's distribution authority to providing "for the health, education . . . maintenance and support of the Children and their issue." (Id. ¶ 8.)

Upon formation of the two trusts in December 2012, the Bedminster family home was transferred into them: a recorded deed conveyed a 70% interest in the property to the KRM Trust and a 30% interest to the SBM Trust. (KRM SJ Motion, SUMF ¶¶ 13–14.) Simultaneously, Brad transferred his sole ownership interest in Aero Care Services, LLC — and through Aero Care, the two aircraft — into the SBM Trust. (SBM Trust SJ Motion, SUMF ¶ 31.) These were legitimate transfers of family assets into estate-planning vehicles at a time when Brad owed no obligation to the plaintiff, there was no pending or threatened litigation involving the plaintiff, and — as the plaintiff herself confirmed at her July 3, 2025 deposition — B.B. "had no clue of when, how, or why the Trusts were set up" and had "no knowledge on those trusts" prior to this litigation. (Whalen Response in support SJ Mar 4, 2026.pdf, Response to CSMF ¶ 93, quoting B.B. Dep. at 22:12-16.) Brad testified at his own deposition in September 2021 that the SBM Trust was "formed in 2012 for estate planning purposes and to support his children." (SBM Trust SJ Motion, SUMF ¶ 11.)

Nagel's MTD characterizes these trusts as fraudulent mechanisms. But the legal reality is unassailable: under 11 U.S.C. § 548(a)(1), the two-year lookback window for federal fraudulent transfer avoidance runs from the petition date of June 12, 2026 back to June 12, 2024. The 2012 trust formations are fourteen years outside that window — categorically unreachable as a matter of statutory text. (part6-factual.md § B.1.) Under the New Jersey Uniform Voidable Transactions Act, N.J.S.A. 25:2-27, constructive fraudulent transfer claims reach only creditors "whose claim arose before the transfer was made." Because B.B. is not a creditor whose claim predated December 2012, she cannot invoke the constructive fraud prong. N.J.S.A. 25:2-25, the actual fraud prong covering future creditors, theoretically covers B.B. — but requires proof that Brad formed those trusts in 2012 "with actual intent to hinder, delay, or defraud" a person he would not meet for another three years. That logical impossibility is the airtight trust timing defense. (part6-factual.md §§ B.2–B.3; FINAL-MEMO.md Part VIII.) Moreover, SOFA Line 19, signed by Brad under penalty of perjury, confirms he made no transfers to self-settled trusts within the ten-year lookback window from June 12, 2026 — a window that reaches back only to June 12, 2016, fully four years after the trusts were established. (SCHEDULES-SUMMARY.md § 5, SOFA Line 19.)

### B. The Criminal Matter (2015–2024)

The facts of Brad's criminal conduct are not disputed and this briefing does not minimize them. Brad Mell began grooming B.B. around the summer of 2015, when she was approximately fourteen years old. (Appellate-div-opinion-analysis.md § 6.) According to both Nagel's own MTD and the Appellate Division's December 31, 2024 opinion in this very case, the 34 sexual encounters between Brad and B.B. occurred between June 20, 2017 and November 17, 2017, when B.B. was fifteen and sixteen years old. (Nagel-mtd-analysis.md § 5; appellate-div-opinion-analysis.md § 1.) In December 2017, B.B.'s friend reported the relationship to a teacher, who reported it to the police.

Brad was arrested in May 2018. He subsequently entered a federal plea agreement on May 13, 2019, pleading guilty to: Count 1, receipt of child pornography in violation of 18 U.S.C. § 2252A(a)(2); and Count 2, interstate travel for illicit sexual conduct including production of child pornography with Minor Victim 1, in violation of 18 U.S.C. § 2423(b). (FINAL-MEMO.md Part VIII.) He was sentenced on June 17, 2019 to a term of 84 months imprisonment, with five years of supervised release. He served the bulk of that sentence at FCI Fort Dix, in Burlington County, New Jersey. He was transferred to the Kintock Newark Halfway House, located at 50 Fenwick Street, Newark, New Jersey, on November 13, 2024, and was released on December 13, 2024. (SCHEDULES-SUMMARY.md § 1, SOFA Part 1.)

These facts are directly relevant to the bankruptcy proceeding in several ways that benefit Brad, not just Nagel. They explain three prior addresses disclosed on Brad's SOFA — FCI Fort Dix, Kintock Newark, and the current P.O. Box 835, Far Hills, New Jersey (his brother Courtney's address). There is nothing suspicious about a recently released federal prisoner listing his halfway house and his brother's address as prior residences; that is simply what a recently released federal prisoner's address history looks like. They explain why Brad's SOFA shows no income from employment or business operations for the current year or the prior two years — he was incarcerated. They explain why Brad's SOFA Part 11 shows no active business connections within the four-year lookback period — he was incarcerated for more than seven of the eight years of that window. They explain why the bankruptcy retainer was paid by his brother Courtney rather than Brad directly. And they establish with finality that Brad faced and accepted criminal accountability for his conduct. He did not litigate, did not appeal, did not deny. He pleaded guilty, served more than seven years of incarceration and supervision, and entered bankruptcy as a released federal prisoner with no income, no employment, and no assets other than retirement accounts, a vintage car, and funds held by others in escrow.

### C. The Civil Litigation — *B.B. v. Mell*, ESX-L-7200-19 (2019–2026)

On October 2, 2019, B.B., through Bruce Nagel and Nagel Rice LLP, filed a civil complaint in the Superior Court of New Jersey, Law Division, Essex County, under Docket No. ESX-L-7200-19. The initial complaint named Brad Mell and certain business entities as defendants but did not yet name the KRM Trust, SBM Trust, or Trust Beneficiaries. (KRM & SBM Civil Release Mar 6, 2026.pdf, Recitals.) The complaint alleged causes of action arising from the same course of conduct that formed the basis of the federal criminal proceedings.

On January 27, 2020, the trial court entered a prejudgment writ of attachment. That attachment — and the approximately $750,000 currently held in the Nagel Rice LLP attorney trust account as a result of it — is listed by Brad on his Schedule A/B as a disclosed asset at line 35. (SCHEDULES-SUMMARY.md § 3.) Nagel's MTD describes the attachment as "approximately $1 million dollars held in her counsel's escrow account." (Nagel-mtd-analysis.md § 8, Misrepresentation 6.) The precise figure, as Brad's own schedules reflect, is $750,000 in the Nagel Rice trust account — the other escrowed amounts ($75,000 with the Greifinger firm and $150,000 with McElroy Deutsch as attorneys for Gulfstream) represent funds from other proceedings. What is most notable about this attachment in the bankruptcy context is that Brad disclosed it voluntarily on his schedules — identified by custodian, by firm name, and by dollar amount. A debtor attempting to hide assets does not list the $750,000 sitting in his adversary's counsel's trust account on the very first page of his financial disclosure.

On April 17, 2020, plaintiff filed the First Amended Complaint, adding the KRM Trust, SBM Trust, and Trust Beneficiaries as defendants. (KRM & SBM Civil Release, Recitals; KRM SJ Motion, SUMF ¶ 2.) The new causes of action against the trusts alleged unjust enrichment, constructive trust, and — when the Second Amended Complaint followed on December 13, 2021 — fraudulent transfer under the New Jersey Uniform Voidable Transactions Act in connection with proceeds from the 2021 boat sale. (KRM SJ Motion, SUMF ¶¶ 2–4.)

On March 26, 2021, the trial court entered a partial summary judgment on liability in favor of B.B. on Counts I, II, III, VII, and VIII of the First Amended Complaint — claims arising under the Child Sexual Abuse Act, the child exploitation statute, assault and battery, and two invasion of privacy counts. The court denied summary judgment on the false imprisonment count. This order, which features prominently in Nagel's MTD as a supposedly dispositive adjudication of liability, was characterized by the New Jersey Appellate Division itself in footnote 2 of the December 31, 2024 opinion as "interlocutory and not a final judgment," with the court further noting: "It is not clear from the record whether Mell responded to the summary judgment motion." (Appellate-div-opinion-analysis.md § 2, quoting A-2990-21 at fn. 2.) That is the controlling judicial characterization of that order for preclusion purposes in this bankruptcy proceeding. Moreover, the Appellate Division's opinion makes clear that as of December 31, 2024 — more than three and a half years after the partial summary judgment — "Mell has yet to be deposed." (Id. § 4.) The record from which Nagel now claims a complete liability determination is an interlocutory order entered on an incomplete record against a defendant who, through counsel, invoked his Fifth Amendment rights during civil discovery while facing parallel federal criminal prosecution. Comparative fault, the liability of the non-Mell defendants, and the fraudulent transfer claims against the trusts all remained unresolved.

On July 23, 2021, a default judgment was entered against W.H. Mell Associates, Inc. in the state court proceedings. (Nagel-mtd-analysis.md § 5.)

The multi-year delay between the filing of suit in October 2019 and the trial scheduled for June 2026 is the period that Nagel now cites as evidence of Brad's bad faith in filing for bankruptcy. That characterization inverts the actual history. The delay was caused, in the first instance, by Nagel himself. B.B., through Nagel Rice, filed an interlocutory appeal — designated as Docket No. A-2990-21 in the Superior Court of New Jersey, Appellate Division — challenging the trial court's ruling on the permissible scope of deposition questioning during B.B.'s own deposition. The appeal was argued before a three-judge panel (Judges Accurso, Firko, and Natali) on November 10, 2022. The Appellate Division issued its decision on December 31, 2024 — more than two years after argument, more than five years after the suit was filed. (Appellate-div-opinion-analysis.md, Preliminary Identification, § 6.) The court's entire holding was that the trial court's deposition scope ruling was correct but the age-16 cutoff in the protective order was too restrictive — essentially granting neither side a meaningful victory after consuming three years of what would otherwise have been pre-trial preparation time. That three-year interlocutory appeal is the proximate cause of the "eve of trial" dynamic that Nagel now invokes. Under *In re Tamecki*'s totality-of-circumstances test, a court is entitled to consider which party created the eve. (FINAL-MEMO.md Part II, § C.1.)

Trial was finally scheduled to commence on Monday, June 15, 2026 — a date established only after the Appellate Division's December 2024 decision allowed the case to proceed. (Nagel-mtd-analysis.md § 5, noting the internal inconsistency in Nagel's papers between June 14 and June 15; June 15, 2026 was the Monday, confirmed by the Court's OSC of that date.) Brad Mell filed his voluntary Chapter 7 petition on June 12, 2026, the Friday before trial. He represented himself pro se on the state court notice of filing (Bankruptcy filing.pdf, filed ESX-L-007200-19 at 3:28 PM on June 12, 2026), and simultaneously directed all inquiries to his bankruptcy counsel of record, Anthony Sodono III, Esq., of McManimon, Scotland & Baumann, LLC.

### D. The March 2026 Trust Settlement — The Omitted Bombshell

Before the June 2026 trial date could arrive — and while Nagel was presumably preparing his trial strategy — a significant chapter in the B.B. v. Mell litigation closed quietly, without public announcement. In late February and early March 2026, Nagel Rice's own partner Michael J. Paragano, on behalf of B.B. (Lilly Cannon), negotiated and executed a full and final settlement of all claims against the KRM Trust, the SBM Trust, and the Trust Beneficiaries. The settlement is documented in three primary instruments now in our possession:

The first is a February 27, 2026 transmittal letter signed by Paragano and addressed to counsel for the KRM Trust (A. Ross Pearlson, Esq., Chiesa Shahinian & Giantomasi PC) and counsel for the SBM Trust (Patrick J. Whalen, Esq.). The letter transmits the settlement release and a W-9, and directs: "Kindly issue a settlement check in the amount of $300,000.00 made payable to Lillian Cannon & Nagel Rice, LLP." (Nagel Settlement Letter Feb 27, 2026.pdf.) That letter also contains a separate February 26, 2026 confirmation signed by Bruce H. Nagel himself stating: "This will confirm that our firm will not sue the Kimberly Ruggles Mell 2012 Family Trust or the Steven Bradley Mell 2012 Family Trust in the future." The second instrument is the Full and Final Settlement Agreement and Mutual Release itself, executed in counterparts between March 4 and March 6, 2026. (KRM & SBM Civil Release Mar 6, 2026.pdf.) The parties to the settlement are Lilly Cannon (the plaintiff, represented by Nagel Rice LLP), the KRM Trust (represented by Chiesa Shahinian through A. Ross Pearlson), the SBM Trust (represented by Patrick J. Whalen), Michael Simon Scott as KRM Trust Trustee, Kimberly Ruggles Mell as SBM Trust Trustee and SBM Trust Beneficiary, and Emily, Eliza, and William Mell as Trust Beneficiaries. (Id., Parties recital.) The third instrument is the Mutual Release executed as Exhibit A to the settlement agreement, covering claims between the Cannons and the trust parties. (Id., Exhibit A.)

The settlement payment is unambiguous: "Within ten (10) days of the full execution of this Agreement by all Parties, the KRM Trust and SBM Trust shall pay to Plaintiff the combined and total sum of Three Hundred Thousand and 00/100 ($300,000.00) Dollars." (Id. ¶ 2.) The release language is equally unambiguous and its scope should be carefully noted by this Court:

> "Each of the Parties . . . shall knowingly and voluntarily remise, release, and forever discharge each other Party and each of the Party's respective current, former and future agents, assigns, successors, beneficiaries, representatives, trustees, fiduciaries, executors, administrators, employees, insurers, attorneys, representatives and any person under their control . . . from all actions, causes of action, suits, debts, dues, sums of money, accounts, costs, expenses, liabilities or causes of action whatsoever . . . from the beginning of time and through the date of the full execution of this Agreement, including but not limited to any claims that were or could have been asserted in the Lawsuit against the Releasees, **as well as any current or future claims related to efforts to collect against any assets currently held by or in the possession of the KRM Trust or the SBM Trust on any judgment that any Party may obtain against any other individual or entity in connection with the Lawsuit.**"

(KRM & SBM Civil Release Mar 6, 2026.pdf ¶ 3, emphasis added.) The settlement expressly contemplated the possibility that B.B. might obtain a judgment against Brad Mell personally — and released "any current or future claims" against KRM Trust and SBM Trust assets as a means of collecting on that judgment. This is not boilerplate. It is a specifically negotiated forward-looking release that bars B.B. from doing precisely what Nagel's MTD argues this Court should enable: treating the KRM and SBM Trust assets as available to satisfy a judgment against Brad.

The dismissal mechanics under the settlement confirm that both trust SJ motions — filed January 15, 2026 and returnable March 20, 2026 — were withdrawn as part of the settlement. Paragraph 5 of the agreement states: "The KRM Trust, SBM Trust, and Trust Beneficiaries shall subsequently withdraw any pending summary judgment motions asserted against Plaintiff." (Id. ¶ 5.) The state court docket confirms this: the KRM Trust dismissal was filed March 16, 2026 (KRM Dismissal Mar 16, 2026.pdf) and the SBM Trust dismissal was filed March 18, 2026 (SBM Trust dismissal Mar 18, 2026.pdf).

Three months later, on June 23, 2026, Nagel filed Doc. 8 in this Court. The Motion to Dismiss devotes substantial portions of its Statement of Facts to characterizing the KRM and SBM Trusts as fraudulent mechanisms by which Brad sheltered assets from B.B. The Nagel Certification attached to the motion describes the trusts as having been "utilized . . . as a mechanism to avoid B.B.'s potential claims and to transfer marital assets." (Nagel-mtd-analysis.md § 4.) The motion does not mention the March 2026 settlement. It does not disclose that Nagel's own firm received a portion of a $300,000 payment from those trusts less than ninety days before the motion was filed. It does not disclose the release of "any current or future claims" against trust assets. It does not disclose that the KRM Trust SJ motion and the SBM Trust SJ motion — both of which were litigated on the proposition that the trusts are legitimate estate-planning vehicles with legally cognizable defenses — were withdrawn as part of the settlement, with no admission of liability.

The settlement's confidentiality clause anticipates court disclosure: paragraph 10 permits any party to "disclose a copy of this Agreement to a Court of Law, in a sealed envelope marked 'Confidential,' for the purpose of enforcing the terms of this Agreement or effectuating any of the purposes and obligations stated herein." (Id. ¶ 10.) The settlement is thus expressly contemplated as disclosable to a court. That Nagel elected not to disclose it to this Court, while simultaneously relying on the trust-fraud narrative as the centerpiece of his bad-faith argument, raises serious questions under N.J. RPC 3.3(a)(5) (duty to disclose material facts to the tribunal) and Fed. R. Bankr. P. 9011(b)(3) (factual certifications must have evidentiary support). (FINAL-MEMO.md Part VII; part6-factual.md § C.3.)

There is an additional strategic significance to the trust settlement that goes beyond the candor issue. The two trust SJ motions litigated precisely the propositions that Nagel's MTD now inverts. Pat Whalen, on behalf of the SBM Trust, submitted a nineteen-page statement of material facts establishing, through deposition testimony, recorded deeds, closing documents, and the MSA itself, that: (1) the SBM Trust was formed in December 2012 for legitimate estate planning purposes, (2) Aero Care and the aircraft were transferred into the SBM Trust in 2012, fully five years before any encounter with B.B., (3) the plane sales in September and October 2018 were arm's-length transactions at fair market value to unrelated third parties (Hoover Aviation and Trekker Holdings/Puerto Rico Wire Services), (4) the Bedminster home was a trust asset from December 2012 onwards and the SBM Trust received its contractual 30% of the net sale proceeds in April 2019 in accordance with the MSA, and (5) B.B. herself acknowledged at deposition that she "had no knowledge on those trusts." (SBM Trust SJ Motion Jan 15, 2026.pdf, SUMF ¶¶ 6–63.) Pearlson's motion on behalf of the KRM Trust made the same showing with respect to the KRM Trust's 70% interest in the Bedminster home, the trust's $964,884 share of sale proceeds, and the boat-sale distributions representing repayment of a documented loan from June 2016 — more than a year before the conduct involving B.B. began. (KRM SJ Motion, SUMF ¶¶ 5–22.) The settlement that followed those SJ motions was not a concession by the trusts of any liability. The agreement expressly states: "the Parties desire to resolve the Claims and Third-Party Claims amicably, without any factual or legal concessions or admissions of liability of any kind." (KRM & SBM Civil Release ¶ 4.) The trusts' agreement to pay $300,000 to resolve litigation that had been pending for six years — with trials and depositions and third-party claims — represents a commercial resolution of ongoing litigation risk, nothing more. It does not establish that the trusts were fraudulent. It establishes that Nagel and his client concluded it was worth $300,000 to be rid of the litigation, and it establishes that B.B. released trust assets as a collection vehicle. Nagel cannot now tell this Court that those same trusts are engines of fraud while his firm is cashing the settlement check.

The motion package consists of three components: (1) the Notice of Motion (two pages), (2) the Memorandum of Law in Support (twenty-three pages), and (3) the Certification of Bruce H. We point it out because it sets the right expectations for what follows in the motion's twenty-three pages of argument.

The motion seeks four forms of relief simultaneously: (1) dismissal of the Chapter 7 petition under 11 U.S.C. § 707(a) for alleged bad faith; (2) a declaration of nondischargeability under § 523(a)(6) for willful and malicious injury; (3) relief from the automatic stay under § 362(d)(1) for cause; and (4) abstention under what Nagel cites as 28 U.S.C. § 1134(c)(1) and (c)(2). Nagel has written the motion as a kitchen-sink document that throws everything at the wall without disciplining the internal logic. That incoherence is an opportunity for the opposition.

### 3.C: Nagel's § 707(a) Argument — Analysis and Assessment

Nagel's § 707(a) argument is the centerpiece of the motion and the one that raises the most genuine difficulty. Counsel should treat it seriously. It is not a frivolous argument. It fails, but it requires a complete and disciplined response.

**The Standard Nagel Invokes**

Nagel correctly states the Tamecki standard: "[G]ood faith requires at least a showing of honest intention." In re Hunt, 521 B.R. at 485. He correctly invokes the burden-shifting framework: "Once a party calls into question a petitioner's good faith, the burden shifts to the petitioner to prove his good faith." In re Tamecki, 229 F.3d at 207. He correctly recites the Glunk five-factor test. (Nagel-mtd-analysis.md § 2.) None of this is wrong. The problem is how Nagel applies these standards to Brad's facts — and what he omits.

**The Murray Inversion — Nagel Cites an Adverse Case**

Nagel cites In re Murray for the proposition that dismissal may result from "circumstances falling short of bad faith but nevertheless representing an inappropriate use of the Code." (Nagel-mtd-analysis.md § 2, citing Murray, 543 B.R. at 490.) But Murray is an *involuntary* bankruptcy petition case. Wilk Auslander LLP, Murray's former employer, filed an involuntary Chapter 7 petition against Murray after obtaining a judgment. The bad faith in Murray was the *creditor's* bad faith in weaponizing the involuntary process as a "judgment enforcement device" in a two-party dispute. The Second Circuit affirmed dismissal because "bankruptcy is not a judgment enforcement device." 900 F.3d 53 (2d Cir. 2018). (Part2-707a.md § B.4.)

This case does not support Nagel's position. It is a bludgeon he has accidentally aimed at himself. If "bankruptcy is not a judgment enforcement device," then § 707(a) is not a mechanism by which Nagel can undo Brad's voluntary bankruptcy filing simply because he wants his damages trial without the constraints of the automatic stay and discharge. Nagel's motion to dismiss is closer to the judgment-enforcement conduct Murray condemned than to anything on Brad's side of this motion. Any experienced bankruptcy practitioner — and Judge O'Hagan is an experienced bankruptcy practitioner — will recognize this immediately. The opposition brief should open the § 707(a) argument by pointing this out.

**The Pari Passu Problem — Nagel's Most Dangerous Factual Argument**

The most substantive argument in Nagel's § 707(a) brief is the implicit "pari passu destruction" argument: Brad filed bankruptcy so that B.B. would have to compete with family members, business entities, and insiders who are also listed as creditors, thereby diluting her pro rata share of any distribution. Nagel phrases it this way: "Most of Mell's creditors are family members or entities he controls (or controlled). B.B.'s case, in contrast, is about Mell's sexual abuse of a minor... Making B.B. compete with other creditors to see her judgment from the State Court Action satisfied will severely hamper her ability to collect from Mell." (Nagel-mtd-analysis.md § 7.)

This argument has intuitive appeal. We need to address it directly, not sidestep it. The response has two parts. First, the argument inverts the bankruptcy process. The pari passu distribution rule — that unsecured creditors share equally in available assets — is not an injustice the bankruptcy court is asked to remedy. It is the fundamental design of the Code. Congress created the priority scheme in 11 U.S.C. § 507 specifically to determine who gets paid in what order. Domestic support obligations come first (§ 507(a)(1)); tax claims come eighth (§ 507(a)(8)); general unsecured creditors share the remainder. Nagel's complaint that B.B. will have to compete with other creditors is a complaint against the Bankruptcy Code itself. ### 3.D: Nagel's § 523(a)(6) Argument — Strengths and Weaknesses

Nagel's § 523(a)(6) argument reads as follows in the brief: sexual abuse of a minor is "by definition" willful and malicious; the partial summary judgment establishes all elements of liability via collateral estoppel; therefore the entire debt is nondischargeable. This three-step argument has at least three independent fatal defects at the threshold before any court reaches the merits.

**Defect One: Procedural — No Adversary Proceeding**

As noted above, Fed. R. Bankr. P. 7001(6) requires an adversary proceeding for determinations of dischargeability under § 523(a)(6). Fed. R. Bankr. P. 4007 notes that the bankruptcy court has exclusive jurisdiction to determine dischargeability under § 523(a)(2), (4), and (6), and that adversary proceeding rules apply. Nagel has not filed an adversary complaint. He has sought § 523(a)(6) relief by motion in the main case. This is procedurally improper.

Note, however, that the deadline for filing a complaint objecting to discharge or seeking nondischargeability under § 523(a)(6) runs from 60 days after the first date set for the meeting of creditors under § 341. Counsel should track this deadline carefully and monitor whether Nagel files a proper adversary complaint before the deadline. If Nagel files an adversary complaint before July 16 (or between July 16 and the deadline), the procedural defect is cured and the merits become live. The deadline should be calculated and placed on the opposition team's calendar immediately.

**Defect Two: The Geiger Standard — "Self-Evident" Inference Is Foreclosed**

The centerpiece of Nagel's § 523(a)(6) argument is his extended quotation from In re Fleetwood (Bankr. D. Alaska 2013), which holds that willfulness and malice are "self-evident" in child sexual abuse cases. (Nagel-mtd-analysis.md § 3.) The quote asserts that "where the damages arise from sexual abuse, willfulness is apparent because the subjective motive to inflict harm 'is self-evident, either because the tortfeasor knows his conduct is certain or almost certain to cause harm, or because he should know and therefore the intent is inferred as a matter of law.'" Id.

This argument is directly foreclosed by the Supreme Court in Kawaauhau v. Geiger, 523 U.S. 57, 61 (1998), which held that "the word 'willful' in (a)(6) modifies the word 'injury,' indicating that nondischargeability takes a deliberate or intentional injury, not merely a deliberate or intentional act that leads to injury." The Court specifically rejected inference from the nature of the act: "Had Congress meant to exempt debts resulting from unintentionally inflicted injuries, it might have described instead 'willful acts that cause injury.'" 523 U.S. at 63. The "self-evident" approach Fleetwood endorses is an objective inference test — inferring intent to injure from the nature of the act. Geiger expressly rejected that approach. (Part3-523a6.md § A.1.)

Additionally, Fleetwood is a Bankr. D. Alaska opinion — not Third Circuit, not Supreme Court. It is not binding in this Court. The Third Circuit's own § 523(a)(6) standard, articulated in In re Conte, 33 F.3d 303, 305, 308 (3d Cir. 1994) — as reconciled with Geiger — requires that the debtor either purposefully inflicted the injury or acted with subjective knowledge that injury was substantially certain to result. The "substantially certain" prong is subjective under Geiger: what did this debtor actually know, not what a reasonable person would have known. (Part3-523a6.md § A.2.)

Nagel also cites Berlingeri (Bankr. D.N.J. 2000) and characterizes it as holding that claims based on intentional acts done to cause injury are nondischargeable. (Nagel-mtd-analysis.md § 3.) Berlingeri is a D.N.J. bankruptcy court opinion — persuasive but not binding — and the relevant passage is about "intentional acts done to cause injury," which is the standard that Geiger itself adopted. The question is not whether the standard exists but whether it is satisfied. Based on available research, Berlingeri's § 523(a)(6) finding appears to rest on a consent judgment stipulation in that case that conceded willfulness — not on inference from the nature of the conduct. If that is correct, Berlingeri is distinguishable: there is no consent judgment here, and the discovery sanction that constitutes the "liability" determination made no mental state finding. (Part3-523a6.md § B.3; CASES-OBTAINED.md, Berlingeri analysis.) Counsel must obtain the full text of Berlingeri before filing the opposition.

**Defect Three: The "Actually Litigated" Requirement for Collateral Estoppel**

Even if the § 523(a)(6) subjective intent standard could theoretically be satisfied on the underlying facts, Nagel cannot establish it through collateral estoppel from the March 2021 partial summary judgment. The Third Circuit requires that for collateral estoppel to operate in a § 523(a)(6) proceeding, the specific § 523(a)(6) mental state elements — willful intent to cause the specific injury and malice — must have been "actually litigated and determined" in the prior proceeding. In re Docteroff, 133 F.3d 210, 214-15 (3d Cir. 1997); In re Wilson, 116 F.3d 87, 90-92 (3d Cir. 1997).

The March 2021 partial summary judgment was not a merits determination. The Appellate Division described it as "interlocutory and not a final judgment" and noted uncertainty about whether Mell even responded to the summary judgment motion. (Part3-523a6.md § E.2.) N.J. Court Rule 4:23-5(a)(2) — the sanction rule under which the PSJ may have been entered — provides that pleadings are struck and a default judgment entered as a discovery sanction upon proof of non-compliance. It involves no evidentiary hearing, no testimony, and no findings of fact on any element of any claim. No judge ever made a finding that Brad acted willfully or maliciously within the meaning of § 523(a)(6). A discovery sanction that deems well-pleaded allegations admitted for civil liability purposes does not satisfy the "actually litigated" requirement for any specific element.

Nagel will cite Docteroff for the "deliberately prevents resolution" exception — the rule that a party who bad-faith obstructs a proceeding will be deemed to have actually litigated the issues. The response: Brad invoked his Fifth Amendment right against self-incrimination during civil discovery while parallel federal criminal proceedings were pending against him. The Fifth Amendment is a constitutionally protected right. Exercising a constitutional right is not "deliberate prevention of resolution" in the Docteroff sense. Docteroff's exception requires conduct that is sanctionable because it is unjustified — bad-faith obstruction with no legal basis. Brad's invocation of the Fifth had the strongest possible legal basis: the United States Constitution. Moreover, even under Docteroff's exception, collateral estoppel can only preclude relitigation of issues that were actually established in the prior proceeding. The R. 4:23-5(a)(2) sanction established procedural default, not the specific § 523(a)(6) mental state elements. (Part3-523a6.md § E.5.)

**Counsel's Honest Assessment of the § 523(a)(6) Merits**

We owe you a candid view of the § 523(a)(6) merits independent of the procedural defects. The merits present a genuine challenge that the opposition cannot gloss over. The underlying conduct here is not like the malpractice case in Geiger, where the doctor prescribed the wrong medication negligently. Brad's conduct was intentional — the criminal conviction establishes that he intentionally traveled for illicit sexual conduct and intentionally received child pornography. The question under Geiger is whether he subjectively intended to cause the specific injuries alleged in each civil count, not merely whether he intended to perform the acts.

There is space between the criminal conviction's intent elements (intent to travel, intent to receive) and § 523(a)(6)'s required intent (intent to cause the specific injury to the specific plaintiff). That space — and Lilly Cannon's own deposition testimony describing a "non-forced relationship" — is the factual terrain in which the § 523(a)(6) contest will be fought if Nagel properly initiates an adversary proceeding. The honest answer is that this is a hard merits issue where the law is genuinely contested and where the outcome in a full adversary proceeding is not certain. Our procedural and collateral estoppel defenses are strong. The merits of the underlying § 523(a)(6) claim — if properly pleaded and tried — are genuinely contested. Counsel should not represent to the client or to this Court that the debt will be discharged; that determination requires a full adversary proceeding with fact-finding on intent.

### 3.F: Nagel's Abstention Argument — Assessment

The abstention section is the most legally defective portion of the motion, and it is defective in multiple independent ways, beginning with the threshold citation error.

**The § 1134 Error**

Nagel cites "28 U.S.C. § 1134(c)(2)" and "28 U.S.C. § 1134(c)(1)" as the statutory bases for mandatory and permissive abstention respectively. Section 1134 of Title 28 does not exist. Chapter 85 of Part IV of Title 28 (District Courts; Jurisdiction) contains sections numbered 1330 through 1369. There is no § 1134. The abstention provisions governing bankruptcy are at 28 U.S.C. § 1334(c)(1) (permissive) and § 1334(c)(2) (mandatory). (Part4-abstention.md, Threshold Alert; nagel-mtd-analysis.md §§ 6, 8, Misrepresentation 1.) A motion premised on a statute that does not exist is deficient on its face.

**Mandatory Abstention Is Legally Impossible Here**

Setting aside the citation error, the substantive mandatory abstention argument fails on the face of the statute. Section 1334(c)(2) — the actual statute — applies only to a proceeding "based upon a State law claim or State law cause of action, related to a case under title 11 but not arising under title 11 or arising in a case under title 11." A § 523(a)(6) nondischargeability action arises directly under 11 U.S.C. § 523 — a provision of the Bankruptcy Code. It arises under title 11, not merely "relates to" a case under title 11. Therefore the mandatory abstention statute by its express text does not apply. (Part4-abstention.md § A.4.)

This is confirmed by 28 U.S.C. § 157(b)(2)(I), which expressly enumerates "determinations as to the dischargeability of particular debts" as core proceedings. Core proceedings "arise under" or "arise in" title 11 — by definition. Mandatory abstention under § 1334(c)(2) is categorically unavailable for core proceedings. Fed. R. Bankr. P. 4007's advisory committee note further confirms exclusive bankruptcy court jurisdiction over § 523(a)(2), (4), and (6). Exclusive jurisdiction cannot be divested by an abstention motion.

**Stoe v. Flaherty — Nagel's Own Case Defeats Him**

Nagel cites Stoe v. Flaherty, 436 F.3d 209, 213 (3d Cir. 2006), as support for the mandatory abstention test. Stoe is the controlling Third Circuit authority on mandatory abstention. Nagel has misread it. Stoe's second element requires that the claim "does not 'arise under' title 11 and does not 'arise in' a case under title 11." A § 523(a)(6) dischargeability determination arises directly under title 11. Nagel cannot satisfy element (2) of the case he cites. Stoe defeats him, not supports him. (Part4-abstention.md § B.1.)

**The Zunic Order Independently Kills Element Four**

Stoe's fourth element requires that "an action 'is commenced' in a state forum of appropriate jurisdiction." Courts require a currently pending, not merely refiled, state court action. The Zunic order dismissed B.B. v. Mell without prejudice on June 12, 2026 — the same day Brad filed the petition. The state court action was not pending when Nagel filed the abstention motion on June 23. It is not pending for the July 16 hearing. Three of Stoe's five elements fail independently: element (2) (arises under title 11), element (3) (bankruptcy court has exclusive § 523 jurisdiction independent of § 1334), and element (4) (no pending state court action). All five elements must be satisfied. (Part4-abstention.md §§ A.5, A.6, C.1.)

**The Internal Incoherence**

Perhaps the most revealing flaw in the abstention argument is its internal contradiction. Nagel simultaneously asks this Court to: (a) abstain from hearing the case and send it back to state court for a damages trial; and (b) declare the debt nondischargeable under § 523(a)(6). These positions are irreconcilable. A § 523(a)(6) determination requires this Court to find "willful and malicious injury" — which requires adjudicating the factual circumstances that Nagel claims should be decided in state court. A court cannot abstain from the predicate (the factual circumstances of the underlying injury) while retaining the conclusion (the nondischargeability declaration). Nagel's argument literally requires this Court to make findings it just sent to state court. (Part4-abstention.md § C.4.)

### 4.A: § 707(a) Response — Defeating Bad Faith Dismissal

**The Threshold Positioning**

Before addressing any specific factor, the opposition must establish the proper standard and then never leave it. Tamecki's "egregious cases" language is not decorative — it is a genuine gate-keeping standard that limits bad faith dismissal to cases involving "concealed or misrepresented assets and/or sources of income, lavish lifestyles and intention to avoid a large single debt based upon conduct akin to fraud, misconduct or gross negligence." In re Tamecki, 229 F.3d 205, 207 (3d Cir. 2000). And Perlin adds the critical additional limitation: "A debtor's ability to repay is not in and of itself sufficient proof of bad faith." In re Perlin, 497 F.3d 364, 369 (3d Cir. 2007). These two sentences, from two binding Third Circuit opinions, are the walls within which this entire argument must be contained. Every argument Nagel makes must be pressed against those walls until he either shows it fits inside the Tamecki-Perlin space (extremely rare) or demonstrates it falls outside (overwhelmingly the case here).

**Opening Move: Invert Murray**

The opposition should open the § 707(a) argument by noting Nagel's misuse of Murray. The opposition should say something like: "Nagel's lead case — In re Murray, 900 F.3d 53 (2d Cir. 2018) — actually defeats his position. **The Affirmative Good Faith Showing**

Under Tamecki's burden framework, once a movant calls good faith into question, the burden shifts to the debtor to produce evidence of honest intention. Once the debtor produces that evidence, the burden shifts back to the movant to prove actual bad faith by the egregious standard. Brad's three binders of financial disclosure — filed within the OSC timeframe, comprehensive, signed under penalty of perjury, disclosing every asset including the $750,000 in Nagel's own trust account — are affirmative good faith evidence that satisfies the debtor's initial burden. In re Perlin, 497 F.3d at 367-68 (dismissal denied because "the Perlins had been truthful with the court and their creditors").

The specific affirmative evidence of good faith that should be highlighted:

— *Comprehensive Schedule Disclosure.* Three binders, thirteen official forms, forty-one pages, disclosing retirement accounts, vehicles, the Ferrari, the $750,000 in the Nagel Rice trust account (identified by custodian name), prior addresses including FCI Fort Dix and Kintock Newark, SOFA entries showing no transfers within two years, no closed accounts within one year, no safe deposit boxes, no offshore accounts. Eubanks was dismissed because the debtor had six undisclosed business entities and three undisclosed properties. Brad is the photographic negative of that debtor.

— *Multiple Creditors.* Twelve creditors appear on the schedules. The IRS ($300,000), Kimberly Mell ($200,000 DSO), and the New Jersey Division of Taxation ($75,000) account for $575,000 in priority claims with nothing to do with B.B. **Distinguishing Myers — The Most Important Analytical Task**

In re Myers, 491 F.3d 120 (3d Cir. 2007), is Nagel's best case and must be distinguished on the facts, not dismissed as non-applicable. Myers is binding Third Circuit authority. It must be engaged directly. The distinction has six independent grounds:

(1) *Chapter.* Myers was Chapter 13. Brad filed Chapter 7. The Chapter 7 fresh start purpose — available to individuals who meet the § 109(b) requirements — is not available for entities and was not the posture in Myers.

(2) *Statutory Eligibility.* Myers did not meet the § 109 eligibility requirements for Chapter 13 at the time of filing. Brad meets all § 109(b) requirements for Chapter 7.

(3) *Court Order Violation.* Myers's husband withdrew $6,000 in violation of a Pennsylvania court order and used it to pay bankruptcy counsel. No such conduct is alleged here.

(4) *Announced Ruling.* The Myers state court had already "announced it intended to rule" before the bankruptcy filing. Here, trial had not commenced. The distinction matters: filing the night before an announced adverse ruling is qualitatively different from filing before a trial has even begun.

(5) *Nature of Liability.* The debt in Myers arose from a fraudulent conveyance claim — debtor's own affirmative misconduct involving fraud. The "liability" here was set by a R. 4:23-5(a)(2) discovery sanction — a procedural consequence of Fifth Amendment invocation, not a merits adjudication of fraud-adjacent conduct.

(6) *Who Created the Delay.* In Myers, the debtor created the procedural posture. Here, Nagel's three-year interlocutory appeal created the trial date that Brad responded to.

### 4.B: § 523(a)(6) Response — Nondischargeability

**Lead with the Procedural Defect**

Open the § 523(a)(6) response with the adversary proceeding requirement under Fed. R. Bankr. P. 7001(6). The Court should not reach the merits of the § 523(a)(6) argument on this motion because the motion is the wrong procedural vehicle. The procedural argument also allows the opposition to set up the monitoring task — counsel should be tracking the adversary complaint deadline and preparing for an adversary proceeding if and when Nagel files one properly.

**The Geiger/Conte Framework — Our Law Controls**

The legal framework is clear: Geiger (Supreme Court, binding) requires subjective intent to cause the specific injury; Conte (Third Circuit, binding) requires either purposeful infliction or subjective knowledge that injury was substantially certain. These two cases are our foundation. Everything else follows from them.

Against those binding authorities, Nagel's Fleetwood argument — "self-evident" willfulness as a matter of law — is foreclosed. The opposition should quote Geiger's exact language on this point: the statute says "willful and malicious injury," not "willful acts that cause injury." 523 U.S. at 63. Congress's word choice was deliberate.

**The Collateral Estoppel Argument — The Core Defense**

The collateral estoppel defense to the § 523(a)(6) claim is strong and should be developed thoroughly. The argument runs:

Step 1: Identify what must be "actually litigated" — the specific mental state elements of § 523(a)(6) (subjective intent to cause the specific injury; malice without just cause or excuse).

Step 2: Show that R. 4:23-5(a)(2) establishes none of those elements. A discovery sanction is a procedural consequence. No judge made any finding on intent, malice, or purpose. The elements were never presented to any factfinder.

Step 3: Preempt the Docteroff argument. Docteroff's "deliberately prevents resolution" exception requires conduct that is sanctionable because it is unjustified — bad-faith obstruction without legal basis. Brad's Fifth Amendment invocation had constitutional justification. The distinction between constitutionally protected silence and bad-faith obstruction is fundamental.

Step 4: Even if Docteroff applied, what would have been "deemed litigated"? Civil liability on each count — not § 523(a)(6) mental state elements. The state court did not adjudicate willfulness and malice under federal bankruptcy standards. Those elements were never before any New Jersey court.

**Berlingeri — Handle With Care**

Berlingeri (246 B.R. 196 (Bankr. D.N.J. 2000)) is a Nagel-cited case that needs Westlaw verification before the opposition is filed. If Berlingeri held that intent to cause injury was established by a consent judgment in which the debtor stipulated to the facts constituting willful and malicious injury, then Berlingeri is distinguishable — there is no consent judgment here, and the partial summary judgment is an interlocutory discovery sanction. If Berlingeri adopted a post-Geiger subjective intent standard and applied it to a child sexual abuse context, the question is how the court got from the facts to the conclusion. If it used the "self-evident" inference approach, it is inconsistent with Geiger. Do not cite or dismiss Berlingeri in the brief without reading the full opinion first.

**The Count-by-Count Element Problem**

Nagel's § 523(a)(6) argument treats the entire civil action as a monolith — all counts are "sexual abuse of a minor," and therefore all are willful and malicious as a matter of law. This approach is analytically deficient. Even if some counts could satisfy Geiger, Nagel must establish willfulness and malice count-by-count, and the elements of each New Jersey civil statute must be compared to § 523(a)(6)'s requirements individually. N.J.S.A. 2A:61B-1 (Child Sexual Abuse Act) requires "intentional touching for the purpose of sexually arousing or sexually gratifying the actor" — intent to gratify, not intent to injure. **Honest Assessment**

### 4.D: Abstention Response

**Open With the Citation Error — Precisely and Without Overplaying It**

The citation error signals to Judge O'Hagan that the brief was carelessly researched; one sentence is sufficient to make that point. Do not repeat it.

**The Statutory Text Closes the Door**

Section 1334(c)(2), the mandatory abstention statute, applies only to proceedings that are "related to" a case under title 11 "but not arising under title 11 or arising in a case under title 11." A § 523(a)(6) nondischargeability action arises directly under 11 U.S.C. § 523. It arises under title 11. The statutory text itself forecloses mandatory abstention. No case-by-case analysis is needed on this point — the plain text resolves it.

Support with 28 U.S.C. § 157(b)(2)(I): "Core proceedings include but are not limited to the following: . . . (I) determinations as to the dischargeability of particular debts." Quote the full verbatim text. This is not an ambiguous enumeration — it is an express statutory statement that dischargeability is a core proceeding, and core proceedings arise under or arise in title 11, not merely relate to it.

**Stoe Element-by-Element Defeat**

After the statutory text argument, walk through each element of the Stoe five-factor test and show which elements fail. At minimum, elements (2), (3), and (4) all fail independently:

Element (2): The § 523(a)(6) claim does not merely "relate to" the bankruptcy case — it arises under title 11. This element's failure is dispositive.

Element (3): Federal courts would have jurisdiction absent § 1334 because the bankruptcy court has exclusive jurisdiction over § 523(a)(6) dischargeability under its inherent bankruptcy authority and Fed. R. Bankr. P. 4007. This element fails.

Element (4): No action "is commenced" in state court. The Zunic order dismissed B.B. v. Mell on June 12, 2026. Any resumption requires this Court's permission. A dismissed action is not a "commenced" action for mandatory abstention purposes. This element fails.

All five must be satisfied. Three fail independently. Mandatory abstention is unavailable.

**The Logical Incoherence Point**

Close the abstention section with the logical incoherence argument. If Nagel wants this Court to: (a) abstain from adjudicating the case and send it to state court; while simultaneously asking this Court to: (b) determine that the debt is nondischargeable under § 523(a)(6) — he is asking for two irreconcilable things. ### 4.E: The Pari Passu Structural Argument — Weaving It Through

The "pari passu destruction" narrative is Nagel's most emotionally compelling argument, and it runs through every section of his brief without ever being labeled as such. The argument is: Brad filed bankruptcy to dilute B.B.'s recovery by forcing her to share the available assets with family members and insiders who are themselves listed creditors. The subtext is: this is unfair, and the Court should not permit it.

This argument must be addressed head-on across all four response sections, not deflected or ignored. The counter-argument has three components:

First, pari passu distribution is not a trick Brad deployed — it is the design of the United States Bankruptcy Code. Congress in 11 U.S.C. § 726 established the order of distribution in Chapter 7 cases. Congress in § 507 established the priority scheme. Congress in § 523 established which debts are excepted from discharge. The "harm" of pro rata distribution is what Congress intended. It is the entire point of bankruptcy. A creditor's complaint that she must share assets with other creditors is a complaint against the Code, not against the debtor.

Second, the priority structure actually works against the pari passu argument in this case. The highest-priority creditors — Kimberly Mell's $200,000 DSO (first priority under § 507(a)(1)) and the IRS's $300,000 claim (eighth priority under § 507(a)(8)) — are nondischargeable obligations that Brad cannot extinguish through Chapter 7 regardless of outcome. The "dilution" Nagel fears from these creditors is built into the Code's nondischargeability provisions. B.B.'s claim — if established and if not dischargeable — will be determined against the same asset pool as any other unsecured creditor. That is not an injustice engineered by Brad. It is the statutory distribution scheme.

Third, Brad's retirement accounts — $1,090,000 in total — are fully exempt under 11 U.S.C. § 522(d)(12) regardless of whether this case is dismissed or proceeds. Those accounts are legally unavailable to B.B. whether she is in the bankruptcy or not. The pari passu argument's implicit premise — that B.B. would be better off in state court collecting from a fund held by Nagel's own firm — ignores that every other creditor would be pursuing that fund simultaneously once discharge is denied.

**The Legal Framework**

The Third Circuit's § 362(k)(1) standard, established in In re Lansaw, 853 F.3d 657, 669 (3d Cir. 2017), requires three elements: (1) a violation of the automatic stay occurred; (2) the violation was willfully committed; and (3) the debtor suffered damages as a result. (Part5-stay.md § C-1.)

Willfulness under the Third Circuit standard requires only knowledge of the bankruptcy filing plus an intentional act. In re Lansaw, 853 F.3d at 669. Knowledge of the specific stay provisions is not required — only knowledge of the bankruptcy itself. In re University Medical Center, 973 F.2d 1065, 1068 (3d Cir. 1992). Good faith is not a defense. Cal. Coast Univ. v. Aleckna (In re Aleckna), No. 20-1309, 2021 WL 4097155, at *4 (3d Cir. Sept. 9, 2021).

Applied here: Nagel had actual knowledge. Paragano is Nagel's law partner — agency imputation principles under the Restatement (Third) of Agency § 5.03 impute Nagel's knowledge to Paragano. Lansaw authorized and affirmed: (1) actual damages including attorneys' fees; (2) emotional distress damages (no medical evidence required where violations are patently egregious and debtor testifies credibly); and (3) punitive damages — $40,000 in Lansaw itself. In re Lansaw, 853 F.3d at 669.

## SECTION 5.0: PRELIMINARY NOTE ON PROCEDURAL ARCHITECTURE

Before addressing any of Nagel's four theories of relief, the opposition brief must establish a threshold point that will orient Judge O'Hagan before he reads a single word of the § 707(a) argument. That threshold point is this: Nagel has assembled four mutually incompatible forms of relief in a single motion filed in the main case. The motion is not merely wrong on the merits — it is architecturally incoherent.

Consider what the motion asks. First, it asks this Court to dismiss the Chapter 7 case in its entirety under § 707(a). Second, it asks this Court to declare B.B.'s debt nondischargeable under § 523(a)(6). Third, it asks this Court to lift the automatic stay under § 362(d)(1) so that B.B. may proceed with a damages trial in New Jersey Superior Court. Fourth, it asks this Court to abstain from exercising jurisdiction under 28 U.S.C. § 1334(c) and return the matter to state court. These four requests exist in irreconcilable tension. If the case is dismissed under § 707(a), there is no discharge to object to — the § 523(a)(6) declaration becomes moot the moment the case disappears. If the § 523(a)(6) declaration is granted, the case is retained — meaning dismissal was denied. If the Court abstains and sends the matter to state court, it cannot simultaneously adjudicate the § 523(a)(6) question that requires this Court to find "willful and malicious injury" under federal bankruptcy law. Abstention sends the predicate facts to state court; § 523(a)(6) retains the federal conclusion in this Court — a logical impossibility.

The opposition brief should make this architectural point cleanly and early, in a paragraph following the preliminary statement. It signals to Judge O'Hagan — a former USAO Senior Bankruptcy Counsel who has argued these statutes personally — that the motion is a kitchen-sink document assembled under time pressure without disciplined internal logic. It also frames the structural observation that Nagel's "lead case" — *In re Murray*, 900 F.3d 53 (2d Cir. 2018) — is an involuntary bankruptcy case in which the bad faith was the creditor's weaponization of the bankruptcy process as a judgment enforcement device. Murray holds that "bankruptcy is not a judgment enforcement device." That sentence, accurately applied, describes Nagel's motion more precisely than it describes Brad's filing.

The procedural defect in the § 523(a)(6) request is independently dispositive as to that specific relief: Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 7001(6) provides that "a proceeding to determine the dischargeability of a debt" is an adversary proceeding. An adversary proceeding is a separate lawsuit commenced within the bankruptcy case, governed by Part VII of the Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure, initiated by a complaint, a summons, and an answer. *See* Fed. R. Bankr. P. 7003 (complaint initiates adversary proceeding); 7004 (summons and service); 7012 (answer). Nagel has filed a motion — Doc. 8 in the main case — not a complaint in an adversary proceeding. This Court lacks the procedural posture to enter a nondischargeability order on a motion. A motion in the main case deprives the debtor of all of these protections.

## SECTION 5.1: THE § 707(a) BAD FAITH DISMISSAL ARGUMENT FAILS

### 5.1.A: The Governing Standard — Tamecki and Perlin Define the Gate

The Third Circuit's bad faith dismissal standard under § 707(a) begins and ends with two binding authorities: *In re Tamecki*, 229 F.3d 205 (3d Cir. 2000), and *In re Perlin*, 497 F.3d 364 (3d Cir. 2007).

*Tamecki* is the foundational authority. The Third Circuit there held that § 707(a)'s "cause" requirement encompasses a debtor's bad faith, but expressly limited this doctrine to "egregious cases" involving "concealed or misrepresented assets and/or sources of income, and [lavish] lifestyles and the intention to avoid a large single debt based upon conduct akin to fraud, misconduct or gross negligence." *In re Tamecki*, 229 F.3d at 207 (internal quotation omitted). The Court also established the burden-shifting framework that governs the analysis: once a movant raises a colorable bad faith challenge, the burden shifts to the debtor to demonstrate good faith; if the debtor produces evidence of good faith, the burden shifts back to the movant to establish bad faith by the egregious standard. *Id.*

The critical word in *Tamecki* is "egregious." It is not decorative language. Congress created § 707(a) to address cases involving substantive abuse of the bankruptcy process, not to create a judicial escape valve for creditors dissatisfied with the statutory rules of distribution. The Third Circuit in *Tamecki* endorsed the Eighth Circuit's formulation that bad faith dismissal is reserved for cases of "debtor misconduct" — not simply cases where a creditor believes the debtor does not deserve a discharge. *Id.* at 207 (quoting *In re Zick*, 931 F.2d 1124, 1127 (6th Cir. 1991)). The first sentence of the § 707(a) argument in the opposition brief should quote *Tamecki*'s "egregious cases" formulation verbatim and make the gate explicit: Brad's case does not come within twenty miles of that threshold.

*Perlin* adds the second mandatory limiting principle: "A debtor's ability to repay is not in and of itself sufficient proof of bad faith." *In re Perlin*, 497 F.3d at 369. This rule is directly applicable here. Nagel's motion implicitly — and at times explicitly — rests on the theory that Brad has assets (retirement accounts, escrowed funds) and therefore should not have access to Chapter 7. *Perlin* forecloses that argument as a standalone basis for dismissal. Ability to pay is one factor in a multifactor totality analysis, not a sufficient condition. The opposition should quote *Perlin* at page 369 and then demonstrate that every other factor in the analysis favors Brad.

### 5.1.B: The Murray Inversion — Nagel's Lead Case Condemns His Motion

Nagel cites *In re Murray*, 543 B.R. 484 (Bankr. S.D.N.Y. 2016), *aff'd*, 900 F.3d 53 (2d Cir. 2018), as authority supporting bad faith dismissal of a voluntary Chapter 7 petition. (Nagel-mtd-analysis.md § 2.) This citation is the most important analytical error in Nagel's brief, and the opposition brief should make the correction the first substantive paragraph of the § 707(a) argument. The reason is simple: the opposition correcting the error will immediately communicate to Judge O'Hagan — who knows *Murray* — that the movant did not read the case carefully and the respondent did.

*Murray* is an involuntary bankruptcy case. Wilk Auslander LLP, an attorney's former employer, filed an *involuntary* Chapter 7 petition against Murray as a device to collect a judgment it had obtained against him. The court — at both the bankruptcy court level and the Second Circuit — dismissed the involuntary petition not because Murray had been bad faith but because the petitioning creditor had been bad faith. The Second Circuit held that "bankruptcy is not a judgment enforcement device" and that filing an involuntary petition as a mechanism to collect on a two-party judgment constitutes an abuse of the bankruptcy process for which dismissal is appropriate. *In re Murray*, 900 F.3d 53, 60 (2d Cir. 2018).

Applied to the facts of this case, *Murray*'s logic cuts sharply against Nagel. Nagel filed this motion because he wants to collect on what he characterizes as a damages judgment against Brad — the same reason the creditor in *Murray* filed the involuntary petition. The Second Circuit condemned exactly that use of bankruptcy. The *Murray* principle, accurately stated, is that a creditor cannot weaponize the bankruptcy process to force a debtor into a position advantageous for that creditor's collection efforts. That is precisely what this motion attempts to do: dismiss Brad's bankruptcy so that Nagel can proceed with a state court damages trial against a debtor who would, absent bankruptcy, face a multi-creditor free-for-all with Nagel holding a privileged position as both attorney and physical custodian of $750,000 in Brad's funds.

Nagel cited *Murray* without reading it carefully enough to see that it condemned his position, not supported it. The opposition brief should make this point in a single, precise paragraph — not belabored, but clear.

### 5.1.C: Nagel's Remaining Cases — All Either Support Brad or Are Inapplicable

Nagel cites twenty-nine cases across his brief. For the § 707(a) argument specifically, the following require targeted treatment beyond the *Murray* inversion:

**In re Hunt, 521 B.R. 479 (Bankr. M.D. Pa. 2014) — DENIED.** Nagel cites *Hunt* for the articulation of the legal standard — and the opposition should be delighted. *Hunt* denied § 707(a) dismissal on facts materially worse than Brad's. The *Hunt* debtor reaffirmed a Lotus Elise, a Mercedes SLK, and a Range Rover; he also had a boat. The debtor owed $2.1 million to a single creditor who represented 83% of his total debt — a far more concentrated single-creditor situation than Brad's, where B.B.'s claim is unknown/contingent/disputed and sits behind $575,000 in senior priority claims. The *Hunt* court denied dismissal under *Tamecki* because the debtor's schedules were complete, his lifestyle was not lavish, and the filing had a legitimate Chapter 7 purpose. *In re Hunt*, 521 B.R. at 485–90. Brad's facts are better than the *Hunt* debtor's on every dimension: no reaffirmed luxury vehicles, no single-creditor concentration, complete schedules, zero monthly income, and a deficit of $1,052 per month.

**In re DeSoto (Bankr. D.N.J. 2006) — DENIED.** Nagel cites *DeSoto* for the proposition that a two-party dispute can support bad faith dismissal. (Nagel-mtd-analysis.md § 2.) But *DeSoto* denied dismissal on that very ground, holding that a two-party dispute is not per se evidence of bad faith. The opposition should note this cleanly: Nagel's own case holds the opposite of what he suggests.

**In re Myers, 491 F.3d 120 (3d Cir. 2007) — DISTINGUISHABLE ON SIX INDEPENDENT GROUNDS.** *Myers* is Nagel's best case and the one that demands the most careful treatment. *Myers* is binding Third Circuit authority in which the court affirmed dismissal of a Chapter 13 petition for bad faith. The opposition cannot dismiss *Myers* as inapplicable — it must engage it directly and distinguish it on the facts.

The six independent grounds of distinction are:

(1) *Chapter filed.* Myers filed Chapter 13; Brad filed Chapter 7. The bad faith analysis in Chapter 13 incorporates the plan confirmation requirements of § 1325(a)(3) (good faith plan requirement) and § 1325(a)(7) (good faith petition filing requirement). Chapter 7's § 707(a) analysis has no plan component and is more narrowly focused on the "egregious" threshold established by *Tamecki*. The Chapter 13 good faith analysis is inherently broader.

## SECTION 5.2: THE § 523(a)(6) RELIEF IS PROCEDURALLY IMPROPER

### 5.2.A: Rule 7001(6) Requires an Adversary Proceeding — This Motion Is the Wrong Vehicle

The threshold objection to Nagel's § 523(a)(6) request is procedural and is not subject to any legitimate counter-argument. Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 7001(6) provides, in full: "The following are adversary proceedings: . . . (6) a proceeding to determine the dischargeability of a debt." An adversary proceeding is a separate contested matter, commenced by the filing of a complaint, governed by the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure as incorporated through Part VII of the Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure, and resulting in a judgment after service, an answer period, and (if necessary) trial.

Nagel has not filed an adversary proceeding. He has filed a motion — Doc. 8 — in the main bankruptcy case. A motion in the main case is not an adversary proceeding under Rule 7001(6). This Court has no procedural mechanism to enter a nondischargeability judgment on a motion in the main case. The Rule's requirement of an adversary proceeding is not a technicality that can be waived by calling the relief sought a "motion" — it reflects the substantive due process protections that a debtor is owed before a debt is determined to be nondischargeable: full discovery under Part VII, the right to plead affirmative defenses in an answer, and the right to a trial if facts are genuinely disputed. Stripping those protections by converting an adversary proceeding into a motion prejudices the debtor and violates the Rules. *See* Fed. R. Bankr. P. 7003, 7004, 7012.

The disposition of this defect is straightforward: the Court should dismiss Nagel's § 523(a)(6) request without prejudice, to the extent it is seeking a nondischargeability determination, for failure to initiate an adversary proceeding as required by Rule 7001(6). The opposition brief should make this argument first, clearly, and move on. The Court does not need argument on the merits of § 523(a)(6) unless and until Nagel properly initiates an adversary proceeding.

The follow-on point — track the deadline — is operationally critical. Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 4007(c) establishes a sixty-day deadline from the first date set for the § 341 creditors' meeting for filing a § 523(a)(6) nondischargeability complaint. Bankruptcy counsel must calculate this deadline as soon as the § 341 meeting date is set and place it on the litigation calendar. If Nagel fails to file a complaint before that deadline, his § 523(a)(6) claim is forever time-barred. The Rules Committee set this deadline specifically because § 523(a)(6) is one of the "self-executing" discharge exceptions — meaning the debt is discharged unless the creditor timely objects. *See* Fed. R. Bankr. P. 4007 advisory committee note (1983). Monitoring this deadline is one of the highest-priority tasks between now and the July 16 hearing.

### 5.2.B: The Merits — Geiger and Conte Foreclose Nagel's "Self-Evident" Inference Theory

If the Court, despite the procedural defect, proceeds to evaluate the § 523(a)(6) merits as guidance to Nagel on whether to file a properly initiated adversary proceeding, the merits analysis also defeats Nagel's theory.

**The Governing Framework.**

The Supreme Court settled the § 523(a)(6) intent standard in *Kawaauhau v. Geiger*, 523 U.S. 57 (1998). The Court held: "The word 'willful' in (a)(6) modifies the word 'injury,' indicating that nondischargeability takes a deliberate or intentional injury, not merely a deliberate or intentional act that leads to injury." *Id.* at 61. The Court explicitly rejected the approach of inferring intent to injure from the nature of the act: "Had Congress meant to exempt debts resulting from unintentionally inflicted injuries, it might have described instead 'willful acts that cause injury.'" *Id.* at 63. A debtor who does not subjectively intend to cause the specific injury cannot be found to have inflicted a "willful" injury under § 523(a)(6), regardless of how culpable the act appears from an objective perspective.

The Third Circuit incorporated *Geiger* into its own § 523(a)(6) standard in *In re Conte*, 33 F.3d 303 (3d Cir. 1994), which predates *Geiger* but articulates a standard that Third Circuit courts have construed consistently with *Geiger*'s holding. Under *Conte*, § 523(a)(6) requires either (1) that the debtor purposefully inflicted the injury, or (2) that the debtor acted with subjective knowledge that injury was substantially certain to result. *In re Conte*, 33 F.3d at 308. The "substantially certain" prong is subjective, not objective — it asks what this debtor actually knew about the likely consequences of his conduct, not what a reasonable person in his position would have known.

**Nagel's Fleetwood Argument Is Foreclosed by Geiger.**

Nagel's § 523(a)(6) argument relies centrally on *In re Fleetwood* (Bankr. D. Alaska 2013) for the proposition that willfulness and malice in child sexual abuse cases are "self-evident" — a matter of inference from the nature of the act, not proof of subjective intent. (Nagel-mtd-analysis.md § 3.) This approach was expressly foreclosed by *Geiger* twenty-five years ago. The "self-evident" willfulness argument is an objective inference test: because sexual abuse of a minor is inherently harmful, the intent to harm is inferred as a matter of law. *Geiger* rejected exactly this approach. The statute says "willful and malicious injury" — the Court held that means subjective intent to cause the specific injury, not objective inference from the nature of the act. *Geiger*, 523 U.S. at 61–63.

*Fleetwood* is also not binding in this Court. It is a single bankruptcy court decision from the District of Alaska — not Third Circuit, not Supreme Court, not even persuasive authority from within the Third Circuit. This Court's decisions must follow *Geiger* (Supreme Court, binding) and *Conte* as construed post-*Geiger* (Third Circuit, binding). *Fleetwood* cannot override either.

**The Count-by-Count Element Problem.**

Even if Nagel properly filed an adversary complaint, he would face the obligation to prove § 523(a)(6) willfulness count by count — not as a monolith across "sexual abuse of a minor." Each civil claim has specific intent elements that must be mapped against § 523(a)(6)'s requirements. N.J.S.A. 2A:61B-1 (Child Sexual Abuse Act) requires "intentional touching for the purpose of sexually arousing or sexually gratifying the actor" — the purpose is the actor's gratification, not the infliction of injury on the plaintiff. The gap between "intent to gratify" and "intent to injure" is precisely the gap that *Geiger* requires Nagel to fill with affirmative proof of subjective intent to cause the specific injury. On the record that exists — a discovery sanction, a Fifth Amendment invocation, and an Appellate Division characterization of the partial summary judgment as "interlocutory and not a final judgment" — Nagel has not filled that gap.

### 5.2.C: The Collateral Estoppel Argument — Why the Discovery Sanction Cannot Establish § 523(a)(6) Elements

Nagel argues that the March 26, 2021 partial summary judgment (PSJ) establishes all elements of his § 523(a)(6) claim through collateral estoppel. This argument fails for reasons that are both threshold and structural.

**The "Actually Litigated" Requirement.**

The Third Circuit requires that for collateral estoppel to operate in a § 523(a)(6) proceeding, the specific mental state elements — subjective intent to cause the specific injury and malice — must have been "actually litigated and determined" in the prior proceeding. *In re Docteroff*, 133 F.3d 210, 214–15 (3d Cir. 1997); *In re Wilson*, 116 F.3d 87, 90–92 (3d Cir. 1997). An issue is "actually litigated" when it was submitted to and decided by a factfinder after presentation of evidence and argument — not when it is deemed admitted by procedural default. A judgment that results from a failure to respond to a motion or from a discovery sanction does not satisfy the "actually litigated" standard because no factfinder ever made a determination on the merits of any issue.

This principle defeats Nagel's collateral estoppel argument at the threshold. The New Jersey Appellate Division, in its December 31, 2024 opinion (the very decision that finally ended Nagel's three-year interlocutory appeal), described the March 2021 PSJ in footnote 2 as "interlocutory and not a final judgment." The Appellate Division also noted: "It is not clear from the record whether Mell responded to the summary judgment motion." If Mell did not respond, the PSJ was entered under N.J. Court Rule 4:23-5(a)(2) — which allows entry of a judgment as a sanction for discovery non-compliance — or under N.J. Court Rule 4:46 based on the debtor's procedural default. Neither procedural mechanism involves an evidentiary hearing, testimony, or any factfinder determination on any element of any claim. No judge in the New Jersey Superior Court ever made a finding that Brad Mell subjectively intended to cause the specific injuries alleged in any count of the complaint, or that he acted with malice without just cause or excuse. Those elements were never presented to any factfinder and were never determined by any court. The "actually litigated" requirement is not satisfied.

*In re Azeglio*, 422 B.R. 490 (Bankr. D.N.J. 2010), is directly on point. In *Azeglio*, the Bankruptcy Court for the District of New Jersey denied collateral estoppel effect in a § 523(a)(6) proceeding because the mental state elements required by § 523(a)(6) had not been actually litigated in the prior state court proceeding. The prior state court judgment established civil liability, but it did not establish the specific subjective intent elements required by the federal bankruptcy standard. *Id.* at [page — VERIFY]. *Azeglio* is binding persuasive authority from this same district applying exactly the standard Nagel must satisfy and failing to satisfy it on facts similar to those here.

**The Fifth Amendment Invocation Cannot Be Transformed into Bad-Faith Obstruction.**

Nagel may invoke *Docteroff*'s exception — the principle that a party who "deliberately prevents" resolution of an issue through bad-faith obstruction may be deemed to have "actually litigated" the issue. *In re Docteroff*, 133 F.3d at 214–15. But the *Docteroff* exception applies only to conduct that is sanctionable because it lacks any legal justification — bad-faith obstruction without legal basis. Brad's invocation of the Fifth Amendment right against self-incrimination during civil discovery is not bad-faith obstruction. It is the exercise of a right guaranteed by the Fifth Amendment to the United States Constitution, invoked because parallel federal criminal proceedings were pending against Brad at the time.

The invocation of the Fifth Amendment in civil discovery is a well-established procedural right with a substantial body of law governing its consequences and limits. It is not a discovery sanction offense; it is a constitutional protection. A party who invokes a constitutional right is not "deliberately preventing resolution" of an issue in the *Docteroff* sense. *Docteroff*'s exception reaches conduct that has no legal justification — stonewalling, destruction of evidence, perjury. It does not reach constitutionally protected silence. Moreover, even under *Docteroff*'s exception, what could be "deemed litigated" through the exception would be the issues presented in the state court proceeding — civil liability on the pleaded counts — not the specific federal bankruptcy law elements of subjective willfulness and malice under § 523(a)(6), which were never before the New Jersey Superior Court.

**Berlingeri and Nagel's D.N.J. Authority.**

Nagel cites *In re Berlingeri*, 246 B.R. 196 (Bankr. D.N.J. 2000), for the proposition that § 523(a)(6) reaches "intentional acts done to cause injury." *Berlingeri* is a bankruptcy court decision from this district — persuasive but not binding — and its holding on § 523(a)(6) is narrower than Nagel suggests. Berlingeri's § 523(a)(6) finding appears to rest on a consent judgment stipulation in which the debtor expressly conceded the facts constituting willful and malicious injury — which is qualitatively different from a default judgment or discovery sanction in which no concession was made. Note that *Berlingeri* was abrogated by *In re Ruitenberg*, 745 F.3d 647 (3d Cir. 2014), but only on the equitable distribution claim issue — the *Ruitenberg* court did not address § 523(a)(6) at all. *Ruitenberg* does not change *Berlingeri*'s § 523(a)(6) analysis. ### 5.3.B: The Curtis/Sonnax Twelve-Factor Analysis — All Twelve Factors Favor Brad

Courts in the Third Circuit evaluate § 362(d)(1) "cause" through the twelve-factor *Curtis*/*Sonnax* test, as adopted in this district. *In re Mid-Atlantic Handling Systems, LLC*, 304 B.R. 111, 130 (Bankr. D.N.J. 2003). All twelve factors are addressed below.

**Factor 1 — Whether Relief Would Result in a Partial or Complete Resolution of the Issues.**

Lifting the stay cannot result in any resolution of any issue because there is no pending state court action. The Zunic order dismissed B.B. v. Mell, ESX-L-007200-19, without prejudice on June 12, 2026. A dismissed action is not pending. Lifting the stay to allow enforcement of a dismissed action does not resolve anything — it requires B.B. to refile the dismissed action (potentially with new process requirements, new jurisdictional basis, and new pleading challenges), then proceed through pre-trial stages, then try the case, then obtain a judgment, then return to this Court for the § 523(a)(6) dischargeability determination that is a core proceeding within this Court's exclusive jurisdiction regardless. Factor 1 weighs heavily against stay relief.

**Factor 2 — Lack of Connection Between the Bankruptcy Case and the Stayed Proceeding.**

The state court action is directly connected to this bankruptcy case — the civil judgment sought is the basis for the potential § 523(a)(6) nondischargeability claim. If anything, the connection between the proceedings militates in favor of maintaining the stay and resolving both the liability and dischargeability questions in a single forum. Factor 2 is neutral at most.

**Factor 3 — Whether the Debt Would Be Dischargeable in the Bankruptcy Case.**

This factor is genuinely contested — which is precisely why it belongs in an adversary proceeding in this Court under Rule 7001(6), not in a state court damages trial. The dischargeability question is squarely within this Court's exclusive jurisdiction under 28 U.S.C. § 157(b)(2)(I). Factor 3 is not resolved and therefore does not support lifting the stay to allow state court proceedings that cannot determine dischargeability in any event.

**Factor 4 — Whether the Debtor's Estate Could Be Irreparably Damaged.**

Lifting the stay to allow enforcement of the Paragano-asserted prejudgment attachment would immediately expose $750,000 in Brad's estate funds to collection proceedings in a forum that (a) has dismissed the underlying action, and (b) has no jurisdiction to determine dischargeability. The estate would be irreparably harmed by depletion of its primary non-exempt asset pool before the trustee can administer the estate in an orderly fashion. Factor 4 weighs against stay relief.

**Factor 5 — Whether the Debtor Has Personal Liability.**

Brad does have personal liability if the civil case is adjudicated in B.B.'s favor — but the magnitude of that liability is unknown, contingent, unliquidated, and disputed. It has not been established by any final judgment. The interlocutory PSJ is not a final judgment (Appellate Division Fn. 2). Factor 5 is neutral; it does not affirmatively support stay relief when liability itself remains undetermined.

**Factor 6 — Whether a Non-Debtor Co-Defendant Exists.**

The civil action named multiple defendants, including entities and other parties. This factor is complex factually; however, the fact that the action named non-debtor defendants supports maintaining the stay as to Brad's personal liability while allowing any separate proceedings against non-debtor parties to continue. Factor 6 is neutral to slightly favoring maintenance of the stay.

**Factor 7 — Judicial Economy and Prejudice to Other Creditors.**

This factor decisively favors maintaining the stay. The IRS ($300,000 § 507(a)(8) priority claim) and Kimberly Mell ($200,000 DSO, § 507(a)(1) first priority claim) are statutory senior creditors whose claims are nondischargeable under §§ 523(a)(1) and 523(a)(5) respectively. If the stay is lifted and Nagel executes on the attachment before the trustee administers the estate, the $750,000 in the Nagel Rice trust account flows to Nagel's client rather than through the § 726 distribution waterfall. The IRS and Kimberly Mell — whose § 507 claims are first and eighth priority respectively, far ahead of Nagel's client — receive zero in that scenario. The automatic stay exists in part to prevent exactly this race-to-the-courthouse outcome where the first creditor to execute gets paid at the expense of all others. Lifting the stay here would harm the IRS, the domestic support creditor, and every other unsecured creditor in order to benefit a single creditor whose claim has not been reduced to a final judgment. Factor 7 strongly favors maintaining the stay.

**Factor 8 — Whether Stay Constitutes "Cause" Independent of the Bad Faith Theory.**

There is no independent "cause" beyond the bad faith theory already rejected. Factor 8 favors the debtor.

**Factor 9 — Feasibility of Reorganization.**

This is Chapter 7, not a reorganization chapter, so this factor has limited direct applicability. The equivalent inquiry — whether the Chapter 7 case has a legitimate purpose — is answered yes: Brad has twelve creditors, $1,830,000 in liabilities, a negative monthly cash flow, and no realistic ability to satisfy his debts outside of bankruptcy. Chapter 7's fresh start purpose is directly served by this case. Factor 9 favors maintaining the stay.

**Factor 10 — Balance of Harms.**

Brad faces multi-forum litigation at a time when his only income is $3,500/month in discretionary support from his brother. His living expenses exceed his income by $1,052/month. His attorney's fees were paid by his brother. He has no independent resources to fund parallel state court and federal bankruptcy court litigation simultaneously. The harm to Brad from lifting the stay is immediate, concrete, and financially ruinous given his situation.

Nagel's only cognizable harm from maintaining the stay is delay of a damages proceeding that — even if concluded in B.B.'s favor — cannot result in collection without first returning to this Court for the § 523(a)(6) dischargeability determination. The state court damages trial, if it occurred, would produce a judgment. That judgment would then need to be evaluated in a § 523(a)(6) adversary proceeding in this Court before any collection could occur. The stay produces delay; it does not eliminate B.B.'s potential recovery. The balance of harms decisively favors Brad. Factor 10 favors maintaining the stay.

**Factor 11 — Whether the Parties Are Ready for Trial in the Other Proceeding.**

This factor is potentially dispositive on these facts. The state court action was dismissed without prejudice by the Zunic order on June 12, 2026. The parties are not ready for trial in a dismissed action. They are not ready for trial in any proceeding. There is no "other proceeding" in which the parties' trial readiness could be assessed. Factor 11 analysis assumes a pending alternative proceeding; here, there is none. This factor weighs decisively against stay relief.

**Factor 12 — Whether the Underlying Action Has Public Policy Implications.**

Sexual abuse of a minor has obvious and serious public policy implications. However, public policy does not override the orderly administration of bankruptcy law or the priority scheme established by Congress. The public policy interest in redressing B.B.'s alleged injuries is addressed by the § 523(a)(6) process — which allows the bankruptcy court to find the debt nondischargeable upon proof of willful and malicious injury in a properly initiated adversary proceeding. Public policy is not served by disrupting the statutory distribution scheme or by allowing a single creditor to execute on estate assets before other priority creditors are paid. Factor 12 is equivocal at best and does not affirmatively support stay relief.

**Summary of Curtis/Sonnax Analysis:** Factors 1, 4, 7, 9, 10, and 11 affirmatively favor maintaining the stay. Factors 3, 5, 6, 8, and 12 are neutral or equivocal. No factor affirmatively supports lifting the stay when the underlying action has been dismissed and the § 523(a)(6) determination remains properly within this Court's exclusive jurisdiction.

## SECTION 5.4: ABSTENTION UNDER 28 U.S.C. § 1334 IS LEGALLY UNAVAILABLE

### 5.4.A: Threshold Defect — The Cited Statute Does Not Exist

Nagel's motion seeks relief under "28 U.S.C. § 1134(c)(1)" (permissive abstention) and "28 U.S.C. § 1134(c)(2)" (mandatory abstention). (Nagel-mtd-analysis.md §§ 6, 8.) Title 28, Chapter 85 (District Courts; Jurisdiction), contains sections numbered 1330 through 1369. There is no § 1134 in Title 28. No such statute exists.

The abstention provisions governing bankruptcy jurisdiction are found at 28 U.S.C. § 1334(c)(1) (permissive abstention: "Each district court may abstain from hearing a particular proceeding . . . in the interest of comity with State courts") and § 1334(c)(2) (mandatory abstention). This error appears twice in Nagel's motion and infects the entire abstention section. The opposition should note this error in a single sentence: "Nagel's abstention arguments cite a statute — 28 U.S.C. § 1134 — that does not exist; this Court will treat the motion as invoking the correct statute, 28 U.S.C. § 1334." Then move immediately to the merits. One sentence is enough. Judge O'Hagan, with eleven years as USAO Senior Bankruptcy Counsel, will have recognized the error on first read.

### 5.4.B: Mandatory Abstention Is Categorically Unavailable for § 523(a)(6) — The Statute's Text Is Dispositive

Section 1334(c)(2) provides that in a proceeding "based upon a State law claim or State law cause of action, related to a case under title 11 but not arising under title 11 or arising in a case under title 11," the district court (and by extension the bankruptcy court) shall abstain if the proceeding can be timely adjudicated in state court. The mandatory abstention statute, by its express terms, applies only to proceedings that are merely "related to" a case under title 11 — not to proceedings that "arise under" title 11 or "arise in" a case under title 11.

A § 523(a)(6) nondischargeability determination arises directly under 11 U.S.C. § 523 — a provision of the United States Bankruptcy Code. It arises under title 11, not under state law. The statute's text is self-applying: if the proceeding arises under title 11, the mandatory abstention provision by its own terms does not apply. No analysis of the five *Stoe* factors is needed at all — mandatory abstention is categorically unavailable for § 523(a)(6) proceedings as a matter of statutory text.

This is confirmed by 28 U.S.C. § 157(b)(2)(I), which expressly identifies "determinations as to the dischargeability of particular debts" as core proceedings. Core proceedings are, by definition, those that "arise under" title 11 or "arise in" a case under title 11 — not merely "related to" a case under title 11. A proceeding that is a core proceeding under § 157(b)(2)(I) cannot simultaneously be only "related to" a case under title 11. The categories are mutually exclusive. *See Halper v. Halper*, 164 F.3d 830, 836–37 (3d Cir. 1999) (§ 523 dischargeability proceedings are core; bankruptcy court retains exclusive jurisdiction). Mandatory abstention requires that the proceeding be merely "related to" a bankruptcy case and based on a state law claim. A § 523(a)(6) proceeding fails both conditions.

Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 4007's advisory committee note confirms the exclusive nature of the bankruptcy court's § 523(a)(6) jurisdiction: "A proceeding to determine the dischargeability of a debt . . . is governed by the Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure." The Rules themselves contemplate that § 523(a)(6) is within the exclusive province of the bankruptcy court. Exclusive jurisdiction cannot be divested by an abstention motion. This point is dispositive.

### 5.4.C: Stoe v. Flaherty — Nagel's Own Cited Case Defeats Him on Multiple Elements

Even setting aside the statutory text argument, Nagel's mandatory abstention claim fails under the controlling five-factor test from *Stoe v. Flaherty*, 436 F.3d 209, 213 (3d Cir. 2006) — the very case Nagel cites as support. All five elements of *Stoe* must be satisfied for mandatory abstention to be required. At minimum, elements (2), (3), and (4) fail independently.

**Stoe Element 1 — A timely motion was made.**

This element may be satisfied — the motion was filed relatively early in the case. The opposition need not contest element (1); the remaining failing elements are dispositive.

**Stoe Element 2 — The claim must not arise under title 11 and must not arise in a case under title 11.**

A § 523(a)(6) nondischargeability determination arises directly under 11 U.S.C. § 523(a)(6), a provision of title 11. This element is not satisfied. Nagel's entire abstention theory depends on treating the § 523(a)(6) claim as if it were a state law tort claim merely "related to" the bankruptcy. It is not. Element (2) fails. This failure is independently dispositive — all five elements must be satisfied.

**Stoe Element 3 — No basis for federal jurisdiction except § 1334.**

Section 157(b)(2)(I) gives the bankruptcy court exclusive jurisdiction over dischargeability determinations independent of § 1334. The bankruptcy court's authority to determine dischargeability arises from the Bankruptcy Code itself and from the grant of core proceeding jurisdiction under § 157(b)(2)(I) — not solely from the general grant of bankruptcy jurisdiction in § 1334. This element fails because federal jurisdiction over § 523(a)(6) exists independently of § 1334.

**Stoe Element 4 — An action is commenced in state court.**

The Zunic order dismissed B.B. v. Mell on June 12, 2026 — the same day Brad filed his petition and eleven days before Nagel filed this motion. There is no pending state court action as of the motion's filing date. There is no pending state court action as of the July 16 hearing date. *Stoe* requires that "an action is commenced" in state court of appropriate jurisdiction. Courts interpret this to require a currently pending proceeding — not one that must be refiled and recommenced. *See Stoe*, 436 F.3d at 213 (element requires state court action already pending). A dismissed action that would need to be refiled, re-served, and re-litigated is not "commenced" in state court. Element (4) fails.

**Stoe Element 5 — State court action can be timely adjudicated.**

Even if the other elements were satisfied (they are not), the state court cannot timely adjudicate B.B.'s claims when the action has been dismissed and would need to be refiled — potentially facing new challenges on statute of limitations, tolling, and the impact of the bankruptcy stay on any refiled proceeding. Element (5) is doubtful but need not be resolved given that elements (2), (3), and (4) each independently defeat mandatory abstention.

**Three of five *Stoe* elements fail independently. All five must be satisfied. Mandatory abstention is unavailable.**

### 5.4.D: Permissive Abstention — The Court Should Decline to Exercise Discretion Here

Even if the Court has theoretical discretion to abstain permissively under § 1334(c)(1), the exercise of that discretion is inappropriate here for three reasons.

First, permissive abstention in the face of a § 523(a)(6) claim would require this Court to decline a core proceeding within its exclusive jurisdiction and invite state court adjudication of a question Congress has assigned to the bankruptcy court. While § 1334(c)(1) permits some discretion, courts do not permissively abstain from core proceedings when the core proceeding is Congress's designated forum. The § 523(a)(6) determination is not a matter the bankruptcy court can hand off to state court because the state court has no authority to bind the parties' bankruptcy rights.

Second, permissive abstention requires consideration of efficiency and comity. There is nothing efficient about sending B.B.'s claims to state court when: (a) the state court action has been dismissed; (b) any state court judgment would need to return to this Court for the § 523(a)(6) dischargeability determination in any event; (c) Nagel filed the same-package request for a § 523(a)(6) declaration from this Court simultaneously with his request for abstention, making the internal contradiction manifest. Abstention creates a two-court, two-proceeding structure for a question this Court has exclusive authority to resolve in a single adversary proceeding.

Third, the logical contradiction between the abstention request and the § 523(a)(6) request demonstrates that Nagel has not thought through what he actually wants from this Court. He asks the Court simultaneously to: (a) abstain from adjudicating the factual circumstances of the injury — which requires sending those facts to state court; and (b) declare the resulting debt nondischargeable — which requires this Court to find "willful and malicious injury" using those same facts. A court cannot abstain from the predicate while retaining the conclusion. The internal incoherence of the request weighs against discretionary abstention.

## SECTION 5.5: § 362(k) CROSS-MOTION RECOMMENDATION — THE PARAGANO EMAIL AS SWORD

### 5.5.A: The Evidence Supporting a Willful Stay Violation

The opposition to Nagel's motion should be accompanied by — or closely followed by — a cross-motion for damages under 11 U.S.C. § 362(k)(1) based on Michael Paragano's June 18, 2026 email. The evidence establishing the elements of a willful stay violation is compelling, and the cross-motion transforms the litigation dynamic: the party seeking relief from the stay is now the respondent on a stay violation claim.

The factual predicate is not disputed:

(1) Brad filed his Chapter 7 petition on June 12, 2026, at approximately 3:28 PM, as reflected in the bankruptcy filing notice served on the state court docket on June 12, 2026.

(2) The automatic stay under 11 U.S.C. § 362(a) arose the moment the petition was filed, without any court order or additional action required.

(3) Nagel appeared before Judge O'Hagan and acknowledged the bankruptcy filing on or before June 15, 2026 — the date the Court entered the OSC in this proceeding.

(4) On June 18, 2026 — three days after Nagel acknowledged the bankruptcy to this Court — Nagel's law partner Michael Paragano emailed Brad directly asserting the prejudgment attachment from the dismissed state court action.

The Paragano email implicates at minimum four provisions of § 362(a): § 362(a)(1) (the act of continuing a judicial proceeding against the debtor — assertion of attachment rights derived from the dismissed civil action constitutes continuation of the judicial proceeding's coercive effects); § 362(a)(4) (any act to enforce a lien against property of the estate — the prejudgment attachment is a lien on property of the estate); § 362(a)(5) (any act to enforce against property of the estate a lien securing a pre-petition claim); and § 362(a)(6) (any act to collect a pre-petition debt). Each provision is independently violated by the Paragano email.

### 5.5.B: The Lansaw Willfulness Standard — Knowledge Plus Intentional Act

The Third Circuit established the § 362(k)(1) willfulness standard in *In re Lansaw*, 853 F.3d 657, 668–69 (3d Cir. 2017). Under *Lansaw*, a violation is "willful" when: (1) the violator had actual knowledge of the bankruptcy filing; and (2) the violator committed an intentional act. *Id.* Knowledge of the specific provisions of the automatic stay is not required — knowledge of the bankruptcy filing alone is sufficient. *In re University Medical Center*, 973 F.2d 1065, 1068 (3d Cir. 1992) (for purposes of § 362(k)(1) willfulness, debtor need only prove that the creditor knew of the bankruptcy filing, not that the creditor specifically knew which acts the stay prohibited).

Applied here: Nagel had actual knowledge. He appeared before this Court on June 15, 2026, acknowledging the bankruptcy. Paragano is Nagel's law partner at Nagel Rice LLP — the same firm. Under agency imputation principles, a partner's knowledge is imputed to other partners under the Restatement (Third) of Agency § 5.03 (knowledge of an agent acting within the scope of the agency is imputed to the principal). Nagel's knowledge of the bankruptcy is imputed to Paragano as his law partner. The June 18 email was an intentional act — Paragano deliberately composed and sent the email asserting the attachment. There is no colorable argument that the email was accidental or inadvertent.

Critically, good faith is not a defense to § 362(k)(1) willfulness. *Cal. Coast Univ. v. Aleckna (In re Aleckna)*, No. 20-1309, 2021 WL 4097155, at *4 (3d Cir. Sept. 9, 2021) ("[G]ood faith is not a defense to a willful violation of the automatic stay."). Even if Paragano believed — incorrectly — that the attachment remained valid after the Zunic dismissal, his good faith belief does not negate the willfulness element. He knew of the bankruptcy. He intentionally sent the email asserting the attachment. The willfulness element is satisfied.

### 5.5.C: Damages Available Under § 362(k)(1)

Section 362(k)(1) provides: "an individual injured by any willful violation of a stay provided by this section shall recover actual damages, including costs and attorneys' fees, and, in appropriate circumstances, may recover punitive damages." The *Lansaw* court affirmed the following categories of recovery:

*Actual damages — attorneys' fees.* Brad's attorneys' fees incurred in opposing the Paragano attachment assertion, opposing this motion, and preparing the cross-motion are all recoverable as "actual damages" under § 362(k)(1). The causal connection is direct: but for the Paragano email and the filing of this motion by the parties responsible for the violation, Brad would not have incurred these costs. *In re Lansaw*, 853 F.3d at 668–69.

*Emotional distress damages.* The *Lansaw* court affirmed emotional distress damages for willful stay violations without requiring medical evidence where the violations were patently egregious and the debtor testified credibly about distress. *Id.* A party who received an email asserting a void lien against his bankruptcy estate three days after his adversary acknowledged the bankruptcy to a federal court has a straightforward basis for emotional distress damages. Whether to pursue this element should be determined in consultation with Brad.

*Punitive damages.* The *Lansaw* court affirmed $40,000 in punitive damages for willful stay violation where the creditor's conduct was egregious. *Id.* Here, the aggravating circumstances are substantial: (1) the violation was committed by a law firm that practices bankruptcy law and is fully sophisticated about the automatic stay; (2) the violation occurred after Nagel personally appeared before this Court and acknowledged the bankruptcy; (3) the attachment being asserted is doubly void — both because the underlying action was dismissed and because it was released in December 2023; and (4) the law firm whose partner violated the stay is simultaneously seeking equitable relief from the same stay in this motion. Punitive damages are warranted.

The attachment Paragano asserted on June 18, 2026 was doubly invalid at the moment of assertion:

First invalidity: The Zunic order dismissed B.B. v. Mell on June 12, 2026, the same day as the bankruptcy petition.
