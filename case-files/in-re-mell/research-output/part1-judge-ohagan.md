## SECTION III: CONTROLLING LAW — § 707(a) BAD FAITH DISMISSAL

*Because no O'Hagan opinions exist, this section sets out the Third Circuit and D.N.J. precedents that will govern his analysis.*

### Threshold Issue: Does § 707(a) Permit Bad-Faith Dismissal?

Yes, in the Third Circuit. The court holds that lack of good faith constitutes "cause" under § 707(a). **This is binding on O'Hagan.**

### Primary Controlling Authorities

---

### In re Tamecki, 229 F.3d 205 (3d Cir. 2000)

**Citation:** In re Tamecki, 229 F.3d 205 (3d Cir. 2000)
**Court:** United States Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit
**Date:** 2000
**Topics:** § 707(a) bad faith dismissal; standard of review; burden of proof

**Holding:** The Third Circuit affirmed dismissal of a Chapter 7 case under § 707(a) for lack of good faith. The court held that bad faith can constitute "cause" under § 707(a). Established the "honest intention" test assessed on an "ad hoc" basis.

**Key Standard:**
> "Dismissal based on lack of good faith should be confined carefully and is generally utilized only in those egregious cases that entail concealed or misrepresented assets and/or sources of income, lavish lifestyles, and intention to avoid a large single debt based upon conduct akin to fraud, misconduct or gross negligence."

**Burden-Shifting:**
> "When a debtor's good faith is put in issue, the burden shifts to him to prove his good faith."

The debtor must demonstrate "honest intention" in filing. The test is ad hoc — courts look at the totality of circumstances rather than any fixed multi-factor test.

**Facts of Tamecki:** Debtor filed Chapter 7 listing a single credit card debt of ~$35,000 as his only liability. His sole substantial asset was a $100,000 equity interest in a marital home held as tenancy by the entirety — properly claimed exempt. The Third Circuit affirmed dismissal.

**Subsequent History:** Cited with approval in In re Perlin, 497 F.3d 364 (3d Cir. 2007).

**Classification:** BINDING on Judge O'Hagan.

**Relevance to In re Mell:** This is the controlling standard O'Hagan will apply to any § 707(a) motion. Note the "egregious" threshold — the standard is stringent. Mere ability to repay is not grounds for dismissal.

---

### In re Perlin, 497 F.3d 364 (3d Cir. 2007)

**Citation:** In re Perlin, 497 F.3d 364 (3d Cir. 2007) (also cited as Perlin v. Hitachi Capital America Corp.)
**Court:** United States Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit
**Date:** 2007
**Topics:** § 707(a) bad faith dismissal; ability to repay as factor; totality of circumstances

**Holding:** The Third Circuit reversed the bankruptcy court's grant of a creditor's motion to dismiss under § 707(a), holding that while a debtor's ability to repay may be considered as part of the good faith analysis, it is not in and of itself sufficient proof of bad faith. No reasonable factfinder would find bad faith on the Perlin record.

**Key Standard:**
> "A debtor's ability to repay is not in and of itself sufficient proof of bad faith."

**Additional Holdings:**
- A bankruptcy court may consider a debtor's substantial income and expenses together with other factors in assessing good faith.
- Bad faith dismissal is "a severe consequence that is appropriate only in egregious cases that entail concealed or misrepresented assets and/or sources of income, lavish lifestyles, and intention to avoid a single debt based upon conduct akin to fraud, misconduct or gross negligence."

**Classification:** BINDING on Judge O'Hagan.

**Relevance to In re Mell:** Critical limitation on § 707(a) motions. Even if Brad (the debtor) has disposable income or could theoretically repay some debts, that alone cannot support dismissal. The movant must show something more — concealment, misrepresentation, lavish spending, or evasion of a specific large debt through fraud-adjacent conduct.

### D.N.J. § 707(a) Standard Summary (for Briefing)

Based on Tamecki, Perlin, and the consensus view applied in D.N.J., O'Hagan will likely evaluate bad faith under § 707(a) using the totality of circumstances, with relevant factors including:

1. Whether the debtor filed to avoid a single large debt
2. Whether the debtor concealed or misrepresented assets or income
3. Whether the debtor maintained a lavish lifestyle inconsistent with the filing
4. Whether the debtor's conduct is akin to fraud, misconduct, or gross negligence
5. Whether the filing was timed to manipulate or delay a particular creditor
6. Whether schedules are complete and accurate
7. The debtor's honest intention in seeking bankruptcy relief

**THRESHOLD IS STRINGENT.** The standard is limited to "egregious cases." Ordinary inability to repay and routine financial distress do not support § 707(a) dismissal.

## SECTION IV: CONTROLLING LAW — § 523(a)(6) WILLFUL AND MALICIOUS INJURY

### Primary Controlling Authorities

---

### Kawaauhau v. Geiger, 523 U.S. 57 (1998)

**Citation:** Kawaauhau v. Geiger, 523 U.S. 57 (1998)
**Court:** U.S. Supreme Court
**Date:** March 3, 1998
**Topics:** § 523(a)(6); "willful and malicious injury" standard; deliberate vs. negligent injury

**Holding:** The Supreme Court held unanimously that the word "willful" in § 523(a)(6) modifies the word "injury" — the debtor must intend to cause the injury itself, not merely intend to do the act that resulted in the injury. Debts from recklessly or negligently inflicted injuries are not excepted from discharge under § 523(a)(6).

**Key Quote:**
> "[T]he word 'willful' in § 523(a)(6) modifies the word 'injury,' indicating that nondischargeability takes a deliberate or intentional injury, not merely a deliberate or intentional act that leads to injury."

**Effect:** Eliminated the possibility that "willful" encompasses recklessness or negligence.

**Classification:** BINDING on Judge O'Hagan as Supreme Court precedent.

**Relevance to In re Mell:** Any § 523(a)(6) claim must prove Brad deliberately intended to injure the plaintiff — not merely that he intended an act that happened to cause injury. This is a high threshold that benefits the debtor.

---

### In re Conte, 33 F.3d 303 (3d Cir. 1994)

**Citation:** In re Conte, 33 F.3d 303 (3d Cir. 1994)
**Court:** United States Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit
**Date:** 1994 (pre-Geiger, but refined by subsequent application)
**Topics:** § 523(a)(6); "willful and malicious" injury; two-part standard

**Holding:** The Third Circuit held that an injury is willful and malicious under § 523(a)(6) only if the actor (1) purposefully inflicted the injury, or (2) acted with substantial certainty that injury would result. Actions that are merely negligent or reckless are insufficient. The "malicious" element requires a wrongful act done intentionally that necessarily causes injury and is done without just cause or excuse — ill will or spite is not required.

**Key Standard (post-Geiger synthesis):**
> "An injury is willful and malicious under the Code only if the actor purposefully inflicted the injury or acted with substantial certainty that injury would result."

**Two-Part Framework:**
- **Willful:** The debtor subjectively intended the injury, or knew with substantial certainty that the injury would result.
- **Malicious:** The wrongful act was intentional, necessarily caused injury, and was done without just cause or excuse. Does not require spite or hatred.

**Classification:** BINDING on Judge O'Hagan as controlling Third Circuit precedent.

**Note on Post-Geiger Interaction:** Conte was decided before Geiger. Post-Geiger, the Third Circuit continues to apply the substantial certainty standard consistent with Geiger's requirement of intentional injury (not merely intentional act). Counsel should verify the most recent Third Circuit application.

**Relevance to In re Mell:** The standard for § 523(a)(6) in this Court is two-pronged (Conte) and requires intentional injury (Geiger). Mere intentional wrongdoing that accidentally causes harm will not suffice. However, the "substantial certainty" prong means that a debtor who knew harm was substantially certain to result cannot escape § 523(a)(6) by denying subjective intent.

### Collateral Estoppel and § 523(a)(6) in D.N.J.

---

### In re Azeglio, 422 B.R. 490 (Bankr. D.N.J. 2010)

**Citation:** In re Azeglio, 422 B.R. 490 (Bankr. D.N.J. 2010)
**Court:** U.S. Bankruptcy Court, D.N.J.
**Topics:** § 523(a)(6); collateral estoppel; "actually litigated" requirement; default judgment preclusion

**Holding:** The D.N.J. court rejected application of collateral estoppel to a prior state court judgment where the issue was not "actually litigated" within the meaning required for preclusion. New Jersey courts agree collateral estoppel may not be applied based on a default judgment. The case also addressed the situation where a defendant participated in pretrial activity but failed to appear at trial, finding collateral estoppel inapplicable.

**Key Standard:**
> "Collateral estoppel applies only when the defendant in the initial action had the opportunity to fully and actively participate in the actual trial, and the manner in which the trial was conducted merited a bar to relitigation of the issues determined."

**Requirements for Collateral Estoppel in D.N.J. Bankruptcy Court:**
1. The issue in the second action is the same as in the prior action
2. The issue was actually litigated in the prior action
3. The prior determination was by valid and final judgment
4. The determination was essential to the prior judgment

**Key Limitation:** If the prior judgment was entered by default (or where defendant did not fully participate in trial), collateral estoppel does not apply — even if there was pretrial participation.

**Additional Limitation:** If the state court action required only proof of negligence or recklessness, the resulting judgment cannot collaterally estop the debtor from contesting willful/malicious intent under § 523(a)(6).

**Classification:** Directly applicable D.N.J. precedent. Persuasive/highly influential on Judge O'Hagan.

**Relevance to In re Mell:** If the creditor is attempting to use a prior state court judgment to establish § 523(a)(6) nondischargeability via collateral estoppel, Azeglio establishes the requirements and limits. Default judgments do not support collateral estoppel. The state court finding must have specifically adjudicated willful and malicious intent (not mere negligence or recklessness) for preclusion to apply.

## SECTION V: CONTROLLING LAW — § 362(d) AUTOMATIC STAY RELIEF

### General Standard

Under 11 U.S.C. § 362(d)(1), a court may terminate, annul, modify, or condition the automatic stay "for cause." "Cause" is not defined in the Bankruptcy Code, and its determination requires a fact-intensive inquiry.

### Factor Test Applied in D.N.J.

D.N.J. courts apply a multi-factor test in determining whether "cause" exists for stay relief, including elements of the "Sonnax factors" (from In re Sonnax Indus., 907 F.2d 1280 (2d Cir. 1990)), though the specific factors may vary by judge and type of proceeding. Common factors applied in the D.N.J. include:

1. Whether relief would result in partial or complete resolution of the issues
2. Lack of any connection with or interference with the bankruptcy case
3. Whether the other proceeding involves the debtor as a fiduciary
4. Whether a specialized tribunal with necessary expertise has been established
5. Whether the debtor's insurer has assumed full responsibility for defense
6. Whether the action primarily involves third parties
7. Whether litigation in another forum would prejudice the interests of other parties
8. The interests of judicial economy
9. The debtor's ability to reorganize or propose a plan
10. Impact on the debtor's estate

## SECTION VI: CONTROLLING LAW — 28 U.S.C. § 1334(c) ABSTENTION

### Mandatory Abstention (§ 1334(c)(2))

Under 28 U.S.C. § 1334(c)(2), a federal court must abstain from hearing a proceeding if ALL of the following five elements are met:

1. A timely motion to abstain is filed
2. The proceeding is based upon a state law claim or state law cause of action
3. The proceeding is "related to" a case under Title 11, but does NOT "arise in" Title 11 or "arise under" Title 11
4. The action could not have been commenced in a federal court absent § 1334 jurisdiction
5. An action is commenced (or can be) and can be timely adjudicated in a state forum of appropriate jurisdiction

**CRITICAL RULE: Mandatory abstention is inapplicable to "core" proceedings.** If the proceeding arises in a Title 11 case or arises under Title 11 (i.e., is a core proceeding under 28 U.S.C. § 157), mandatory abstention under § 1334(c)(2) cannot apply as a matter of law. This is the consensus rule, and D.N.J. courts have applied it.

**Source:** Multiple circuits including courts citing D.N.J. precedent confirm this rule. See also D.N.J. district court opinion at USCOURTS-njd-3_15-cv-05726 discussing mandatory abstention standards.

### Permissive Abstention (§ 1334(c)(1))

Under § 1334(c)(1), a court may permissively abstain from any proceeding arising under Title 11, or arising in or related to a Title 11 case, "in the interest of justice, or in the interest of comity with State courts or respect for State law."

**Factor Test for Permissive Abstention (applied in D.N.J.):**
1. Effect on efficient administration of the bankruptcy estate
2. Extent to which issues of state law predominate
3. Difficulty or unsettled nature of applicable state law
4. Comity with state courts
5. Degree of relatedness or remoteness to the main bankruptcy case
6. Existence of the right to a jury trial
7. Prejudice to involuntarily removed defendants
8. Whether this is a non-core, related-to proceeding

**D.N.J. Application:** In Case No. 23-AP-01298 (Bankr. D.N.J. Feb. 2024) (Judge Altenburg), the court granted permissive abstention and dismissed an adversary proceeding without leave to amend, finding that "there was absolutely no effect on the administration of the estate." This illustrates D.N.J. courts' willingness to permissively abstain where the bankruptcy nexus is attenuated.

**Relevance to In re Mell:** If the adversary proceeding involves a state law tort or contract claim with only incidental bankruptcy nexus, and a parallel state court action exists or could be commenced, permissive abstention under § 1334(c)(1) is a viable argument. However, if the claim is a core proceeding (e.g., a § 523 nondischargeability action or § 727 discharge objection), neither mandatory nor permissive abstention will succeed.

### Governing Standards in D.N.J.

Under Fed. R. Bankr. P. 9011 (bankruptcy's equivalent of Fed. R. Civ. P. 11), an attorney presenting any petition, pleading, written motion, or other paper certifies that:

## SECTION VIII: EFFECT OF STATE COURT DISMISSAL ON BANKRUPTCY JURISDICTION

### Research Finding

No O'Hagan opinions were found on this topic. No specific D.N.J. published opinions on the precise question of a state court action's dismissal for the pendency of bankruptcy proceedings, and the effect of that dismissal on bankruptcy court jurisdiction, were located in open sources.

### General Framework

Where a state court dismisses an action on account of the debtor's bankruptcy filing, the bankruptcy court retains exclusive jurisdiction over the affected claims for the duration of the bankruptcy case and for matters arising under Title 11. The state court dismissal does not divest the bankruptcy court of jurisdiction; rather, it confirms the bankruptcy court as the proper forum.

The relevant questions are:
1. Whether the dismissed state court action can be revived after discharge/plan confirmation
2. Whether the bankruptcy court should exercise jurisdiction or equitably abstain
3. Whether claims were preserved during the bankruptcy stay

Counsel should identify: (1) the precise state court action that was dismissed; (2) the procedural history; and (3) whether the claims were re-filed in the adversary proceeding.
