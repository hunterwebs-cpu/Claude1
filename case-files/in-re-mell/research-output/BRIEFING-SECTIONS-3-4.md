If the case is dismissed, there is no discharge, so a nondischargeability determination is moot. If the case is retained for a nondischargeability determination, dismissal has been denied. Nagel has written the motion as a kitchen-sink document that throws everything at the wall without disciplining the internal logic. That incoherence is an opportunity for the opposition.

What Nagel does not mention is that the majority of the delay between the filing of suit in October 2019 and the trial date of June 2026 was caused by his own interlocutory appeal. The Appellate Division issued its decision on December 31, 2024 — more than two years after argument, more than five years after the suit was filed. The Appellate Division's ruling was largely a victory for the trial court: the deposition scope ruling was correct, but the age-16 cutoff in the protective order was somewhat too narrow. The gap between the Appellate Division's decision (December 2024) and the trial date (June 2026) is approximately eighteen months.

Nagel created the "eve" he now invokes. A court applying the totality-of-circumstances standard under In re Tamecki, 229 F.3d 205, 207 (3d Cir. 2000), is entitled to consider which party caused the scheduling dynamics that preceded the filing.

If Mell did not respond, the partial summary judgment may have been entered under N.J. Court Rule 4:23-5(a)(2) as a discovery sanction — which involves no evidentiary hearing, no testimony, and no merits finding on any element of any claim. Third, the partial summary judgment covered only Counts I, II, III, VII, and VIII of the First Amended Complaint, not the operative Second Amended Complaint. These are not just evidentiary points for the § 523(a)(6) argument. They go to the good faith of the motion itself. The motion's foundational factual premise — that liability has been fully and finally adjudicated — is not supported by the Appellate Division's own characterization of the record.

### 3.C: Nagel's § 707(a) Argument — Analysis and Assessment

Nagel's § 707(a) argument is the centerpiece of the motion and the one that raises the most genuine difficulty. Counsel should treat it seriously. It is not a frivolous argument. It fails, but it requires a complete and disciplined response.

**The Standard Nagel Invokes**

Nagel correctly states the Tamecki standard: "[G]ood faith requires at least a showing of honest intention." In re Hunt, 521 B.R. at 485. He correctly invokes the burden-shifting framework: "Once a party calls into question a petitioner's good faith, the burden shifts to the petitioner to prove his good faith." In re Tamecki, 229 F.3d at 207. He correctly recites the Glunk five-factor test. (Nagel-mtd-analysis.md § 2.) None of this is wrong. The problem is how Nagel applies these standards to Brad's facts — and what he omits.

**The Murray Inversion — Nagel Cites an Adverse Case**

Nagel cites In re Murray for the proposition that dismissal may result from "circumstances falling short of bad faith but nevertheless representing an inappropriate use of the Code." (Nagel-mtd-analysis.md § 2, citing Murray, 543 B.R. at 490.) But Murray is an *involuntary* bankruptcy petition case. Wilk Auslander LLP, Murray's former employer, filed an involuntary Chapter 7 petition against Murray after obtaining a judgment. The bad faith in Murray was the *creditor's* bad faith in weaponizing the involuntary process as a "judgment enforcement device" in a two-party dispute. The Second Circuit affirmed dismissal because "bankruptcy is not a judgment enforcement device." 900 F.3d 53 (2d Cir. 2018). (Part2-707a.md § B.4.)

This case does not support Nagel's position. It is a bludgeon he has accidentally aimed at himself. If "bankruptcy is not a judgment enforcement device," then § 707(a) is not a mechanism by which Nagel can undo Brad's voluntary bankruptcy filing simply because he wants his damages trial without the constraints of the automatic stay and discharge. Nagel's motion to dismiss is closer to the judgment-enforcement conduct Murray condemned than to anything on Brad's side of this motion. Any experienced bankruptcy practitioner — and Judge O'Hagan is an experienced bankruptcy practitioner — will recognize this immediately. The opposition brief should open the § 707(a) argument by pointing this out.

**The Pari Passu Problem — Nagel's Most Dangerous Factual Argument**

The most substantive argument in Nagel's § 707(a) brief is the implicit "pari passu destruction" argument: Brad filed bankruptcy so that B.B. would have to compete with family members, business entities, and insiders who are also listed as creditors, thereby diluting her pro rata share of any distribution. Nagel phrases it this way: "Most of Mell's creditors are family members or entities he controls (or controlled). B.B.'s case, in contrast, is about Mell's sexual abuse of a minor... Making B.B. compete with other creditors to see her judgment from the State Court Action satisfied will severely hamper her ability to collect from Mell." (Nagel-mtd-analysis.md § 7.)

This argument has intuitive appeal. We need to address it directly, not sidestep it. The response has two parts. First, the argument inverts the bankruptcy process. The pari passu distribution rule — that unsecured creditors share equally in available assets — is not an injustice the bankruptcy court is asked to remedy. It is the fundamental design of the Code. Congress created the priority scheme in 11 U.S.C. § 507 specifically to determine who gets paid in what order. Domestic support obligations come first (§ 507(a)(1)); tax claims come eighth (§ 507(a)(8)); general unsecured creditors share the remainder. Nagel's complaint that B.B. will have to compete with other creditors is a complaint against the Bankruptcy Code itself. Second, many of Brad's listed creditors — the IRS ($300,000), Kimberly Mell ($200,000 DSO), and the New Jersey Division of Taxation ($75,000) — hold claims that are senior to B.B.'s in priority and that are nondischargeable regardless of the outcome here. The Code's priority scheme does not disfavor B.B. because of anything Brad did; it reflects Congress's judgment about which obligations take precedence in an orderly distribution.

Nagel's argument also relies on the premise that the listed creditors are entities Brad "controls" and are therefore somehow not legitimate. This is factually contested. Courtney Mell, Brad's brother, holds a $200,000 personal loan claim and is the sole source of Brad's current income. Diana Mell holds a $75,000 loan claim. These are real obligations to real people. Retired Judge Glenn Berman holds a $60,000 claim through Greenbaum Rowe. These are not shams. And the KRM Trust and SBM Trust creditors are listed as contingent/unliquidated/disputed — which is the proper way to schedule them given the March 2026 settlement's release language.

**The § 523(a)(6) Procedural Error — Motion, Not Adversary Proceeding**

Buried within Nagel's § 707(a) argument is a procedural defect that the opposition should flag cleanly but without belaboring. Nagel seeks a declaration of nondischargeability under § 523(a)(6) by motion — as part of the package of relief in Doc. 8. Under Fed. R. Bankr. P. 7001(6), a determination of the dischargeability of a debt requires an adversary proceeding, not a motion filed in the main case. No adversary proceeding has been filed. The motion is procedurally defective as to the § 523(a)(6) relief. (Nagel-mtd-analysis.md, Counsel Note 6.) This point should be made but not treated as a dispositive win — Nagel could theoretically file an adversary complaint before the July 16 hearing, and the substantive analysis of the § 523(a)(6) claim must be addressed regardless.

**The § 1134 Citation Error**

Under the abstention argument (which we treat at 3.F below), Nagel twice cites "28 U.S.C. § 1134(c)(2)" and "28 U.S.C. § 1134(c)(1)." This statute does not exist. The correct citation is § 1334. (Part4-abstention.md, Threshold Alert.) We mention it in this section as well because citation errors of this kind — to a nonexistent provision — are meaningful signals about the overall reliability of the legal research underlying the motion.

### 3.D: Nagel's § 523(a)(6) Argument — Strengths and Weaknesses

Nagel's § 523(a)(6) argument reads as follows in the brief: sexual abuse of a minor is "by definition" willful and malicious; the partial summary judgment establishes all elements of liability via collateral estoppel; therefore the entire debt is nondischargeable. This three-step argument has at least three independent fatal defects at the threshold before any court reaches the merits.

**Defect One: Procedural — No Adversary Proceeding**

As noted above, Fed. R. Bankr. P. 7001(6) requires an adversary proceeding for determinations of dischargeability under § 523(a)(6). Fed. R. Bankr. P. 4007 notes that the bankruptcy court has exclusive jurisdiction to determine dischargeability under § 523(a)(2), (4), and (6), and that adversary proceeding rules apply. Nagel has not filed an adversary complaint. He has sought § 523(a)(6) relief by motion in the main case. This is procedurally improper.

Note, however, that the deadline for filing a complaint objecting to discharge or seeking nondischargeability under § 523(a)(6) runs from 60 days after the first date set for the meeting of creditors under § 341. Counsel should track this deadline carefully and monitor whether Nagel files a proper adversary complaint before the deadline. If Nagel files an adversary complaint before July 16 (or between July 16 and the deadline), the procedural defect is cured and the merits become live. The deadline should be calculated and placed on the opposition team's calendar immediately.

**Defect Two: The Geiger Standard — "Self-Evident" Inference Is Foreclosed**

The centerpiece of Nagel's § 523(a)(6) argument is his extended quotation from In re Fleetwood (Bankr. D. Alaska 2013), which holds that willfulness and malice are "self-evident" in child sexual abuse cases. (Nagel-mtd-analysis.md § 3.) The quote asserts that "where the damages arise from sexual abuse, willfulness is apparent because the subjective motive to inflict harm 'is self-evident, either because the tortfeasor knows his conduct is certain or almost certain to cause harm, or because he should know and therefore the intent is inferred as a matter of law.'" Id.

This argument is directly foreclosed by the Supreme Court in Kawaauhau v. Geiger, 523 U.S. 57, 61 (1998), which held that "the word 'willful' in (a)(6) modifies the word 'injury,' indicating that nondischargeability takes a deliberate or intentional injury, not merely a deliberate or intentional act that leads to injury." The Court specifically rejected inference from the nature of the act: "Had Congress meant to exempt debts resulting from unintentionally inflicted injuries, it might have described instead 'willful acts that cause injury.'" 523 U.S. at 63. The "self-evident" approach Fleetwood endorses is an objective inference test — inferring intent to injure from the nature of the act. Geiger expressly rejected that approach. (Part3-523a6.md § A.1.)

Additionally, Fleetwood is a Bankr. D. Alaska opinion — not Third Circuit, not Supreme Court. It is not binding in this Court. The Third Circuit's own § 523(a)(6) standard, articulated in In re Conte, 33 F.3d 303, 305, 308 (3d Cir. 1994) — as reconciled with Geiger — requires that the debtor either purposefully inflicted the injury or acted with subjective knowledge that injury was substantially certain to result. The "substantially certain" prong is subjective under Geiger: what did this debtor actually know, not what a reasonable person would have known. (Part3-523a6.md § A.2.)

Nagel also cites Berlingeri (Bankr. D.N.J. 2000) and characterizes it as holding that claims based on intentional acts done to cause injury are nondischargeable. (Nagel-mtd-analysis.md § 3.) Berlingeri is a D.N.J. bankruptcy court opinion — persuasive but not binding — and the relevant passage is about "intentional acts done to cause injury," which is the standard that Geiger itself adopted. The question is not whether the standard exists but whether it is satisfied. Based on available research, Berlingeri's § 523(a)(6) finding appears to rest on a consent judgment stipulation in that case that conceded willfulness — not on inference from the nature of the conduct. If that is correct, Berlingeri is distinguishable: there is no consent judgment here, and the discovery sanction that constitutes the "liability" determination made no mental state finding. (Part3-523a6.md § B.3; CASES-OBTAINED.md, Berlingeri analysis.) Counsel must obtain the full text of Berlingeri before filing the opposition.

**Defect Three: The "Actually Litigated" Requirement for Collateral Estoppel**

Even if the § 523(a)(6) subjective intent standard could theoretically be satisfied on the underlying facts, Nagel cannot establish it through collateral estoppel from the March 2021 partial summary judgment. The Third Circuit requires that for collateral estoppel to operate in a § 523(a)(6) proceeding, the specific § 523(a)(6) mental state elements — willful intent to cause the specific injury and malice — must have been "actually litigated and determined" in the prior proceeding. In re Docteroff, 133 F.3d 210, 214-15 (3d Cir. 1997); In re Wilson, 116 F.3d 87, 90-92 (3d Cir. 1997).

The March 2021 partial summary judgment was not a merits determination. The Appellate Division described it as "interlocutory and not a final judgment" and noted uncertainty about whether Mell even responded to the summary judgment motion. (Part3-523a6.md § E.2.) N.J. Court Rule 4:23-5(a)(2) — the sanction rule under which the PSJ may have been entered — provides that pleadings are struck and a default judgment entered as a discovery sanction upon proof of non-compliance. It involves no evidentiary hearing, no testimony, and no findings of fact on any element of any claim. No judge ever made a finding that Brad acted willfully or maliciously within the meaning of § 523(a)(6). A discovery sanction that deems well-pleaded allegations admitted for civil liability purposes does not satisfy the "actually litigated" requirement for any specific element.

Nagel will cite Docteroff for the "deliberately prevents resolution" exception — the rule that a party who bad-faith obstructs a proceeding will be deemed to have actually litigated the issues. The response: Brad invoked his Fifth Amendment right against self-incrimination during civil discovery while parallel federal criminal proceedings were pending against him. The Fifth Amendment is a constitutionally protected right. Exercising a constitutional right is not "deliberate prevention of resolution" in the Docteroff sense. Docteroff's exception requires conduct that is sanctionable because it is unjustified — bad-faith obstruction with no legal basis. Brad's invocation of the Fifth had the strongest possible legal basis: the United States Constitution. Moreover, even under Docteroff's exception, collateral estoppel can only preclude relitigation of issues that were actually established in the prior proceeding. The R. 4:23-5(a)(2) sanction established procedural default, not the specific § 523(a)(6) mental state elements. (Part3-523a6.md § E.5.)

**Counsel's Honest Assessment of the § 523(a)(6) Merits**

We owe you a candid view of the § 523(a)(6) merits independent of the procedural defects. The merits present a genuine challenge that the opposition cannot gloss over. The underlying conduct here is not like the malpractice case in Geiger, where the doctor prescribed the wrong medication negligently. Brad's conduct was intentional — the criminal conviction establishes that he intentionally traveled for illicit sexual conduct and intentionally received child pornography. The question under Geiger is whether he subjectively intended to cause the specific injuries alleged in each civil count, not merely whether he intended to perform the acts.

There is space between the criminal conviction's intent elements (intent to travel, intent to receive) and § 523(a)(6)'s required intent (intent to cause the specific injury to the specific plaintiff). That space — and Lilly Cannon's own deposition testimony describing a "non-forced relationship" — is the factual terrain in which the § 523(a)(6) contest will be fought if Nagel properly initiates an adversary proceeding. The honest answer is that this is a hard merits issue where the law is genuinely contested and where the outcome in a full adversary proceeding is not certain. Our procedural and collateral estoppel defenses are strong. The merits of the underlying § 523(a)(6) claim — if properly pleaded and tried — are genuinely contested. Counsel should not represent to the client or to this Court that the debt will be discharged; that determination requires a full adversary proceeding with fact-finding on intent.

### 3.E: Nagel's Stay Relief Argument — Assessment

Nagel's § 362(d)(1) stay relief argument is the briefest section of the motion (approximately two pages) and is almost entirely derivative of the § 707(a) bad faith argument. Nagel's theory is simple: "As set forth above, Mell has sought bankruptcy in bad faith. See, supra, § I. Accordingly, this case should be stayed for the same reasons which support dismissal of this case." (Nagel-mtd-analysis.md § 7.) He then quotes the mid-Atlantic Handling seven-factor test and asserts, in one conclusory paragraph, that the equities support lifting the stay because B.B. has litigated for seven years and the trial is prepared.

The structural problem with this approach: Nagel's entire "cause" for stay relief under § 362(d)(1) rests on the same bad faith theory as his § 707(a) dismissal motion. If the Court denies the § 707(a) motion — as it should — the only "cause" argument for stay relief collapses simultaneously. There is no independent § 362(d)(1) argument in the motion beyond the bad faith claim. Nagel has not invoked § 362(d)(2) (lack of adequate protection), § 362(d)(3) (single asset real estate), or any other statutory ground for relief. The motion lives or dies with the § 707(a) argument.

There is also a threshold factual problem with stay relief that Nagel's motion completely fails to address: the Zunic order. The state court action was dismissed without prejudice on June 12, 2026 — the same day Brad filed the petition, before Nagel even filed this motion. The Curtis/Sonnax twelve-factor test for stay relief under § 362(d)(1) — which Nagel invokes through Mid-Atlantic Handling — requires courts to consider, among other things: Factor 1 (whether stay lift results in partial or complete resolution), Factor 11 (whether parties are ready for trial in the other proceeding), and Factor 12 (balance of harms). Factors 1, 11, and 12 are all potentially dispositive — and all favor maintaining the stay — because there is no pending state court proceeding into which any "relief" could land. Lifting the stay cannot result in a resolution of a dismissed action. The parties are not ready for trial in a proceeding that no longer exists. (Part5-stay.md § A-2.)

**The Prejudgment Attachment Claim**

Nagel's stay relief section asserts that "there is a judgment of liability against Mell and an attachment of approximately $1 million of his monies held in escrow for a judgment in favor of B.B." (Nagel-mtd-analysis.md § 7.) The "$1 million" figure overstates the attachment; Brad's own schedules disclose $750,000 in the Nagel Rice trust account, $75,000 with the Greifinger firm, and $150,000 with McElroy Deutsch as attorneys for Gulfstream — different amounts and different custodians. (BRIEFING-SECTIONS-1-2.md § E.) More importantly, the prejudgment attachment from the dismissed state court action may itself be legally extinct following the Zunic dismissal order. A prejudgment attachment derives its vitality from the pending action. When the Zunic order dismissed the case, the foundation for the attachment was eliminated. (Part5-stay.md § C-3.)

And there is the Paragano email. On June 18, 2026 — three days after Nagel had acknowledged the bankruptcy filing to this Court — Nagel's law partner Michael Paragano emailed Brad directly, asserting the prejudgment attachment. This email is a potential willful stay violation under 11 U.S.C. § 362(k)(1) and In re Lansaw, 853 F.3d 657 (3d Cir. 2017). We address this comprehensively in Section 4.F below.

### 3.F: Nagel's Abstention Argument — Assessment

The abstention section is the most legally defective portion of the motion, and it is defective in multiple independent ways, beginning with the threshold citation error.

**The § 1134 Error**

Nagel cites "28 U.S.C. § 1134(c)(2)" and "28 U.S.C. § 1134(c)(1)" as the statutory bases for mandatory and permissive abstention respectively. Section 1134 of Title 28 does not exist. Chapter 85 of Part IV of Title 28 (District Courts; Jurisdiction) contains sections numbered 1330 through 1369. There is no § 1134. The abstention provisions governing bankruptcy are at 28 U.S.C. § 1334(c)(1) (permissive) and § 1334(c)(2) (mandatory). (Part4-abstention.md, Threshold Alert; nagel-mtd-analysis.md §§ 6, 8, Misrepresentation 1.) A motion premised on a statute that does not exist is deficient on its face.

**Mandatory Abstention Is Legally Impossible Here**

Setting aside the citation error, the substantive mandatory abstention argument fails on the face of the statute. Section 1334(c)(2) — the actual statute — applies only to a proceeding "based upon a State law claim or State law cause of action, related to a case under title 11 but not arising under title 11 or arising in a case under title 11." A § 523(a)(6) nondischargeability action arises directly under 11 U.S.C. § 523 — a provision of the Bankruptcy Code. It arises under title 11, not merely "relates to" a case under title 11. Therefore the mandatory abstention statute by its express text does not apply. (Part4-abstention.md § A.4.)

This is confirmed by 28 U.S.C. § 157(b)(2)(I), which expressly enumerates "determinations as to the dischargeability of particular debts" as core proceedings. Core proceedings "arise under" or "arise in" title 11 — by definition. Mandatory abstention under § 1334(c)(2) is categorically unavailable for core proceedings. Fed. R. Bankr. P. 4007's advisory committee note further confirms exclusive bankruptcy court jurisdiction over § 523(a)(2), (4), and (6). Exclusive jurisdiction cannot be divested by an abstention motion.

**Stoe v. Flaherty — Nagel's Own Case Defeats Him**

Nagel cites Stoe v. Flaherty, 436 F.3d 209, 213 (3d Cir. 2006), as support for the mandatory abstention test. Stoe is the controlling Third Circuit authority on mandatory abstention. A § 523(a)(6) dischargeability determination arises directly under title 11. Nagel cannot satisfy element (2) of the case he cites. Stoe defeats him, not supports him. (Part4-abstention.md § B.1.)

The Zunic order dismissed B.B. v. Mell without prejudice on June 12, 2026 — the same day Brad filed the petition. The state court action was not pending when Nagel filed the abstention motion on June 23. It is not pending for the July 16 hearing. **The Internal Incoherence**

Perhaps the most revealing flaw in the abstention argument is its internal contradiction. Nagel simultaneously asks this Court to: (a) abstain from hearing the case and send it back to state court for a damages trial; and (b) declare the debt nondischargeable under § 523(a)(6). A court cannot abstain from the predicate (the factual circumstances of the underlying injury) while retaining the conclusion (the nondischargeability declaration). Nagel's argument literally requires this Court to make findings it just sent to state court. (Part4-abstention.md § C.4.)

### 4.B: § 523(a)(6) Response — Nondischargeability

**Lead with the Procedural Defect**

**The Geiger/Conte Framework — Our Law Controls**

The legal framework is clear: Geiger (Supreme Court, binding) requires subjective intent to cause the specific injury; Conte (Third Circuit, binding) requires either purposeful infliction or subjective knowledge that injury was substantially certain. These two cases are our foundation. Everything else follows from them.

Against those binding authorities, Nagel's Fleetwood argument — "self-evident" willfulness as a matter of law — is foreclosed. The opposition should quote Geiger's exact language on this point: the statute says "willful and malicious injury," not "willful acts that cause injury." 523 U.S. at 63. Congress's word choice was deliberate.

**The Collateral Estoppel Argument — The Core Defense**

The collateral estoppel defense to the § 523(a)(6) claim is strong and should be developed thoroughly. The argument runs:

Step 1: Identify what must be "actually litigated" — the specific mental state elements of § 523(a)(6) (subjective intent to cause the specific injury; malice without just cause or excuse).

Step 2: Show that R. 4:23-5(a)(2) establishes none of those elements. A discovery sanction is a procedural consequence. No judge made any finding on intent, malice, or purpose. The elements were never presented to any factfinder.

Step 3: Preempt the Docteroff argument. Docteroff's "deliberately prevents resolution" exception requires conduct that is sanctionable because it is unjustified — bad-faith obstruction without legal basis. Brad's Fifth Amendment invocation had constitutional justification. The distinction between constitutionally protected silence and bad-faith obstruction is fundamental.

Step 4: Even if Docteroff applied, what would have been "deemed litigated"? Civil liability on each count — not § 523(a)(6) mental state elements. The state court did not adjudicate willfulness and malice under federal bankruptcy standards. Those elements were never before any New Jersey court.

**Berlingeri — Handle With Care**

Berlingeri (246 B.R. 196 (Bankr. D.N.J. 2000)) is a Nagel-cited case that needs Westlaw verification before the opposition is filed. If Berlingeri held that intent to cause injury was established by a consent judgment in which the debtor stipulated to the facts constituting willful and malicious injury, then Berlingeri is distinguishable — there is no consent judgment here, and the partial summary judgment is an interlocutory discovery sanction. If Berlingeri adopted a post-Geiger subjective intent standard and applied it to a child sexual abuse context, the question is how the court got from the facts to the conclusion. If it used the "self-evident" inference approach, it is inconsistent with Geiger. Do not cite or dismiss Berlingeri in the brief without reading the full opinion first.

**The Count-by-Count Element Problem**

Nagel's § 523(a)(6) argument treats the entire civil action as a monolith — all counts are "sexual abuse of a minor," and therefore all are willful and malicious as a matter of law. This approach is analytically deficient. Even if some counts could satisfy Geiger, Nagel must establish willfulness and malice count-by-count, and the elements of each New Jersey civil statute must be compared to § 523(a)(6)'s requirements individually. N.J.S.A. 2A:61B-1 (Child Sexual Abuse Act) requires "intentional touching for the purpose of sexually arousing or sexually gratifying the actor" — intent to gratify, not intent to injure. The gap between those two formulations is exactly what Geiger requires be filled with proof. Nagel has not attempted to do that analysis. In an adversary proceeding, he will have to.

**The Statutory Text Closes the Door**

A § 523(a)(6) nondischargeability action arises directly under 11 U.S.C. § 523. It arises under title 11. The statutory text itself forecloses mandatory abstention. Support with 28 U.S.C. § 157(b)(2)(I): "Core proceedings include but are not limited to the following: . . . (I) determinations as to the dischargeability of particular debts." Quote the full verbatim text. This is not an ambiguous enumeration — it is an express statutory statement that dischargeability is a core proceeding, and core proceedings arise under or arise in title 11, not merely relate to it.

### 4.E: The Pari Passu Structural Argument — Weaving It Through

The "pari passu destruction" narrative is Nagel's most emotionally compelling argument, and it runs through every section of his brief without ever being labeled as such. This argument must be addressed head-on across all four response sections, not deflected or ignored. The counter-argument has three components:

First, pari passu distribution is not a trick Brad deployed — it is the design of the United States Bankruptcy Code. Congress in 11 U.S.C. § 726 established the order of distribution in Chapter 7 cases. Congress in § 507 established the priority scheme. ### 4.F: The § 362(k) Cross-Motion Recommendation

**What the Evidence Shows**

The Paragano email of June 18, 2026 presents a textbook willful stay violation. The facts are:

— Brad filed his Chapter 7 petition on June 12, 2026.

— Brad's bankruptcy counsel filed notice of the automatic stay in the state court proceeding the same afternoon (Bankruptcy filing.pdf, filed ESX-L-007200-19 at 3:28 PM on June 12, 2026).

— Judge Zunic dismissed the state court action on June 12, 2026.

— Nagel appeared before this Court and acknowledged the bankruptcy filing on or before June 15, 2026 — the date the Court entered the OSC.

— On June 18, 2026 — three days after Nagel's acknowledged knowledge of the bankruptcy — Michael Paragano, Nagel's law partner, emailed Brad directly asserting a prejudgment attachment from the dismissed state court action.

**The Legal Framework**

The Third Circuit's § 362(k)(1) standard, established in In re Lansaw, 853 F.3d 657, 669 (3d Cir. 2017), requires three elements: (1) a violation of the automatic stay occurred; (2) the violation was willfully committed; and (3) the debtor suffered damages as a result. (Part5-stay.md § C-1.)

Willfulness under the Third Circuit standard requires only knowledge of the bankruptcy filing plus an intentional act. In re Lansaw, 853 F.3d at 669. Knowledge of the specific stay provisions is not required — only knowledge of the bankruptcy itself. In re University Medical Center, 973 F.2d 1065, 1068 (3d Cir. 1992). Good faith is not a defense. Cal. Coast Univ. v. Aleckna (In re Aleckna), No. 20-1309, 2021 WL 4097155, at *4 (3d Cir. Sept. 9, 2021).

Applied here: Nagel had actual knowledge. Paragano is Nagel's law partner — agency imputation principles under the Restatement (Third) of Agency § 5.03 impute Nagel's knowledge to Paragano. The June 18 email was an intentional act with imputed knowledge of the bankruptcy. That is sufficient for willfulness under Lansaw.

The violations the Paragano email triggered: § 362(a)(1) (continuation of a judicial proceeding against the debtor); § 362(a)(4) (act to enforce a lien against property of the estate — the prejudgment attachment); § 362(a)(5) (act to enforce against estate property a lien securing a pre-petition claim); and § 362(a)(6) (act to collect a pre-petition claim). Each provision is independently violated.

**The Double Invalidity**

The Paragano email asserted a prejudgment attachment that was doubly invalid: (1) the underlying state court action had been dismissed by the Zunic order on June 12, eliminating the foundation for the attachment under New Jersey law; and (2) the Paragano email appears to assert an attachment interest that was separately released in December 2023 — over two years before the bankruptcy filing. Asserting a legally nonexistent right post-petition violates the stay regardless of the asserter's subjective belief about the right's validity. In re Aleckna: good faith is not a defense.

**Available Damages**

Lansaw authorized and affirmed: (1) actual damages including attorneys' fees; (2) emotional distress damages (no medical evidence required where violations are patently egregious and debtor testifies credibly); and (3) punitive damages — $40,000 in Lansaw itself. In re Lansaw, 853 F.3d at 669.

Brad's fees in opposing the stay violation and this motion are recoverable as "actual damages" under § 362(k)(1). The direct assertion of a void lien against a represented debtor three days after his own firm's partner acknowledged the bankruptcy to this Court is not a borderline case. Punitive damages are warranted.

**Action Required**

The § 362(k) cross-motion should be filed. We recommend filing it simultaneously with the opposition brief (or shortly before the July 16 hearing). This accomplishes several things:

First, it puts Nagel and Paragano on defense at the very hearing where Nagel is seeking to put Brad on defense. The movant seeking stay relief is now the respondent on a willful stay violation claim.

Second, it demonstrates to Judge O'Hagan — a former government litigator who takes stay compliance seriously — that the party seeking equitable relief from the automatic stay has already violated that same stay. Courts routinely factor this into their equitable discretion under § 362(d)(1).

Third, attorneys' fees recovered under § 362(k)(1) as "actual damages" directly offset Brad's cost of opposing this motion. The client has a right to know that this avenue exists.

Fourth, it creates settlement leverage. Nagel knows his partner violated the stay. A § 362(k) motion converts that knowledge into a litigation cost.

One remaining item requires Westlaw verification before filing: the joint-and-several liability of Nagel Rice LLP as a law firm entity (distinct from Nagel and Paragano as individuals) under § 362(k). Prior research notes that "In re Shaw (Bankr. D.N.J.)" cited for this proposition in earlier drafts is a hallucinated case that does not exist, and that In re LeGrand (California) is a § 524 discharge injunction case — the wrong statute. A verified § 362(k) authority for law firm joint-and-several liability must be obtained from Westlaw before that argument is made in the filing. The argument itself is sound; the citation must be verified.
