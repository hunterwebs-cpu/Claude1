# RESEARCH MEMO: State Court Orders Have No Operative Force in Independent Federal Bankruptcy Proceeding

**Case:** In re S. Bradley Mell, No. 26-16834-EJO (Bankr. D.N.J. 2026)
**Issue:** Whether state court orders from B.B. v. Mell, ESX-L-7200-19 (Essex Cty. Super. Ct.) carry binding or operative force in this independent Chapter 7 bankruptcy proceeding.
**Prepared:** July 1, 2026
**Classification:** ATTORNEY WORK PRODUCT — PRIVILEGED AND CONFIDENTIAL

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The movant (Nagel Rice LLP, on behalf of B.B.) relies on five state court orders from a separate Essex County civil case as if those orders command the bankruptcy court. They do not. The argument fails on four independent grounds, each supported by binding or strongly persuasive authority:

1. **28 U.S.C. § 1450 does not apply.** That statute preserves state court orders only when a case is "removed" to federal court. This bankruptcy case was not removed from Essex County — it is a new, independent federal proceeding filed under 11 U.S.C. § 301. The text of § 1450, confirmed by its placement in Chapter 89 of Title 28 ("District Courts; Removal of Cases from State Courts"), makes plain that the statute has no application here.

2. **State court injunctions do not bind courts in separate proceedings.** The Supreme Court held in Baker v. General Motors Corp., 522 U.S. 222 (1998), that a state court injunction "cannot dictate to a court in another jurisdiction" how to handle proceedings involving parties who were not before the issuing court. The five Essex County orders cannot command this Court.

3. **Federal court pseudonymity requires a federal court order.** The Third Circuit's standard for proceeding pseudonymously in federal court (Doe v. Megless, 654 F.3d 404 (3d Cir. 2011)) must be applied by each federal court in each proceeding. A state court pseudonymity order from a separate civil case does not satisfy this requirement. Fed. R. Bankr. P. 1005 independently mandates that the petition caption include the debtor's real name and all other names used within eight years.

4. **The Bankruptcy Code's own rules govern the bankruptcy proceeding.** The Third Circuit recently held that the Bankruptcy Code (§ 107) "differs from and displaces" external standards governing what is kept confidential in bankruptcy proceedings. ESML Holdings Inc. v. Mesabi Metallics Company LLC, No. 23-2954 (3d Cir. Apr. 16, 2025). State court protective orders from separate proceedings are not a source of law in this Court.

## PART I: THE § 1450 ARGUMENT — THE MOVANT'S FOUNDATIONAL PREMISE IS WRONG

### A. The Text of § 1450 Limits It to Removed Cases

**28 U.S.C. § 1450** (text confirmed from federal primary sources):

> "Whenever any action is removed from a State court to a district court of the United States, any attachment or sequestration of the goods or estate of the defendant in such action in the State court shall hold the goods or estate to answer the final judgment or decree in the same manner as they would have been held to answer final judgment or decree had it been rendered by the State court. All bonds, undertakings, or security given by either party in such action prior to its removal shall remain valid and effectual notwithstanding such removal. All injunctions, orders, and other proceedings had in such action prior to its removal shall remain in full force and effect until dissolved or modified by the district court."

**Key words:** "Whenever any action is **removed** from a State court." The statute is triggered by one event and one event only: **removal**. A debtor's voluntary bankruptcy petition is not a removal.

**Source:** 28 U.S.C. § 1450; Chapter 89 of Title 28 ("District Courts; Removal of Cases from State Courts"), covering §§ 1441–1455; confirmed at:
- https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title28-section1450&num=0&edition=prelim
- https://law.justia.com/codes/us/2020/title-28/part-iv/chapter-89/sec-1450/

### B. The Structural Context Confirms the Limitation

Every section of Chapter 89 (Title 28, §§ 1441–1455) addresses removal of cases from state to federal courts. Section 1450 is simply the provision within that removal scheme that addresses what happens to pre-removal orders, attachments, and bonds. The chapter heading is dispositive: "District Courts; Removal of Cases from State Courts." The entire chapter is about removed cases. Section 1450 cannot be lifted out of that context and applied to a new, independent federal case.

### C. Fed. R. Bankr. P. 9027(j) Confirms the Same Limitation

Fed. R. Bankr. P. 9027 implements the bankruptcy removal procedure and mirrors § 1450 in subdivision (j), which provides that "all injunctions, orders, and other proceedings had prior to removal shall remain in full force and effect until dissolved or modified by the court." Rule 9027(j) — like § 1450 — is premised entirely on prior removal of the same case.

**Source:** Fed. R. Bankr. P. 9027(j); confirmed at:
- https://www.law.cornell.edu/rules/frbp/rule_9027
- https://law.abi.org/rules/9027

### D. What Actually Happened Here

When Debtor filed the Chapter 7 petition in this Court, **B.B. v. Mell (ESX-L-7200-19)** was not removed. It remained in Essex County Superior Court — and it was stayed as to the debtor by operation of 11 U.S.C. § 362(a). Two separate cases now exist: (1) the state civil case, stayed in Essex County; and (2) this independent federal bankruptcy case, governed by Title 11 and the Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure. Section 1450 governs neither of them, because no removal occurred.

**No case was removed. Section 1450 does not apply. End of argument.**

## PART II: STATE COURT INJUNCTIONS CANNOT COMMAND THIS COURT

### Case 1: Baker v. General Motors Corp., 522 U.S. 222 (1998)

**Citation:** Baker v. General Motors Corp., 522 U.S. 222, 118 S. Ct. 657, 139 L. Ed. 2d 580 (1998)
**Court:** United States Supreme Court
**Binding Status:** BINDING on all federal courts, including the United States Bankruptcy Court for the District of New Jersey
**Source:** https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/522/222/; also at https://www.law.cornell.edu/supct/html/96-653.ZO.html

**Facts:** GM obtained a Michigan state court injunction prohibiting its former employee (Elwell) from testifying against GM in other litigation. The Bakers, plaintiffs in a Missouri wrongful-death suit against GM, subpoenaed Elwell. GM argued the Michigan injunction required Missouri courts to exclude Elwell's testimony.

**Holding:** The Supreme Court unanimously held that the Michigan injunction could not command the Missouri court. The Full Faith and Credit Clause requires that state court *judgments* be given preclusive effect elsewhere, but does not require other courts to enforce a state court's *injunction* as an operative command on their proceedings.

**Verbatim Key Quote:**
> "a Michigan court cannot, by entering the injunction to which Elwell and GM stipulated, dictate to a court in another jurisdiction that evidence relevant in the Bakers' case" shall be excluded.

**Additional key language confirmed from secondary sources:**
- "Michigan's judgment cannot reach beyond the Elwell-GM controversy to control proceedings against GM brought in other States, by other parties."
- "Michigan has no power over those parties."

**How It Supports Our Argument:**

The *Baker* principle maps directly onto this case. The Essex County Superior Court (State court in civil case ESX-L-7200-19) cannot, by entering its protective/pseudonymity/contact/sanctions orders, dictate to this Court how to conduct the bankruptcy proceeding. The parties to the Essex County case (to the extent B.B. is proceeding pseudonymously and that order purports to govern this Court's proceedings) are in a different court, a different proceeding, and a different case. The Essex County court has no power over this bankruptcy case. Its orders are facts in evidence; they are not operative commands on this Court.

**Note on Scope:** Baker was a Full Faith and Credit case between two state courts. The principle applies with equal or greater force when a state court order is asserted against a *federal* court, because state courts have even less authority over federal courts than over courts of sister states. See 28 U.S.C. § 1334(a) (district courts have "original and exclusive jurisdiction of all cases under title 11").

### Case 2: ESML Holdings Inc. v. Mesabi Metallics Company LLC, No. 23-2954 (3d Cir. Apr. 16, 2025)

**Citation:** ESML Holdings Inc. v. Mesabi Metallics Company LLC, No. 23-2954 (3d Cir. Apr. 16, 2025) (precedential)
**Also related:** In re Essar Steel Minnesota, LLC (Bankr. D. Del. Case No. 16-11626)
**Court:** United States Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit
**Binding Status:** BINDING (Third Circuit, precedential)
**Source:** https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/USCOURTS-ca3-23-02954/pdf/USCOURTS-ca3-23-02954-0.pdf (403 on direct fetch — [VERIFY BEFORE FILING via Westlaw or CourtListener for full reporter citation])

**Holding:** The Third Circuit held that 11 U.S.C. § 107 of the Bankruptcy Code, not common law, governs public access to confidential documents in bankruptcy proceedings. The Bankruptcy Code's rules "differ from and displace" external standards for what may be sealed or kept confidential in a bankruptcy case.

**Verbatim Key Quote (confirmed from search results):**
> The bankruptcy code "differs from and displaces the common law standard for sealing judicial records."

**How It Supports Our Argument:**

The Third Circuit's holding in ESML Holdings establishes a foundational principle: the Bankruptcy Code is the governing law in the bankruptcy proceeding, not external sources. This applies directly to the movant's attempt to invoke the state court's confidentiality and pseudonymity orders here. The question of what is public or confidential in this bankruptcy proceeding is governed by § 107 of the Bankruptcy Code and Fed. R. Bankr. P. 9037, not by a state court protective order issued in a separate civil case under New Jersey court rules.

Section 107(a) provides that "a paper filed in a case under this title and the dockets of a bankruptcy court are public records and open to examination by an entity at reasonable times without charge." Section 107(b) provides specific, narrow exceptions for trade secrets and "confidential research, development, or commercial information" — not for a civil defendant's identity in a private tort suit. The Essex County pseudonymity order falls outside § 107(b)'s scope entirely.

## PART III: PSEUDONYMITY IN FEDERAL COURT REQUIRES A FEDERAL COURT ORDER

### Case 3: Doe v. Megless, 654 F.3d 404 (3d Cir. 2011)

**Citation:** Doe v. Megless, 654 F.3d 404 (3d Cir. 2011)
**Court:** United States Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit
**Binding Status:** BINDING (Third Circuit)
**Source:** https://www.courtlistener.com/opinion/222210/doe-v-megless/ (403 on direct fetch); confirmed at https://www.leagle.com/decision/infco20110801061 (citation verified in multiple search results)

**Holding:** The Third Circuit held that a plaintiff's use of a pseudonym in federal litigation "runs afoul of the public's common law right of access to judicial proceedings." A party seeking to proceed pseudonymously must establish, through a multi-factor balancing test, that the specific harm from disclosure outweighs the public interest in open proceedings. The district court must conduct this balancing in each case.

**Verbatim Key Quote (confirmed from search results):**
> "a plaintiff's use of a pseudonym 'runs afoul of the public's common law right of access to judicial proceedings.'"

**The Megless Factors (6 pro-pseudonymity, 3 anti-pseudonymity):**

*Factors favoring anonymity:*
1. The extent to which the identity of the litigant has been kept confidential
2. The bases upon which disclosure is feared or sought to be avoided, and the substantiality of these bases
3. The magnitude of the public interest in maintaining the confidentiality of the litigant's identity
4. Whether there is an atypically weak public interest in knowing the litigant's identities
5. The undesirability of an outcome adverse to the pseudonymous party attributable to refusal to disclose
6. Whether the party seeking pseudonymity has illegitimate ulterior motives

*Factors against anonymity:*
1. The universal level of public interest in access to the identities of litigants
2. Whether, because of the subject matter of this litigation, the status of the litigant as a public figure, or otherwise, there is a particularly strong interest in knowing the litigant's identities
3. Whether the opposition to pseudonymity by counsel, the public, or the press is illegitimately motivated

**How It Supports Our Argument:**

The movant's reliance on the Essex County pseudonymity order skips the entire Megless analysis entirely. The Third Circuit requires that *this Court* conduct the balancing test in *this proceeding*. A state court order from a separate civil case cannot substitute for the federal court's own determination. Each federal court applies Megless independently.

Moreover, the creditor in this bankruptcy case is not a plaintiff bringing a sensitive claim — she is a creditor-movant seeking to dismiss the debtor's petition. The typical rationale for pseudonymity (protecting a victim from retaliation or preventing chilling of certain claims) does not apply when the pseudonymous party is an aggressive movant seeking affirmative relief from this Court.

Additionally, the creditor has already filed a motion (Doc. 8) that identifies her sufficiently for the Court and the trustee to evaluate her claim. The pseudonymity order's practical function in state court — protecting her identity from the public — does not automatically translate into an entitlement to proceed pseudonymously in this Court's docket.

### Rule: Fed. R. Bankr. P. 1005 — Caption Requirement

**Text (confirmed from https://www.law.cornell.edu/rules/frbp/rule_1005):**
> "The title of the case must include the debtor's name, employer identification number, last four digits of the social-security number or individual debtor's taxpayer-identification number, any other federal taxpayer-identification number, and all other names used within eight years before filing the petition."

Rule 1005 mandates that the bankruptcy petition caption include the debtor's **real name** and all other names used. By structural parallel, parties in the case are identified by their actual names, not pseudonyms imported from a separate state court proceeding. Any exception to this transparency requirement requires a bankruptcy court order under Megless, not reliance on a state court order.

## PART IV: THE PREJUDGMENT ATTACHMENT HAS NO OPERATIVE FORCE AGAINST THE ESTATE

### Governing Statutes

**11 U.S.C. § 362(a)(1)** (automatic stay):
> "(a) Except as provided in subsection (b) of this section, a petition filed under section 301... operates as a stay, applicable to all entities, of — (1) the commencement or continuation, including the issuance or employment of process, of a judicial, administrative, or other action or proceeding against the debtor that was or could have been commenced before the commencement of the case under this title..."

**11 U.S.C. § 362(a)(4)** (automatic stay):
> "...any act to create, perfect, or enforce any lien against property of the estate..."

**Effect:** As of the moment the Chapter 7 petition was filed, the automatic stay halted any act to enforce the Essex County prejudgment attachment against property of the estate. The state court civil case is stayed as to the debtor under § 362(a)(1). The prejudgment attachment lien cannot be enforced under § 362(a)(4).

### The Prejudgment Attachment is Not a Perfected Judgment Lien

The Essex County order granting the prejudgment attachment is an interlocutory order in a pending civil case. No judgment has been entered in B.B. v. Mell. Under New Jersey law (R. 4:60), a prejudgment attachment is a provisional remedy — it preserves the status quo pending judgment, but it does not become a final judgment lien until judgment is entered.

Upon the filing of the bankruptcy petition:

1. **The underlying civil case is stayed.** No final judgment can be entered in B.B. v. Mell without first obtaining relief from the automatic stay under § 362(d). The creditor has not sought such relief.

2. **The attachment cannot be enforced.** Section 362(a)(4) stays all acts to enforce any lien against estate property. The provisional attachment is a lien that cannot be enforced without violating the stay.

3. **Preference avoidance may apply.** If the prejudgment attachment was obtained within 90 days before the petition date, the trustee may avoid it as a preference under 11 U.S.C. § 547(b). The attachment enables the creditor to receive more than she would in a Chapter 7 liquidation (where she is an unsecured creditor), satisfying the preference elements. [VERIFY: date of prejudgment attachment order vs. petition date of bankruptcy case filed 2026]

4. **The trustee's strong-arm powers under § 544(a) may apply.** The Chapter 7 trustee has the rights of a hypothetical lien creditor as of the petition date. If the prejudgment attachment was not properly perfected under New Jersey law, or if it is inferior to the trustee's hypothetical lien, it can be avoided.

**Practical effect:** The movant cannot use the prejudgment attachment as leverage in this bankruptcy proceeding without first obtaining stay relief from this Court. The attachment does not "carry over" to the bankruptcy case as an operative lien that commands the bankruptcy court's deference.

**Source for § 362(a)(4) and attachments:** Duane Morris, "Automatic Stay Prevents Attachment of After-Acquired Judgment Lien" (discussing 11 U.S.C. § 362(a)(4) as preventing attachment creditors from enforcing pre-petition attachment liens against estate property after bankruptcy filing) — https://www.duanemorris.com/articles/automatic_stay_prevents_attachment_of_after_aquired_judgement_lien_5360.html; ABI, "The Effect of Bankruptcy on a Prejudgment Attachment Lien" (Pullman & Comley, Sept. 2014) (noting that § 362(a)(4) "precludes any act to create, perfect, or enforce a lien against property of the estate once a petition has been filed, preventing a creditor from obtaining a judgment to perfect an attachment lien upon the filing of a bankruptcy petition") [VERIFY BEFORE FILING — PDF accessible at https://www.pullcom.com/media/publication/300_2014-09-06_Bankruptcy_ABI_World_IJG.pdf].

## PART V: CASES THAT COULD CUT AGAINST OUR ARGUMENT (AND WHY THEY DON'T)

### 1. Full Faith and Credit Act, 28 U.S.C. § 1738

**The argument:** The movant might argue that § 1738 requires this Court to give "full faith and credit" to the state court orders, including the partial summary judgment on liability (interlocutory) and the sanctions order.

**Why it doesn't apply:**

(a) **Section 1738 applies to final judgments, not interlocutory orders.** The partial summary judgment is specifically characterized as interlocutory. Interlocutory orders are not entitled to preclusive effect under § 1738. See *Migra v. Warren City School Dist. Bd. of Ed.*, 465 U.S. 75, 81 (1984) (§ 1738 applies to "judicial proceedings" that are final).

(b) **Full Faith and Credit requires preclusive effect (res judicata/collateral estoppel), not operative effect.** Section 1738 means that if a state court renders a final judgment on an issue, a federal court must give it the same preclusive effect as it would have in the state where it was rendered. It does not mean that a state court *injunction* or *protective order* becomes a command on the federal court. *Baker v. GMC* drew exactly this distinction: Full Faith and Credit = preclusion of re-litigation; it does not = extraterritorial enforcement of the state court's own orders.

(c) **Even for final judgments, § 1738 goes to preclusive effect, not "operative force."** A state court judgment that B.B. prevailed on liability (if it were final, which it is not) would be relevant to any § 523(a)(6) nondischargeability analysis — but only if all elements of collateral estoppel are met. That is a separate adversary proceeding issue, not a basis for treating the interlocutory liability order as a command in this Court.

### 2. The Contact-Barring Order

**The argument:** The movant might argue that the state court contact-barring order prohibiting Mell from contacting B.B. should apply in this bankruptcy case to prohibit the debtor from communicating with the creditor.

**Why it doesn't apply:**

(a) **Baker v. GMC:** The Essex County court cannot dictate the terms of proceedings in this Court.

(b) **Bankruptcy communications are governed by the Code and Rules.** Any communication between the debtor (or his counsel) and a creditor in the context of this case is governed by the Bankruptcy Code, the FRBP, and bankruptcy court orders — not by a state civil court's contact order.

(c) **Automatic stay context.** The contact-barring order runs against the debtor. The automatic stay now protects the debtor's estate. If the creditor's counsel initiates contacts or process to enforce the contact-barring order in a way that impacts the debtor's rights in this bankruptcy, that itself may be a stay violation under § 362(a).

(d) **Relief, if any, must come from this Court.** If the creditor believes she needs protection from contact in the context of this bankruptcy proceeding, she must seek a protective order from this Court under Fed. R. Bankr. P. 7026 or the Court's inherent authority — not point to a state court order issued in a different case.

### 3. Rule 9027(j) Argument (Removal-Context Preservation)

**The argument:** The movant might cite Rule 9027(j) for the proposition that "all injunctions, orders, and other proceedings had prior to removal shall remain in full force and effect."

**Why it doesn't apply:**

Rule 9027 governs removal procedure. Subdivision (j) expressly states: "all injunctions, orders, and other proceedings had **prior to removal** shall remain in full force and effect until dissolved or modified by the court." The predicate — prior to removal — does not exist here. There was no removal. Rule 9027(j) does not apply.

## PART VI: PROPOSED ARGUMENT FRAMEWORK FOR OPPOSITION

**Order of argument (strongest procedural defect first):**

1. **Threshold:** Section 1450 does not apply — the statute's text requires removal, which never occurred. This is a threshold argument that defeats the movant's foundational premise before reaching any merits. (Baker, statutory text)

2. **Structural:** State court orders from a separate civil case cannot command this Court — Baker v. GMC (SCOTUS, binding). The movant cannot use the Essex County orders as operative commands; they are merely facts in evidence.

3. **Pseudonymity:** Creditor cannot proceed pseudonymously in this Court without this Court's own order. Megless (Third Circuit, binding) requires this Court's independent balancing. Rule 1005 requires real-name disclosure. ESML Holdings (Third Circuit, April 2025) confirms that the Bankruptcy Code's own rules govern this proceeding.

4. **Attachment:** The prejudgment attachment is stayed under § 362(a)(4) and may be avoidable as a preference or under § 544(a). It cannot be treated as an operative lien against the estate without this Court's relief from stay — which was never sought.
