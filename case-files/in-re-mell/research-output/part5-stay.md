## SECTION A — DEFEATING NAGEL'S § 362(d)(1) MOTION

### A-1. Burden of Proof: Controlling Standard

#### Statutory Framework: 11 U.S.C. § 362(d) and § 362(g)

Under **11 U.S.C. § 362(g)**:

> "In any hearing under subsection (d) or (e) of this section concerning relief from the stay of any act under subsection (a) of this section — (1) the party requesting such relief has the burden of proof on the issue of the debtor's equity in property; and (2) the party opposing such relief has the burden of proof on all other issues."

**Burden Allocation Under § 362(d)(1):**

The burden is a **shifting** one:

1. **Initial burden — Movant (Nagel):** The movant must first establish a *prima facie* case that "cause" exists for relief from the stay. Courts uniformly hold that the party moving for stay relief bears the initial burden of production to demonstrate cause. *See* In re Sonnax Indus., Inc., 907 F.2d 1280, 1285 (2d Cir. 1990) ("the movant bears the burden of making an initial showing of cause").

2. **Burden shift — Debtor (Brad Mell):** Once movant makes a prima facie showing, "the burden of proof then shifts to the debtor to demonstrate that cause does not exist or that equities weigh against granting relief." *See generally* 11 U.S.C. § 362(g)(2).

3. **Important limitation:** Under § 362(g)(1), the *movant* bears the burden on the issue of the debtor's equity in property. Under § 362(g)(2), the *debtor* bears the burden on "all other issues." Courts have interpreted this to mean that once movant establishes a prima facie case, the debtor must rebut it, but the ultimate burden on equity remains with the movant.

**Third Circuit Authority:**

The Third Circuit addressed automatic stay principles in **McCartney v. Integra National Bank North, 106 F.3d 506, 509 (3d Cir. 1997)**, noting the broad scope of the automatic stay and that relief requires a proper showing. While *McCartney* addressed extension of the stay to non-debtors rather than § 362(d)(1) "cause," it established that the automatic stay is broadly construed in the Third Circuit.

**Key proposition (multi-circuit consensus including Third Circuit):** The movant for stay relief must first make a prima facie showing of cause; failure to make that prima facie showing requires denial of the motion without the debtor having to offer any rebuttal.

**Application to Nagel's Motion:**

Nagel's sole "cause" argument rests on the same bad faith theory he advances for § 707(a) dismissal. As detailed in the Part 4 research memorandum, that bad faith argument fails on the facts. If Nagel cannot establish a prima facie case of bad faith "cause," the stay relief motion fails at the threshold — the burden never shifts to Brad at all.

### A-2. In re Mid-Atlantic Handling Systems, LLC, 304 B.R. 111 (Bankr. D.N.J. 2003)

**Citation:** In re Mid-Atl. Handling Sys., LLC, 304 B.R. 111 (Bankr. D.N.J. 2003)  
**Court:** United States Bankruptcy Court, District of New Jersey  
**Status:** Persuasive authority (D.N.J. bankruptcy court); frequently cited in the District

#### The Twelve-Factor Test (Curtis/Sonnax Factors)

The *Mid-Atlantic Handling* court applied the twelve-factor framework derived from *In re Curtis*, 40 B.R. 795 (Bankr. D. Utah 1984), and endorsed by *In re Sonnax Industries, Inc.*, 907 F.2d 1280 (2d Cir. 1990). These twelve factors have been widely adopted by courts in the District of New Jersey.

The twelve factors are:

> (1) whether relief would result in a partial or complete resolution of the issues;
> (2) lack of any connection with or interference with the bankruptcy case;
> (3) whether the other proceeding involves the debtor as a fiduciary;
> (4) whether a specialized tribunal with the necessary expertise has been established to hear the cause of action;
> (5) whether the debtor's insurer has assumed full responsibility for defending it;
> (6) whether the action primarily involves third parties;
> (7) whether litigation in another forum would prejudice the interests of other creditors;
> (8) whether the judgment claim arising from the other action is subject to equitable subordination;
> (9) whether movant's success in the other proceeding would result in a judicial lien avoidable by the debtor;
> (10) the interests of judicial economy and the expeditious and economical resolution of litigation;
> (11) whether the parties are ready for trial in the other proceeding; and
> (12) impact of the stay on the parties and the balance of harms.

*In re Curtis*, 40 B.R. 795, 799-800 (Bankr. D. Utah 1984); *In re Sonnax Indus.*, 907 F.2d at 1286.

**The *Mid-Atlantic Handling* Court's Analysis:**

The court identified **factors 1, 7, and 10 as the most relevant** in that case, and found that on balance those factors pointed toward granting stay relief. The court found factors 3, 5, 8, and 9 inapplicable.

**Critical observation:** In *Mid-Atlantic Handling*, there WAS a pending state court proceeding. The court's analysis of "factor 1" (whether relief would result in partial or complete resolution) was predicated on an active state court case that could provide resolution if the stay were lifted.

### A-4. In re Murray, 543 B.R. 484 (Bankr. S.D.N.Y. 2016)

**Full Citation:** In re Murray, 543 B.R. 484 (Bankr. S.D.N.Y. 2016), aff'd sub nom. Wilk Auslander LLP v. Murray (In re Murray), 565 B.R. 527 (S.D.N.Y. 2017), aff'd, 900 F.3d 53 (2d Cir. 2018)

**The Murray Bad Faith Framework:**

In *In re Murray*, the bankruptcy court dismissed an involuntary Chapter 7 petition filed by a single judgment creditor (Wilk Auslander LLP) for cause under § 707(a), finding that the petition was an improper use of the bankruptcy system because it was filed solely to enforce a judgment in what was essentially a two-party dispute. The court enumerated nine factors supporting dismissal, including:

> (1) the bankruptcy court was the most recent battlefield in a long-running, two-party dispute;
> (2) [the creditor] brought the case solely to enforce a judgment;
> (3) there were no competing creditors;
> (4) there was no need for pari passu distribution;
> (5) assuming there were fraudulent transfers to be avoided, [the creditor] could do so in another forum;
> (6) [the creditor] had adequate remedies to enforce its judgment under non-bankruptcy law;
> (7) [the creditor] invoked the bankruptcy laws solely to secure a benefit — the ability to execute on both [the debtor's] and his wife's interests in their apartment under 11 U.S.C. § 363(h) — that it does not have under non-bankruptcy law;
> (8) no assets would be lost or dissipated in the event that the bankruptcy case did not continue; and
> (9) [the debtor] did not want the bankruptcy action to proceed.

*In re Murray*, 900 F.3d 53, 57-58 (2d Cir. 2018) (summarizing bankruptcy court's factors).

### A-5. Where § 707(a) Bad Faith Fails, § 362(d)(1) Bad Faith Cause Also Fails

**Legal Proposition:**

Where a movant seeks both (1) dismissal under § 707(a) for bad faith and (2) stay relief under § 362(d)(1) based on the same bad faith argument, a court's denial of the dismissal motion logically forecloses the stay relief motion on the same grounds.

**Cases Supporting This Linkage:**

**General principle (multi-circuit):** The filing of a bankruptcy in bad faith can constitute "cause" pursuant to 11 U.S.C. § 362(d)(1) to lift the stay. *See, e.g.*, In re Albany Partners, Ltd., 749 F.2d 670, 674 (11th Cir. 1984) (bad faith as cause). The inverse is necessarily true: if the bankruptcy was NOT filed in bad faith, bad faith cannot serve as "cause" for stay relief.

**Logical structure argument:**
- Nagel's "cause" = Brad filed in bad faith
- Court finds: Brad did NOT file in bad faith (§ 707(a) argument fails)
- Therefore: Nagel's § 362(d)(1) "cause" = no cause established
- Result: Stay relief must be denied — movant has not met prima facie burden

**Additional argument:** Even if the Court were to decline to rule on the § 707(a) motion first, Nagel's bad faith argument fails on the merits for the reasons argued in the § 707(a) opposition. The Sonnax/Curtis factor analysis (above) independently requires denial.

## SECTION B — JUDICIAL ECONOMY COUNTER-ARGUMENTS

### B-1. Chapter 7 Trustee's § 704 Obligations Create Independent Grounds for Denial

**Legal Framework:**

11 U.S.C. § 704(a) imposes mandatory duties on the Chapter 7 trustee, including:
- § 704(a)(1): collect and reduce to money the property of the estate
- § 704(a)(4): investigate the financial affairs of the debtor
- § 704(a)(6): examine proofs of claims and object to allowance of improper claims

**The Judicial Economy Argument:**

When a Chapter 7 trustee has independent obligations to investigate the estate — including investigating potential fraudulent or preferential transfers, valuing claims, and managing estate property — lifting the stay to permit a creditor to pursue damages in state court creates **duplicative proceedings** and undermines the trustee's ability to fulfill those duties.

**Specifically:**
- If Nagel's state court damages claim is restored and litigated to judgment, the trustee will still need to evaluate that claim's dischargeability and the amount allowed as a proof of claim.
- The trustee's investigation of any transfers by Brad may overlap with the factual record developed in state court, creating res judicata and issue preclusion problems.
- The trustee cannot effectively administer the estate while the primary asset/liability dispute is being litigated in a foreign forum.

**Case Support:**

Courts have consistently recognized that the bankruptcy court — not the state court — is the proper forum when trustee administration issues are pending. *See generally* Sonnax factor 2 (connection to/interference with bankruptcy case) and factor 7 (prejudice to other creditors). Where a trustee has ongoing § 704 duties, lifting the stay merely exports the litigation without resolving the trustee's parallel obligations.

### B-2. Pending § 523(a)(6) Dischargeability Dispute as a Core Proceeding

**Legal Framework:**

28 U.S.C. § 157(b)(2)(I) expressly lists "determinations as to the dischargeability of particular debts" as **core proceedings**. This Court has exclusive jurisdiction over the dischargeability of Brad's debt to Nagel under § 523(a)(6).

**The Duplicative Proceedings Argument:**

If stay relief is granted to permit Nagel to litigate his claims in state court:

1. **State court adjudicates liability and damages** → Nagel obtains a judgment
2. **This Court must still adjudicate dischargeability** under § 523(a)(6)
3. **Result:** Two separate courts adjudicating overlapping factual issues (willful and malicious injury for § 523(a)(6) vs. the underlying tort for state court damages)

The stay relief would not **eliminate** litigation before this Court — it would **add** a parallel state court proceeding. This is precisely what the automatic stay is designed to prevent.

**Binding proposition:** A bankruptcy court's jurisdiction over dischargeability is exclusive. *See* 11 U.S.C. § 523(c)(1) (requiring the creditor to file an adversary proceeding or request determination of dischargeability in the bankruptcy court). Lifting the stay to permit state court litigation of the same underlying conduct does not eliminate this Court's role; it creates impermissible duplication.

**Third Circuit and DNJ Authority:**

The Third Circuit has held that dischargeability determinations are core proceedings exclusively within the bankruptcy court's jurisdiction. *See* Halper v. Halper, 164 F.3d 830, 836-37 (3d Cir. 1999) (dischargeability under § 523 is a core proceeding; bankruptcy court has jurisdiction). The D.N.J. follows this rule uniformly.

**Application:** Because this Court must adjudicate the § 523(a)(6) dischargeability question regardless of what a state court might do, granting stay relief provides Nagel no net procedural advantage — it only imposes additional costs on Brad and burdens two judicial systems.

### C-1. Elements of Willful Stay Violation Under § 362(k)(1) — Third Circuit Standard

**Statute:** 11 U.S.C. § 362(k)(1):

> "an individual injured by any willful violation of a stay provided by this section shall recover actual damages, including costs and attorneys' fees, and, in appropriate circumstances, may recover punitive damages."

**Three-Element Test (Third Circuit):**

To recover damages under § 362(k)(1), a debtor must prove by a preponderance of the evidence:

> **(1) that a violation of the automatic stay occurred;**  
> **(2) that the violation was willfully committed; and**  
> **(3) that the debtor suffered damages as a result of the violation.**

*See* In re Lansaw, 853 F.3d 657, 669 (3d Cir. 2017); *accord* Cal. Coast Univ. v. Aleckna (In re Aleckna), No. 20-1309, 2021 WL 4097155, at *3 (3d Cir. Sept. 9, 2021).

**The "Willful" Standard — Third Circuit Definition:**

The Third Circuit has established that "willfulness" under § 362(k) requires only:

> **Knowledge of the bankruptcy filing + intentional act that violates the stay.**

The creditor does not need specific intent to violate the law. Good faith is not a defense to willfulness. As the Third Circuit held in *In re Lansaw*:

> "[A] creditor 'willfully' violates the automatic stay under § 362(k)(1) when the creditor does so with knowledge of the bankruptcy."

*In re Lansaw*, 853 F.3d at 669 (3d Cir. 2017).

The Third Circuit further held in *Cal. Coast Univ. v. Aleckna* (2021):

> "Even a 'good faith' defense by a creditor in that it did not believe its actions violated the automatic stay is not a defense to willfulness, and willfulness is separate and distinct from good faith."

*In re Aleckna*, No. 20-1309, 2021 WL 4097155, at *4 (3d Cir. Sept. 9, 2021).

**The Critical Knowledge Distinction (Third Circuit):**

> "The principal advantage of an individual prosecuting a stay violation under § 362(k) is that the [plaintiff] must only prove that the actor had knowledge of the bankruptcy, while [a civil contempt plaintiff] must prove that the actor had knowledge of the automatic stay."

*In re University Medical Center*, 973 F.2d 1065, 1068 (3d Cir. 1992) (emphasis added).

This is the controlling Third Circuit standard: **knowledge of the bankruptcy filing** — not knowledge of the automatic stay's specific provisions — is sufficient to establish willfulness.

**Qualification — The "Sufficiently Uncertain Law" Defense:**

The Third Circuit in *In re University Medical Center* recognized a narrow defense: if the law regarding whether an act violated the stay was "sufficiently uncertain" (i.e., there was persuasive authority supporting the creditor's position), a willfulness finding may be defeated. *In re Univ. Med. Ctr.*, 973 F.2d at 1068.

However, in *In re Aleckna* (2021), the Third Circuit clarified that this defense is narrow: the defendant must show that the specific legal question was "sufficiently unsettled" — i.e., there was actual persuasive authority supporting the view that the conduct was lawful. *In re Aleckna*, 2021 WL 4097155, at *5.

**Application to Paragano's Conduct:**

- Nagel acknowledged the bankruptcy filing to this Court on or before June 15, 2026
- Paragano is Nagel's law partner — he is imputed with Nagel's actual knowledge of the filing (see C-2(c) below)
- On June 18, 2026, Paragano directly contacted Brad — the represented debtor — asserting a prejudgment attachment from the **dismissed** state court action
- There is nothing "sufficiently uncertain" about whether directly contacting a represented debtor to assert a lien/attachment after a bankruptcy filing violates the automatic stay — this is settled law

**Result:** Every element of the *Lansaw* / *Aleckna* willfulness standard is met.

#### C-2(b): Assertion of a Prejudgment Attachment Post-Petition as a Stay Violation

**Statutory Analysis:**

11 U.S.C. § 362(a)(4) stays "any act to create, perfect, or enforce any lien against property of the estate." 11 U.S.C. § 362(a)(5) stays "any act to create, perfect, or enforce against property of the debtor any lien to the extent that such lien secures a claim that arose before the commencement of the case."

**The Prejudgment Attachment Issue:**

A prejudgment attachment lien that has not been reduced to judgment before the bankruptcy filing is subject to the automatic stay. Courts have held:

> The automatic stay of 11 U.S.C. § 362(a)(4) precludes any act to create, perfect, or enforce a lien against property of the estate once a petition has been filed. A creditor is prevented from obtaining a judgment to perfect an attachment lien once the bankruptcy petition is filed.

*See, e.g.*, Duane Morris alert (citing district court holdings that automatic stay prevents post-petition attachment of judgment liens against estate property).

**Critical point for Brad's case:**

The state court action was **dismissed by the Zunic order on June 12, 2026** — before Paragano's email on June 18. Therefore:

1. The prejudgment attachment arose in a case that no longer exists as a viable proceeding
2. Asserting that attachment post-dismissal, post-petition, is an act to "enforce" a lien in violation of § 362(a)(4) and/or § 362(a)(5)
3. Even a pre-petition prejudgment attachment cannot be asserted post-petition without stay relief

**Result:** Paragano's June 18 email asserting the prejudgment attachment is independently a violation of § 362(a)(4) and/or § 362(a)(5), in addition to the § 362(a)(6) collection attempt violation.

#### C-2(c): Imputation of Knowledge Within a Law Firm

**Legal Proposition:**

Where one partner in a law firm has actual knowledge of a client's bankruptcy filing, that knowledge is imputed to all partners in the firm. This is basic agency law applied to the professional partnership context. *See* Restatement (Third) of Agency § 5.03 (knowledge of agent is imputed to principal).

**Application:**

- Nagel had actual knowledge of the bankruptcy filing (he acknowledged it to this Court on or before June 15, 2026)
- Paragano is Nagel's partner and represents Nagel in the state court matter
- Paragano is charged with Nagel's actual knowledge under agency imputation principles
- Paragano's act of sending the June 18 email was therefore a willful act by a party with imputed actual knowledge of the bankruptcy filing

**Supporting Proposition:**

Courts have held that "a party with knowledge of bankruptcy proceedings is charged with knowledge of the automatic stay." This principle applies fully to law firms: the firm's knowledge is the partner's knowledge, and vice versa.

> **RESEARCH NOTE:** The Third Circuit has not published a case squarely holding that one partner's knowledge of a bankruptcy filing is imputed to all firm partners for § 362(k) purposes. The proposition rests on general agency imputation principles. Supervising attorney should check whether the D.N.J. has addressed this specific issue and whether there are partner imputation cases from the Second or Fourth Circuits that could be cited as persuasive authority.

### C-3. Prejudgment Attachment from Dismissed Case Does Not Survive Petition

**The Double Problem:**

Nagel's prejudgment attachment is doubly invalid:

**First problem — Dismissal of the underlying action:**

A prejudgment attachment lien derives its vitality from the pending action in which it was obtained. When the Zunic order dismissed the state court action on June 12, 2026, the foundation for the prejudgment attachment was eliminated. Under New Jersey law, a prejudgment attachment obtained in a civil action is dependent on the continuation of that action — dismissal of the action extinguishes the basis for the attachment.

**Second problem — The automatic stay bars assertion:**

Even if the prejudgment attachment had survived the dismissal (which it did not), the automatic stay bars any act to enforce or assert it post-petition. 11 U.S.C. § 362(a)(4) stays "any act to create, perfect, or enforce any lien against property of the estate." Post-petition assertion of the attachment is a § 362(a)(4) violation.

**Third problem — Unperfected attachment is not a secured claim:**

Courts have held that a prejudgment attachment lien that was not reduced to judgment before the bankruptcy filing does not create perfected secured status. *See, e.g.*, Delaware case law holding that "a prejudgment attachment of real estate under Delaware law that had not been 'perfected' by judgment prior to debtor's discharge was not 'a sufficient lien to create a secured status.'" The same principle applies in New Jersey.

**Result:**

Nagel has no valid, perfected, enforceable lien arising from the prejudgment attachment. His June 18 email asserting that attachment was not merely a stay violation — it asserted a legally nonexistent right. This is relevant to both (1) the § 362(k) willfulness analysis (the assertion was factually and legally groundless) and (2) the factor 9 analysis under the Curtis/Sonnax test (whether movant's success would result in an avoidable lien).

### C-4. Stay Violator Seeking Stay Relief — Equitable and Procedural Bar

**The Core Argument:**

A party who has willfully violated the automatic stay is not entitled to ask this Court for equitable relief from that same stay. The maxim that "he who seeks equity must do equity" applies with full force in bankruptcy.

**Cases and Propositions:**

While no Third Circuit published case was found squarely holding that a willful stay violator is *per se* barred from obtaining stay relief, the following propositions apply:

**1. Equitable Discretion:**

Stay relief under § 362(d)(1) is a matter of the court's equitable discretion. *See* 11 U.S.C. § 362(d) ("the court shall grant relief... unless... [the balancing factors]"). Courts routinely consider the movant's conduct in exercising that discretion.

**2. Factor 12 — Balance of Harms:**

Under Curtis/Sonnax factor 12, the court weighs the "impact of the stay on the parties and the balance of harms." A party who has itself violated the stay cannot complain of the harms imposed by the stay — that party has already acted as if the stay did not exist. This vitiates any claim that the stay causes harm to the movant.

**3. Unclean Hands:**

The doctrine of unclean hands precludes equitable relief where the party seeking that relief has acted in bad faith or inequitably in the same transaction. Paragano's post-petition contact asserting a void lien is precisely the kind of conduct that invokes unclean hands as a defense to the stay relief motion.

**4. Bad Faith Weighing Against Stay Relief:**

Courts have recognized that a creditor's bad faith conduct — including conduct after the bankruptcy filing — is a factor weighing against granting stay relief. The same conduct that establishes a willful stay violation (knowledge of filing + intentional assertion of the void attachment) demonstrates that Nagel/Paragano are not acting in good faith with respect to this Court.

**5. Practical Argument:**

This Court should not lift the stay to return this matter to a state court forum where Nagel and Paragano have already demonstrated they will act in disregard of the bankruptcy process. Lifting the stay rewards the very conduct — the unauthorized post-petition assertion of a void lien — that § 362(k) is designed to deter.

> **RESEARCH NOTE:** The supervising attorney should search specifically for cases from the Third Circuit or D.N.J. using terms "stay relief" + "denied" + "creditor violated" or "prior stay violation" + "equitable" + "unclean hands." No specific published Third Circuit case was found through available search tools holding that a prior stay violation precludes stay relief. This argument should be framed as an equitable discretion argument under Curtis/Sonnax factor 12 and the balance of harms, supplemented by unclean hands doctrine.

### C-5. Sanctions Under § 362(k): Damages and Cases

**Statutory Measure of Damages:**

Under 11 U.S.C. § 362(k)(1), an injured individual shall recover:
- Actual damages (including costs and attorneys' fees)
- Emotional distress damages (recognized by the Third Circuit in *In re Lansaw*)
- Punitive damages "in appropriate circumstances"

**Third Circuit Damages Precedent:**

**In re Lansaw, 853 F.3d 657 (3d Cir. 2017):**

The Third Circuit in *Lansaw* affirmed an award of:
- $2,600 in attorneys' fees
- $7,500 in emotional distress damages
- $40,000 in punitive damages

The Court held:

> "actual damages" under § 362(k)(1) include damages for emotional distress resulting from a willful violation of the automatic stay.

*In re Lansaw*, 853 F.3d at 669.

The Court further held that where stay violations are "patently egregious," a plaintiff's credible testimony that the violations caused emotional distress is sufficient without medical evidence. *Id.*

**Punitive Damages Standard:**

Punitive damages are available "in appropriate circumstances" under § 362(k)(1). Courts have awarded punitive damages where the stay violation was egregious, willful, and designed to coerce the debtor. A law firm partner directly contacting a represented debtor to assert a void lien, after the firm's partner-colleague acknowledged the bankruptcy to the court, presents a compelling case for punitive damages.

**Joint and Several Liability for Law Firm:**

Both Nagel (the client-creditor) and the law firm (Paragano's firm) are subject to joint and several liability for the stay violation. "Clients are held accountable for the acts and omissions of their attorneys," and law firms can be held jointly and severally liable for their attorneys' stay violations. **[CITATION NEEDED — "In re Shaw (D.N.J.)" was a hallucination; verified D.N.J./3d Cir. authority for law firm joint-and-several liability under § 362(k) must be obtained from Westlaw before filing.]**

**What Conduct Triggered Sanctions (Illustrative Cases):**

1. **Direct post-petition contact asserting pre-petition claim** → § 362(a)(6) violation → actual damages + attorneys' fees
2. **Post-petition assertion of a lien or attachment** → § 362(a)(4)/(5) violation → actual damages + potential punitive damages
3. **Egregious or repeated violations** → punitive damages (*Lansaw* — $40,000 punitive damages for landlord's multiple violations)
4. **Law firm involvement** → joint and several liability for firm and client

**Application to Brad's Facts:**

Paragano's June 18 email presents the following violations:
- § 362(a)(1): continuation of a state court action (asserting rights derived from it)
- § 362(a)(4): act to enforce lien (the prejudgment attachment) against estate property
- § 362(a)(5): act to enforce lien securing pre-petition claim
- § 362(a)(6): act to collect pre-petition claim

Each violation is independently actionable. The combination — particularly the assertion of a void lien from a dismissed action, by a law partner of the attorney who had just acknowledged the bankruptcy to this Court — is egregious and supports both actual and punitive damages.
