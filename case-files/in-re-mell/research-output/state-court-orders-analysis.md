## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Nagel's Motion to Dismiss (Doc. 8) deploys five state court orders from B.B. v. Mell, ESX-L-7200-19 (Essex County Superior Court) as if they carry binding or operative force in this federal bankruptcy proceeding. They do not. Two independent and reinforcing bodies of law defeat this strategy entirely.

**Federal law** establishes that state court orders entered in a separate civil action have no operative force in an independent federal bankruptcy proceeding. This is not a removed case. The mechanism for carrying over state court orders — 28 U.S.C. § 1450 — applies only to removed cases. No comparable mechanism exists here. Each of Nagel's five orders must be evaluated as a factual claim, not as a legally operative directive, and each fails that analysis. Pseudonymous status in federal court requires a federal court order, not importation of a state court confidentiality designation. Sanctions orders and interlocutory rulings are not "final judgments" for collateral estoppel purposes.

**New Jersey law** provides an independent and equally fatal ground: Judge Zunic dismissed B.B. v. Mell without prejudice on June 12, 2026 — the same day Brad Mell filed his bankruptcy petition. The dismissal order does not mention or preserve any prior orders. Under New Jersey procedural law, a dismissal without prejudice terminates all outstanding interlocutory orders, attachments, and protective orders unless the dismissal order specifically preserves them. The Zunic order preserves nothing. Every order Nagel relies on died with the case.

**The synthesis:** These two independent arguments should be deployed together. The federal law argument operates regardless of what NJ law says (state court orders cannot govern federal proceedings). The NJ law argument operates regardless of the federal law point (the orders do not even survive in state court). Either argument alone is sufficient to neutralize Nagel's order-based arguments. Together, they are conclusive.

**Strongest argument for oral argument lead:** The NJ dismissal point is concrete, factual, and impossible to dispute — Nagel filed his motion eleven days after the dismissal order and never mentions it. This omission from Doc. 8 may independently warrant candor sanctions under RPC 3.3.

## PART II: FEDERAL LAW — STATE COURT ORDERS HAVE NO OPERATIVE FORCE IN INDEPENDENT FEDERAL PROCEEDINGS

### Subsection A: The General Principle — State Court Orders Do Not Travel to Independent Federal Courts

#### A.1 — The Removal Statute Framework Makes the Rule Clear

The only federal mechanism for carrying state court orders into a federal proceeding is found in **28 U.S.C. § 1450**, which provides:

> "Whenever any action is removed from a State court to a district court of the United States, any attachment or sequestration of the goods or estate of the defendant in such action in the State court shall hold the goods or estate to answer the final judgment or decree in the same manner as they were held to answer final judgment or decree in the State court, if they were held to answer final judgment or decree in the State court. All bonds, undertakings or security given by either party in such action prior to its removal shall remain valid and effectual notwithstanding such removal. All injunctions, orders, and other proceedings had in such action prior to its removal shall remain in full force and effect until dissolved or modified by the district court."

**Critically, § 1450 applies exclusively to removed cases.** The statute uses the word "removed" throughout. Its entire purpose is to preserve continuity in a case that transitions from state to federal court — the same case, the same parties, on the same claims.

This is NOT a removed case. B.B. v. Mell was not removed from Essex County Superior Court to this Court. This is a **separate, independent federal bankruptcy proceeding** initiated by Brad Mell's Chapter 7 petition on June 12, 2026. There is no § 1450 mechanism that carries the state court's orders into this proceeding. **The absence of a removal does not merely mean § 1450 doesn't apply — it means there is no mechanism at all for state court orders to have operative force here.** [VERIFY BEFORE FILING — confirm § 1450 text against official U.S. Code; confirm courts interpreting § 1450 as limited to removed cases]

#### A.2 — Federal Courts Have Independent Authority; They Are Not Bound by Orders of Other Courts in Separate Proceedings

A federal court operates under federal rules and federal authority. It is not bound by orders entered in a different case — whether in state or federal court. The principle is elementary and derives from the structure of the federal judicial system:

- State court injunctions and orders bind the parties before the state court in the state proceeding. When that proceeding ends (or when the parties are before a different court in a different proceeding), those orders have no continuing operative force as orders.
- A state court order can be treated as a fact (establishing that something was ordered), or its findings may be given preclusive effect through the doctrines of res judicata and collateral estoppel — but only when all the elements of those doctrines are met. (See Subsection C below on why those elements are not met here.)
- A state court order cannot, merely by virtue of having been entered, compel conduct in a separate federal proceeding.

**Application here:** Nagel treats the state court orders as if this Court is bound to give them the same force as if they had been entered by this Court itself. This is incorrect. The state court's January 2020 attachment order, the March 2021 partial summary judgment, the March 2024 sanctions order, the March 2025 contact-barring order, and the confidentiality orders are all orders in B.B. v. Mell. This Court is not a continuation of that proceeding. [VERIFY BEFORE FILING — confirm with Third Circuit cases addressing this principle directly]

#### A.3 — The Automatic Stay Further Subordinates State Court Proceedings

Once Brad Mell filed his Chapter 7 petition on June 12, 2026, the automatic stay under **11 U.S.C. § 362(a)** took effect. The stay:

> "operates as a stay, applicable to all entities, of — ... (4) any act to create, perfect, or enforce any lien against property of the estate; ... (6) any act to collect, assess, or recover a claim against the debtor that arose before the commencement of the case under this title."

11 U.S.C. § 362(a)(4), (6).

The state court's prior orders do not supersede the automatic stay. To the contrary: the automatic stay supersedes prior state court orders insofar as those orders would require action inconsistent with the stay. Paragano's June 18, 2026 email asserting the attachment and instructing Brad not to move the Ferrari is a post-petition act to enforce a lien against estate property — a direct § 362(a)(4) violation, with consequences analyzed in Part IV.

### Subsection B: Pseudonymity in Federal Court Requires a Federal Court Order

#### B.1 — Third Circuit Standard: Doe v. Megless, 654 F.3d 404 (3d Cir. 2011)

**Citation:** Doe v. Megless, 654 F.3d 404 (3d Cir. 2011)  
**Court:** United States Court of Appeals, Third Circuit  
**Status:** BINDING — Third Circuit, directly controlling in D.N.J.  
**Source:** [VERIFY BEFORE FILING — confirm via CourtListener or Justia]

In Megless, the Third Circuit addressed the standard a federal court must apply when a party seeks to proceed pseudonymously. The court recognized that under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 10(a), parties must identify themselves in the caption of a federal complaint. Proceeding pseudonymously is a departure from the default rule of judicial transparency.

The Third Circuit held that a party seeking pseudonymous status in federal court must make a showing to the federal court applying a balancing test that weighs:
- Factors favoring anonymity (including fear of violence, retaliation, and privacy interests in sensitive matters)
- Factors disfavoring anonymity (including the public interest in open proceedings, prejudice to the opposing party, and the court's interest in administering justice in open proceedings)

**Key proposition:** The federal court — not any other court — makes the determination on pseudonymous status in federal proceedings. A party cannot proceed pseudonymously in federal court based on a state court confidentiality order. The party must move the federal court.

**Application here:** B.B. has not filed any motion in this Court seeking leave to proceed pseudonymously. Nagel acknowledges this in Doc. 8, noting she has not moved to seal but promises to do so "if the Court asks." That is backwards: the burden is on B.B. to move this Court, not to wait for the Court to request a motion. Until this Court grants pseudonymous status, B.B. cannot rely on a state court confidentiality designation as her authority for using "B.B." in federal filings.

**Additional point:** The state court confidentiality orders terminated when B.B. v. Mell was dismissed on June 12, 2026. See Part III. Even if those orders had not terminated, they would have no force in this independent federal proceeding.

#### B.2 — Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 10(a)

> "Every pleading must have a caption with the court's name, a title, a file number, and a Rule 7(a) designation. The title of the complaint must name all the parties; the title of other pleadings, after naming the first party on each side, may refer generally to other parties."

Fed. R. Civ. P. 10(a). The exception to public identification requires a federal court order. Nagel cannot substitute a state court's confidentiality order for the required federal court order.

### Subsection C: Sanctions Orders and Interlocutory Rulings — No Preclusive Effect

#### C.1 — Third Circuit Collateral Estoppel Standard: In re Docteroff, 133 F.3d 210 (3d Cir. 1997)

**Citation:** Wolstein v. Docteroff (In re Docteroff), 133 F.3d 210 (3d Cir. 1997)  
**Court:** United States Court of Appeals, Third Circuit  
**Status:** BINDING — Third Circuit, directly controlling in D.N.J.

The Third Circuit in Docteroff held that collateral estoppel in bankruptcy proceedings (including § 523(a) proceedings) requires all of the following elements:

1. **Identical issue:** The issue decided in the prior proceeding must be identical to the issue now presented
2. **Actually litigated:** The issue must have been actually litigated — not defaulted, not resolved by sanction
3. **Essential to the judgment:** The determination must have been essential (not dicta or alternative grounds) to the prior judgment
4. **Final judgment:** The prior proceeding must have produced a final judgment
5. **Full and fair opportunity:** The party against whom preclusion is asserted must have had a full and fair opportunity to litigate

**The "actually litigated" requirement:** The Third Circuit held at 133 F.3d at 215 that the requirement that an issue be "actually litigated" typically bars application of collateral estoppel to default judgments. The court recognized a limited exception for cases where a party actively participated in litigation and then willfully obstructed discovery as a deliberate strategy — but held that exception requires a party who had a full and fair opportunity to litigate and chose bad-faith obstruction.

**Why this exception does not apply to Brad Mell:** Brad invoked his Fifth Amendment rights in civil discovery. This is a constitutionally protected act. The invocation of a constitutional privilege is categorically different from the deliberate bad-faith obstruction at issue in Docteroff. See Baxter v. Palmigiano, 425 U.S. 308 (1976) (civil courts may draw adverse inferences from Fifth Amendment invocations but this is not equivalent to a merits finding).

#### C.2 — The March 26, 2021 Partial Summary Judgment Is Not a "Final Judgment"

The NJ Appellate Division expressly held in its December 31, 2024 opinion (A-2990-21, B.B. v. Mell):

> "[2] Although B.B.'s counsel repeatedly notes B.B. has already obtained judgment for liability on several of her claims, **the order for partial summary judgment is interlocutory and not a final judgment.** It is not clear from the record whether Mell responded to the summary judgment motion."

This is dispositive on the "final judgment" element. The NJ Appellate Division — the court with direct appellate jurisdiction over this very order — says it is interlocutory and not a final judgment. Nagel cannot argue in this Court that the PSJ is a "final judgment" when the intermediate appellate court that reviewed it expressly held the opposite.

Collateral estoppel requires a final judgment. Without a final judgment, there is no preclusion. The March 2021 PSJ cannot establish willful and malicious injury through collateral estoppel.

#### C.3 — The March 2024 Sanctions Order Is Not a Merits Adjudication

The March 2024 sanctions order sanctioned Brad for publishing names in violation of the state court's confidentiality orders. This is a conduct/procedural sanction. Its preclusive status:

- **Identical issue:** A sanctions order for publishing names does not adjudicate the "identical issue" of whether Brad's bankruptcy filing constitutes § 707(a) bad faith. These are completely different issues. A party may be sanctioned for litigation misconduct and still be a legitimate Chapter 7 debtor.

- **Not a final judgment:** A sanctions order entered in ongoing litigation is not a final judgment on the merits of the action. It is a mid-case procedural order.

- **Actually litigated?** A sanctions order may resolve the issue of whether a party violated a court order, but it does not resolve whether that party's later bankruptcy filing is in bad faith. The "identical issue" requirement fails before the "actually litigated" inquiry is even reached.

**The § 707(a) inquiry is independent:** Even if every state court finding about Brad's conduct were accepted as undisputed fact, this Court must independently apply the Tamecki "egregious cases" standard to determine whether the bankruptcy filing warrants dismissal. A state court's view of Brad's state court litigation conduct does not bind this Court's § 707(a) analysis. The state court never asked — and never answered — whether Brad's bankruptcy filing was in bad faith under federal bankruptcy law.

#### C.4 — The March 2025 Contact-Barring Order: Same Defects

The March 2025 order barring Brad from contacting B.B.'s counsel directly suffers from identical preclusion defects:
- It is a conduct order in an ongoing proceeding (now dismissed), not a final judgment
- The issue it resolves (whether Brad violated a no-contact directive in state court litigation) is not identical to § 707(a) bad faith
- It was never "actually litigated" in the sense required for preclusion
- Its preclusive scope — if any — is limited to re-litigation of the underlying conduct it addressed, not to new federal bankruptcy issues

#### C.5 — In re Azeglio, 422 B.R. 490 (Bankr. D.N.J. 2010)

**Citation:** In re Azeglio, 422 B.R. 490 (Bankr. D.N.J. 2010)  
**Court:** United States Bankruptcy Court, District of New Jersey  
**Status:** Persuasive authority — directly on point in D.N.J.  
**Source:** [VERIFY BEFORE FILING — confirm via CourtListener or Justia]

The Azeglio court held that a default judgment does not satisfy the "actually litigated" requirement for collateral estoppel purposes in § 523 bankruptcy proceedings. A judgment entered because a party failed to appear or respond is not a judgment entered after the issues were contested and decided on the merits.

**Application:** If the March 2021 PSJ was entered under R. 4:23-5(a)(2) after Brad invoked his Fifth Amendment rights and the NJ court applied the rule mechanically without holding any evidentiary hearing, then under Azeglio the PSJ is akin to a default judgment for preclusion purposes — the willfulness and malice elements of § 523(a)(6) were never actually litigated.

## PART III: NEW JERSEY LAW — STATE COURT ORDERS DO NOT SURVIVE DISMISSAL

### Subsection A: The General Rule — Dismissal Terminates Interlocutory Orders

#### A.1 — N.J. Court Rule 4:37-1 (Voluntary Dismissal)

**Text:**  
Rule 4:37-1(a) permits voluntary dismissal "without court order" by filing a notice of dismissal before an adverse party serves an answer or moves for summary judgment. R. 4:37-1(b) permits voluntary dismissal by court order "on such terms and conditions as the court deems proper."

[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — confirm current text at njcourts.gov/court-rules]

#### A.2 — N.J. Court Rule 4:37-2 (Involuntary Dismissal)

**Text:**  
Rule 4:37-2 governs court-ordered dismissal. R. 4:37-2(d) provides: "Unless the court in its order for dismissal otherwise specifies, a dismissal under this rule and any dismissal not provided for in these rules, other than a dismissal for lack of jurisdiction, for improper venue or for failure to join a party under Rule 4:28, operates as an adjudication upon the merits."

**Critical implication:** The rule's use of "unless the court in its order for dismissal otherwise specifies" is the operative language. Here, the Zunic dismissal order **does not specify** that any prior orders are preserved. Under the plain language of the rule, the dismissal is as entered — nothing more. There is no savings clause for any of Nagel's five orders.

[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — confirm current R. 4:37-2 text and that the Zunic order contains no preservation language]

#### A.3 — General New Jersey Principle: A Dismissal Terminates Interlocutory Orders

Under general New Jersey procedural principles, a court's dismissal of an action terminates the court's jurisdiction to enforce interlocutory orders entered in that action. Once a case is dismissed:
- The court no longer has an active docket
- There are no parties before the court on whom interlocutory orders can operate
- Interlocutory orders (which by definition did not finally resolve the case) have no independent life after the case ends, unless specifically preserved

The key distinction is between **final judgments** (which survive dismissal because they represent the court's final resolution of the matter and may be enforced like any judgment) and **interlocutory orders** (which are procedural directives to parties during ongoing litigation and expire when the litigation ends).

Of Nagel's five orders:
- The prejudgment attachment is not a final judgment — it is an interlocutory protective measure
- The partial summary judgment on liability is expressly characterized as interlocutory by the NJ Appellate Division
- The sanctions order is a procedural/conduct order in ongoing litigation
- The contact-barring order is a conduct order in ongoing litigation
- The confidentiality orders are protective orders in ongoing litigation

None of these are final judgments. All are interlocutory. All terminated when Judge Zunic dismissed B.B. v. Mell without prejudice on June 12, 2026, without preserving them.

### Subsection B: The Prejudgment Attachment Does Not Survive Dismissal

#### B.1 — N.J.S.A. 2A:26-1 et seq. — The NJ Attachment Statute

New Jersey's attachment statute is found at N.J.S.A. 2A:26-1 et seq. Prejudgment attachment under NJ law:
- Is an ancillary remedy — it exists to secure a potential judgment that has not yet been entered
- Requires an underlying action; when the underlying action is dismissed, the attachment has no action to secure
- Is not a final judgment; it does not independently establish liability or create a lien that survives the dismissal of the underlying action

**The January 27, 2020 attachment order** was entered in B.B. v. Mell. It attached funds in escrow as security for a potential judgment in that action. That action was dismissed on June 12, 2026. The attachment was a pre-judgment measure — the "judgment" it was securing never came. Without a final judgment, the attachment cannot be enforced as a judgment.

### Subsection E: The Zunic "Revival Instructions" — Does This Change the Analysis?

The Zunic order (June 12, 2026) includes "revival instructions" permitting B.B. to re-file the state court action after the bankruptcy court resolves the matter. This is a common mechanism in NJ practice when a stay — including a bankruptcy stay — interrupts pending litigation.

**Does this preserve the prior orders?**  
No, for the following reasons:

1. **The revival instructions preserve the right to refile, not the prior orders:** The function of a "with leave to revive" dismissal is to toll applicable statutes of limitations and preserve the parties' substantive claims for refiling. It is not a mechanism for suspending all prior orders until the case is revived. The orders either survive independently or they do not — the revival instruction addresses claims, not orders.

2. **The dismissal order does not mention preservation:** If the Zunic court intended to preserve specific prior orders — particularly the attachment, which is an active measure with financial consequences — the court would have said so. Courts routinely include preservation language when they intend interim orders to continue pending refiling. The absence of such language here is telling.

3. **NJ practice confirms this reading:** Revival instructions in NJ practice operate to allow the case to be re-opened and for the court to then re-enter whatever orders are appropriate in the revived proceeding. They do not operate as a perpetual suspension of all prior orders pending revival.

4. **The bankruptcy court is the forum for interim protection:** If B.B. believes interim protection of the attached funds or other relief is necessary during the pendency of the bankruptcy, the appropriate vehicle is a motion in this Court — for relief from the automatic stay, for an order protecting specific assets, or for other appropriate relief. The NJ revival instructions do not substitute for this.

## PART IV: SANCTIONS ORDERS AS CONTRIBUTING CAUSE OF BANKRUPTCY

### A. The Framework: Legitimate Purpose for Bankruptcy Relief

Under Third Circuit law, the § 707(a) inquiry asks whether the filing was made in bad faith — but this requires examining the totality of circumstances, including whether the debtor had legitimate reasons for seeking bankruptcy relief. In re Tamecki, 229 F.3d 205, 207 (3d Cir. 2000). Dismissal is reserved for "egregious cases." The debtor need only show an honest purpose; he is not required to show perfect timing.

In re Perlin, 497 F.3d 364 (3d Cir. 2007) held that ability to pay alone does not establish bad faith, and that the court should consider the debtor's "entire course of conduct — before, during, and after the filing." 497 F.3d at 372. The course of conduct inquiry cuts both ways: it permits the debtor to show that financial distress accumulated over time and resulted legitimately in a Chapter 7 filing.

### B. The Argument: Cumulative Litigation Burden as Legitimate Cause

**The timeline of escalating state court burdens on Brad Mell:**

| Date | Event | Financial/Practical Burden |
|------|-------|---------------------------|
| October 2019 | B.B. v. Mell filed | Defense costs begin |
| January 2020 | Prejudgment attachment | $1M in escrow frozen; cannot access funds |
| March 2021 | Partial summary judgment (discovery sanction) | Defense burden escalates; effectively liability-found on key counts |
| Through 2021-2024 | Multiple interlocutory appeals, discovery disputes | Years of ongoing defense costs |
| March 2024 | Sanctions order | Direct financial sanction; additional legal burden |
| March 2025 | Contact-barring order | Additional compliance burden; reputational impact |
| Through 2026 | Trial preparation for damages | Imminent risk of multi-million dollar damages verdict |
| June 12, 2026 | Bankruptcy petition filed | After 6.5 years of litigation and mounting financial pressure |

**The argument:** Brad's Chapter 7 filing is not the product of bad faith evasion of a single tort claimant. It is the product of six-and-a-half years of escalating litigation, mounting defense costs, a frozen $1M in escrow, sanctions, and the imminent prospect of a damages trial. These are precisely the cumulative financial burdens that bankruptcy relief is designed to address.

The state court's own orders — particularly the attachment (January 2020), the sanctions (March 2024), and the contact-barring order (March 2025) — are not evidence of Brad's bad faith. They are contributing causes of Brad's financial distress. The fact that a debtor has been subject to sanctions and adverse orders in prior litigation does not make his eventual bankruptcy filing bad faith. If anything, it explains and contextualizes why he needed bankruptcy protection.

### C. Authority Supporting Litigation-Cost Driven Bankruptcy

Courts have recognized that cumulative litigation costs, attorney fee awards, and litigation-driven financial distress constitute legitimate precipitating causes for bankruptcy:

**The general principle:** Legitimate discharge purposes under Chapter 7 include debts arising from civil litigation, fee awards, judgments, and other litigation-related obligations. A debtor who files to obtain discharge of a tort judgment is not per se acting in bad faith, even if the judgment is large and involves a single creditor. The § 707(a) bad faith inquiry looks to the conduct of the filing, not the nature of the debt being discharged.

**Application:** Brad is not filing to discharge a "judgment" — the state court has never entered a final judgment, and the damages amount is unknown. He is filing in the face of multi-year litigation exposure, frozen assets, mounting legal costs, and the prospect of an unquantified damages verdict. This is a textbook legitimate use of Chapter 7.

### D. Distinguishing the "Eve of Trial" Argument

Nagel's central narrative is that Brad filed "on the eve of trial" in bad faith. The cumulative litigation burden argument provides two counter-narratives:

1. **Cause:** The filing was not driven by a desire to avoid the trial — it was driven by six-and-a-half years of accumulating financial pressure, of which the trial was the final precipitating event.

2. **The orders caused the bankruptcy:** The very sanctions Nagel deploys as "bad faith" evidence are, more accurately, among the causes of Brad's financial distress that necessitated bankruptcy. A party who is sanctioned repeatedly in expensive litigation and then files for bankruptcy protection has not committed bad faith — he has responded to his financial situation with the tool Congress provided.

## PART V: SYNTHESIS — HOW TO DEPLOY THESE ARGUMENTS

### Argument Architecture

These arguments are not alternatives — they are layers. Each layer independently defeats Nagel's reliance on the state court orders, and together they present an airtight framework.

**Layer 1 (Lead, oral argument opener):** The state court action was dismissed on June 12, 2026. Every order Nagel cites comes from a case that no longer exists. Nagel filed his motion eleven days after the dismissal without mentioning the dismissal once. This Court is being asked to rely on orders from a dismissed case.

**Layer 2 (Federal law, independent):** Even if B.B. v. Mell had not been dismissed, the orders would have no operative force in this independent federal bankruptcy proceeding. This is not a removed case. 28 U.S.C. § 1450 does not apply. The automatic stay supersedes state court actions from the moment of the petition. State court orders cannot govern federal court procedure or supply the equivalent of a federal court order on pseudonymous status.

**Layer 3 (Preclusion, for § 523 and § 707 arguments):** Even if the orders somehow had operative force here, they lack preclusive effect. The PSJ is interlocutory (NJ App. Div. says so explicitly). The sanctions orders are not merits adjudications. The "actually litigated" requirement of Docteroff is not met. The § 707(a) inquiry is independent of any state court finding.

**Layer 4 (Affirmative counter-narrative):** The very orders Nagel cites as bad faith evidence are themselves contributing causes of Brad's need for bankruptcy relief. The escalating litigation burden — attachments, sanctions, compliance costs, frozen assets — is what drove Brad to seek Chapter 7 protection, not a strategic desire to evade the trial.

### Candor Issues (RPC 3.3 / Rule 9011)

Nagel's Doc. 8 was filed on June 23, 2026 — eleven days after the Zunic dismissal. Doc. 8 makes no mention of the June 12, 2026 dismissal of B.B. v. Mell. It refers throughout to the state court orders as if they are currently operative in an active case.
