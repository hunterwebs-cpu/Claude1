## 2. NAGEL'S EXACT STATEMENT OF THE § 707(A) BAD FAITH STANDARD

> "The Bankruptcy Code permits a court to dismiss a Chapter 7 bankruptcy case for 'cause.' 11 U.S.C. § 707(a).[3] Although this word is not defined, 'cause' for purposes of § 707(a) includes a debtor's lack of 'good faith' (or stated differently, 'bad faith') in filing his or her bankruptcy petition." In re DeSoto, No. 05 29744 DHS, 2006 WL 4452833, at *3 (Bankr. D.N.J. Apr. 20, 2006) (quotation omitted). "Thus, a Chapter 7 case may be dismissed 'for cause' if the debtor fails to demonstrate good faith in the filing of the bankruptcy petition." Id., citing In re Tamecki, 229 F.3d 205, 207 (3d Cir. 2000). See also In re Jones, 114 B.R. 917, 926 (Bankr. N.D. Ohio 1990) ("Although the jurisdictional requirement of good faith is not explicitly stated in the statute, it is inherent in the purposes of bankruptcy relief"). Dismissal may also result from "circumstances falling short of bad faith but nevertheless representing an inappropriate use of the Code." In re Murray, 543 B.R. 484, 490 and n.32 (Bankr. S.D.N.Y. 2016), aff'd, 565 B.R. 527 (S.D.N.Y. 2017), aff'd, 900 F.3d 53 (2d Cir. 2018) (collecting cases).

> "[G]ood faith requires at least a showing of honest intention." In re Hunt, 521 B.R. at 485. Notably, "merely because a course of action is legal" — or was taken on the advice of third-party professionals — "does not mean that the action equates with good faith." In re Cantelli, No. 12-30748, 2013 WL 66425, at *10 (Bankr. N.D. Ohio Jan. 4, 2013).

**Five-factor bad faith test (from In re Glunk):**

> "Factors to be considered in determining lack of good faith include, for example, (1) the debtor's (extravagant) lifestyle and ability to pay; (2) the disproportionate impact that bankruptcy relief would have on one particular creditor, or only a few creditors, as compared to other creditors; (3) forum shopping or efforts to manipulate the judicial process to thwart the orderly determination of a creditor claim pending in another court; (4) prepetition fraudulent conduct to place assets beyond the reach of creditors or less than full and candid disclosure in the bankruptcy process itself; and (5) an end result, if bankruptcy relief is permitted, that is perceived to be fundamentally unfair or excessive. In re Glunk, 342 B.R. 717, 734 (Bankr. E.D. Pa. 2006)."

**Totality of circumstances standard:**

> "In determining whether the filing was made in good faith, courts 'must decide whether the petitioner has abused the provisions, purpose, or spirit of bankruptcy law.' In re Tamecki, 229 F.3d at 207. The 'good faith' determination is made based on 'the totality of the facts and circumstances.' In re Hunt, 521 B.R. at 483."

**Burden-shifting:**

> "'Once a party calls into question a petitioner's good faith, the burden shifts to the petitioner to prove his good faith.' In re Tamecki, 229 F.3d at 207."

**Footnote 3:**

> "[3] The three specific examples provided by § 707(a) are 'non-exclusive examples of "cause" meriting dismissal.' In re Hunt, 521 B.R. 479, 483 (Bankr. M.D. Pa. 2014). See also, e.g., In re Zick, 931 F.2d 1124, 1126-27 (6th Cir. 1991)."

## 3. NAGEL'S EXACT STATEMENT OF THE § 523(A)(6) STANDARD

> "The Bankruptcy Code provides that any debt 'for willful and malicious injury by the debtor to another entity' is not dischargeable in bankruptcy. 11 U.S.C. § 523(a)(6).[4] '[A]n injury is willful and malicious pursuant to § 523(a)(6) '... only if the actor purposefully inflicted the injury or acted with substantial certainty that injury would result.'' In re McHugh, No. 11-14612 GMB, 2012 WL 2930792, at *5 (Bankr. D.N.J. July 18, 2012), quoting In re Conte, 33 F.3d 303, 305 (3d Cir. 1994). Thus, '[c]laims based on injury caused by intentional acts done to cause injury are typically non-dischargeable under 11 U.S.C. § 523(a)(6).' In re Berlingeri, 246 B.R. 196, 201 (Bankr. D.N.J. 2000), abrogated on other grounds as recognized by In re Ruitenberg, 745 F.3d 647, 651 (3d Cir. 2014)."

**Nagel's quotation from In re Fleetwood on sexual abuse of minors:**

> "Where the damages arise from sexual abuse, willfulness is apparent because the subjective motive to inflict harm 'is self evident, either because the tortfeasor knows his conduct is certain or almost certain to cause harm, or because he should know and therefore the intent is inferred as a matter of law.' Such inference flows from the 'generally accepted conception of harm as being inherent in the act of sexually abusing a child.' … [Moreover,] the nature of the wrongful act necessarily establishes malice because sexual abuse of a minor is inherently an intentional, wrongful act which unquestionably causes damage. Nor can there be any cause or excuse for sexual abuse … malice may be inferred from a wrongful act if it is established that the act was willful."
> In re Fleetwood, No. A12-00113-GS, 2013 WL 2178096, at *6 (Bankr. D. Alaska May 17, 2013) (footnotes and quotations omitted).

**Footnote 4:**

> "[4] 'The term "entity" includes person, estate, trust, governmental unit, and United States trustee.' 11 U.S.C. § 101(15)."

## 6. NAGEL'S EXACT ARGUMENT ON ABSTENTION

### Mandatory Abstention — Cited Statute:

Nagel cites **28 U.S.C. § 1134(c)(2)** for mandatory abstention. This is incorrect — the correct citation is **28 U.S.C. § 1334(c)(2)**. Section 1134 does not exist in Title 28; the bankruptcy jurisdiction statute is § 1334.

**Nagel's verbatim statement of the mandatory abstention test:**

> "Mandatory abstention pursuant to 28 U.S.C. § 1134(c)(2) by a district court is appropriate when five requirements are met: (1) the proceeding is based on a state law claim or cause of action; (2) the claim or cause of action is 'related to' a case under title 11, but does not 'arise under' title 11 and does not 'arise in' a case under title 11, (3) federal courts would not have jurisdiction over the claim but for its relation to a bankruptcy case; (4) an action 'is commenced' in a state forum of appropriate jurisdiction; and (5) the action can be 'timely adjudicated' in a state forum of appropriate jurisdiction."
> Stoe v. Flaherty, 436 F.3d 209, 213 (3d Cir. 2006), as amended (Mar. 17, 2006).

**Application of Five Factors:**

> "1) The first factor is satisfied because all eighteen counts of the SAC assert causes of action based on common law or New Jersey statutes, see Nagel Cert. at Exh. 'A' at Counts 1– 18."

> "2) The State Court Action does not 'arise under' Title 11 because none of 'the claims asserted in the matter are predicated on a right created or determined by title 11.' In re Mid-Atl. Handling Sys., LLC, 304 B.R. 111, 120 (Bankr. D.N.J. 2003). None of B.B.'s claims satisfy this criterion. However, the State Court Action is 'related to' a case under title 11 – namely, this matter — because it is a 'non-core' proceeding[] … whose outcome could conceivably have an effect on the administration of the bankruptcy estate.' Id. Since B.B. already has a judgment of liability against Mell – and all that is left to be determined is the quantum of damages — the outcome of the State Court Action will clearly effect the administration of the bankruptcy estate."

> "3) The Third factor is satisfied because there would be no federal jurisdiction absent this bankruptcy action. B.B. does not move under any federal statute or assert any federal cause of action. Nor is there any diversity between her and any of the defendants.[6]"

> "4) The fourth factor is satisfied because the State Court Action was commenced in a state forum of appropriate jurisdiction."

> "5) The fifth factor is satisfied because the State Court Action can be timely adjudicated. Indeed, but for Mell filing his bankruptcy petition, the trial would have already begun."

**Footnote 5:**

> "[5] '[A] core proceeding invokes a substantive right provided by title 11 or by its nature, could arise only in the context of a bankruptcy case … In contrast, a non-core proceeding belongs to the broader universe of all proceedings that are not core proceedings but are nevertheless related to a bankruptcy case.' In re Mid-Atl. Handling, 304 B.R. at 122 (quotations and internal quotation marks omitted). The State Court Action is obviously not a core proceeding."

**Footnote 6:**

> "[6] While Mell was living a Pennsylvania prison at the time the State Court Action was filed, he was a resident of New Jersey at the time all the operative and underlying facts took place. Nagel Cert. at ¶ 2."

### Permissive Abstention — Cited Statute:

Nagel again cites **28 U.S.C. § 1134(c)(1)** — same error, should be § 1334(c)(1).

**Nagel's verbatim statement on permissive abstention:**

> "Permissive abstention is provided for by 28 U.S.C. § 1134(c)(1), which provides that 'nothing in this section prevents a district court in the interest of justice, or in the interest of comity with State courts or respect for State law, from abstaining from hearing a particular proceeding arising under title 11 or arising in or related to a case under title 11.' When deciding to exercise discretion by abstaining from a matter, a court will consider among the following factors: 1) the effect on the efficient administration of the bankruptcy estate; 2) the extent to which issues of state law predominate; 3) the difficulty or unsettled nature of the applicable state law; 4) comity; 5) the degree of relatedness or remoteness of the proceeding to the main bankruptcy case; 6) the existence of the right to a jury trial; and 7) prejudice to the involuntarily removed defendants."
> In re Mid-Atl. Handling, 304 B.R. at 126.

**Application:**

> "Here, the interests of justice support permissive abstention: State law completely predominates, as the State court Action claims are based entirely on common law and New Jersey statutory causes of action; the State Court Action is remote from the instant action. Most of Mell's creditors are family members or entities he controls (or controlled). B.B.'s case, in contrast, is about Mell's sexual abuse of a minor; B.B. has a right to a jury trial in the State Court Action; and B.B. would be extremely prejudiced by her case not being remanded to state court. That is where she litigated for over seven years. The case is completely prepared for trial, which can begin immediately. Making B.B. compete with other creditors to see her judgment from the State Court Action satisfied will severely hamper her ability to collect from Mell."

## 7. NAGEL'S EXACT ARGUMENT ON STAY RELIEF

> "The Bankruptcy Code permits relief from the automatic stay provided by 11 U.S.C. § 362(a) for 'cause.' 11 U.S.C. § 362(d)(1). The word 'cause' is not defined, but '[m]otions for dismissal and for relief from the stay are governed by like standards.' In re Murray, 543 B.R. at 490 n.30. 'Bad faith has frequently been held to provide sufficient cause to warrant both types of relief.' Id., quoting In re Setzer, 47 B.R. 340, 344 (Bankr. E.D.N.Y. 1985). 'Bankruptcy courts have considerable discretion when determining if stay relief is appropriate.' In re Potter, No. 11-40237 (JNP), 2021 WL 4494263, at *7 (Bankr. D.N.J. Sept. 30, 2021). 'The movant initially bears the burden to assert "cause" for relief.' Id. 'If the movant meets that burden, the ultimate burden of proof … then shifts to the debtor to show why relief should not be granted.' Id."

> "As set forth above, Mell has sought bankruptcy in bad faith. See, supra, § I. Accordingly, this case should be stayed for the same reasons which support dismissal of this case."

**Seven-Factor Test for Stay Relief:**

> "In determining whether to grant relief from the automatic stay so as to permit a party in interest to continue prosecuting a matter in another forum, courts will often rely upon factors that include: whether relief would result in a partial or complete resolution of the issues; lack of any connection with or interference with the bankruptcy case; whether litigation in another forum would prejudice the interests of other creditors; the interests of judicial economy and the expeditious and economical resolution of litigation; whether the parties are ready for trial in the other proceeding; and impact of the stay on the parties and the balance of the harms. In re Mid-Atl. Handling Sys., LLC, 304 B.R. 111, 130 (Bankr. D.N.J. 2003) (paragraph breaks added)."

**Application:**

> "As already explained, there is a judgment of liability against Mell and an attachment of approximately $1 million of his monies held in escrow for a judgment in favor of B.B. Both parties are prepared to begin trial immediately, which will yield a final resolution by determining the quantum of the judgment against Mell and would certainly serve judicial economy. The State Court Action also bears little connection to the instant matter, where the listed creditors are largely closely connected individuals or entities. And most importantly, the equities support lifting the stay. B.B. has litigated for seven years, when Mell filed for bankruptcy on the eve of trial. She deserves her day in court against the person who sexually sexually abused her as a minor."

## 8. FACTUAL MISREPRESENTATIONS IDENTIFIABLE FROM THE RECORD

### Misrepresentation 1 — The § 1134 Citation Error (Recurring)

**Nagel's citation:** "28 U.S.C. § 1134(c)(2)" (mandatory abstention) and "28 U.S.C. § 1134(c)(1)" (permissive abstention).

**Correct citation:** 28 U.S.C. § **1334**(c)(2) and § **1334**(c)(1).

Section 1134 of Title 28 does not exist. The bankruptcy jurisdiction and abstention provision is at 28 U.S.C. § 1334. This error recurs in both mandatory and permissive abstention sections. This is not a dispositive error for purposes of the motion, but may be worth noting in an opposition.

### Misrepresentation 2 — Internal Date Inconsistency on Trial Start Date

**Nagel's brief (Statement of Facts):** "a preemptory trial was scheduled to start on Monday June 15, 2026"

**Nagel's Certification (¶ 10):** "trial on the damages portion of B.B.'s case was finally scheduled to begin on Monday, June 14, 2026"

June 14, 2026 is a Sunday. June 15, 2026 is a Monday. The brief's version (June 15) is internally consistent with "Monday" and with the Court's OSC docketed on June 15. The Certification contains an error (June 14 for the Monday trial date).

Additionally, in the Statement of Facts, Nagel writes: "on Friday, June 12, **2016**, at 2:31 p.m." — the year 2016 is plainly a typographical error for 2026.

### Misrepresentation 3 — Case Number Inconsistency

The Notice of Motion (Doc. 8) states the case number as **26-16843** ("In re Steven Bradley Mell, Case No.: 26-16843"). However, the docket header on the same document reads "Case 26-16834-EJO." This discrepancy between 26-16843 (in the caption block) and 26-16834 (in the CM/ECF docket stamp) appears throughout the document. Drafting counsel should verify the correct case number against the CM/ECF docket.

### Misrepresentation 4 — Characterization of March 26, 2021 Partial Summary Judgment

Throughout the brief, Nagel describes the March 26, 2021 order as a "judgment of liability against Mell" and a "summary judgment against Mell on liability for sexual abuse." This characterization is incomplete in the following respects:

- The Appellate Division in the December 31, 2024 opinion (this same case) expressly stated: "the order for partial summary judgment is interlocutory and not a final judgment." (Footnote 2)
- The Appellate Division also noted: "It is not clear from the record whether Mell responded to the summary judgment motion." If the partial summary judgment was entered under R. 4:23-5(a)(2) as a discovery sanction (as opposed to a merits ruling), Nagel's characterization of it as a merits "judgment of liability" would be inaccurate or at least incomplete.
- The partial summary judgment covered only Counts I, II, III, VII, and VIII of the First Amended Complaint (not the SAC, which was operative at time of trial). Nagel's statement that "B.B. has obtained a judgment of liability against Mell" overstates the finality and scope of the order.

### Misrepresentation 5 — Nagel States Mell Was "Then Fifty-Two (52) Years Old" But the Appellate Division States "Then Fifty-One-Year-Old"

**Nagel's MTD:** "B.B. was fifteen years old when she was first sexually abused by then fifty-two (52) year old Steven Bradley Mell"

**Appellate Division opinion:** B.B. engaged in sex acts with "then fifty-one-year-old 'wealthy businessman' defendant S. Bradley Mell"

This discrepancy (51 vs. 52) may reflect the difference between when the grooming began and when the first sexual act occurred, or may simply be an error. The Appellate Division described him as 51 at the time of the acts. Drafting counsel should verify Mell's birth year against the record.

### Misrepresentation 6 — Description of the Attachment as "Approximately $1 Million"

Nagel repeatedly states B.B. has "a pre-judgment writ of attachment against Mell for approximately $1 million dollars held in her counsel's escrow account." The attachment order (Exh. E, dated January 27, 2020) would need to be reviewed for the precise figure. The 2020 Appellate Division opinion (465 N.J. Super. 331) addressed the attachment. Drafting counsel should verify whether $1 million is the current escrowed amount or the original attached amount, as the attachment predates six additional years of litigation during which the attached amounts may have changed.

### Potential Misrepresentation 7 — Mell's Failure to File Required Documents

Nagel states: "As of this date [June 23, 2026], Mell has not corrected these deficiencies." (Referring to the June 15, 2026 OSC.) This is a factual assertion made eight days after the OSC. Debtor's counsel should verify whether any filings were made between June 15 and June 23, 2026.

## COUNSEL NOTES FOR OPPOSITION DRAFTING

1. **The § 1334 citation error** (Nagel cites § 1134) may be worth noting but is not dispositive. Nagel's mandatory abstention argument should be treated as if correctly citing § 1334(c)(2), and any opposition should address the merits.

2. **Mell's response on the PSJ:** The Appellate Division noted it is "not clear from the record whether Mell responded to the summary judgment motion." If the PSJ was entered as a default/sanction under R. 4:23-5(a)(2) rather than on the merits, that is a critical distinction for § 523(a)(6) non-dischargeability analysis — a default sanction order does not necessarily constitute a merits finding of "willful and malicious" injury.

3. **Nagel argues the § 523(a)(6) claim broadly** but does not address whether the partial summary judgment order itself establishes willfulness and malice under Third Circuit law, or whether that must be separately litigated in an adversary proceeding. The In re Conte / In re McHugh standard requires purposeful infliction or substantial certainty of harm — not just any intentional act.

4. **The "all that is left to be determined is the quantum of damages"** statement in Section IV(a)(2) may be challenged. The comparative fault issue (raised extensively in the Appellate Division opinion), the liability of non-Mell defendants, and the fraudulent transfer claims are all unresolved.

5. **Nagel does not address** 11 U.S.C. § 523(a)(9) (injury caused by debtor operating motor vehicle while intoxicated) or § 523(a)(4) (fraud or defalcation in a fiduciary capacity) — only § 523(a)(6).

6. **No adversary proceeding appears to have been filed.** Non-dischargeability determinations under § 523(a)(6) typically require an adversary proceeding under Fed. R. Bankr. P. 7001(6). The motion appears to seek this relief by motion rather than adversary complaint.
