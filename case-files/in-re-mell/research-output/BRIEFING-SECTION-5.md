## SECTION 5.0: PRELIMINARY NOTE ON PROCEDURAL ARCHITECTURE

Before addressing any of Nagel's four theories of relief, the opposition brief must establish a threshold point that will orient Judge O'Hagan before he reads a single word of the § 707(a) argument. That threshold point is this: Nagel has assembled four mutually incompatible forms of relief in a single motion filed in the main case. The motion is not merely wrong on the merits — it is architecturally incoherent.

Consider what the motion asks. First, it asks this Court to dismiss the Chapter 7 case in its entirety under § 707(a). Second, it asks this Court to declare B.B.'s debt nondischargeable under § 523(a)(6). Third, it asks this Court to lift the automatic stay under § 362(d)(1) so that B.B. may proceed with a damages trial in New Jersey Superior Court. Fourth, it asks this Court to abstain from exercising jurisdiction under 28 U.S.C. § 1334(c) and return the matter to state court. These four requests exist in irreconcilable tension. If the case is dismissed under § 707(a), there is no discharge to object to — the § 523(a)(6) declaration becomes moot the moment the case disappears. If the § 523(a)(6) declaration is granted, the case is retained — meaning dismissal was denied. If the Court abstains and sends the matter to state court, it cannot simultaneously adjudicate the § 523(a)(6) question that requires this Court to find "willful and malicious injury" under federal bankruptcy law. Abstention sends the predicate facts to state court; § 523(a)(6) retains the federal conclusion in this Court — a logical impossibility.

The opposition brief should make this architectural point cleanly and early, in a paragraph following the preliminary statement. It signals to Judge O'Hagan — a former USAO Senior Bankruptcy Counsel who has argued these statutes personally — that the motion is a kitchen-sink document assembled under time pressure without disciplined internal logic. It also frames the structural observation that Nagel's "lead case" — *In re Murray*, 900 F.3d 53 (2d Cir. 2018) — is an involuntary bankruptcy case in which the bad faith was the creditor's weaponization of the bankruptcy process as a judgment enforcement device. Murray holds that "bankruptcy is not a judgment enforcement device." That sentence, accurately applied, describes Nagel's motion more precisely than it describes Brad's filing.

The procedural defect in the § 523(a)(6) request is independently dispositive as to that specific relief: Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 7001(6) provides that "a proceeding to determine the dischargeability of a debt" is an adversary proceeding. An adversary proceeding is a separate lawsuit commenced within the bankruptcy case, governed by Part VII of the Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure, initiated by a complaint, a summons, and an answer. *See* Fed. R. Bankr. P. 7003 (complaint initiates adversary proceeding); 7004 (summons and service); 7012 (answer). Nagel has filed a motion — Doc. 8 in the main case — not a complaint in an adversary proceeding. This Court lacks the procedural posture to enter a nondischargeability order on a motion. The defect is not technical; it is structural. The rules require an adversary proceeding because the debtor is entitled to full Rule 7 discovery, to plead affirmative defenses in an answer, and to a full trial before a nondischargeability judgment is entered. A motion in the main case deprives the debtor of all of these protections.

Note also that the deadline for Nagel to file a complaint objecting to discharge or seeking nondischargeability under § 523(a)(6) runs sixty days after the first date set for the meeting of creditors pursuant to 11 U.S.C. § 341. *See* Fed. R. Bankr. P. 4007(c). Bankruptcy counsel must calculate this deadline, place it on the calendar, and monitor whether Nagel files a proper adversary complaint. If he does not file within the § 341 deadline, his § 523(a)(6) claim will be time-barred regardless of the merits.

## SECTION 5.1: THE § 707(a) BAD FAITH DISMISSAL ARGUMENT FAILS

### 5.1.A: The Governing Standard — Tamecki and Perlin Define the Gate

The Third Circuit's bad faith dismissal standard under § 707(a) begins and ends with two binding authorities: *In re Tamecki*, 229 F.3d 205 (3d Cir. 2000), and *In re Perlin*, 497 F.3d 364 (3d Cir. 2007).

*Tamecki* is the foundational authority. The Third Circuit there held that § 707(a)'s "cause" requirement encompasses a debtor's bad faith, but expressly limited this doctrine to "egregious cases" involving "concealed or misrepresented assets and/or sources of income, and [lavish] lifestyles and the intention to avoid a large single debt based upon conduct akin to fraud, misconduct or gross negligence." *In re Tamecki*, 229 F.3d at 207 (internal quotation omitted). The Court also established the burden-shifting framework that governs the analysis: once a movant raises a colorable bad faith challenge, the burden shifts to the debtor to demonstrate good faith; if the debtor produces evidence of good faith, the burden shifts back to the movant to establish bad faith by the egregious standard. *Id.*

The critical word in *Tamecki* is "egregious." It is not decorative language. Congress created § 707(a) to address cases involving substantive abuse of the bankruptcy process, not to create a judicial escape valve for creditors dissatisfied with the statutory rules of distribution. The Third Circuit in *Tamecki* endorsed the Eighth Circuit's formulation that bad faith dismissal is reserved for cases of "debtor misconduct" — not simply cases where a creditor believes the debtor does not deserve a discharge. *Id.* at 207 (quoting *In re Zick*, 931 F.2d 1124, 1127 (6th Cir. 1991)). The first sentence of the § 707(a) argument in the opposition brief should quote *Tamecki*'s "egregious cases" formulation verbatim and make the gate explicit: Brad's case does not come within twenty miles of that threshold.

*Perlin* adds the second mandatory limiting principle: "A debtor's ability to repay is not in and of itself sufficient proof of bad faith." *In re Perlin*, 497 F.3d at 369. This rule is directly applicable here. Nagel's motion implicitly — and at times explicitly — rests on the theory that Brad has assets (retirement accounts, escrowed funds) and therefore should not have access to Chapter 7. *Perlin* forecloses that argument as a standalone basis for dismissal. Ability to pay is one factor in a multifactor totality analysis, not a sufficient condition. The opposition should quote *Perlin* at page 369 and then demonstrate that every other factor in the analysis favors Brad.

### 5.1.B: The Murray Inversion — Nagel's Lead Case Condemns His Motion

Nagel cites *In re Murray*, 543 B.R. 484 (Bankr. S.D.N.Y. 2016), *aff'd*, 900 F.3d 53 (2d Cir. 2018), as authority supporting bad faith dismissal of a voluntary Chapter 7 petition. (Nagel-mtd-analysis.md § 2.) This citation is the most important analytical error in Nagel's brief, and the opposition brief should make the correction the first substantive paragraph of the § 707(a) argument. The reason is simple: the opposition correcting the error will immediately communicate to Judge O'Hagan — who knows *Murray* — that the movant did not read the case carefully and the respondent did.

*Murray* is an involuntary bankruptcy case. Wilk Auslander LLP, an attorney's former employer, filed an *involuntary* Chapter 7 petition against Murray as a device to collect a judgment it had obtained against him. The court — at both the bankruptcy court level and the Second Circuit — dismissed the involuntary petition not because Murray had been bad faith but because the petitioning creditor had been bad faith. The Second Circuit held that "bankruptcy is not a judgment enforcement device" and that filing an involuntary petition as a mechanism to collect on a two-party judgment constitutes an abuse of the bankruptcy process for which dismissal is appropriate. *In re Murray*, 900 F.3d 53, 60 (2d Cir. 2018).

Applied to the facts of this case, *Murray*'s logic cuts sharply against Nagel. Nagel filed this motion because he wants to collect on what he characterizes as a damages judgment against Brad — the same reason the creditor in *Murray* filed the involuntary petition. The Second Circuit condemned exactly that use of bankruptcy. The *Murray* principle, accurately stated, is that a creditor cannot weaponize the bankruptcy process to force a debtor into a position advantageous for that creditor's collection efforts. That is precisely what this motion attempts to do: dismiss Brad's bankruptcy so that Nagel can proceed with a state court damages trial against a debtor who would, absent bankruptcy, face a multi-creditor free-for-all with Nagel holding a privileged position as both attorney and physical custodian of $750,000 in Brad's funds.

Nagel cited *Murray* without reading it carefully enough to see that it condemned his position, not supported it. The opposition brief should make this point in a single, precise paragraph — not belabored, but clear.

### 5.1.D: The Glunk Factor-by-Factor Analysis — Brad Satisfies None

**Factor 1 — Lavish Lifestyle / Ability to Pay:**

Brad's schedule disclosures are the antithesis of lavish lifestyle. Schedule I shows monthly income of $3,500 from his brother's support. Schedule J shows monthly expenses of $4,552. Monthly deficit: negative $1,052. Brad cannot cover his basic living expenses. He lives in his brother's home at discounted rent ($2,000/month, acknowledged as below market on Schedule J, line 24). The *Perlin* limitation is directly applicable: a debtor's ability to repay his debts "is not in and of itself sufficient proof of bad faith." *In re Perlin*, 497 F.3d at 369. And even the "ability" argument is problematic on Brad's facts — the assets Nagel points to are the $1,090,000 in retirement accounts, which are 100% exempt under 11 U.S.C. § 522(d)(12) as ERISA-qualified plans. Exempt property is legally unavailable to creditors. The fact that Brad has exempt assets does not constitute an ability to pay non-exempt creditors. A debtor who has $1 million in an ERISA account and zero non-exempt liquid assets has no practical ability to pay — the ERISA statute would be meaningless if courts could order debtors to liquidate protected retirement savings.

The $750,000 in the Nagel Rice trust account — the single most significant non-exempt asset — is attached under a prejudgment attachment from a civil action that was dismissed by the Zunic order on June 12, 2026 (the same day as the bankruptcy petition). It is not a liquid asset available for voluntary payment. The $75,000 Ferrari has only $22,500 in exempt equity, leaving approximately $52,500 in non-exempt value — barely enough to cover a fraction of the $575,000 in senior priority claims.

Brad does not have a lavish lifestyle. Brad does not have the practical ability to pay creditors voluntarily. Factor 1 fails.

**Factor 2 — Disproportionate Impact on a Single Creditor:**

Factor 2 is not satisfied.

**Factor 3 — Forum Shopping / Thwarting State Court:**

Filing a Chapter 7 petition in the United States Bankruptcy Court for the District of New Jersey is not forum shopping. It is the exercise of a federal statutory right created by Congress in 11 U.S.C. § 301. Any individual with primarily non-consumer debts (as Brad disclosed — Schedule 106Sum: debts NOT primarily consumer debts) who meets the § 109(b) eligibility requirements has an absolute right to file a voluntary Chapter 7 petition. The federal bankruptcy court is the statutorily designated forum for discharge and dischargeability determination. Filing there is not forum shopping; it is using the correct statutory forum.

The thwarting-state-court framing is also undermined by causation. Who created the "eve of trial" timing that Nagel characterizes as improper forum shopping? The answer is that the interlocutory appeal — filed by Nagel Rice on behalf of B.B. in 2021, argued November 10, 2022, and decided December 31, 2024 — delayed the trial for more than three years. The trial date of June 15, 2026 was set eighteen months after the Appellate Division finally allowed the case to proceed. Under the totality-of-circumstances standard, which expressly encompasses all relevant circumstances, *In re Tamecki*, 229 F.3d at 207, this Court is entitled to consider which party created the scheduling dynamics Nagel now seeks to exploit. The answer does not favor Nagel.

Moreover, as of June 12, 2026 — the date of the petition — there was no state court action to thwart. Judge Zunic dismissed the civil action on June 12, 2026, before Nagel filed this motion on June 23, 2026. Forum shopping means choosing a more favorable forum over an available alternative. There is no available alternative. Factor 3 fails.

**Factor 4 — Prepetition Fraudulent Conduct:**

Nagel's fraudulent conduct argument centers on the KRM Trust and SBM Trust, both formed in December 2012. The trust timing argument has an insurmountable problem: the trusts predate B.B.'s lawsuit (filed 2019) by seven years and predate any cognizable debt to B.B. by at least four to five years.

Trust formations from December 2012 are fourteen years outside the § 548 lookback window. Under the New Jersey Uniform Fraudulent Transfer Act (NJUFTA), a constructive fraud claim requires the claimant to be a "present creditor" at the time of the transfer. Lilly Cannon was not a present creditor in December 2012. She was, at most, a future creditor — a status that the NJUFTA's actual fraud prong theoretically reaches, but only upon proof of subjective intent to defraud a specifically intended future victim. A fraudulent conveyance claim requires proof that Brad, in December 2012, formed these trusts with the actual intent to defraud someone he had not yet met and to whom no debt yet existed. That is a logical impossibility that Nagel has not attempted to prove.

The SOFA is explicit: Line 19 (10-year self-settled trust transfers) — Brad checked NO. No transfers to self-settled trusts within the ten-year SOFA window (before June 2016). The December 2012 trust formations predate even this ten-year lookback.

And critically: Nagel settled with the KRM Trust and SBM Trust in February and March 2026, accepting $300,000 and releasing "any current or future claims related to efforts to collect against any assets currently held by or in the possession of the KRM Trust or the SBM Trust." (KRM & SBM Civil Release, Mar. 6, 2026, ¶ 3.) A party cannot argue that the entities he settled with and released from liability three months ago are fraudulent conveyance vehicles. The March 2026 settlement is the most powerful rebuttal to the trust fraud narrative — and it is a fact that Nagel did not disclose to this Court in the motion. This omission raises issues under N.J. RPC 3.3(a)(5) (duty to disclose material facts to a tribunal). Factor 4 is not established.

**Factor 5 — Fundamentally Unfair Result:**

The Glunk "fundamentally unfair" factor asks whether the Chapter 7 discharge would produce a result that is fundamentally unfair to honest creditors. The inquiry is not whether a particular creditor prefers a different result — it is whether the operation of the Code itself produces an outcome that violates basic notions of fairness. *In re Glunk*, 342 B.R. at 734.

The answer here is no — and the argument inverts in Brad's favor. The "fundamentally unfair" result in this case is not Brad's discharge. It is what happens to the IRS and the domestic support creditor if the stay is lifted and Nagel is allowed to proceed to a state court damages trial. The $500,000 held in escrowed funds at Nagel Rice ($750,000), Greifinger ($75,000), and McElroy Deutsch ($150,000) — totaling $975,000 — is the primary pool of non-exempt assets. If Nagel obtains a state court judgment while the stay is lifted and executes on that attachment, the IRS ($300,000 § 507(a)(8) priority) and Kimberly Mell ($200,000 DSO, § 507(a)(1) first priority) receive nothing. Nagel's own creditors — the governmental entities and the domestic support obligee who stand first and second in the § 507 priority waterfall — are the parties who suffer a fundamentally unfair result if this Court grants Nagel's stay relief and allows pre-petition attachment enforcement.

Moreover, Nagel has already collected $300,000 from the Trust Settlement. He received value from the trusts he characterizes as fraudulent vehicles. The "fundamentally unfair" equitable argument becomes difficult to maintain when the movant has already collected more than the majority of remaining small unsecured creditors combined. Factor 5 is not established in Nagel's favor — it is established against him.

## SECTION 5.2: THE § 523(a)(6) RELIEF IS PROCEDURALLY IMPROPER

### 5.2.A: Rule 7001(6) Requires an Adversary Proceeding — This Motion Is the Wrong Vehicle

The threshold objection to Nagel's § 523(a)(6) request is procedural and is not subject to any legitimate counter-argument. Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 7001(6) provides, in full: "The following are adversary proceedings: . . . (6) a proceeding to determine the dischargeability of a debt." An adversary proceeding is a separate contested matter, commenced by the filing of a complaint, governed by the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure as incorporated through Part VII of the Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure, and resulting in a judgment after service, an answer period, and (if necessary) trial.

Nagel has not filed an adversary proceeding. He has filed a motion — Doc. 8 — in the main bankruptcy case. A motion in the main case is not an adversary proceeding under Rule 7001(6). This Court has no procedural mechanism to enter a nondischargeability judgment on a motion in the main case. The Rule's requirement of an adversary proceeding is not a technicality that can be waived by calling the relief sought a "motion" — it reflects the substantive due process protections that a debtor is owed before a debt is determined to be nondischargeable: full discovery under Part VII, the right to plead affirmative defenses in an answer, and the right to a trial if facts are genuinely disputed. Stripping those protections by converting an adversary proceeding into a motion prejudices the debtor and violates the Rules. *See* Fed. R. Bankr. P. 7003, 7004, 7012.

The disposition of this defect is straightforward: the Court should dismiss Nagel's § 523(a)(6) request without prejudice, to the extent it is seeking a nondischargeability determination, for failure to initiate an adversary proceeding as required by Rule 7001(6). The opposition brief should make this argument first, clearly, and move on. The Court does not need argument on the merits of § 523(a)(6) unless and until Nagel properly initiates an adversary proceeding.

The follow-on point — track the deadline — is operationally critical. Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 4007(c) establishes a sixty-day deadline from the first date set for the § 341 creditors' meeting for filing a § 523(a)(6) nondischargeability complaint. Bankruptcy counsel must calculate this deadline as soon as the § 341 meeting date is set and place it on the litigation calendar. If Nagel fails to file a complaint before that deadline, his § 523(a)(6) claim is forever time-barred. The Rules Committee set this deadline specifically because § 523(a)(6) is one of the "self-executing" discharge exceptions — meaning the debt is discharged unless the creditor timely objects. *See* Fed. R. Bankr. P. 4007 advisory committee note (1983). Monitoring this deadline is one of the highest-priority tasks between now and the July 16 hearing.

### 5.2.B: The Merits — Geiger and Conte Foreclose Nagel's "Self-Evident" Inference Theory

If the Court, despite the procedural defect, proceeds to evaluate the § 523(a)(6) merits as guidance to Nagel on whether to file a properly initiated adversary proceeding, the merits analysis also defeats Nagel's theory.

**The Governing Framework.**

The Supreme Court settled the § 523(a)(6) intent standard in *Kawaauhau v. Geiger*, 523 U.S. 57 (1998). The Court held: "The word 'willful' in (a)(6) modifies the word 'injury,' indicating that nondischargeability takes a deliberate or intentional injury, not merely a deliberate or intentional act that leads to injury." *Id.* at 61. The Court explicitly rejected the approach of inferring intent to injure from the nature of the act: "Had Congress meant to exempt debts resulting from unintentionally inflicted injuries, it might have described instead 'willful acts that cause injury.'" *Id.* at 63. A debtor who does not subjectively intend to cause the specific injury cannot be found to have inflicted a "willful" injury under § 523(a)(6), regardless of how culpable the act appears from an objective perspective.

The Third Circuit incorporated *Geiger* into its own § 523(a)(6) standard in *In re Conte*, 33 F.3d 303 (3d Cir. 1994), which predates *Geiger* but articulates a standard that Third Circuit courts have construed consistently with *Geiger*'s holding. Under *Conte*, § 523(a)(6) requires either (1) that the debtor purposefully inflicted the injury, or (2) that the debtor acted with subjective knowledge that injury was substantially certain to result. *In re Conte*, 33 F.3d at 308. The "substantially certain" prong is subjective, not objective — it asks what this debtor actually knew about the likely consequences of his conduct, not what a reasonable person in his position would have known.

**Nagel's Fleetwood Argument Is Foreclosed by Geiger.**

Nagel's § 523(a)(6) argument relies centrally on *In re Fleetwood* (Bankr. D. Alaska 2013) for the proposition that willfulness and malice in child sexual abuse cases are "self-evident" — a matter of inference from the nature of the act, not proof of subjective intent. (Nagel-mtd-analysis.md § 3.) This approach was expressly foreclosed by *Geiger* twenty-five years ago. The "self-evident" willfulness argument is an objective inference test: because sexual abuse of a minor is inherently harmful, the intent to harm is inferred as a matter of law. *Geiger* rejected exactly this approach. The statute says "willful and malicious injury" — the Court held that means subjective intent to cause the specific injury, not objective inference from the nature of the act. *Geiger*, 523 U.S. at 61–63.

*Fleetwood* is also not binding in this Court. It is a single bankruptcy court decision from the District of Alaska — not Third Circuit, not Supreme Court, not even persuasive authority from within the Third Circuit. This Court's decisions must follow *Geiger* (Supreme Court, binding) and *Conte* as construed post-*Geiger* (Third Circuit, binding). *Fleetwood* cannot override either.

**The Count-by-Count Element Problem.**

Even if Nagel properly filed an adversary complaint, he would face the obligation to prove § 523(a)(6) willfulness count by count — not as a monolith across "sexual abuse of a minor." Each civil claim has specific intent elements that must be mapped against § 523(a)(6)'s requirements. N.J.S.A. 2A:61B-1 (Child Sexual Abuse Act) requires "intentional touching for the purpose of sexually arousing or sexually gratifying the actor" — the purpose is the actor's gratification, not the infliction of injury on the plaintiff. The gap between "intent to gratify" and "intent to injure" is precisely the gap that *Geiger* requires Nagel to fill with affirmative proof of subjective intent to cause the specific injury. On the record that exists — a discovery sanction, a Fifth Amendment invocation, and an Appellate Division characterization of the partial summary judgment as "interlocutory and not a final judgment" — Nagel has not filled that gap.

### 5.2.C: The Collateral Estoppel Argument — Why the Discovery Sanction Cannot Establish § 523(a)(6) Elements

Nagel argues that the March 26, 2021 partial summary judgment (PSJ) establishes all elements of his § 523(a)(6) claim through collateral estoppel. This argument fails for reasons that are both threshold and structural.

**The "Actually Litigated" Requirement.**

The Third Circuit requires that for collateral estoppel to operate in a § 523(a)(6) proceeding, the specific mental state elements — subjective intent to cause the specific injury and malice — must have been "actually litigated and determined" in the prior proceeding. *In re Docteroff*, 133 F.3d 210, 214–15 (3d Cir. 1997); *In re Wilson*, 116 F.3d 87, 90–92 (3d Cir. 1997). An issue is "actually litigated" when it was submitted to and decided by a factfinder after presentation of evidence and argument — not when it is deemed admitted by procedural default. A judgment that results from a failure to respond to a motion or from a discovery sanction does not satisfy the "actually litigated" standard because no factfinder ever made a determination on the merits of any issue.

This principle defeats Nagel's collateral estoppel argument at the threshold. The New Jersey Appellate Division, in its December 31, 2024 opinion (the very decision that finally ended Nagel's three-year interlocutory appeal), described the March 2021 PSJ in footnote 2 as "interlocutory and not a final judgment." The Appellate Division also noted: "It is not clear from the record whether Mell responded to the summary judgment motion." If Mell did not respond, the PSJ was entered under N.J. Court Rule 4:23-5(a)(2) — which allows entry of a judgment as a sanction for discovery non-compliance — or under N.J. Court Rule 4:46 based on the debtor's procedural default. Neither procedural mechanism involves an evidentiary hearing, testimony, or any factfinder determination on any element of any claim. No judge in the New Jersey Superior Court ever made a finding that Brad Mell subjectively intended to cause the specific injuries alleged in any count of the complaint, or that he acted with malice without just cause or excuse. Those elements were never presented to any factfinder and were never determined by any court. The "actually litigated" requirement is not satisfied.

*In re Azeglio*, 422 B.R. 490 (Bankr. D.N.J. 2010), is directly on point. In *Azeglio*, the Bankruptcy Court for the District of New Jersey denied collateral estoppel effect in a § 523(a)(6) proceeding because the mental state elements required by § 523(a)(6) had not been actually litigated in the prior state court proceeding. The prior state court judgment established civil liability, but it did not establish the specific subjective intent elements required by the federal bankruptcy standard. *Id.* at [page — VERIFY]. *Azeglio* is binding persuasive authority from this same district applying exactly the standard Nagel must satisfy and failing to satisfy it on facts similar to those here.

**The Fifth Amendment Invocation Cannot Be Transformed into Bad-Faith Obstruction.**

Nagel may invoke *Docteroff*'s exception — the principle that a party who "deliberately prevents" resolution of an issue through bad-faith obstruction may be deemed to have "actually litigated" the issue. *In re Docteroff*, 133 F.3d at 214–15. But the *Docteroff* exception applies only to conduct that is sanctionable because it lacks any legal justification — bad-faith obstruction without legal basis. Brad's invocation of the Fifth Amendment right against self-incrimination during civil discovery is not bad-faith obstruction. It is the exercise of a right guaranteed by the Fifth Amendment to the United States Constitution, invoked because parallel federal criminal proceedings were pending against Brad at the time.

The invocation of the Fifth Amendment in civil discovery is a well-established procedural right with a substantial body of law governing its consequences and limits. It is not a discovery sanction offense; it is a constitutional protection. A party who invokes a constitutional right is not "deliberately preventing resolution" of an issue in the *Docteroff* sense. *Docteroff*'s exception reaches conduct that has no legal justification — stonewalling, destruction of evidence, perjury. It does not reach constitutionally protected silence. Moreover, even under *Docteroff*'s exception, what could be "deemed litigated" through the exception would be the issues presented in the state court proceeding — civil liability on the pleaded counts — not the specific federal bankruptcy law elements of subjective willfulness and malice under § 523(a)(6), which were never before the New Jersey Superior Court.

**Berlingeri and Nagel's D.N.J. Authority.**

Nagel cites *In re Berlingeri*, 246 B.R. 196 (Bankr. D.N.J. 2000), for the proposition that § 523(a)(6) reaches "intentional acts done to cause injury." *Berlingeri* is a bankruptcy court decision from this district — persuasive but not binding — and its holding on § 523(a)(6) is narrower than Nagel suggests. Berlingeri's § 523(a)(6) finding appears to rest on a consent judgment stipulation in which the debtor expressly conceded the facts constituting willful and malicious injury — which is qualitatively different from a default judgment or discovery sanction in which no concession was made. Note that *Berlingeri* was abrogated by *In re Ruitenberg*, 745 F.3d 647 (3d Cir. 2014), but only on the equitable distribution claim issue — the *Ruitenberg* court did not address § 523(a)(6) at all. *Ruitenberg* does not change *Berlingeri*'s § 523(a)(6) analysis. [VERIFY: Obtain full text of *Berlingeri* to confirm the consent judgment basis for the § 523(a)(6) holding before filing.] If confirmed, *Berlingeri* is distinguishable: there is no consent judgment in this case, and Brad made no concession of the § 523(a)(6) mental state elements in any proceeding.

*Halper v. Halper*, 164 F.3d 830, 836–37 (3d Cir. 1999), confirms that § 523(a)(6) dischargeability determinations are core proceedings under 28 U.S.C. § 157(b)(2)(I) subject to this Court's exclusive jurisdiction. This is relevant to the abstention section (5.4 below) but also reinforces that the proper forum for any § 523(a)(6) determination is this Court in an adversary proceeding, not the New Jersey Superior Court in a damages trial.

### 5.3.B: The Curtis/Sonnax Twelve-Factor Analysis — All Twelve Factors Favor Brad

Courts in the Third Circuit evaluate § 362(d)(1) "cause" through the twelve-factor *Curtis*/*Sonnax* test, as adopted in this district. *In re Mid-Atlantic Handling Systems, LLC*, 304 B.R. 111, 130 (Bankr. D.N.J. 2003). All twelve factors are addressed below.

**Factor 1 — Whether Relief Would Result in a Partial or Complete Resolution of the Issues.**

Lifting the stay cannot result in any resolution of any issue because there is no pending state court action. The Zunic order dismissed B.B. v. Mell, ESX-L-007200-19, without prejudice on June 12, 2026. A dismissed action is not pending. Factor 1 weighs heavily against stay relief.

**Factor 2 — Lack of Connection Between the Bankruptcy Case and the Stayed Proceeding.**

The state court action is directly connected to this bankruptcy case — the civil judgment sought is the basis for the potential § 523(a)(6) nondischargeability claim. If anything, the connection between the proceedings militates in favor of maintaining the stay and resolving both the liability and dischargeability questions in a single forum. Factor 2 is neutral at most.

**Factor 3 — Whether the Debt Would Be Dischargeable in the Bankruptcy Case.**

This factor is genuinely contested — which is precisely why it belongs in an adversary proceeding in this Court under Rule 7001(6), not in a state court damages trial. The dischargeability question is squarely within this Court's exclusive jurisdiction under 28 U.S.C. § 157(b)(2)(I). Factor 3 is not resolved and therefore does not support lifting the stay to allow state court proceedings that cannot determine dischargeability in any event.

**Factor 4 — Whether the Debtor's Estate Could Be Irreparably Damaged.**

The estate would be irreparably harmed by depletion of its primary non-exempt asset pool before the trustee can administer the estate in an orderly fashion. Factor 4 weighs against stay relief.

**Factor 5 — Whether the Debtor Has Personal Liability.**

Brad does have personal liability if the civil case is adjudicated in B.B.'s favor — but the magnitude of that liability is unknown, contingent, unliquidated, and disputed. It has not been established by any final judgment. The interlocutory PSJ is not a final judgment (Appellate Division Fn. 2). Factor 5 is neutral; it does not affirmatively support stay relief when liability itself remains undetermined.

**Factor 6 — Whether a Non-Debtor Co-Defendant Exists.**

The civil action named multiple defendants, including entities and other parties. This factor is complex factually; however, the fact that the action named non-debtor defendants supports maintaining the stay as to Brad's personal liability while allowing any separate proceedings against non-debtor parties to continue. Factor 6 is neutral to slightly favoring maintenance of the stay.

**Factor 7 — Judicial Economy and Prejudice to Other Creditors.**

This factor decisively favors maintaining the stay. The IRS ($300,000 § 507(a)(8) priority claim) and Kimberly Mell ($200,000 DSO, § 507(a)(1) first priority claim) are statutory senior creditors whose claims are nondischargeable under §§ 523(a)(1) and 523(a)(5) respectively. The IRS and Kimberly Mell — whose § 507 claims are first and eighth priority respectively, far ahead of Nagel's client — receive zero in that scenario. The automatic stay exists in part to prevent exactly this race-to-the-courthouse outcome where the first creditor to execute gets paid at the expense of all others. Lifting the stay here would harm the IRS, the domestic support creditor, and every other unsecured creditor in order to benefit a single creditor whose claim has not been reduced to a final judgment. Factor 7 strongly favors maintaining the stay.

**Factor 8 — Whether Stay Constitutes "Cause" Independent of the Bad Faith Theory.**

There is no independent "cause" beyond the bad faith theory already rejected. Factor 8 favors the debtor.

**Factor 9 — Feasibility of Reorganization.**

This is Chapter 7, not a reorganization chapter, so this factor has limited direct applicability. The equivalent inquiry — whether the Chapter 7 case has a legitimate purpose — is answered yes: Brad has twelve creditors, $1,830,000 in liabilities, a negative monthly cash flow, and no realistic ability to satisfy his debts outside of bankruptcy. Chapter 7's fresh start purpose is directly served by this case. Factor 9 favors maintaining the stay.

**Factor 10 — Balance of Harms.**

Brad faces multi-forum litigation at a time when his only income is $3,500/month in discretionary support from his brother. His living expenses exceed his income by $1,052/month. His attorney's fees were paid by his brother. He has no independent resources to fund parallel state court and federal bankruptcy court litigation simultaneously. The harm to Brad from lifting the stay is immediate, concrete, and financially ruinous given his situation.

Nagel's only cognizable harm from maintaining the stay is delay of a damages proceeding that — even if concluded in B.B.'s favor — cannot result in collection without first returning to this Court for the § 523(a)(6) dischargeability determination. The state court damages trial, if it occurred, would produce a judgment. That judgment would then need to be evaluated in a § 523(a)(6) adversary proceeding in this Court before any collection could occur. The stay produces delay; it does not eliminate B.B.'s potential recovery. The balance of harms decisively favors Brad. Factor 10 favors maintaining the stay.

**Factor 11 — Whether the Parties Are Ready for Trial in the Other Proceeding.**

This factor is potentially dispositive on these facts. The state court action was dismissed without prejudice by the Zunic order on June 12, 2026. The parties are not ready for trial in a dismissed action. They are not ready for trial in any proceeding. There is no "other proceeding" in which the parties' trial readiness could be assessed. Factor 11 analysis assumes a pending alternative proceeding; here, there is none. This factor weighs decisively against stay relief.

**Factor 12 — Whether the Underlying Action Has Public Policy Implications.**

Sexual abuse of a minor has obvious and serious public policy implications. However, public policy does not override the orderly administration of bankruptcy law or the priority scheme established by Congress. The public policy interest in redressing B.B.'s alleged injuries is addressed by the § 523(a)(6) process — which allows the bankruptcy court to find the debt nondischargeable upon proof of willful and malicious injury in a properly initiated adversary proceeding. Public policy is not served by disrupting the statutory distribution scheme or by allowing a single creditor to execute on estate assets before other priority creditors are paid. Factor 12 is equivocal at best and does not affirmatively support stay relief.

**Summary of Curtis/Sonnax Analysis:** Factors 1, 4, 7, 9, 10, and 11 affirmatively favor maintaining the stay. Factors 3, 5, 6, 8, and 12 are neutral or equivocal. No factor affirmatively supports lifting the stay when the underlying action has been dismissed and the § 523(a)(6) determination remains properly within this Court's exclusive jurisdiction.

## SECTION 5.4: ABSTENTION UNDER 28 U.S.C. § 1334 IS LEGALLY UNAVAILABLE

### 5.4.A: Threshold Defect — The Cited Statute Does Not Exist

Nagel's motion seeks relief under "28 U.S.C. § 1134(c)(1)" (permissive abstention) and "28 U.S.C. § 1134(c)(2)" (mandatory abstention). (Nagel-mtd-analysis.md §§ 6, 8.) Title 28, Chapter 85 (District Courts; Jurisdiction), contains sections numbered 1330 through 1369. There is no § 1134 in Title 28. No such statute exists.

The abstention provisions governing bankruptcy jurisdiction are found at 28 U.S.C. § 1334(c)(1) (permissive abstention: "Each district court may abstain from hearing a particular proceeding . . . in the interest of comity with State courts") and § 1334(c)(2) (mandatory abstention). This error appears twice in Nagel's motion and infects the entire abstention section. The opposition should note this error in a single sentence: "Nagel's abstention arguments cite a statute — 28 U.S.C. § 1134 — that does not exist; this Court will treat the motion as invoking the correct statute, 28 U.S.C. § 1334." Then move immediately to the merits. One sentence is enough. Judge O'Hagan, with eleven years as USAO Senior Bankruptcy Counsel, will have recognized the error on first read.

### 5.4.B: Mandatory Abstention Is Categorically Unavailable for § 523(a)(6) — The Statute's Text Is Dispositive

Section 1334(c)(2) provides that in a proceeding "based upon a State law claim or State law cause of action, related to a case under title 11 but not arising under title 11 or arising in a case under title 11," the district court (and by extension the bankruptcy court) shall abstain if the proceeding can be timely adjudicated in state court. The mandatory abstention statute, by its express terms, applies only to proceedings that are merely "related to" a case under title 11 — not to proceedings that "arise under" title 11 or "arise in" a case under title 11.

A § 523(a)(6) nondischargeability determination arises directly under 11 U.S.C. § 523 — a provision of the United States Bankruptcy Code. It arises under title 11, not under state law. The statute's text is self-applying: if the proceeding arises under title 11, the mandatory abstention provision by its own terms does not apply. No analysis of the five *Stoe* factors is needed at all — mandatory abstention is categorically unavailable for § 523(a)(6) proceedings as a matter of statutory text.

This is confirmed by 28 U.S.C. § 157(b)(2)(I), which expressly identifies "determinations as to the dischargeability of particular debts" as core proceedings. Core proceedings are, by definition, those that "arise under" title 11 or "arise in" a case under title 11 — not merely "related to" a case under title 11. A proceeding that is a core proceeding under § 157(b)(2)(I) cannot simultaneously be only "related to" a case under title 11. The categories are mutually exclusive. *See Halper v. Halper*, 164 F.3d 830, 836–37 (3d Cir. 1999) (§ 523 dischargeability proceedings are core; bankruptcy court retains exclusive jurisdiction). Mandatory abstention requires that the proceeding be merely "related to" a bankruptcy case and based on a state law claim. A § 523(a)(6) proceeding fails both conditions.

Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 4007's advisory committee note confirms the exclusive nature of the bankruptcy court's § 523(a)(6) jurisdiction: "A proceeding to determine the dischargeability of a debt . . . is governed by the Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure." The Rules themselves contemplate that § 523(a)(6) is within the exclusive province of the bankruptcy court. Exclusive jurisdiction cannot be divested by an abstention motion. This point is dispositive.

### 5.4.C: Stoe v. Flaherty — Nagel's Own Cited Case Defeats Him on Multiple Elements

Even setting aside the statutory text argument, Nagel's mandatory abstention claim fails under the controlling five-factor test from *Stoe v. Flaherty*, 436 F.3d 209, 213 (3d Cir. 2006) — the very case Nagel cites as support. All five elements of *Stoe* must be satisfied for mandatory abstention to be required. At minimum, elements (2), (3), and (4) fail independently.

**Stoe Element 1 — A timely motion was made.**

This element may be satisfied — the motion was filed relatively early in the case. The opposition need not contest element (1); the remaining failing elements are dispositive.

**Stoe Element 2 — The claim must not arise under title 11 and must not arise in a case under title 11.**

A § 523(a)(6) nondischargeability determination arises directly under 11 U.S.C. § 523(a)(6), a provision of title 11. This element is not satisfied. Nagel's entire abstention theory depends on treating the § 523(a)(6) claim as if it were a state law tort claim merely "related to" the bankruptcy. It is not. Element (2) fails. This failure is independently dispositive — all five elements must be satisfied.

**Stoe Element 3 — No basis for federal jurisdiction except § 1334.**

Section 157(b)(2)(I) gives the bankruptcy court exclusive jurisdiction over dischargeability determinations independent of § 1334. The bankruptcy court's authority to determine dischargeability arises from the Bankruptcy Code itself and from the grant of core proceeding jurisdiction under § 157(b)(2)(I) — not solely from the general grant of bankruptcy jurisdiction in § 1334. This element fails because federal jurisdiction over § 523(a)(6) exists independently of § 1334.

**Stoe Element 4 — An action is commenced in state court.**

The Zunic order dismissed B.B. v. Mell on June 12, 2026 — the same day Brad filed his petition and eleven days before Nagel filed this motion. There is no pending state court action as of the motion's filing date. There is no pending state court action as of the July 16 hearing date. *Stoe* requires that "an action is commenced" in state court of appropriate jurisdiction. Courts interpret this to require a currently pending proceeding — not one that must be refiled and recommenced. *See Stoe*, 436 F.3d at 213 (element requires state court action already pending). A dismissed action that would need to be refiled, re-served, and re-litigated is not "commenced" in state court. Element (4) fails.

**Stoe Element 5 — State court action can be timely adjudicated.**

Even if the other elements were satisfied (they are not), the state court cannot timely adjudicate B.B.'s claims when the action has been dismissed and would need to be refiled — potentially facing new challenges on statute of limitations, tolling, and the impact of the bankruptcy stay on any refiled proceeding. Element (5) is doubtful but need not be resolved given that elements (2), (3), and (4) each independently defeat mandatory abstention.

**Three of five *Stoe* elements fail independently. All five must be satisfied. Mandatory abstention is unavailable.**

### 5.4.D: Permissive Abstention — The Court Should Decline to Exercise Discretion Here

Even if the Court has theoretical discretion to abstain permissively under § 1334(c)(1), the exercise of that discretion is inappropriate here for three reasons.

First, permissive abstention in the face of a § 523(a)(6) claim would require this Court to decline a core proceeding within its exclusive jurisdiction and invite state court adjudication of a question Congress has assigned to the bankruptcy court. While § 1334(c)(1) permits some discretion, courts do not permissively abstain from core proceedings when the core proceeding is Congress's designated forum. The § 523(a)(6) determination is not a matter the bankruptcy court can hand off to state court because the state court has no authority to bind the parties' bankruptcy rights.

Second, permissive abstention requires consideration of efficiency and comity. There is nothing efficient about sending B.B.'s claims to state court when: (a) the state court action has been dismissed; (b) any state court judgment would need to return to this Court for the § 523(a)(6) dischargeability determination in any event; (c) Nagel filed the same-package request for a § 523(a)(6) declaration from this Court simultaneously with his request for abstention, making the internal contradiction manifest. Abstention creates a two-court, two-proceeding structure for a question this Court has exclusive authority to resolve in a single adversary proceeding.

Third, the logical contradiction between the abstention request and the § 523(a)(6) request demonstrates that Nagel has not thought through what he actually wants from this Court. He asks the Court simultaneously to: (a) abstain from adjudicating the factual circumstances of the injury — which requires sending those facts to state court; and (b) declare the resulting debt nondischargeable — which requires this Court to find "willful and malicious injury" using those same facts. A court cannot abstain from the predicate while retaining the conclusion. The internal incoherence of the request weighs against discretionary abstention.

## SECTION 5.5: § 362(k) CROSS-MOTION RECOMMENDATION — THE PARAGANO EMAIL AS SWORD

### 5.5.A: The Evidence Supporting a Willful Stay Violation

The opposition to Nagel's motion should be accompanied by — or closely followed by — a cross-motion for damages under 11 U.S.C. § 362(k)(1) based on Michael Paragano's June 18, 2026 email. The evidence establishing the elements of a willful stay violation is compelling, and the cross-motion transforms the litigation dynamic: the party seeking relief from the stay is now the respondent on a stay violation claim.

The factual predicate is not disputed:

(1) Brad filed his Chapter 7 petition on June 12, 2026, at approximately 3:28 PM, as reflected in the bankruptcy filing notice served on the state court docket on June 12, 2026.

(2) The automatic stay under 11 U.S.C. § 362(a) arose the moment the petition was filed, without any court order or additional action required.

(3) Nagel appeared before Judge O'Hagan and acknowledged the bankruptcy filing on or before June 15, 2026 — the date the Court entered the OSC in this proceeding.

(4) On June 18, 2026 — three days after Nagel acknowledged the bankruptcy to this Court — Nagel's law partner Michael Paragano emailed Brad directly asserting the prejudgment attachment from the dismissed state court action.

The Paragano email implicates at minimum four provisions of § 362(a): § 362(a)(1) (the act of continuing a judicial proceeding against the debtor — assertion of attachment rights derived from the dismissed civil action constitutes continuation of the judicial proceeding's coercive effects); § 362(a)(4) (any act to enforce a lien against property of the estate — the prejudgment attachment is a lien on property of the estate); § 362(a)(5) (any act to enforce against property of the estate a lien securing a pre-petition claim); and § 362(a)(6) (any act to collect a pre-petition debt). Each provision is independently violated by the Paragano email.

### 5.5.B: The Lansaw Willfulness Standard — Knowledge Plus Intentional Act

The Third Circuit established the § 362(k)(1) willfulness standard in *In re Lansaw*, 853 F.3d 657, 668–69 (3d Cir. 2017). Under *Lansaw*, a violation is "willful" when: (1) the violator had actual knowledge of the bankruptcy filing; and (2) the violator committed an intentional act. *Id.* Knowledge of the specific provisions of the automatic stay is not required — knowledge of the bankruptcy filing alone is sufficient. *In re University Medical Center*, 973 F.2d 1065, 1068 (3d Cir. 1992) (for purposes of § 362(k)(1) willfulness, debtor need only prove that the creditor knew of the bankruptcy filing, not that the creditor specifically knew which acts the stay prohibited).

Applied here: Nagel had actual knowledge. He appeared before this Court on June 15, 2026, acknowledging the bankruptcy. Paragano is Nagel's law partner at Nagel Rice LLP — the same firm. Under agency imputation principles, a partner's knowledge is imputed to other partners under the Restatement (Third) of Agency § 5.03 (knowledge of an agent acting within the scope of the agency is imputed to the principal). Nagel's knowledge of the bankruptcy is imputed to Paragano as his law partner. The June 18 email was an intentional act — Paragano deliberately composed and sent the email asserting the attachment. There is no colorable argument that the email was accidental or inadvertent.

Critically, good faith is not a defense to § 362(k)(1) willfulness. *Cal. Coast Univ. v. Aleckna (In re Aleckna)*, No. 20-1309, 2021 WL 4097155, at *4 (3d Cir. Sept. 9, 2021) ("[G]ood faith is not a defense to a willful violation of the automatic stay."). Even if Paragano believed — incorrectly — that the attachment remained valid after the Zunic dismissal, his good faith belief does not negate the willfulness element. He knew of the bankruptcy. He intentionally sent the email asserting the attachment. The willfulness element is satisfied.

### 5.5.C: Damages Available Under § 362(k)(1)

Section 362(k)(1) provides: "an individual injured by any willful violation of a stay provided by this section shall recover actual damages, including costs and attorneys' fees, and, in appropriate circumstances, may recover punitive damages." The *Lansaw* court affirmed the following categories of recovery:

*Actual damages — attorneys' fees.* Brad's attorneys' fees incurred in opposing the Paragano attachment assertion, opposing this motion, and preparing the cross-motion are all recoverable as "actual damages" under § 362(k)(1). The causal connection is direct: but for the Paragano email and the filing of this motion by the parties responsible for the violation, Brad would not have incurred these costs. *In re Lansaw*, 853 F.3d at 668–69.

*Emotional distress damages.* The *Lansaw* court affirmed emotional distress damages for willful stay violations without requiring medical evidence where the violations were patently egregious and the debtor testified credibly about distress. *Id.* A party who received an email asserting a void lien against his bankruptcy estate three days after his adversary acknowledged the bankruptcy to a federal court has a straightforward basis for emotional distress damages. Whether to pursue this element should be determined in consultation with Brad.

*Punitive damages.* The *Lansaw* court affirmed $40,000 in punitive damages for willful stay violation where the creditor's conduct was egregious. *Id.* Here, the aggravating circumstances are substantial: (1) the violation was committed by a law firm that practices bankruptcy law and is fully sophisticated about the automatic stay; (2) the violation occurred after Nagel personally appeared before this Court and acknowledged the bankruptcy; (3) the attachment being asserted is doubly void — both because the underlying action was dismissed and because it was released in December 2023; and (4) the law firm whose partner violated the stay is simultaneously seeking equitable relief from the same stay in this motion. Punitive damages are warranted.

### 5.5.D: The Double Invalidity of the Paragano Attachment Assertion

The attachment Paragano asserted on June 18, 2026 was doubly invalid at the moment of assertion:

First invalidity: The Zunic order dismissed B.B. v. Mell on June 12, 2026, the same day as the bankruptcy petition. A prejudgment attachment in New Jersey is an ancillary remedy that requires a pending action. N.J. Court Rule 4:60-1 et seq. When the action is dismissed, the attachment's legal foundation is eliminated. As of June 18, 2026, the state court action had been dismissed for six days. The attachment Paragano asserted had no legal foundation under New Jersey law.

Second invalidity: Prior research reflects that the attachment interest may have been separately released in December 2023 — more than two years before the bankruptcy filing — under terms that would have extinguished the lien separately from the March 2026 Trust Settlement. [VERIFY: Obtain and review December 2023 release documents before asserting this point in the filing. If verified, this provides an additional ground of invalidity independent of the Zunic dismissal.] Asserting a legally nonexistent right post-petition violates the stay regardless of the asserter's subjective belief about the right's validity. *Aleckna*: good faith is not a defense.

### 5.5.E: Strategic Considerations for the Cross-Motion

**File it.** The cross-motion should be filed simultaneously with the opposition brief or shortly before the July 16 hearing. The reasons are multiple:

*It reverses the table.* The party seeking equitable relief from the automatic stay is now the respondent on a willful stay violation claim. This asymmetry — at the very hearing where Nagel is seeking to put Brad on defense — is tactically significant. It signals that the estate is not merely defending but is pursuing its own affirmative rights.

*It signals to Judge O'Hagan.* A former USAO Senior Bankruptcy Counsel takes the automatic stay seriously. The government litigated to enforce the stay. When he learns that Nagel's law partner asserted a void attachment against a represented debtor three days after Nagel acknowledged the bankruptcy to this Court — in a motion that simultaneously seeks equitable relief from that same stay — he will draw his own conclusions about the equities. The cross-motion makes sure he has the evidence before him.

*It creates settlement leverage.* Nagel knows his partner violated the stay. A § 362(k)(1) motion converts that knowledge into a litigation cost that Nagel Rice LLP must manage. The law firm whose trust account holds $750,000 of Brad's money is now a potential defendant for punitive damages. That changes the settlement calculus.

*It generates actual damages recovery.* Brad's costs of opposing this motion and the Paragano letter are real expenses that Brad has no independent ability to pay. Recovery of attorneys' fees as actual damages under § 362(k)(1) directly defrays the cost of this litigation.

**One unresolved citation issue.** Prior research identified that a citation for the proposition that law firms are jointly and severally liable for § 362(k)(1) violations committed by their partners was previously attributed to "In re Shaw (Bankr. D.N.J.)" — a case that does not exist (confirmed in FINAL-MEMO.md and part5-stay.md). Before the cross-motion is filed, Westlaw must be searched for a verified authority for the joint-and-several liability proposition as applied to law firm partnerships. The substantive argument is legally sound — partners are jointly and severally liable for torts committed by their partners in the scope of partnership business under the Revised Uniform Partnership Act (N.J.S.A. 42:1A-36) and Restatement principles — but the law firm § 362(k)(1) joint-and-several citation must come from a verified case, not a hallucinated one. The cross-motion should not be filed until this citation is verified through Westlaw.

**The Rule 9011 consideration.** If the decision is made to assert that Nagel's failure to disclose the March 2026 Trust Settlement constitutes a material omission to the tribunal in violation of N.J. RPC 3.3(a)(5), the Rule 9011(c)(1)(A) twenty-one-day safe-harbor period for sanctions motions must be managed against the hearing date. Nagel filed the MTD on June 23, 2026. The hearing is July 16, 2026. Twenty-one days from June 23 is July 14, 2026 — two days before the hearing. If the Rule 9011 notice is served immediately (by June 27, 2026), the safe-harbor period expires on July 18, 2026 — two days after the hearing. This timing makes a pre-hearing Rule 9011 motion procedurally complicated. The recommended approach is to disclose the settlement in the opposition brief itself (as recommended in Section 4.A above) and preserve the Rule 9011 and RPC 3.3 arguments for a post-hearing motion if the motion to dismiss is denied and the case continues. The disclosure in the opposition brief puts the Court on notice of the omission and invites the Court's own inquiry; the formal motion is preserved for the appropriate subsequent moment.
