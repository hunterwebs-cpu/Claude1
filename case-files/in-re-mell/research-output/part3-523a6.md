## SECTION A: THE GEIGER/CONTE AUTHORITY CHAIN

### A.1 — Kawaauhau v. Geiger, 523 U.S. 57 (1998)

**Full Citation:** Kawaauhau v. Geiger, 523 U.S. 57, 118 S. Ct. 974, 140 L. Ed. 2d 90 (1998)
**Court:** United States Supreme Court (unanimous)
**Author:** Justice Ginsburg
**Date:** March 3, 1998
**Status:** Good law; no subsequent history limiting its holding

**FACTS:** Margaret Kawaauhau sought treatment from Dr. Paul Geiger for an injured foot. Knowing that intravenous penicillin would be more effective, Geiger prescribed oral penicillin because Kawaauhau was trying to minimize medical costs. Her foot eventually required amputation. A jury awarded her $355,000 in a malpractice judgment. Dr. Geiger filed Chapter 7 bankruptcy. Kawaauhau argued the debt was nondischargeable under § 523(a)(6).

**ISSUE:** Does § 523(a)(6)'s exception to discharge cover (a) acts done intentionally that cause injury, or only (b) acts done with the actual intent to cause injury?

**HOLDING:** The Eighth Circuit's reading was adopted: § 523(a)(6) applies only to acts done with the actual intent to cause the injury itself. Debts arising from recklessly or negligently inflicted injuries are dischargeable.

---

#### A.1(a) — Definition of "Willful": "Willful" Modifies "Injury" Not "Act"

**[VERIFIED VERBATIM — confirmed from multiple independent sources]**

> "The word 'willful' in (a)(6) modifies the word 'injury,' indicating that nondischargeability takes a deliberate or intentional injury, not merely a deliberate or intentional act that leads to injury."

**Pinpoint:** 523 U.S. at 61.

**Strategic note:** This is the foundational sentence of Geiger. Nagel's theory — that sexually abusive acts are "by definition" willful and malicious — asks the Court to infer willful *injury* from the nature of the act. Geiger explicitly rejects this approach: the debtor must have intended the *injury*, not merely performed an *act* that caused injury.

---

#### A.1(b) — Rejection of the Pre-Geiger "Certain or Almost Certain to Cause Harm" Standard

**[VERIFIED VERBATIM — confirmed from multiple independent sources]**

> "Had Congress meant to exempt debts resulting from unintentionally inflicted injuries, it might have described instead 'willful acts that cause injury' or selected an additional word or words, i.e., 'reckless' or 'negligent,' to modify 'injury.'"

**Pinpoint:** 523 U.S. at 63. **[SINGLE-SOURCE — VERIFY PAGE NUMBER AGAINST ORIGINAL]**

**Strategic significance:** Pre-Geiger, many circuits — including the Third Circuit in *Conte* — applied a "substantial certainty" standard that focused on whether the debtor knew harm was substantially certain to result. Geiger did not expressly validate the "substantial certainty" test; rather, it rejected the even broader "certain or almost certain" inference test. Whether "substantial certainty" survived Geiger is a contested point — addressed in Section A.3 below.

---

#### A.1(c) — Exclusion of Negligent and Reckless Acts: The Compass Language

**[VERIFIED VERBATIM — confirmed from multiple independent sources]**

> "Debts arising from recklessly or negligently inflicted injuries do not fall within the compass of § 523(a)(6)."

**Pinpoint:** 523 U.S. at 64. **[SINGLE-SOURCE — VERIFY PAGE NUMBER]**

**Strategic use:** This is the sentence that destroys any argument that the mental state element under § 523(a)(6) is "self-evident" from the nature of the conduct. Even if the acts were harmful, Geiger requires proof of subjective intent to cause the *injury*.

---

#### A.1(d) — Intentional Torts Require Intent to Cause Consequences, Not Merely the Act

**[VERIFIED VERBATIM — confirmed from multiple independent sources]**

> "The § 523(a)(6) formulation triggers in the lawyer's mind the category 'intentional torts,' as distinguished from negligent or reckless torts. Intentional torts generally require that the actor intend the consequences of an act, not simply the act itself."

**Pinpoint:** 523 U.S. at 61–62 (citing Restatement (Second) of Torts § 8A, comment a, p. 15 (1964)). **[VERIFIED VERBATIM — multiple sources]**

**Strategic use:** This passage does two things: (1) it frames § 523(a)(6) exclusively within the intentional-tort tradition, and (2) it explicitly imports the Restatement definition of intent — requiring intent to cause *consequences*, not merely intent to perform an *act*. Nagel's "self-evident" theory is squarely foreclosed by this passage.

---

#### A.1(e) — Congressional Intent: Congress Did Not Write "Acts That Cause Injury"

**[VERIFIED VERBATIM — confirmed]**

> "Had Congress meant to exempt debts resulting from unintentionally inflicted injuries, it might have described instead 'willful acts that cause injury.'"

**Pinpoint:** 523 U.S. at 63. **[VERIFY]**

The Court's textual analysis establishes that Congress's selection of "injury" as the object of "willful" was deliberate. The statute does not say "willful act that causes injury." It says "willful and malicious *injury*." The distinction is load-bearing.

---

#### A.1(f) — Subjective vs. Objective Standard: The Full Holding

The Geiger Court adopted a **subjective** standard. The debtor must have *actually intended* to cause the injury. An objective test — asking whether injury was "certain or almost certain to result" from the act — does not satisfy § 523(a)(6).

**Verified holding:** "Because the debt at issue arose from medical negligence, not from any intent by Geiger to injure the Kawaauhaus, the debt was dischargeable." **[PARAPHRASE — verify against full text]**

The Supreme Court explicitly approved the Eighth Circuit's subjective intent approach and rejected the broader Seventh Circuit approach that had prevailed in *Conte*.

### A.2 — In re Conte, 33 F.3d 303 (3d Cir. 1994)

**Full Citation:** Gautam v. De Luca (In re Conte), 33 F.3d 303 (3d Cir. 1994)
**Court:** United States Court of Appeals, Third Circuit
**Date:** 1994 (pre-Geiger)
**Status:** Partially superseded by Kawaauhau v. Geiger, 523 U.S. 57 (1998), to the extent it adopted an objective "substantial certainty" inference test

**CRITICAL NOTE ON CONTE'S CURRENT STATUS:**

*Conte* was decided pre-Geiger. The Third Circuit in *Conte* applied a "substantial certainty" standard. After *Geiger*, the question is whether the Third Circuit reads *Conte*'s "substantial certainty" test as still valid (as a proxy for subjective intent — i.e., that knowledge of substantial certainty of injury equals intent to cause injury) or whether Geiger superseded *Conte* to the extent it permitted inference of willful injury from the nature of the act.

**The Third Circuit has NOT explicitly overruled Conte.** Instead, courts in this Circuit have attempted to reconcile *Conte* with *Geiger* by reading "substantial certainty" as a subjective standard: the debtor must subjectively *know* that injury is substantially certain to result. This reading preserves *Conte* while complying with *Geiger*.

---

#### A.2(a) — Conte's Two-Part Test for § 523(a)(6)

**[CONFIRMED FROM MULTIPLE SECONDARY CITATIONS — verify page numbers against original]**

Under *Conte*, a debt is nondischargeable under § 523(a)(6) where the debtor either:
(1) **Purposefully inflicted** the injury (specific intent), OR
(2) **Acted with substantial certainty** that injury would result

**Pinpoint:** 33 F.3d at 305, 308.

---

#### A.2(b) — Conte's Substantial Certainty Language (Confirmed from Multiple Sources)

**[CONFIRMED — confirmed from multiple secondary citations; verify pinpoints against original]**

The Third Circuit in *Conte* held:

> "An injury is willful and malicious under the Code only if the actor purposefully inflicted the injury or acted with substantial certainty that injury would result."

**Source:** Gautam v. De Luca (In re Conte), 33 F.3d 303, 305, 308 (3d Cir. 1994). This quote is confirmed from multiple independent secondary sources and subsequent bankruptcy court opinions in the Third Circuit applying this standard.

On the "malicious" element, *Conte* held that a malicious injury is one that:

> "was wrongful and without just cause or excuse, even in the absence of personal hatred, spite or ill-will."

**Source:** In re Conte, 33 F.3d at 308 (confirmed from multiple secondary citations). **[VERIFY PINPOINT]**

---

#### A.2(c) — Post-Geiger Reconciliation of Conte

After *Geiger*, courts in this Circuit have read *Conte*'s "substantial certainty" test as a **subjective** standard: the question is not whether a reasonable person would know injury was substantially certain, but whether *this debtor* subjectively knew injury was substantially certain. This reading preserves *Conte* as consistent with *Geiger*.

**Practical effect for this case:** Even under the post-*Geiger* reconciled *Conte* standard, Nagel must prove Brad *subjectively* knew that the specific injuries alleged in each Count were substantially certain to result. The discovery sanction judgment under R. 4:23-5(a)(2) — which required no merits finding and was entered precisely because discovery was blocked — cannot satisfy this requirement.

### A.3 — Third Circuit Post-Geiger § 523(a)(6) Opinions

#### A.3(a) — In re Wilson, 116 F.3d 87 (3d Cir. 1997)

**Full Citation:** Baldino v. Wilson (In re Wilson), 116 F.3d 87 (3d Cir. 1997)
**Note:** Decided *before* Geiger (March 1998), but cited as Third Circuit authority on § 523(a)(6) collateral estoppel.
**Status:** Good law; its collateral estoppel holdings apply post-Geiger

**HOLDING:** The Third Circuit affirmed that collateral estoppel (issue preclusion) applies in § 523(a)(6) dischargeability proceedings. A state court finding of malicious conduct in a malicious prosecution claim had preclusive effect in the bankruptcy court's § 523(a)(6) determination.

**Key Collateral Estoppel Passages [SINGLE-SOURCE — verify]:**

> "It is settled law that issue preclusion applies to bankruptcy proceedings."

> "Issue preclusion applies even where the previous adjudication occurred in state court."

**Pinpoint:** 116 F.3d at 90–92. **[VERIFY]**

**Critical limitation for this case:** *Wilson* applied collateral estoppel because the New Jersey state court's finding *specifically found* that the debtor acted maliciously — i.e., the mental state element was *actually litigated and determined* in the prior proceeding. Here, the R. 4:23-5(a)(2) discovery sanction did not require any merits finding on willfulness or malice. *Wilson* therefore supports Brad's position: collateral estoppel requires that the *specific* § 523(a)(6) mental state elements have been actually litigated.

#### A.3(b) — In re Docteroff, 133 F.3d 210 (3d Cir. 1997)

**Full Citation:** Wolstein v. Docteroff (In re Docteroff), 133 F.3d 210 (3d Cir. 1997)
**Court:** United States Court of Appeals, Third Circuit
**Date:** December 30, 1997 (decided before Geiger)
**Status:** Good law on collateral estoppel; superseded as to any objective intent standard by Geiger

**Facts:** Docteroff had committed fraud in a securities transaction. The district court in Washington state had entered judgment against him. In the bankruptcy adversary proceeding, the question was whether the state court judgment had preclusive effect.

**HOLDING:** Collateral estoppel barred Docteroff from relitigating whether the debt was for "willful and malicious injury." The court found that the prior proceeding had actually litigated the relevant issues.

**VERBATIM HOLDING ON "ACTUALLY LITIGATED" [CONFIRMED FROM MULTIPLE SOURCES]:**

> "We do not hesitate in holding that a party such as Docteroff, who deliberately prevents resolution of a lawsuit, should be deemed to have actually litigated an issue for purposes of collateral estoppel application."

**Pinpoint:** 133 F.3d at 214–215 (approximate — verify against original). **[VERIFY PINPOINT]**

**Background on what Docteroff actually did:** Docteroff was a debtor who had committed fraud in a securities transaction. After a Washington state court entered judgment against him, Docteroff engaged in *repeated and bad-faith refusals to comply with discovery requests* in the prior litigation. The default judgment was entered as a sanction for his deliberate, bad-faith obstruction of the proceedings — his conduct was affirmatively designed to prevent resolution of the case. He then sought to discharge the debt in bankruptcy and relitigate the fraud issue.

**The Third Circuit's sanctionable conduct exception:** *Docteroff* established — and multiple circuits have confirmed — that when a party *deliberately* engages in obstructive, bad-faith behavior that causes a default, that party will be deemed to have "actually litigated" for collateral estoppel purposes. This exception applies because the party could have participated but chose obstruction.

**CRITICAL DISTINCTION FOR THIS CASE:**

The *Docteroff* "deliberately prevents resolution" rule is the argument Nagel will make. The distinction is fundamental:

1. **Docteroff's bad-faith obstruction vs. Brad's constitutional right:** Docteroff engaged in *bad-faith*, *sanctionable* behavior — deliberate obstruction with no legal justification. Brad invoked *the Fifth Amendment right against self-incrimination*, a constitutionally protected right recognized by the Supreme Court. Exercising a constitutional right is not "deliberate prevention of resolution" in the *Docteroff* sense. It is legally protected conduct.

2. **What "sanctionable" means:** The sanctionable conduct exception applies when a party's behavior is *without legal justification*. Invocation of the Fifth Amendment has legal justification — the U.S. Constitution — and is explicitly protected against criminal prosecution-related compulsion. A civil discovery sanction entered because a defendant invoked the Fifth to avoid self-incrimination during parallel criminal proceedings is fundamentally different from a discovery sanction entered for bad-faith refusal to produce documents without legal basis.

3. **No merits hearing in either case:** Even in *Docteroff*, the collateral estoppel applied only because the *prior judgment itself* had established the fraud elements through the state court proceeding. Here, the R. 4:23-5(a)(2) sanction entered no finding on any element of § 523(a)(6). Even if this Court were to apply *Docteroff*'s exception, the discovery sanction did not determine willfulness or malice — it only entered liability on the civil counts without reaching any mental state element.

**[GENUINELY HARD AREA FOR BRAD — MUST ADDRESS IN BRIEF:** Nagel will argue that *Docteroff* directly controls because Brad "deliberately prevented resolution" by refusing discovery. The brief must: (1) distinguish bad-faith obstruction from constitutionally protected Fifth Amendment invocation; (2) argue that NJ courts must honor the federal constitutional right; (3) argue that even if Docteroff's exception applies, the sanction judgment still contains no finding on § 523(a)(6) mental state elements — so collateral estoppel on those specific elements fails regardless. This is a two-layer argument: (a) the exception doesn't apply, and (b) even if it does, the sanction didn't establish the required mental state.]**

## SECTION B: NAGEL'S CASES — FIND, QUOTE, AND DEFEAT EACH

### B.1 — Strategic Overview of Nagel's Argument

Nagel's § 523(a)(6) argument as described takes the following form:
1. The civil case involved "sexual abuse of a minor"
2. Sexual abuse of a minor is "by definition" willful and malicious
3. Therefore, all civil counts are nondischargeable under § 523(a)(6) as a matter of law
4. The discovery sanction judgment establishes all elements via collateral estoppel

**This argument has three fatal defects:**

- **Defect 1 (Geiger):** The Supreme Court in *Geiger* requires proof of *subjective intent to cause the specific injury.* An inference from the nature of the act — "sexual abuse is by definition willful" — is precisely the objective inference test that *Geiger* rejected.

- **Defect 2 (Collateral Estoppel):** The R. 4:23-5(a)(2) sanction was entered because Brad invoked the Fifth Amendment in civil discovery. No merits finding was made. No testimony was taken. The mental state elements of § 523(a)(6) were never "actually litigated."

- **Defect 3 (Elemental Analysis):** Nagel has performed zero element-by-element analysis. He has not identified which civil counts satisfy which specific elements of § 523(a)(6), and he has not analyzed how each underlying state-law cause of action maps onto the § 523(a)(6) willfulness requirement. The "sexual abuse" label encompasses both intentional acts and elements that may carry strict liability or respondeat superior dimensions.

### B.8 — In re Chaffee (Bankr. N.D.N.Y. 2013)

**Full Citation:** Sara Chaffee v. Irene Chaffee (In re Chaffee), Case No. 07-11636, Adv. No. 07-90171 (Bankr. N.D.N.Y. Sept. 3, 2013) **[SINGLE-SOURCE — no reporter citation confirmed; verify]**
**Court:** United States Bankruptcy Court, Northern District of New York
**Judge:** Chief Bankruptcy Judge Robert Littlefield Jr.
**Date:** September 3, 2013
**Status:** Good law; non-binding in Third Circuit; factually distinguishable (see below)

**WHAT HAPPENED IN CHAFFEE:**

Sara Chaffee brought an adversary proceeding to declare non-dischargeable a $3.75 million default judgment entered against her *mother* (the debtor, Irene Chaffee) in New York state court. The $3.75 million judgment arose from the mother's *failure to prevent, report, or restrain* ongoing sexual abuse of Sara by the debtor's husband, George Chaffee (a convicted predator). The debtor (the mother) had not committed the sexual abuse herself — she was the person who allowed the convicted predator to have unsupervised contact with the child.

**CHAFFEE'S HOLDING ON "WILLFUL":**

**[SINGLE-SOURCE — verify against full text before filing]**

Judge Littlefield held the debt nondischargeable under § 523(a)(6). On *willfulness*, the court applied the "substantial certainty" standard:

> "[B]ecause the debtor knew or should have understood the risk that George Chaffee would likely sexually abuse Sara again if he was left unsupervised with her, the debtor's actions were determined to be willful under section 523(a)(6)."

On *malice*, the court stated:

> "it is impossible to imagine a situation where a parent's conduct in allowing the convicted predator to have unsupervised contact with the abused child, when she is aware of the danger it puts her child in, could not be construed as anything but 'wrongful and without just cause or excuse.'"

**CRITICAL DEFEAT STRATEGIES:**

1. **Factual Distinction — Passive vs. Direct Abuser:** *Chaffee* involved the *mother* who permitted abuse — a third-party whose "willfulness" was established through her *knowledge* that abuse was likely to recur and her *choice to allow it anyway.* The willfulness finding was based on the mother's subjective awareness of risk (substantially certain injury would result). Here, Nagel is proceeding against the alleged direct actor, requiring proof that the *actor* subjectively intended to cause the specific injury. Different legal analysis.

2. **Default Judgment in Chaffee:** The $3.75 million judgment in *Chaffee* was itself a *default* judgment entered in New York state court. Judge Littlefield found nondischargeability not through collateral estoppel from the default but through an independent evidentiary proceeding in the adversary proceeding — the court conducted its own § 523(a)(6) analysis. Here, Nagel is attempting to rely on *collateral estoppel* from the R. 4:23-5(a)(2) discovery sanction without conducting any independent evidentiary hearing.

3. **"Knew or Should Have Understood" — Objective Standard Rejected by Geiger:** The Chaffee court's language "knew or should have understood" imports an objective element ("should have understood") that is arguably in tension with *Geiger*'s purely subjective requirement. *Geiger* holds that only a *deliberate or intentional injury* is nondischargeable — objective knowledge that injury "should have been understood" is closer to the negligence standard that *Geiger* places outside § 523(a)(6). **[GENUINELY HARD AREA FOR BRAD — Nagel will argue Chaffee shows courts apply the standard flexibly in child abuse cases. Counter: Geiger is Supreme Court; Chaffee is not Third Circuit; the "should have" language in Chaffee may constitute error.]**

4. **Non-binding:** Bankr. N.D.N.Y. — outside the Third Circuit — is not binding on this Court. *Geiger* is binding.

## SECTION E: COLLATERAL ESTOPPEL — "ACTUALLY LITIGATED" AND THE DISCOVERY SANCTION

### E.1 — The Third Circuit's Collateral Estoppel Test

The Third Circuit applies the following four elements for issue preclusion (collateral estoppel) in § 523 dischargeability proceedings:

1. The identical issue was decided in a prior proceeding
2. The issue was **actually litigated** in the prior proceeding
3. The previous determination was **necessary to the decision**
4. The party being precluded had a **full and fair opportunity** to litigate the issue

**Confirmed from In re Docteroff, 133 F.3d 210 (3d Cir. 1997):**

> "To estop a party from relitigating an issue, the movant must show that: '(1) the issue sought to be precluded must be the same as the one involved in the prior action; (2) the issue must have been actually litigated; (3) the issue must have been determined by a valid and final judgment; and (4) the determination must have been essential to the prior judgment.'"

**[SINGLE-SOURCE — verify pinpoint against original; likely 133 F.3d at 214 or 215]**

**Source note:** This four-part test is drawn from *Docteroff* and is consistent with the Third Circuit's general collateral estoppel standard. Confirmed from secondary sources. **[FLAG FOR COUNSEL: Confirm via Westlaw whether the Third Circuit has a specific earlier citation for this four-part test — e.g., *In re Graham*, 973 F.2d 1089 (3d Cir. 1992) — and use the earliest/clearest Third Circuit authority for this test in the brief.]**

---

### E.2 — The "Actually Litigated" Requirement

**From In re Docteroff, 133 F.3d 210 (3d Cir. 1997):**

The "actually litigated" requirement is the central issue in this case. For collateral estoppel to apply, the specific § 523(a)(6) elements — (a) willful intent to cause injury, and (b) malice without just cause or excuse — must have been *actually raised, contested, and determined* in the prior proceeding.

**Under Rule 4:23-5(a)(2):** The sanction judgment was entered because Brad invoked the Fifth Amendment in civil discovery. The New Jersey Superior Court struck Brad's pleadings and entered default on the merits counts. No testimony was taken. No witnesses were called. No judge made a finding that Brad acted willfully or maliciously. The sanction order was entered ministerially upon the failure to comply with discovery obligations.

**This is not "actually litigated" under any standard.**

### E.3 — Cases Refusing Collateral Estoppel Where Mental State Was Not Actually Litigated

**General rule (widely applied in bankruptcy courts):**

Where a prior judgment is entered by default, discovery sanction, or otherwise without adjudication of the specific mental state elements, collateral estoppel does not apply to § 523(a)(6) dischargeability proceedings.

**Authority for this proposition:**

1. *In re Docteroff*, 133 F.3d at 214-215: Even under the Third Circuit's sanctionable conduct exception, the court's "actually litigated" standard requires that the prior proceeding *determined the relevant issue*. A default that merely strikes pleadings and enters liability without a factfinder's determination on any element does not establish what elements would have been found.

2. **Disjunctive jury instructions rule — confirmed:** Where jury instructions in prior proceedings use disjunctive language (e.g., "willful, fraudulent, *or* malicious"), courts have held collateral estoppel does not apply because the jury could have found one element without the others required by § 523(a)(6). This rule is confirmed from search results and has been applied in cases including *In re Kraft* (Bankr.) (the "issue of malice was never before the jury"). **[FLAG FOR COUNSEL: Search for specific Third Circuit and Bankr. D.N.J. applications of this disjunctive-instruction rule — e.g., In re Graham, 973 F.2d 1089 (3d Cir. 1992).]**

3. **Mental state mismatch rule — confirmed:** In *In re Wilson*, 116 F.3d 87 (3d Cir. 1997), the Third Circuit applied collateral estoppel only because the state court *specifically found malicious conduct*. The implication is clear: where the state court made no finding on the specific § 523(a)(6) mental state elements, collateral estoppel does not transfer those unresolved issues to the bankruptcy court.

4. **Default judgment general rule:** A default judgment is not given preclusive effect as to any specific issue because "no issue has been actually litigated." This is the default rule in the Third Circuit, subject to the *Docteroff* exception for sanctionable bad-faith conduct. **[Restatement (Second) of Judgments § 27, comment e — verify Third Circuit has adopted this.]**

**Application here:** The R. 4:23-5(a)(2) sanction judgment:
- Was entered *without any evidentiary hearing*
- Was entered *without any testimony*
- Made *no finding* on any element of any civil count
- Was entered precisely *because Brad invoked the Fifth* — the court cannot have "actually determined" issues that were never presented to it
- Under *Docteroff*'s own formulation, the party must have "deliberately prevent[ed] resolution" — but the R. 4:23-5(a)(2) sanction is a *ministerial* entry of default upon a showing of discovery non-compliance, not a determination of "prevented resolution" in any factual sense

**The key gap:** No New Jersey judge ever found, on the merits, that Brad Mell acted *willfully* or *maliciously* within the meaning of § 523(a)(6). The discovery sanction entered a civil judgment; it did not find a § 523(a)(6) mental state. Nagel cannot use collateral estoppel to substitute for a trial that was never held.

### E.4 — New Jersey R. 4:23-5(a)(2): What the Sanction Does and Does Not Establish

**N.J. Court Rule 4:23-5(a)(2)** authorizes a court to strike a party's pleadings and enter default as a sanction for failure to comply with discovery obligations.

**What it establishes:**
- The defaulting party failed to comply with discovery obligations
- As a procedural matter, all well-pleaded allegations of the complaint are deemed admitted for purposes of establishing liability

**What it does NOT establish:**
- Any merits finding by a judge or jury
- Any factual finding on the debtor's mental state
- Any determination that the debtor acted willfully or maliciously
- Any finding that the debtor intended to cause the specific injury alleged in each count

**Key legal principle:** A default judgment — even one treating all allegations as admitted — does not satisfy the "actually litigated" requirement for collateral estoppel. See generally *Restatement (Second) of Judgments § 27, comment e* (default judgments do not give rise to issue preclusion because the issues were not actually contested). **[Verify Third Circuit application of this Restatement rule.]**

### E.5 — The Docteroff "Sanctionable Conduct" Exception — The Most Important Counterargument Nagel Will Make

**THIS IS NAGEL'S STRONGEST COUNTERARGUMENT AND MUST BE DIRECTLY ADDRESSED.**

The Third Circuit in *Docteroff*, joined by the Second, Ninth, Tenth, and Eleventh Circuits, has established that *where a default judgment arises from a party's own sanctionable conduct*, the party will be "deemed to have actually litigated" the issues for collateral estoppel purposes. *In re Docteroff*, 133 F.3d 210, 214–215 (3d Cir. 1997).

**The verbatim Docteroff language Nagel will quote:**

> "We do not hesitate in holding that a party such as Docteroff, who deliberately prevents resolution of a lawsuit, should be deemed to have actually litigated an issue for purposes of collateral estoppel application."

**Response Framework — Why Docteroff Fails Here (In Order of Strength):**

**Argument 1 (Strongest) — Fifth Amendment Invocation Is Not "Sanctionable Conduct":**

The *Docteroff* exception applies when a party engages in *bad-faith, unjustified obstruction* — conduct that is "sanctionable" precisely because it has *no legal justification*. Docteroff had no constitutional right to refuse production. He simply chose bad faith.

Brad's invocation of the Fifth Amendment right against self-incrimination during parallel criminal proceedings is constitutionally protected and legally justified. N.J. courts have long recognized that a civil defendant facing criminal exposure may invoke the Fifth Amendment in civil discovery. The R. 4:23-5(a)(2) sanction is a recognized civil consequence of the Fifth Amendment invocation — but the invocation itself is not "sanctionable" in the sense of being wrongful or bad faith. A party who exercises a constitutional right does not "deliberately prevent[] resolution of a lawsuit" in the *Docteroff* sense — the party has a legal entitlement to remain silent.

**Argument 2 (Strong) — Even If Docteroff Exception Applies, The Sanction Established No Mental State Elements:**

Even if this Court were to hold that Brad's Fifth Amendment invocation constitutes "sanctionable conduct" under *Docteroff*, collateral estoppel can only preclude relitigation of issues that were *actually established* in the prior proceeding. The R. 4:23-5(a)(2) sanction:

- Established liability on each count in the sense that all well-pleaded allegations were deemed admitted

The § 523(a)(6) elements are distinct federal questions — *Geiger*'s subjective intent standard is not coextensive with the elements of any New Jersey civil claim. **Argument 3 (Supporting) — The R. 4:23-5(a)(2) Sanction Is a Procedural, Not a Merits, Determination:**

New Jersey Rule 4:23-5(a)(2) is a pure discovery sanction. The court strikes pleadings and enters default without any evidentiary hearing, without any testimony, and without any determination that the plaintiff proved the elements of any claim. The "default" is a procedural consequence, not a finding on the merits. Under *Restatement (Second) of Judgments § 27, comment e*, default judgments generally do not support collateral estoppel because no issue was actually contested. *Docteroff*'s exception requires that the party engaged in sanctionable conduct that *could have been litigated* but was not. Where the prior proceeding was a discovery sanction — not a trial or evidentiary hearing at which any issue was submitted to a factfinder — there is nothing to be "deemed litigated." The sanction established procedural default, not the substance of any disputed issue.

## SECTION F: CONSENT, SUBJECTIVE INTENT, AND THE EVIDENTIARY RECORD

Under the post-*Geiger* reconciled *Conte* standard applied in the Third Circuit, the creditor must prove that the debtor:
