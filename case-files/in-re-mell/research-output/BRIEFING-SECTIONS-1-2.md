## SECTION 2: STATEMENT OF FACTS

### A. Background: Steven Bradley Mell and the 2012 Family Trusts

Steven Bradley Mell is a fifty-two-year-old New Jersey resident and former principal of Gulfstream Capital Management LLC, a private investment and asset management firm operating out of Bedminster, New Jersey. Through Gulfstream and its related entities, Brad managed a portfolio of investments and operated a charter aviation business, Aero Care Services, LLC, which owned two aircraft used for business and personal travel. He and his then-wife Kimberly Ruggles Mell maintained a family home at 620 Black River Road, Bedminster, New Jersey, and together they raised three children — Emily, Eliza, and William Mell — who were born and raised in that community.

In December 2012, Brad and Kimberly executed estate planning instruments that would become the principal focus of the trust-fraud allegations in the state court litigation: the Steven Bradley Mell 2012 Family Trust ("SBM Trust") and the Kimberly Ruggles Mell 2012 Family Trust ("KRM Trust"). The timing of these instruments — December 18, 2012, specifically — is the most legally important fact in this case for § 707(a) purposes, and it cannot be overstated. The first sexual encounter between Brad and B.B. did not occur until June 20, 2017. (Appellate-div-opinion-analysis.md § 6; nagel-mtd-analysis.md § 5.) The trusts predate any cognizable debt to B.B. by approximately four years and eight months. At the moment those trust instruments were executed, Brad Mell had never met Lilly Cannon. She was ten years old.

The SBM Trust was established with Brad as the grantor and Kimberly Ruggles Mell as the trustee. The designated beneficiaries were Kimberly and the three children. The trust's express purpose, as set forth in Article 3(A) of the Trust Agreement, was to "provide for the health, education (including, but not limited to, elementary, secondary, undergraduate, graduate and postgraduate education), maintenance and support of KIMBERLY, the Children and their issue, as well as for any expenses incurred by or for them, or any of them, because of any illness, operation, infirmity, or other medical emergency." (SBM Trust SJ Motion Jan 15, 2026.pdf, Statement of Material Facts ¶¶ 10–12, citing Ex. 2 to Whalen Cert.) Brad himself was never a beneficiary of the SBM Trust. (Id. ¶ 46.) The KRM Trust was established with Kimberly Ruggles Mell as the grantor and three independent individuals as trustees — Michael Simon Scott among them — with Brad's three children (Emily, Eliza, and William) as the designated beneficiaries. (KRM SJ Motion Jan 15, 2026-7-45.pdf, Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶¶ 5–8.) The KRM Trust Agreement expressly limited the trustee's distribution authority to providing "for the health, education . . . maintenance and support of the Children and their issue." (Id. ¶ 8.)

Upon formation of the two trusts in December 2012, the Bedminster family home was transferred into them: a recorded deed conveyed a 70% interest in the property to the KRM Trust and a 30% interest to the SBM Trust. (KRM SJ Motion, SUMF ¶¶ 13–14.) Simultaneously, Brad transferred his sole ownership interest in Aero Care Services, LLC — and through Aero Care, the two aircraft — into the SBM Trust. (SBM Trust SJ Motion, SUMF ¶ 31.) These were legitimate transfers of family assets into estate-planning vehicles at a time when Brad owed no obligation to the plaintiff, there was no pending or threatened litigation involving the plaintiff, and — as the plaintiff herself confirmed at her July 3, 2025 deposition — B.B. "had no clue of when, how, or why the Trusts were set up" and had "no knowledge on those trusts" prior to this litigation. (Whalen Response in support SJ Mar 4, 2026.pdf, Response to CSMF ¶ 93, quoting B.B. Dep. at 22:12-16.) Brad testified at his own deposition in September 2021 that the SBM Trust was "formed in 2012 for estate planning purposes and to support his children." (SBM Trust SJ Motion, SUMF ¶ 11.)

Nagel's MTD characterizes these trusts as fraudulent mechanisms. But the legal reality is unassailable: under 11 U.S.C. § 548(a)(1), the two-year lookback window for federal fraudulent transfer avoidance runs from the petition date of June 12, 2026 back to June 12, 2024. The 2012 trust formations are fourteen years outside that window — categorically unreachable as a matter of statutory text. (part6-factual.md § B.1.) Under the New Jersey Uniform Voidable Transactions Act, N.J.S.A. 25:2-27, constructive fraudulent transfer claims reach only creditors "whose claim arose before the transfer was made." Because B.B. is not a creditor whose claim predated December 2012, she cannot invoke the constructive fraud prong. N.J.S.A. 25:2-25, the actual fraud prong covering future creditors, theoretically covers B.B. — but requires proof that Brad formed those trusts in 2012 "with actual intent to hinder, delay, or defraud" a person he would not meet for another three years. That logical impossibility is the airtight trust timing defense. (part6-factual.md §§ B.2–B.3; FINAL-MEMO.md Part VIII.) Moreover, SOFA Line 19, signed by Brad under penalty of perjury, confirms he made no transfers to self-settled trusts within the ten-year lookback window from June 12, 2026 — a window that reaches back only to June 12, 2016, fully four years after the trusts were established. (SCHEDULES-SUMMARY.md § 5, SOFA Line 19.)

### C. The Civil Litigation — *B.B. v. Mell*, ESX-L-7200-19 (2019–2026)

On October 2, 2019, B.B., through Bruce Nagel and Nagel Rice LLP, filed a civil complaint in the Superior Court of New Jersey, Law Division, Essex County, under Docket No. ESX-L-7200-19. The initial complaint named Brad Mell and certain business entities as defendants but did not yet name the KRM Trust, SBM Trust, or Trust Beneficiaries. (KRM & SBM Civil Release Mar 6, 2026.pdf, Recitals.) The complaint alleged causes of action arising from the same course of conduct that formed the basis of the federal criminal proceedings.

On January 27, 2020, the trial court entered a prejudgment writ of attachment. That attachment — and the approximately $750,000 currently held in the Nagel Rice LLP attorney trust account as a result of it — is listed by Brad on his Schedule A/B as a disclosed asset at line 35. (SCHEDULES-SUMMARY.md § 3.) Nagel's MTD describes the attachment as "approximately $1 million dollars held in her counsel's escrow account." (Nagel-mtd-analysis.md § 8, Misrepresentation 6.) The precise figure, as Brad's own schedules reflect, is $750,000 in the Nagel Rice trust account — the other escrowed amounts ($75,000 with the Greifinger firm and $150,000 with McElroy Deutsch as attorneys for Gulfstream) represent funds from other proceedings. What is most notable about this attachment in the bankruptcy context is that Brad disclosed it voluntarily on his schedules — identified by custodian, by firm name, and by dollar amount. A debtor attempting to hide assets does not list the $750,000 sitting in his adversary's counsel's trust account on the very first page of his financial disclosure.

On April 17, 2020, plaintiff filed the First Amended Complaint, adding the KRM Trust, SBM Trust, and Trust Beneficiaries as defendants. (KRM & SBM Civil Release, Recitals; KRM SJ Motion, SUMF ¶ 2.) The new causes of action against the trusts alleged unjust enrichment, constructive trust, and — when the Second Amended Complaint followed on December 13, 2021 — fraudulent transfer under the New Jersey Uniform Voidable Transactions Act in connection with proceeds from the 2021 boat sale. (KRM SJ Motion, SUMF ¶¶ 2–4.)

On March 26, 2021, the trial court entered a partial summary judgment on liability in favor of B.B. on Counts I, II, III, VII, and VIII of the First Amended Complaint — claims arising under the Child Sexual Abuse Act, the child exploitation statute, assault and battery, and two invasion of privacy counts. The court denied summary judgment on the false imprisonment count. This order, which features prominently in Nagel's MTD as a supposedly dispositive adjudication of liability, was characterized by the New Jersey Appellate Division itself in footnote 2 of the December 31, 2024 opinion as "interlocutory and not a final judgment," with the court further noting: "It is not clear from the record whether Mell responded to the summary judgment motion." (Appellate-div-opinion-analysis.md § 2, quoting A-2990-21 at fn. 2.) That is the controlling judicial characterization of that order for preclusion purposes in this bankruptcy proceeding. Moreover, the Appellate Division's opinion makes clear that as of December 31, 2024 — more than three and a half years after the partial summary judgment — "Mell has yet to be deposed." (Id. § 4.) The record from which Nagel now claims a complete liability determination is an interlocutory order entered on an incomplete record against a defendant who, through counsel, invoked his Fifth Amendment rights during civil discovery while facing parallel federal criminal prosecution. Comparative fault, the liability of the non-Mell defendants, and the fraudulent transfer claims against the trusts all remained unresolved.

On July 23, 2021, a default judgment was entered against W.H. Mell Associates, Inc. in the state court proceedings. (Nagel-mtd-analysis.md § 5.)

The multi-year delay between the filing of suit in October 2019 and the trial scheduled for June 2026 is the period that Nagel now cites as evidence of Brad's bad faith in filing for bankruptcy. That characterization inverts the actual history. The delay was caused, in the first instance, by Nagel himself. B.B., through Nagel Rice, filed an interlocutory appeal — designated as Docket No. A-2990-21 in the Superior Court of New Jersey, Appellate Division — challenging the trial court's ruling on the permissible scope of deposition questioning during B.B.'s own deposition. The appeal was argued before a three-judge panel (Judges Accurso, Firko, and Natali) on November 10, 2022. The Appellate Division issued its decision on December 31, 2024 — more than two years after argument, more than five years after the suit was filed. (Appellate-div-opinion-analysis.md, Preliminary Identification, § 6.) The court's entire holding was that the trial court's deposition scope ruling was correct but the age-16 cutoff in the protective order was too restrictive — essentially granting neither side a meaningful victory after consuming three years of what would otherwise have been pre-trial preparation time. That three-year interlocutory appeal is the proximate cause of the "eve of trial" dynamic that Nagel now invokes. Under *In re Tamecki*'s totality-of-circumstances test, a court is entitled to consider which party created the eve. (FINAL-MEMO.md Part II, § C.1.)

Trial was finally scheduled to commence on Monday, June 15, 2026 — a date established only after the Appellate Division's December 2024 decision allowed the case to proceed. (Nagel-mtd-analysis.md § 5, noting the internal inconsistency in Nagel's papers between June 14 and June 15; June 15, 2026 was the Monday, confirmed by the Court's OSC of that date.) Brad Mell filed his voluntary Chapter 7 petition on June 12, 2026, the Friday before trial. He represented himself pro se on the state court notice of filing (Bankruptcy filing.pdf, filed ESX-L-007200-19 at 3:28 PM on June 12, 2026), and simultaneously directed all inquiries to his bankruptcy counsel of record, Anthony Sodono III, Esq., of McManimon, Scotland & Baumann, LLC.

### D. The March 2026 Trust Settlement — The Omitted Bombshell

Before the June 2026 trial date could arrive — and while Nagel was presumably preparing his trial strategy — a significant chapter in the B.B. v. Mell litigation closed quietly, without public announcement. In late February and early March 2026, Nagel Rice's own partner Michael J. Paragano, on behalf of B.B. (Lilly Cannon), negotiated and executed a full and final settlement of all claims against the KRM Trust, the SBM Trust, and the Trust Beneficiaries. The settlement is documented in three primary instruments now in our possession:

The first is a February 27, 2026 transmittal letter signed by Paragano and addressed to counsel for the KRM Trust (A. Ross Pearlson, Esq., Chiesa Shahinian & Giantomasi PC) and counsel for the SBM Trust (Patrick J. Whalen, Esq.). The letter transmits the settlement release and a W-9, and directs: "Kindly issue a settlement check in the amount of $300,000.00 made payable to Lillian Cannon & Nagel Rice, LLP." (Nagel Settlement Letter Feb 27, 2026.pdf.) That letter also contains a separate February 26, 2026 confirmation signed by Bruce H. Nagel himself stating: "This will confirm that our firm will not sue the Kimberly Ruggles Mell 2012 Family Trust or the Steven Bradley Mell 2012 Family Trust in the future." The second instrument is the Full and Final Settlement Agreement and Mutual Release itself, executed in counterparts between March 4 and March 6, 2026. (KRM & SBM Civil Release Mar 6, 2026.pdf.) The parties to the settlement are Lilly Cannon (the plaintiff, represented by Nagel Rice LLP), the KRM Trust (represented by Chiesa Shahinian through A. Ross Pearlson), the SBM Trust (represented by Patrick J. Whalen), Michael Simon Scott as KRM Trust Trustee, Kimberly Ruggles Mell as SBM Trust Trustee and SBM Trust Beneficiary, and Emily, Eliza, and William Mell as Trust Beneficiaries. (Id., Parties recital.) The third instrument is the Mutual Release executed as Exhibit A to the settlement agreement, covering claims between the Cannons and the trust parties. (Id., Exhibit A.)

The settlement payment is unambiguous: "Within ten (10) days of the full execution of this Agreement by all Parties, the KRM Trust and SBM Trust shall pay to Plaintiff the combined and total sum of Three Hundred Thousand and 00/100 ($300,000.00) Dollars." (Id. ¶ 2.) The release language is equally unambiguous and its scope should be carefully noted by this Court:

> "Each of the Parties . . . shall knowingly and voluntarily remise, release, and forever discharge each other Party and each of the Party's respective current, former and future agents, assigns, successors, beneficiaries, representatives, trustees, fiduciaries, executors, administrators, employees, insurers, attorneys, representatives and any person under their control . . . from all actions, causes of action, suits, debts, dues, sums of money, accounts, costs, expenses, liabilities or causes of action whatsoever . . . from the beginning of time and through the date of the full execution of this Agreement, including but not limited to any claims that were or could have been asserted in the Lawsuit against the Releasees, **as well as any current or future claims related to efforts to collect against any assets currently held by or in the possession of the KRM Trust or the SBM Trust on any judgment that any Party may obtain against any other individual or entity in connection with the Lawsuit.**"

(KRM & SBM Civil Release Mar 6, 2026.pdf ¶ 3, emphasis added.) The settlement expressly contemplated the possibility that B.B. might obtain a judgment against Brad Mell personally — and released "any current or future claims" against KRM Trust and SBM Trust assets as a means of collecting on that judgment. This is not boilerplate. It is a specifically negotiated forward-looking release that bars B.B. from doing precisely what Nagel's MTD argues this Court should enable: treating the KRM and SBM Trust assets as available to satisfy a judgment against Brad.

The dismissal mechanics under the settlement confirm that both trust SJ motions — filed January 15, 2026 and returnable March 20, 2026 — were withdrawn as part of the settlement. Paragraph 5 of the agreement states: "The KRM Trust, SBM Trust, and Trust Beneficiaries shall subsequently withdraw any pending summary judgment motions asserted against Plaintiff." (Id. ¶ 5.) The state court docket confirms this: the KRM Trust dismissal was filed March 16, 2026 (KRM Dismissal Mar 16, 2026.pdf) and the SBM Trust dismissal was filed March 18, 2026 (SBM Trust dismissal Mar 18, 2026.pdf).

Three months later, on June 23, 2026, Nagel filed Doc. 8 in this Court. The Motion to Dismiss devotes substantial portions of its Statement of Facts to characterizing the KRM and SBM Trusts as fraudulent mechanisms by which Brad sheltered assets from B.B. The Nagel Certification attached to the motion describes the trusts as having been "utilized . . . as a mechanism to avoid B.B.'s potential claims and to transfer marital assets." (Nagel-mtd-analysis.md § 4.) The motion does not mention the March 2026 settlement. It does not disclose that Nagel's own firm received a portion of a $300,000 payment from those trusts less than ninety days before the motion was filed. It does not disclose the release of "any current or future claims" against trust assets. It does not disclose that the KRM Trust SJ motion and the SBM Trust SJ motion — both of which were litigated on the proposition that the trusts are legitimate estate-planning vehicles with legally cognizable defenses — were withdrawn as part of the settlement, with no admission of liability.

The settlement's confidentiality clause anticipates court disclosure: paragraph 10 permits any party to "disclose a copy of this Agreement to a Court of Law, in a sealed envelope marked 'Confidential,' for the purpose of enforcing the terms of this Agreement or effectuating any of the purposes and obligations stated herein." (Id. ¶ 10.) The settlement is thus expressly contemplated as disclosable to a court. That Nagel elected not to disclose it to this Court, while simultaneously relying on the trust-fraud narrative as the centerpiece of his bad-faith argument, raises serious questions under N.J. RPC 3.3(a)(5) (duty to disclose material facts to the tribunal) and Fed. R. Bankr. P. 9011(b)(3) (factual certifications must have evidentiary support). (FINAL-MEMO.md Part VII; part6-factual.md § C.3.)

There is an additional strategic significance to the trust settlement that goes beyond the candor issue. The two trust SJ motions litigated precisely the propositions that Nagel's MTD now inverts. Pat Whalen, on behalf of the SBM Trust, submitted a nineteen-page statement of material facts establishing, through deposition testimony, recorded deeds, closing documents, and the MSA itself, that: (1) the SBM Trust was formed in December 2012 for legitimate estate planning purposes, (2) Aero Care and the aircraft were transferred into the SBM Trust in 2012, fully five years before any encounter with B.B., (3) the plane sales in September and October 2018 were arm's-length transactions at fair market value to unrelated third parties (Hoover Aviation and Trekker Holdings/Puerto Rico Wire Services), (4) the Bedminster home was a trust asset from December 2012 onwards and the SBM Trust received its contractual 30% of the net sale proceeds in April 2019 in accordance with the MSA, and (5) B.B. herself acknowledged at deposition that she "had no knowledge on those trusts." (SBM Trust SJ Motion Jan 15, 2026.pdf, SUMF ¶¶ 6–63.) Pearlson's motion on behalf of the KRM Trust made the same showing with respect to the KRM Trust's 70% interest in the Bedminster home, the trust's $964,884 share of sale proceeds, and the boat-sale distributions representing repayment of a documented loan from June 2016 — more than a year before the conduct involving B.B. began. (KRM SJ Motion, SUMF ¶¶ 5–22.) The settlement that followed those SJ motions was not a concession by the trusts of any liability. The agreement expressly states: "the Parties desire to resolve the Claims and Third-Party Claims amicably, without any factual or legal concessions or admissions of liability of any kind." (KRM & SBM Civil Release ¶ 4.) The trusts' agreement to pay $300,000 to resolve litigation that had been pending for six years — with trials and depositions and third-party claims — represents a commercial resolution of ongoing litigation risk, nothing more. It does not establish that the trusts were fraudulent. It establishes that Nagel and his client concluded it was worth $300,000 to be rid of the litigation, and it establishes that B.B. released trust assets as a collection vehicle. Nagel cannot now tell this Court that those same trusts are engines of fraud while his firm is cashing the settlement check.

### E. Brad's Financial Condition at Filing — The Schedule Facts

On June 12, 2026, Brad Mell filed a voluntary petition for relief under Chapter 7 of the Bankruptcy Code. (Bankruptcy filing.pdf; US Bankruptcy Court NJ - Live Database.pdf, Docket Entry 1, filed 06/12/2026.) The petition was accompanied by a credit counseling certificate and represented by McManimon, Scotland & Baumann, LLC. On June 25, 2026, two weeks after the initial petition, Brad filed the complete set of schedules, statement of financial affairs, means test form, statement of intention, and all required attachments. (SCHEDULES-SUMMARY.md § 1.) These are not bare-bones filings. The contrast with the kind of schedules that typically support bad-faith dismissals — concealed business entities, hidden bank accounts, undisclosed real estate — is stark and immediate.

The schedules disclose total assets of $2,181,376. That figure is striking on its face, but the substance behind it explains, rather than undermines, the bankruptcy filing. Their existence in the schedules does not evidence wealth available for distribution; it reflects mandatory statutory disclosure of exempt assets. The next largest component is $750,000 listed on Schedule A/B line 35 as the civil attachment funds held in the "Nagel & Rice LLP Attorney Trust Account." (Id.) That $750,000 is the product of the January 27, 2020 prejudgment attachment from the state court proceeding. The balance of the financial asset column consists of $75,000 held in the Greifinger firm's trust account and $150,000 held by McElroy Deutsch as attorneys for Gulfstream, both of which are similarly disclosed without concealment. A 1995 Ferrari 355 with 45,000 miles is listed on Schedule A/B at an estimated value of $75,000. Only $22,500 of that value is claimed exempt (split between the § 522(d)(2) motor vehicle exemption and the § 522(d)(5) wildcard), leaving approximately $52,500 in non-exempt equity available to the bankruptcy estate for the benefit of creditors. Two Jeep Wranglers (2010 and 2017), a 1945 Jeep CJ2A, and a 2012 Yamaha dirt bike round out the vehicle schedule. WNFW LLC — the only disclosed business interest — is listed at 99% ownership with a stated value of $97. (Id.)

Courtney (Peter) Mell, Brad's brother, with a $200,000 personal loan claim (Schedule E/F 4.5), listed as not disputed — the same brother who pays Brad's monthly living support and who funded the $5,000 bankruptcy retainer. Diana Mell, with a $75,000 loan claim (Schedule E/F 4.6). The KRM Trust itself, with an $80,000 deficiency claim listed as contingent/unliquidated/disputed (Schedule E/F 4.4). The SBM Trust, with a $30,000 deficiency claim listed as contingent/unliquidated/disputed (Schedule E/F 4.9). Kimberly Mell for the benefit of W.H. Mell Associates, Inc., with a contingent claim of $800,000 (Schedule E/F 4.7). And Nagel Rice LLP itself — Bruce Nagel's firm, the firm that filed this motion to dismiss — with a $10,000 nonpriority unsecured sanctions claim arising out of the state court litigation (Schedule E/F 4.8, identified by address as "Attn: Bruce H. Nagel, Esq."). (SCHEDULES-SUMMARY.md §§ 2–3.)

Brad's income picture is equally clear, and equally inconsistent with the profile of a strategic abuser of the bankruptcy system. He is not employed. He has no wages, no business income, no rental income, no investment income, no Social Security, no pension, and no government assistance. His only income is $3,500 per month in support from his brother Courtney — the same brother who is also a listed creditor. His monthly expenses, documented on Schedule J, total $4,552: $2,000 for rent (at a discounted rate from Courtney, who also holds the personal loan), $500 for utilities, $1,000 for food, and the balance for basic living necessities. The monthly deficit is negative $1,052. Brad acknowledged on Schedule J, line 24, that he expects his rent to increase, as the current $2,000 rate is below market. (SCHEDULES-SUMMARY.md §§ 4–5.) He cannot cover his basic living expenses from his only income source, which is the discretionary goodwill of his brother. The attorney's retainer — $5,000 — was paid by that brother. (Id. § 1, Attorney Retainer.)

The means test result is unambiguous. Form 122A-1 shows current monthly income of $0.00 for CMI purposes (the brother's support payments are not counted under § 101(10A)). Annualized, that produces zero income against the New Jersey median household income for a single-person household of $87,173. The presumption of abuse checkbox — box 14a — is checked, confirming that no presumption arises. Form 122A-2 was not filed because it was not required. (SCHEDULES-SUMMARY.md § 1, Means Test Result.) Congress designed the means test precisely as a mechanical filter to identify strategic Chapter 7 filers whose income supports meaningful repayment. Brad failed to trigger that filter in either direction — he does not have the income to make it a close question.

The Statement of Financial Affairs confirms the clean SOFA record. No income from employment or business in the current year or either of the prior two years (SOFA Lines 4–5). No payments to any creditor exceeding the applicable threshold within 90 days (SOFA Line 6). No payments to insiders within one year (SOFA Line 7). No gifts over $600 within two years (SOFA Lines 13–14). No property transfers within two years (SOFA Line 18). No self-settled trust transfers within ten years (SOFA Line 19). No closed financial accounts within one year (SOFA Line 20). No safe deposit boxes. No offshore accounts. No gambling losses. (SCHEDULES-SUMMARY.md § 5.) The SOFA also discloses Brad's pending lawsuit against Guardian Insurance for reinstatement of a long-term disability insurance policy (Case No. 3:25-cv-01515-ZNQ-RLS, U.S. Dist. Ct. DNJ), which is both a disclosed asset and evidence of continuing financial hardship that is independent of and predates the bankruptcy filing.

The three prior addresses disclosed on the SOFA — FCI Fort Dix, Kintock Newark Halfway House (50 Fenwick Street, November 13, 2024 to December 13, 2024), and P.O. Box 835, Far Hills — are precisely the addresses one would expect from a recently released federal prisoner. They are not suspicious. They are the transparent disclosure of a person who spent the better part of seven years incarcerated and is now living at his brother's address. The filing of the petition the Friday before trial, under these circumstances, reflects not the calculated strike of a wealthy debtor using bankruptcy as a shield, but the act of a person with a monthly deficit of $1,052, twelve creditors, $300,000 in IRS debt, $200,000 in domestic support obligations, and a potentially unliquidated damages claim of unknown magnitude — who reached the conclusion, on the Thursday before his damages trial, that Chapter 7 was his only available path.

### F. Nagel's Motion and the Eve-of-Trial Filing

The filing of the Chapter 7 petition on June 12, 2026, was immediately followed by the automatic stay arising under 11 U.S.C. § 362(a). Brad's bankruptcy counsel filed notice of the automatic stay in the state court proceedings the same afternoon. (Bankruptcy filing.pdf, filed ESX-L-007200-19 at 3:28:56 PM on June 12, 2026.) On June 15, 2026 — the trial date on which B.B. v. Mell would have commenced — the Bankruptcy Court, Judge O'Hagan presiding, entered an Order to Show Cause why the case should not be dismissed for failure to file required documents (Doc. 4). That OSC was the vehicle by which the Court put Brad on notice to complete his schedules; he complied fully on June 25, 2026. (SCHEDULES-SUMMARY.md § 1; US Bankruptcy Court NJ - Live Database.pdf, Docket Entry 4.)

On June 23, 2026 — eleven days after the petition was filed — Bruce Nagel, for B.B., filed the pending Motion to Dismiss, consisting of the Notice of Motion (Doc. 8), a twenty-three page Brief (Doc. 8-1), and the Certification of Bruce H. Nagel with 115 pages of exhibits (Doc. 8-2). (Nagel-mtd-analysis.md, Document Structure.) The motion was accompanied by a Notice of Appearance (Doc. 9), through which Nagel formally appeared as counsel in this Court. The hearing is scheduled for July 16, 2026 at 10:00 AM in Courtroom 2, Trenton, before Judge O'Hagan. (US Bankruptcy Court NJ - Live Database.pdf.)

Nagel's decision to file the motion when he did requires a word of context. Nagel Rice LLP appears on Brad's Schedule E/F as a creditor — Nagel's own firm holds a $10,000 sanctions claim arising from the same state court litigation in which Nagel is counsel for B.B. (Schedule E/F 4.8.) He is thus simultaneously: (a) counsel for the largest potential creditor of the estate; (b) a named creditor of the estate in his own firm's right; and (c) a party who negotiated, on behalf of his client, the March 2026 settlement that released the trust assets he now attacks. The opposition to this motion must handle the layered nature of that position with care and appropriate precision. The candor and conflict arguments are powerful, but they are best deployed strategically rather than as an opening salvo.

The motion's factual narrative rests on three propositions: that the timing of Brad's filing is inherently suspicious, that the trusts are fraudulent transfer vehicles, and that Brad's schedules are deficient. (Nagel-mtd-analysis.md §§ 4–5, 8.) All three propositions are answerable on the record as it exists. As of June 23, 2026, when Nagel filed the motion, Brad had not yet filed his complete schedules — he filed them on June 25. Nagel's statement at Doc. 8-1, page [reference pending CM/ECF verification], that "as of this date, Mell has not corrected these deficiencies," was accurate when written, rendered moot two days later, and should be noted as such in the opposition without undue emphasis. The MTD also cites as the statutory basis for mandatory abstention "28 U.S.C. § 1134(c)(2)" — a provision that does not exist in Title 28. (Nagel-mtd-analysis.md § 6.) The correct citation is 28 U.S.C. § 1334(c)(2). This error recurs identically in the permissive abstention section. It is a marker, not a dispositive defect — but it signals the character of the submission to a judge who has spent eleven years as USAO bankruptcy counsel and will recognize the error on first read.

The motion's lead bad-faith theory — that Brad filed the petition as a tactical maneuver to avoid a damages trial — does not engage with the actual record. Brad is insolvent by any honest measure. He has a monthly deficit. He has a $300,000 federal tax debt. He has a $200,000 domestic support obligation. He faces a damages trial in which a jury could award a judgment that would dwarf the $750,000 already escrowed, with no mechanism to satisfy such a judgment other than the retirement accounts that are fully exempt. That is not the calculation of a tactical filer. It is the calculation of a person who has run out of options and whose only legitimate path to a financial fresh start runs through the federal bankruptcy court.
