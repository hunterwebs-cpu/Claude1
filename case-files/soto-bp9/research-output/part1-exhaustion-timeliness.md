## PART A — Regulatory Text (28 C.F.R. §§ 542.10–542.19)

Retrieved verbatim from the eCFR Versioner API (title 28, part 542, current through 2026-07-13), which mirrors the govinfo.gov Code of Federal Regulations. This is the actual regulatory text currently in effect — not a paraphrase.

### § 542.10 — Purpose and scope
> (a) *Purpose.* The purpose of the Administrative Remedy Program is to allow an inmate to seek formal review of an issue relating to any aspect of his/her own confinement. An inmate may not submit a Request or Appeal on behalf of another inmate.
> (b) *Scope.* This Program applies to all inmates in institutions operated by the Bureau of Prisons... [67 FR 50805, Aug. 6, 2002]

### § 542.11 — Responsibility
Requires the Warden (among others) to "[e]stablish procedures for receiving, recording, reviewing, investigating, and responding to" Requests, and to "[a]cknowledge receipt of a Request or Appeal by returning a receipt to the inmate." Subsection (b): "Inmates have the responsibility to use this Program in good faith and in an honest and straightforward manner."

### § 542.13 — Informal resolution
> (a) *Informal resolution.* Except as provided in § 542.13(b), an inmate shall first present an issue of concern informally to staff, and staff shall attempt to informally resolve the issue before an inmate submits a Request for Administrative Remedy. Each Warden shall establish procedures to allow for the informal resolution of inmate complaints.
> (b) *Exceptions.* ... An informal resolution attempt may be waived in individual cases at the Warden or institution Administrative Remedy Coordinator's discretion when the inmate demonstrates an acceptable reason for bypassing informal resolution.

### § 542.14 — Initial filing (the core timeliness provision)
> (a) *Submission.* **The deadline for completion of informal resolution and submission of a formal written Administrative Remedy Request, on the appropriate form (BP-9), is 20 calendar days following the date on which the basis for the Request occurred.**
>
> (b) *Extension.* Where the inmate demonstrates a valid reason for delay, an extension in filing time may be allowed. In general, valid reason for delay means a situation which prevented the inmate from submitting the request within the established time frame. Valid reasons for delay include the following: an extended period in-transit during which the inmate was separated from documents needed to prepare the Request or Appeal; an extended period of time during which the inmate was physically incapable of preparing a Request or Appeal; an unusually long period taken for informal resolution attempts; indication by an inmate, verified by staff, that a response to the inmate's request for copies of dispositions requested under § 542.19 of this part was delayed.

**Key textual point for Soto:** The regulation does *not* say "20 days after the underlying event" categorically — it says 20 days after "the basis for the Request occurred." The "basis for the Request" is the thing being grieved. Soto's BP-9/BP-10 is not grieving Dennis's March 17 conduct (presenting a form with a false "homeless" designation, which Soto immediately corrected and which caused no adverse action by itself); it is grieving the July 3, 2026 decision — communicated to Soto for the first time that day — that he is "no longer being considered" for RRC placement based on a retroactive recharacterization of the March 17 signature refusal as a "categorical refusal." That adverse decision, and Soto's injury (loss of RRC consideration), did not exist until July 3. The "basis for the Request" therefore occurred on July 3, not March 17.

### § 542.15 — Appeals
BP-10 to Regional Director: 20 calendar days from the date the Warden signed the response. BP-11 to General Counsel: 30 calendar days from the date the Regional Director signed the response. "When the inmate demonstrates a valid reason for delay, these time limits may be extended. Valid reasons for delay include those situations described in § 542.14(b) of this part."

### § 542.16 — Assistance (the staff-caused-delay provision)
> (a) An inmate may obtain assistance from another inmate or from institution staff in preparing a Request or an Appeal. An inmate may also obtain assistance from outside sources, such as family members or attorneys. However, no person may submit a Request or Appeal on the inmate's behalf, and **obtaining assistance will not be considered a valid reason for exceeding a time limit for submission unless the delay was caused by staff.**

**This is the single most useful regulatory sentence for Soto.** It is an express, textual acknowledgment by the BOP itself that staff-caused delay excuses an inmate's noncompliance with the § 542.14(a) filing clock. Dennis is BOP staff. If the Warden or Regional Director takes the position that the clock ran from March 17, the answer is that any resulting "lateness" was caused by staff — specifically, by Dennis's failure to disclose until July 3 that he was treating the March 17 event as a categorical refusal. An inmate cannot grieve an adverse decision he has not been told about. Section 542.16(a) confirms BOP's own regulations treat staff-caused delay as categorically different from ordinary inmate delay.

### § 542.17 — Resubmission
Rejected Requests must be returned with written notice of the defect; if the defect is correctable, the inmate is entitled to "a reasonable time extension within which to correct the defect and resubmit."

### § 542.18 — Response time
> If accepted, a Request or Appeal is considered filed on the date it is logged into the Administrative Remedy Index as received. Once filed, response shall be made by the Warden or CCM within 20 calendar days; by the Regional Director within 30 calendar days; and by the General Counsel within 40 calendar days. If the Request is determined to be of an emergency nature which threatens the inmate's immediate health or welfare, the Warden shall respond not later than the third calendar day after filing. If the time period for response to a Request or Appeal is insufficient to make an appropriate decision, the time for response may be extended once by 20 days at the institution level, 30 days at the regional level, or 20 days at the Central Office level. Staff shall inform the inmate of this extension in writing. Staff shall respond in writing to all filed Requests or Appeals. **If the inmate does not receive a response within the time allotted for reply, including extension, the inmate may consider the absence of a response to be a denial at that level.**

Note: § 542.18's numeric response-time limits (20/30/40 days) apply to *formal* Requests and Appeals (BP-9/10/11) logged into SENTRY — they do not, on their face, govern the informal BP-8 stage, which is instead governed by whatever procedure the Warden establishes under § 542.13(a) and any local Institution Supplement. That is exactly why Fort Dix's own Institution Supplement (Warden Thompson) imposing a 3-working-day BP-8 response deadline is significant — see Part C below.

### § 542.19 — Access to indexes and responses
Governs public/inmate access to the Administrative Remedy index; not directly relevant to timeliness.

**Full text confirmed by direct API pull from eCFR (govinfo-sourced), current through 2026-07-13.** [67 FR 50805, Aug. 6, 2002]; [61 FR 88, Jan. 2, 1996, as amended at 75 FR 34626, June 18, 2010] (§ 542.14); [61 FR 88, Jan. 2, 1996, as amended at 70 FR 67091, Nov. 4, 2005] (§ 542.15).

## PART B — Program Statement 1330.18 (Administrative Remedy Program)

Retrieved directly from bop.gov/policy/progstat/1330_018.pdf (approved by Director Charles E. Samuels, Jr., January 6, 2014 — this remains the operative PS; BOP has not superseded it as of this research date) and text-extracted for verification against the CFR. **The PS restates the CFR text essentially verbatim** in its numbered sections, with a few additions of practical significance:

1. **Section 7 (Informal Resolution, § 542.13):** Adds that "[t]he Warden is responsible for ensuring that effective informal resolution procedures are in place and that good faith attempts at informal resolution are made in an orderly and timely manner **by both inmates and staff**." (Emphasis added — the obligation of timeliness and good faith runs both ways.)

2. **Section 8 (Initial Filing, § 542.14):** Repeats the 20-calendar-day/"basis for the Request occurred" language verbatim, and separately notes: "**Ordinarily, the inmate should submit written verification from staff for any claimed reason for delay.**" This is a practical instruction Soto's BP-9 packet should follow — attach or reference Dennis's July 3 team-meeting statement (and any written record of it, e.g., the July 15 BP-8 response itself, which is staff's own written admission that the "incident" wasn't disclosed to Soto until July 3) as the "written verification from staff" of the reason for any delay.

3. **Section 10 (Assistance, § 542.16):** Repeats verbatim: "obtaining assistance will not be considered a valid reason for exceeding a time limit for submission **unless the delay was caused by staff**."

4. **Section 12 (Response Time, § 542.18):** Repeats verbatim the 20/30/40-day response windows and the "absence of a response ... may be considered ... a denial" language, and adds that "[t]he date a Request or an Appeal is received in the Administrative Remedy index is entered into SENTRY as the Date Rcv." Confirms this section governs formal Requests/Appeals logged in SENTRY, not the informal BP-8 stage.

5. **Section 17 (Institution Supplement):** "**Each Warden shall forward a copy of any Institution Supplement developed to implement this Program Statement** to the Regional Administrative Remedy Coordinator and to the National Inmate Appeals Administrator in the Central Office." This is the express PS authorization for exactly the kind of local supplement Warden Thompson issued at Fort Dix (the 3-working-day BP-8 response requirement). It confirms the Fort Dix supplement is a valid, BOP-sanctioned implementing document — meaning Dennis's 9-day response is measured against a real, binding local deadline, not an informal practice Soto invented.

## PART C — Case Law

### C.1 — Accrual: does the clock run from the underlying event or from when the inmate is told of the adverse consequence?

No Third Circuit or D.N.J. case squarely construes "the date on which the basis for the Request occurred" in § 542.14(a) as incorporating a discovery rule in a fact pattern like Soto's (retroactive recharacterization of an earlier act as something else, disclosed months later). This is a genuine gap in direct, on-point authority — be candid about that in the BP-9 narrative rather than overclaiming a holding that doesn't exist. The strongest available authority is (1) the regulatory text itself (Part A above — "basis for the Request" naturally reads as the operative decision, not an antecedent fact that caused no injury until acted upon); (2) the staff-caused-delay language of § 542.16(a) and PS § 10 (Part A/B); and (3) the "no need to wait indefinitely on an unresponsive process" and "continuing course of conduct" lines of authority below, which supply the closest analogues.

### C.4 — BOP RRC/halfway-house placement denials and PLRA/administrative exhaustion specifically

This was the weakest area of the research. I did not locate a published, citable Third Circuit or D.N.J. decision squarely resolving an exhaustion-timeliness dispute in the specific context of an RRC/halfway-house placement denial recharacterized after the fact as a "refusal." Be direct about this gap rather than manufacturing a citation.

- **Woodall v. Federal Bureau of Prisons**, 432 F.3d 235 (3d Cir. 2005) (originating in D.N.J., Judge Freda L. Wolfson) — establishes that a challenge to BOP's exercise of discretion over CCC/RRC ("halfway house") placement under 18 U.S.C. § 3621(b) is properly brought via 28 U.S.C. § 2241 habeas corpus because it goes to the execution of the sentence. Not an exhaustion-timeliness case, but useful background: it confirms the Third Circuit/D.N.J. treats RRC placement disputes as cognizable and cognizable via habeas — meaning if the BP-9/BP-10/BP-11 process is exhausted (or its exhaustion is excused as unavailable under Ross/Robinson/Rinaldi), Soto has a clear procedural path to court. **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — confirm current validity; the 2005 regulation Woodall addressed was later mooted by the Second Chance Act of 2007, so cite Woodall only for the § 2241 vehicle point, not for any substantive RRC placement standard, which has since been superseded by statute.]**
- General practitioner sources (not binding, background only) confirm that § 2241 petitions challenging RRC placement denials are routinely dismissed by district courts for failure to exhaust the BP-8 through BP-11 process where the inmate has not completed it and no unavailability exception applies — reinforcing that getting the exhaustion/timeliness question right at the BP-9 stage (rather than skipping to habeas) is the right strategic move here.

## PART D — Synthesis and Application to Soto's BP-9

1. **Lead with the text, not just case law.** Section 542.14(a)'s "basis for the Request occurred" language, read together with § 542.16(a)'s staff-caused-delay carve-out (both confirmed verbatim above, and both mirrored verbatim in PS 1330.18 §§ 8 and 10), is Soto's strongest and most defensible argument: the clock could not start on an adverse action Soto was not told about. Dennis's July 15 response — which for the first time asserts March 17 as "the incident date" — is itself the staff conduct causing any apparent delay, squarely within § 542.16(a)'s "unless the delay was caused by staff" language.

2. **Back it with Brown v. Croak / Small v. Camden County** (binding Third Circuit) for the proposition that a remedy not reasonably communicated to the inmate is unavailable, and with **Ross v. Blake / Rinaldi / Robinson / Shifflett** (binding Supreme Court/Third Circuit) for the broader "unavailability" and "agency's-own-delay" doctrines — particularly Robinson's holding that failure to timely comply with the institution's own procedural rules renders the remedy unavailable, which maps directly onto the Fort Dix Institution Supplement's missed 3-working-day BP-8 deadline (actual: 9 days).

3. **Offer the continuing-violation theory as a fallback**, candidly flagged as persuasive-only (Sheltra, Morgan; no Third Circuit case found either way): March 17 and July 3 are two points in a single, continuing course of conduct by Dennis regarding Soto's RRC referral, such that the July 3 disclosure — not the March 17 form — is the operative "basis for the Request."

4. **Do not overstate C.1 (discovery-rule) authority.** No case was found directly holding that § 542.14(a)'s clock runs from the date of discovery rather than the date of the underlying event. Make this argument from the regulatory text and from § 542.16(a)/Brown/Small, not from a fabricated discovery-rule case.

5. **Every citation flagged [VERIFY BEFORE FILING] above must be checked against a primary source (Westlaw/Lexis if available, or a clean PDF/HTML of the slip opinion) before it is quoted verbatim in a BP-9, BP-10, or any court filing.** Several of the quotes above were reconstructed through search-engine snippets and a Third-Circuit-website/govinfo.gov PDF whose embedded fonts caused partial text corruption on extraction; while cross-checked for internal consistency and, where possible, verified against two independent sources, none of this is a substitute for reading the actual reporter text before it goes into a filing.
