## 1. PS 1330.18 — Administrative Remedy Program (effective January 6, 2014)

Source: https://www.bop.gov/policy/progstat/1330_018.pdf (full text extracted and reviewed).
This Program Statement implements 28 C.F.R. §§ 542.10–542.19. It is the controlling national
policy for the BP-8/BP-9/BP-10/BP-11 process referenced in Soto's case.

### 1.1 Informal Resolution (§542.13 / PS Section 7)

> **7. INFORMAL RESOLUTION §542.13**
>
> a. Informal Resolution. Except as provided in §542.13(b), an inmate shall first present an
> issue of concern informally to staff, and staff shall attempt to informally resolve the issue
> before an inmate submits a Request for Administrative Remedy. **Each warden shall establish
> procedures to allow for the informal resolution of inmate complaints.**
>
> The Warden is responsible for ensuring that effective informal resolution procedures are in
> place and that good faith attempts at informal resolution are made in an orderly and timely
> manner by both inmates and staff. These procedures may not operate to limit inmate access to
> formal filing of a Request.
>
> b. Exceptions. Inmates in CCCs are not required to attempt informal resolution... An informal
> resolution attempt may be waived in individual cases at the Warden or institution
> Administrative Remedy Coordinator's discretion when the inmate demonstrates an acceptable
> reason for bypassing informal resolution.

**Key finding: the national Program Statement itself sets NO fixed deadline for staff to respond
to a BP-8 / informal resolution request.** It only requires that resolution attempts occur "in
an orderly and timely manner" and expressly delegates the job of setting concrete informal-
resolution procedures — which would include any specific response-time deadline such as Fort
Dix's alleged "3 working days" — to the **Warden**, i.e., to the institution supplement. This is
confirmed by Section 17 (below) and by the fact that neither PS 1330.18 nor 28 C.F.R. § 542.13
specifies a number of days for the counselor's/unit team's BP-8 response. This directly answers
research question 1's sub-question: **the 3-working-day deadline Soto describes is not found in
the national PS — it must come from (and would only be enforceable via) the Fort Dix
institution supplement**, which is exactly the source the facts describe (Warden Thompson's
supplement).

The generic BOP institution Admission & Orientation Handbook language (see Section 4 below) is
consistent with this: it tells inmates only that the counselor "will investigate the complaint
and prepare a response as soon as possible" — no specific day-count is given in that generic
document either, reinforcing that any specific numeric deadline is a Fort Dix-specific
supplement provision, not baseline BOP-wide policy.

### 1.2 Initial Filing / 20-Day Deadline (§542.14 / PS Section 8)

> **8. INITIAL FILING §542.14**
>
> a. Submission. **The deadline for completion of informal resolution and submission of a
> formal written Administrative Remedy Request, on the appropriate form (BP-9), is 20 calendar
> days following the date on which the basis for the Request occurred.**

This is the critical clause for the timeliness dispute: the 20-day clock is explicitly a single,
combined clock that covers *both* completing informal resolution *and* filing the BP-9 — it runs
from "the date on which the basis for the Request occurred," not from the date informal
resolution concluded and not from the date the BP-8 was answered. This matters for framing
Soto's argument: if the case manager is treating March 17, 2026 (the paperwork-refusal incident)
as the date "the basis for the Request occurred," Soto's counter-argument should be that the
actual "basis" of *this* BP-9 is not the March 17 incident itself, but the case manager's
subsequent adverse determination — i.e., the decision to (a) deem the BP-8 untimely and (b)
permanently remove him from RRC consideration — which is a distinct, later-occurring event/basis
than March 17. **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING: confirm the exact date Soto was told he was "no longer
being considered at all" for RRC — that date, not March 17, is likely the correct triggering
date for the 20-day clock on the substantive RRC-denial claim.]**

### 1.3 Extension for Valid Reason for Delay (§542.14(b) / PS Section 8.b)

> b. Extension. **Where the inmate demonstrates a valid reason for delay, an extension in filing
> time may be allowed.** In general, valid reason for delay means a situation which prevented the
> inmate from submitting the request within the established time frame. Valid reasons for delay
> include the following: an extended period in-transit during which the inmate was separated
> from documents needed to prepare the Request or Appeal; an extended period of time during
> which the inmate was physically incapable of preparing a Request or Appeal; **an unusually long
> period taken for informal resolution attempts**; indication by an inmate, verified by staff,
> that a response to the inmate's request for copies of dispositions requested under §542.19 of
> this part was delayed.
>
> Ordinarily, the inmate should submit written verification from staff for any claimed reason
> for delay.

This is directly useful: **"an unusually long period taken for informal resolution attempts" is
an enumerated valid reason for delay** under the national policy. Fort Dix's own informal-
resolution response (9 calendar days against an alleged 3-working-day institutional standard) is
independent evidence that could support a "valid reason for delay" argument if Soto's BP-9 filing
is treated as late for any reason connected to that delay. This is a fallback argument distinct
from the primary "the clock never should have started on March 17" argument in §1.2 above.

Note also PS 1330.18 Section 8.b's separate paragraph on requests submitted without completed
informal resolution:

> If an inmate requests an Administrative Remedy form but has not attempted informal
> resolution, staff should counsel the inmate that informal resolution is ordinarily required.
> If the inmate nevertheless refuses to present a request informally, staff should provide the
> form for a formal Request. **Upon receipt of the inmate's submission, the Coordinator shall
> accept the Request if, in the Coordinator's discretion, informal resolution was bypassed for
> valid reasons, or may reject it if there are no valid reasons for bypassing informal
> resolution.**

This provision is about *bypassing* informal resolution altogether, not about lateness once
informal resolution was in fact pursued — but it is worth citing to show that even the threshold
question of BP-8 completion is committed to Coordinator discretion assessing "valid reasons,"
not to a rigid administrative rule allowed to override the inmate's diligence.

### 1.4 Warden's Response Time to a BP-9, and Appeal Deadlines (§542.15, §542.18 / PS Sections 9, 12)

Answering research question 5 — the national response-time deadline for the Warden's response to
an accepted BP-9 (not the informal BP-8):

> **12. RESPONSE TIME §542.18**
>
> If accepted, a Request or Appeal is considered filed on the date it is logged into the
> Administrative Remedy Index as received. **Once filed, response shall be made by the Warden or
> CCM within 20 calendar days**; by the Regional Director within 30 calendar days; and by the
> General Counsel within 40 calendar days. If the Request is determined to be of an emergency
> nature which threatens the inmate's immediate health or welfare, the Warden shall respond not
> later than the third calendar day after filing. **If the time period for response to a Request
> or Appeal is insufficient to make an appropriate decision, the time for response may be
> extended once by 20 days at the institution level**, 30 days at the regional level, or 20 days
> at the Central Office level. Staff shall inform the inmate of this extension in writing. Staff
> shall respond in writing to all filed Requests or Appeals. **If the inmate does not receive a
> response within the time allotted for reply, including extension, the inmate may consider the
> absence of a response to be a denial at that level.**

So: once Soto's BP-9 is accepted/logged, the Warden has 20 calendar days to respond (extendable
once by 20 more days with written notice to Soto), and if no timely response issues, Soto may
treat the silence as a denial and proceed to the BP-10 (Regional Director) appeal. This tracks
28 C.F.R. § 542.18 exactly (confirmed against Cornell LII text of § 542.18, which is verbatim
identical to the language above).

Appeal deadlines (§542.15 / PS Section 9):

> a. Submission. An inmate who is not satisfied with the Warden's response may submit an Appeal
> on the appropriate form (BP-10) to the appropriate Regional Director **within 20 calendar days
> of the date the Warden signed the response.** An inmate who is not satisfied with the Regional
> Director's response may submit an Appeal on the appropriate form (BP-11) to the General
> Counsel **within 30 calendar days of the date the Regional Director signed the response.** When
> the inmate demonstrates a valid reason for delay, these time limits may be extended. Valid
> reasons for delay include those situations described in §542.14(b) of this part. Appeal to the
> General Counsel is the final administrative appeal.

### 1.5 Rejections and Untimeliness (§542.17 / PS Section 11)

> a. Rejections. The Coordinator at any level (CCM, institution, region, Central Office) may
> reject and return to the inmate without response a Request or an Appeal that is written by an
> inmate in a manner that is obscene or abusive, or **does not meet any other requirement of this
> part.**
>
> b. Notice. When a submission is rejected, the inmate shall be provided a written notice, signed
> by the Administrative Remedy Coordinator, explaining the reason for rejection. **If the defect
> on which the rejection is based is correctable, the notice shall inform the inmate of a
> reasonable time extension within which to correct the defect and resubmit** the Request or
> Appeal.
>
> ... c. Appeal of Rejections. When a Request or Appeal is rejected and the inmate is not given
> an opportunity to correct the defect and resubmit, **the inmate may appeal the rejection**,
> including a rejection on the basis of an exception as described in §542.14(d), to the next
> appeal level.

Two points worth flagging for the drafting attorney/pro se filer:
- Untimeliness determinations are formally "rejections," which under Section 11(b) must be
  written, signed by the Administrative Remedy Coordinator, and must explain the reason — this
  is a due-process/procedural checklist against which Soto's case manager's informal "untimely"
  determination (apparently made at the BP-8/counselor level, not by the institution's
  Administrative Remedy Coordinator) should be measured. **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING: confirm who
  actually made the "untimely" determination — a case manager is not the same official as the
  institution's Administrative Remedy Coordinator under §542.11/PS Section 5, and only the
  Coordinator has rejection/rejection-notice authority under Section 11.]**
- Rejections for correctable defects, which would seem to include a timeliness dispute, entitle
  the inmate to a "reasonable time extension" to correct/resubmit and to appeal the rejection
  itself if he disagrees.

> **17. INSTITUTION SUPPLEMENT**
>
> Each Warden shall forward a copy of any Institution Supplement developed to implement this
> Program Statement to the Regional Administrative Remedy Coordinator and to the National Inmate

This is the only place PS 1330.18 mentions institution supplements, and it confirms two things

Source located: https://www.bop.gov/policy/progstat/5100_008_cn-3.pdf (most recent Change

What is confirmed:
- PS 5100.08 governs security level designation (minimum/low/medium/high) and **custody
  classification** (Community custody / In custody / Maximum custody), not RRC placement

- "COMMUNITY" custody is the lowest custody classification and is a *precondition* for RRC

**Conclusion on relevance: PS 5100.08 is tangential, as anticipated in the research request.**
Its only likely relevance to Soto's BP-9 would be if there is a dispute about whether Soto
currently holds (or was ever assigned) "Community" custody status, since that classification is
a listed prerequisite for CCC/RRC transfer under PS 7310.04. If Soto's community-custody status
was itself downgraded or never assigned because of the March 17 incident, that would connect
PS 5100.08 to the case, but nothing in the facts as described indicates a custody-classification
change — the case manager's action was to stop RRC *processing*, not to reclassify custody. I

## 3. PS 7310.04 — Community Corrections Center (CCC) Utilization and Transfer Procedures

Source: https://www.bop.gov/policy/progstat/7310_004.pdf (full text extracted and reviewed).

### 3.1 Statutory Basis (§3621(b) / §3624(c)) — PS Section 5

> 18 U.S.C. § 3621(b) provides: "The Bureau of Prisons shall designate the place of the
> prisoner's imprisonment. The Bureau may designate any available penal or correctional facility
> . . . the Bureau determines to be appropriate and suitable." A CCC meets the definition of a
> "penal or correctional facility." ... Therefore, the Bureau is not restricted by § 3624(c) in
> designating a CCC for an inmate and may place an inmate in a CCC for more than the "last ten
> per centum of the term," or more than six months, if appropriate.

Current (post-Second Chance Act) § 3621(b) requires the Bureau to make placement determinations
based on five statutory factors: (1) the resources of the facility; (2) the nature and
circumstances of the offense; (3) the history and characteristics of the prisoner; (4) any
sentencing-court statement about facility type; and (5) any pertinent Sentencing Commission
policy statement. **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — confirm exact current statutory text of §§ 3621(b)
and 3624(c) directly from the U.S. Code before citing, since PS 7310.04's own text is outdated.]**

### 3.2 Referral Procedure and Who Has Authority (PS Sections 9, 12)

> 12. CCC REFERRAL PROCEDURES. Normally 11 to 13 months before each inmate's probable release
> date, the unit team shall decide whether to refer an inmate to a Community Corrections
> program.
>
> a. Referral to CCM. Staff shall use the Institution Referral form (BP-210)... **The Warden is
> the final decision-making authority for all CCC referrals the unit team recommends.**
>
> If the Warden approves the CCC referral, the unit team shall forward two copies of the
> Institutional Referral form and appropriate attachments to the CCM.

This is directly relevant to research question 3's "who has authority" prong: **the unit team
(which includes the case manager) only *recommends*; final decision-making authority for CCC/RRC
referrals rests with the Warden**, not with an individual case manager. A case manager's
unilateral statement that Soto "is no longer being considered at all" for RRC placement — with no
indication of Warden sign-off, no BP-210 referral decision, and apparently no §11 refusal
memorandum (see below) — appears to exceed the case manager's documented authority under this
Program Statement.

### 3.3 Refusals (PS Section 11) — the provision most directly on point

> **11. REFUSALS. When an eligible inmate refuses CCC placement, staff shall investigate the
> inmate's reasons. Staff may honor an inmate's refusal of CCC placement.**
>
> Suitable reasons to decline placement might include previous CCC failure, potential conflict
> with other residents, and location or remoteness from release residence. When the inmate does
> not present a suitable reason, and the unit team believes that a placement would serve a
> correctional need, the unit team shall make every effort to encourage participation.
>
> **When an inmate refuses placement, a memorandum, signed by the Associate Warden (Programs) and
> the inmate, shall be placed in the Inmate Central File. The memorandum should document the
> inmate's rationale for refusal and all unit team effort to encourage participation.**

This is the single most important provision found for question 3. It establishes that a genuine
"refusal" of CCC/RRC placement is supposed to be:
1. **Investigated** by staff (i.e., staff must look into *why* the inmate refused, not simply
   record that paperwork was not signed);
2. **Documented in a memorandum signed by both the Associate Warden (Programs) and the inmate
   himself** — not by the case manager alone, and not without the inmate's own signature
   memorializing his stated rationale.

On the facts described, Soto did not refuse RRC placement as such — he refused to sign a specific
document because it contained a factual error (falsely listing him as "homeless" when he had
provided his actual home address) and asked for a correction. That is materially different from
declining CCC/RRC programming, and the case manager's recharacterization of that dispute as a
"refusal" triggering permanent removal from consideration does not appear to satisfy — or even
attempt — the investigation-and-dual-signature memorandum procedure this section requires. Absent
such a memorandum (in particular, the Associate Warden's signature and Soto's own signature/
rationale), the "refusal" appears procedurally irregular under the Bureau's own written
requirements. **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING: request/confirm whether any Section 11 refusal memorandum
was ever prepared, signed by the AW(P) and Soto, and placed in his Central File — if none exists,

### 3.4 Limitations on Eligibility (PS Section 10) — the exhaustive exclusion list

> 10. LIMITATIONS ON ELIGIBILITY FOR ALL CCC REFERRALS. Inmates in the following categories shall
> not ordinarily participate in CCC programs:
> a. Inmates who are assigned a "Sex Offender" Public Safety Factor.
> b. Inmates who are assigned a "Deportable Alien" Public Safety Factor.
> c. Inmates who require inpatient medical, psychological, or psychiatric treatment.
> d. Inmates who refuse to participate in the Inmate Financial Responsibility Program.
> e. Inmates who refuse to participate, withdraw, are expelled, or otherwise fail to meet
> attendance and examination requirements in a required Drug Abuse Education Course.
> f. Inmates with unresolved pending charges, or detainers, which will likely lead to arrest,
> conviction, or confinement.
> g. Ordinarily, inmates serving sentences of six months or less.
> h. Inmates who refuse to participate in the Institution Release Preparation Program.
> i. Inmates who pose a significant threat to the community. ...
> j. Inmates whose admission and release status is pretrial, holdover, or detainee.

And, specifically on the significant-threat exclusion:

> (2) When an inmate is excluded under this subsection, **a memorandum, signed by the Warden,
> shall be prepared and placed in the Inmate Central File to explain the rationale for exclusion**
> from CC Programs.

This closed, enumerated list of exclusion categories does **not** include "declined to sign a
document containing a factual error" or anything resembling Soto's situation. Nothing in Section
10 authorizes exclusion for that reason, and the one exclusion category that comes closest in
spirit — "significant threat to the community" — itself requires a **Warden-signed** memorandum
of rationale, not a case-manager-level, undocumented decision.

**Direct answer to the question posed: no provision in PS 7310.04 authorizes a unit team member
or case manager, acting alone, to unilaterally and permanently remove an inmate from all future
RRC consideration based on a single incident unrelated to the § 3621(b) placement factors.** Every
mechanism the Program Statement provides for excluding or refusing an inmate — Section 10's
enumerated categories, Section 10(i)(2)'s Warden-signed exclusion memorandum for the "threat"
category, and Section 11's dual-signed (AW(P) + inmate) refusal memorandum — requires
investigation, documentation, and sign-off above the case-manager level. A single case manager
declaring an inmate "no longer being considered at all" because he declined to sign an
inaccurate form does not fit any of these mechanisms as written.

## 4. Institutional Supplements — Legal Status, Force, and Consequences of Staff Noncompliance

### 4.1 What an institutional supplement is, and its formal basis

As shown in Section 1.6 above, PS 1330.18 Section 17 expressly contemplates and requires
institution supplements implementing the Administrative Remedy Program, filed by the Warden with
the Regional Administrative Remedy Coordinator and National Inmate Appeals Administrator. This
is the general model across BOP program statements: national Program Statements set the floor/
framework, and Wardens issue "Institution Supplements" to fill in facility-specific operational
detail (response deadlines, form-collection points, etc.). Institution supplements are Warden-
issued directives binding on staff at that institution as a matter of internal agency policy and
chain-of-command accountability, even though — like Program Statements themselves — they are not
notice-and-comment regulations.

### 4.2 Do institution supplements/Program Statements have independent "force of law"?

The controlling case on the legal character of BOP Program Statements is **Reno v. Koray, 515
U.S. 50 (1995)**. The Supreme Court there described a BOP Program Statement as "an internal
agency guideline" rather than a "published regulation[] subject to the rigors of the
Administrative Procedure Act," and treated it as akin to an interpretive rule — entitled to
some deference as a permissible construction of the governing statute where reasonable, but not
possessing the independent force of a legislative regulation. **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING: pull the
exact quoted language and pin cite from 515 U.S. 50, 61 (1995) directly from a primary source —
I have only a secondary paraphrase confirmed by multiple summaries, not the Court's exact words,
in hand.]** Institution supplements sit one level further down the hierarchy than Program
Statements (Warden-level implementing guidance rather than Bureau-wide policy), and by the same
logic would not independently create judicially enforceable rights for an inmate against the
agency under the APA.

**However — and this is the point relevant to Soto's filing — that Reno v. Koray framework
governs whether a Program Statement or supplement can be enforced *against* the agency as if it
were a binding regulation in a habeas/APA-type challenge to a substantive BOP determination. It
does not answer, and is not the same question as, whether:**
(a) staff's own **failure to follow their own binding internal policy** is relevant evidence in
    an administrative-exhaustion or fairness dispute, or
(b) an inmate can point to the agency's own noncompliance with its own procedural rules as part
    of showing that the administrative process was **effectively "unavailable"** to him within
    the meaning of the Prison Litigation Reform Act (PLRA).

### 4.3 Ross v. Blake and "availability" of administrative remedies

**Ross v. Blake, 578 U.S. 632 (2016)**, is the controlling Supreme Court authority on when a
prisoner is excused from further administrative exhaustion because the process was not
"available" to him. The Court identified three circumstances rendering a grievance process
unavailable:
1. the process "operates as a simple dead end" — officers unable or consistently unwilling to
   provide relief;
2. the process is "so opaque that it becomes, practically speaking, incapable of use" — so
   confusing that no ordinary prisoner can discern how to use it; and
3. prison administrators "thwart inmates from taking advantage of [the] grievance process
   through machination, misrepresentation, or intimidation."

Soto's situation — where the case manager appears to have (a) recharacterized a legitimate
factual-correction request as a "refusal" without following PS 7310.04's investigation/
memorandum requirements, and (b) applied an incorrect start date to the 20-day BP-9 clock while
the institution's own informal-resolution response came in three times slower than the Fort Dix
supplement's alleged 3-working-day standard — is the kind of fact pattern *Ross* contemplates
under categories 1 and 2: an inmate diligently trying to use the process while the institution's
own noncompliance and shifting/incorrect timeliness rulings make the process function as a "dead
end" or "opaque" trap. This is a framing argument for use in the BP-9/BP-10 narrative and in any
later § 2241 exhaustion defense, not a guarantee of outcome. **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING: this is
general PLRA/exhaustion doctrine developed mostly in the context of state prisoner § 1983 suits
and BOP habeas exhaustion is judicially, not statutorily, imposed for the most part — confirm
with counsel how *Ross* has actually been applied by courts to BOP-specific administrative-remedy
delay/noncompliance fact patterns, ideally with an on-point Third Circuit or D.N.J. case, before
relying on it. I could not locate a squarely on-point case applying *Ross* to a BOP institution
supplement violation in the time available for this research.]**

### 4.4 Practical, non-constitutional argument: BOP's own policy treats staff delay as an
admission-of-record trigger, not a defense

Independent of *Ross*, PS 1330.18 itself (Section 8.b, quoted at §1.3 above) lists "an unusually
long period taken for informal resolution attempts" as an enumerated valid reason for delay that
*extends the inmate's own filing deadline*. That is the cleanest, most directly citable argument:
Fort Dix's own alleged three-working-day standard, and its actual nine-calendar-day performance,
is BOP's own conduct and can be characterized under the Bureau's own rule (§542.14(b)) as
grounds to excuse any filing delay attributable to that informal-resolution period — this does
not require reaching a constitutional or PLRA-availability theory at all; it is a straightforward
application of the extension clause already built into the national policy.

### 4.5 Bottom line for question 4

- Institution supplements bind BOP staff as internal agency directives issued under express
  authority in PS 1330.18 Section 17 (and similar authorizing language likely exists in other
  program statements' institution-supplement provisions, though I did not independently confirm
  that for every PS series). **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING for any PS series other than 1330.18.]**
- Under Reno v. Koray, neither the Program Statement nor an institution supplement has the
  independent "force of law" of a notice-and-comment regulation, which matters mainly for
  substantive challenges to BOP decisions (e.g., a habeas claim that a supplement itself was
  violated as an independent legal wrong) — it does not immunize staff noncompliance from being
  used as evidence in a fairness/exhaustion argument.
- The most directly usable, low-risk argument is the express "valid reason for delay" — "unusually
  long period ... for informal resolution attempts" — clause of PS 1330.18 §542.14(b)/Section 8.b,
  which is BOP's own regulation-derived text and requires no extension of doctrine.
- The higher-risk, more doctrinally involved argument is a *Ross v. Blake* "unavailability" theory,
  which is sound in principle but needs a circuit-specific case check before filing.
