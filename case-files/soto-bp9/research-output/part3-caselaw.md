## BOTTOM LINE UP FRONT

**No reported or unreported case was found squarely on Soto's facts** — i.e., no case addresses a fact pattern where (1) a case manager's own referral paperwork contained a factual error, (2) the inmate refused only to *sign the erroneous document* while affirmatively requesting correction and continuing to seek RRC placement, and (3) staff later recharacterized that narrow refusal-to-sign as a categorical, permanent refusal of RRC placement altogether, imposed unilaterally by line staff without any of the individualized § 3621(b) analysis or the multi-level sign-off BOP's own policy contemplates for genuine placement refusals.

**This appears to be an issue of first impression on the precise fact pattern.** That said, four bodies of analogous authority are directly useful to the BP-9 and any follow-on litigation:

1. A well-developed 2004–2010 line of circuit authority (including Third Circuit precedent binding in Soto's district) holding that BOP **cannot categorically deny RRC/CCC consideration** without individualized application of the five § 3621(b) factors — directly supports the argument that a case manager's unilateral, non-statutory "permanent refusal" designation is ultra vires.
2. A very close 2025 case, **Misseldine v. Lillard** (S.D. Ill.), in which a case manager threatened an inmate that her "paperwork would be resubmitted for a date next year" unless she withdrew a BP-9 about RRC/home-confinement processing — the court took this seriously enough to excuse exhaustion. This is the closest factual analog located and should be cited.
3. BOP's own (older) Program Statement 7310.04 § 11 ("Refusals") establishes that a genuine CCC/RRC placement refusal requires (a) staff investigation of the inmate's stated reasons, (b) a memorandum **signed by both the Associate Warden (Programs) and the inmate**, and (c) documentation of the inmate's rationale and the unit team's efforts to encourage participation. None of that process is alleged to have occurred here — which is itself strong evidence that the case manager's months-later recharacterization was not a bona fide "refusal" under BOP's own procedural framework. **[VERIFY CURRENT VERSION — the version located is dated 12/16/98 and may have been superseded by a later Program Statement (e.g., a renumbered RRC/Second Chance Act directive); the institution should confirm the current governing Program Statement and section number before citing.]**
4. A live circuit split matters here and cuts in Soto's favor: the **Third Circuit** (which governs FCI Fort Dix / D.N.J.) treats challenges to BOP RRC/place-of-confinement decisions as cognizable "execution of sentence" claims under 28 U.S.C. § 2241 (*Woodall v. Federal Bureau of Prisons*), whereas the **Eighth Circuit** has gone the other way, holding such claims non-reviewable "notwithstanding" mandatory statutory language, including via mandamus (*Osorio-Calderon v. Warden FCI Sandstone*, D. Minn. 2025, applying *Kruger* and *Spencer*). Because Soto is in the Third Circuit, a § 2241 vehicle (or an internal administrative remedy demanding individualized § 3621(b) reconsideration) is more likely to be viable than it would be in the Eighth Circuit.

Each case below is presented with full citation, court, year, procedural posture, verbatim key holding/quote with pinpoint cite where available, and an honest mapping to Soto's facts (including where the mapping is loose).

## 1. The Individualized-Consideration Line: BOP Cannot Categorically Deny RRC/CCC Consideration

This is the most useful body of law for Soto's core theory: that a **line-level, non-statutory, categorical rule** ("this inmate is no longer being considered for any RRC placement, period") **cannot substitute for individualized application of the five § 3621(b) factors** (facility resources; nature/circumstances of the offense; history/characteristics of the prisoner; sentencing court's statement; and pertinent Sentencing Commission policy). None of these cases involve a "refused to sign an inaccurate document" fact pattern, but all stand for the proposition that BOP staff (categorically, in these cases via national rule; here, via one line case manager's unilateral say-so) cannot use a single non-statutory factor to foreclose RRC consideration altogether without individualized analysis.

### Woodall v. Federal Bureau of Prisons, 432 F.3d 235 (3d Cir. 2005)
- **Court:** U.S. Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit — **binding in D.N.J., where FCI Fort Dix sits.**
- **Procedural posture:** Appeal from denial of § 2241 habeas petition challenging BOP's 2005 regulation categorically limiting CCC (halfway house) placement to the last 10% of a sentence (not to exceed six months), without regard to the individualized § 3621(b) factors.
- **Holding:** The Third Circuit held (a) that a challenge to BOP's place-of-confinement/CCC decision is cognizable in habeas under § 2241 as an attack on the "execution" of the sentence, and (b) that the categorical regulation contravened § 3621(b)'s requirement that the five factors be applied on an individualized basis. Per secondary-source summary of the opinion: CCCs have "relatively lenient policies . . . as compared to more traditional correctional facilities," and BOP's blanket time-limit rule improperly substituted a categorical policy for the individualized inquiry Congress required. *(Direct pinpoint quotation of the merits holding could not be independently verified in this research session — the jurisdictional holding is quoted verbatim below from a citing Third Circuit case; the merits holding on categorical denial is well-established in secondary sources but should be pulled from the slip opinion before filing.)* **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — confirm exact page/quote from 432 F.3d 235.]**
- **Confirmed verbatim quote (from a later Third Circuit citation to Woodall):** CCC pre-release programs "often include an employment component under which a prisoner may leave on a daily basis to work in the community. Inmates may be eligible for weekend passes, or furloughs." *Council v. Nash*, No. 10-1099, slip op. at 4 (3d Cir. Nov. 9, 2010) (quoting *Woodall*, 432 F.3d at 243).
- **Mapping to Soto:** **Strong on the legal principle, weak on facts.** Woodall is not about a case manager's unilateral mischaracterization of a document dispute — it is about a *national* categorical regulation. But its core holding (categorical, non-individualized denial of RRC consideration violates § 3621(b)) is the load-bearing legal principle for Soto's BP-9: if BOP *itself* cannot lawfully adopt a blanket rule foreclosing RRC consideration without individualized review, a single case manager certainly cannot do so unilaterally, on a rationale (a contested document-signing dispute) that is not even one of the five statutory factors. **This is the single most important citation for the memo's core legal theory.** Also critical: Woodall confirms § 2241 is an available vehicle in the Third Circuit for this type of claim, which matters for exhaustion/remedy strategy (see Part 4 below).

### Fults v. Sanders, 442 F.3d 1088 (8th Cir. 2006)
- **Court:** U.S. Court of Appeals for the Eighth Circuit.
- **Procedural posture:** § 2241 habeas challenge to the same 2005 BOP categorical CCC-placement regulation.
- **Holding:** The Eighth Circuit held the regulation "contrary to the statute's unambiguous language" because "§ 3621(b) requires that discretion be exercised on an individual basis." 442 F.3d at 1091 (as summarized in secondary sources; exact page pinpoint should be verified against the slip opinion).
- **Mapping to Soto:** Same as Woodall — strong on the legal principle (categorical denial without individualized § 3621(b) analysis is unlawful), not on the facts. Also notable: this case is cited in *Osorio-Calderon v. Warden FCI Sandstone* (discussed below) for the proposition that habeas jurisdiction exists over claims alleging BOP violated federal law — useful backup citation on jurisdiction. **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — pinpoint cite.]**

### Elwood v. Jeter, 386 F.3d 842 (8th Cir. 2004)
- **Court:** U.S. Court of Appeals for the Eighth Circuit.
- **Procedural posture:** Appeal from denial of habeas petition challenging BOP policy limiting CCC placement to the lesser of six months or 10% of the sentence.
- **Holding:** The Eighth Circuit reversed, holding 18 U.S.C. § 3621(b) gives BOP discretion to transfer prisoners to CCCs at any time during incarceration, and BOP is obligated to place prisoners so as to afford a reasonable opportunity to prepare for reentry during a reasonable part of the last 10% of the sentence (not extending beyond the last six months), consistent with individualized consideration.
- **Mapping to Soto:** Same limited but useful mapping as Woodall/Fults — establishes the individualized-consideration baseline against which the case manager's unilateral, permanent exclusion should be measured. **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — pinpoint cite.]**

### Levine v. Apker, 455 F.3d 71 (2d Cir. 2006)
- **Court:** U.S. Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit.
- **Procedural posture:** Consolidated habeas challenges to both BOP's December 2002 CCC policy and the February 2005 Rule (the same categorical rule at issue in Woodall/Fults/Elwood).
- **Holding:** The Second Circuit was "the third court of appeals to decide a habeas petition challenging the February 2005 Rule," joining other circuits in striking it down as inconsistent with § 3621(b)'s individualized-factor requirement.
- **Mapping to Soto:** Same category as above — general individualized-consideration principle, not fact-specific to a case manager's unilateral mischaracterization. **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — pinpoint cite for the merits holding.]**

### Wedelstedt v. Wiley, 477 F.3d 1160 (10th Cir. 2007)
- **Court:** U.S. Court of Appeals for the Tenth Circuit.
- **Procedural posture:** Appeal from grant of habeas relief; BOP had adopted a policy statement defining a "categorical exercise of discretion for designating inmates to community confinement," limiting CCC placement to the last 10% of the sentence (not to exceed six months), and refusing to consider the five § 3621(b) factors for any inmate with more time remaining.
- **Holding (as summarized from the opinion):** "The statutory language in § 3621(b) clearly indicates the five enumerated factors are to be applied to individual inmates. The individualized nature of three of the five factors — the nature of the prisoner's offense, the prisoner's history and characteristics, and the sentencing judge's statement — made such consideration impossible through a categorical policy." The court affirmed the habeas writ and ordered BOP to "immediately consider whether Wedelstedt should be transferred to a CCC without regard to the categorical rule."
- **Mapping to Soto:** This is one of the most useful quotes in this line because it explains *why* categorical/blanket rules are incompatible with § 3621(b) — the individualized factors "cannot" be applied through a blanket rule. That reasoning translates directly: a case manager's categorical "no longer being considered for any RRC placement" pronouncement, based on a mischaracterized document dispute, likewise makes it "impossible" for the individualized factors (offense conduct, history/characteristics, sentencing court statement, Sentencing Commission policy, facility resources) to be applied to Soto at all. **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — pinpoint cite.]**

### Rodriguez v. Smith, 541 F.3d 1180 (9th Cir. 2008)
- **Court:** U.S. Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit.
- **Also reported at:** 2008 WL 4070264; 2008 U.S. App. LEXIS 18918.
- **Procedural posture:** Habeas/class-type challenge to the same categorical CCC-placement regulation.
- **Holding:** Joined the Second, Third, Eighth, and Tenth Circuits in holding that BOP's categorical temporal limitation on CCC eligibility contravenes the plain meaning of § 3621(b); BOP cannot categorically deny or limit RRC/Second Chance Act placements without considering the § 3621(b) factors, and the regulation "unlawfully ignored Congress's intent that placements be made on an individualized basis."
- **Mapping to Soto:** Same category as the above cases. Notably, the First Circuit is reported to be the outlier that upheld the BOP regulation — worth flagging if opposing counsel cites First Circuit authority. **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — pinpoint cite.]**

### Miller v. Whitehead, 527 F.3d 752 (8th Cir. 2008)
- **Court:** U.S. Court of Appeals for the Eighth Circuit.
- **Procedural posture:** Habeas challenge involving BOP Program Statement 7310.04's provision that RRC placement beyond 180 days is "highly unusual, and only possible with extraordinary justification," and related guidance requiring Regional Director approval in writing for placements over six months.
- **Holding:** Discussed the tension between PS 7310.04's "extraordinary justification" standard for longer placements and the individualized-review mandate of § 3621(b) as construed in *Fults*.
- **Mapping to Soto:** Useful primarily as a bridge citation connecting the individualized-review case law to PS 7310.04 itself — the same Program Statement whose "Refusals" section (§ 11) is discussed in Part 3 below. **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING — pinpoint cite.]**

**Honest assessment of this entire line:** These cases are 15–20 years old, arise from a since-rescinded BOP regulation (28 C.F.R. §§ 570.20–.21), and do not involve anything resembling Soto's fact pattern of a documentary error, a narrow refusal-to-sign, and a subsequent unilateral recharacterization by a single case manager. Their value is **doctrinal, not factual**: they establish, with essentially unanimous circuit authority (First Circuit excepted), that categorical/blanket denial of RRC consideration without individualized § 3621(b) analysis is unlawful. That doctrine, applied to Soto's facts, supports the argument that the case manager exceeded the scope of authority BOP itself possesses — if BOP as an agency cannot adopt a blanket no-consideration rule, a fortiori a single line case manager cannot impose one unilaterally, especially where the stated "reason" (a document-accuracy dispute) is not one of the five statutory factors at all.

## 2. Closest Factual Analog Located: Case Manager Threats Tied to BP-9/RRC Processing

### Misseldine v. Lillard, No. 25-CV-01418-SPM, 2025 WL (S.D. Ill. Aug. 7, 2025) (Doc. 6, Memorandum and Order)
- **Court:** U.S. District Court for the Southern District of Illinois (McGlynn, J.).
- **Procedural posture:** Preliminary Rule 4 review of a § 2241 habeas petition by an inmate at FCI Greenville, Illinois. This is **not a merits ruling** — it is an order directing the respondent Warden to answer, after the court found the petition survived screening.
- **Facts:** Petitioner argued she met the criteria for home confinement (bypassing RRC placement), that her RRC/halfway-house referral was submitted less than 60 days before her projected placement date (resulting in denial), and that "her case manager refuses to resubmit her paperwork to request home confinement." She had not exhausted her BP-10/BP-11 appeals but asked the court to excuse exhaustion because "exhaustion would be futile due to the threats [she] received from her Case Manager causing the withdrawal of [her] BP9."
- **Key verbatim quote:** "Misseldine also alleges that her case manager (Ms. Lisa Newsome) threatened her regarding her submission of a BP-9 form, saying that Misseldine's 'paperwork would be resubmitted for a date next year' if she did not withdraw her BP-9 form. (Doc. 1, p. 3). **This Court takes such matters very seriously.**" Slip op. at 5 (emphasis added).
- **Disposition:** The court held that "waiver of the exhaustion requirement is necessary here to ensure Warden Lillard files a response addressing these issues," and ordered the government to answer "without commenting on the merits of Misseldine's claims." Slip op. at 5.
- **Mapping to Soto:** **This is the closest factual analog located in this research.** Both cases involve (a) a case manager, (b) tied to RRC/home-confinement processing, (c) using leverage over an inmate's grievance-adjacent conduct (here, threatening to delay resubmission unless the BP-9 was withdrawn; in Soto's case, recharacterizing a refusal-to-sign-an-inaccurate-document as a categorical placement refusal) to affect the inmate's RRC outcome. The court's explicit statement that it "takes such matters very seriously" — sufficient to excuse full exhaustion — signals that federal courts view case-manager conduct that leverages or punishes an inmate's engagement with the administrative remedy/RRC-referral process as a serious, cognizable problem warranting judicial attention, not something to be waved away as ordinary staff discretion. **Important caveat: this is a screening order, not a merits decision.** It tells us the claim was serious enough to survive Rule 4 review and warrant an answer — it does not tell us whether Misseldine ultimately prevailed. Cite it for what it is: persuasive, not precedential, and interlocutory.

## 4. Jurisdiction/Remedy Circuit Split — Matters for Strategy, Favors Soto's District

### Osorio-Calderon v. Warden FCI Sandstone, No. 25-cv-398 (LMP/DLM), Doc. 27 (D. Minn. Sept. 19, 2025)
- **Court:** U.S. District Court for the District of Minnesota (Provinzino, J.), rejecting a magistrate judge's Report & Recommendation.
- **Procedural posture:** § 2241 habeas petition (and alternative mandamus claim under 28 U.S.C. § 1361) seeking an order compelling BOP to transfer petitioner to prerelease custody/RRC based on earned First Step Act time credits, after BOP's Residential Reentry Manager field office twice rejected an RRC placement.
- **Holding:** Applying binding Eighth Circuit precedent (*Kruger v. Erickson*, 77 F.3d 1071 (8th Cir. 1996); *Spencer v. Haynes*, 774 F.3d 467 (8th Cir. 2014)), the court held that **federal courts lack jurisdiction** to review BOP's RRC/prerelease-custody placement decisions because such claims challenge the inmate's "place of confinement," and 18 U.S.C. § 3621(b) provides that "[n]otwithstanding any other provision of law, a designation of a place of imprisonment under this subsection is not reviewable by any court." The court held this bar applies "regardless of the cause of action asserted," foreclosing not just habeas but also the mandamus claim: "Osorio-Calderon lacks a right to the relief sought because . . . Section 3621(b) and Eighth Circuit precedent provide that BOP's placement decisions — such as the decision to transfer an inmate to prerelease custody — are 'not reviewable by any court.'" Slip op. at 10–11.
- **Mapping to Soto:** **Important as a cautionary/strategic data point, not as controlling law** — Soto is at FCI Fort Dix, in the **District of New Jersey, within the Third Circuit**, which (per *Woodall*, above) treats RRC placement challenges as cognizable "execution of sentence" claims under § 2241. Osorio-Calderon illustrates that this is a live and unsettled circuit split (the R&R in that very case argued the Supreme Court's *Trump v. J.G.G.*, 604 U.S. 670 (2025), may have "upended" the place-of-confinement jurisdictional bar, but the district judge rejected that reading and adhered to Eighth Circuit precedent). **Drafting implication:** because Soto's claim is not really "give me an RRC placement" but rather "give me the individualized § 3621(b) review the statute requires, unpolluted by a false 'refusal' pretext," it is better framed (per *Woodall*/*Wedelstedt*) as a challenge to the *process* BOP used (categorical denial without statutory-factor analysis) rather than a request that a court order a specific placement outcome — the former is more likely to be treated as reviewable even under stricter circuits' reasoning, and squarely fits within Third Circuit precedent.

## 5. BOP's Own Policy Framework for "Refusals" — Internal Inconsistency Argument

### BOP Program Statement 7310.04, Community Corrections Center (CCC) Utilization and Transfer Procedure (Dec. 16, 1998), § 11 ("Refusals")
- **Not a court decision — BOP internal policy, useful as an institutional-consistency argument in the BP-9 itself.**
- **[VERIFY CURRENT VERSION BEFORE FILING — the version located and quoted below is dated 12/16/98. BOP's RRC/Second Chance Act policy has been reissued and renumbered multiple times since (the "CCC" terminology predates the 2008 Second Chance Act's "RRC" terminology). Counsel/inmate should confirm the current governing Program Statement (search BOP's policy index for the current RRC referral / Second Chance Act placement directive) before citing a specific section number, but the underlying procedural principle — that genuine refusals require investigation and multi-party sign-off — is a reasonable baseline to assert BOP has historically required, shifting the burden to the institution to show a current policy that dispenses with it.]**
- **Verbatim text:** "**11. REFUSALS.** When an eligible inmate refuses CCC placement, staff shall investigate the inmate's reasons. Staff may honor an inmate's refusal of CCC placement. Suitable reasons to decline placement might include previous CCC failure, potential conflict with other residents, and location or remoteness from release residence. When the inmate does not present a suitable reason, and the unit team believes that a placement would serve a correctional need, the unit team shall make every effort to encourage participation. **When an inmate refuses placement, a memorandum, signed by the Associate Warden (Programs) and the inmate, shall be placed in the Inmate Central File. The memorandum should document the inmate's rationale for refusal and all unit team effort to encourage participation.**" PS 7310.04 at 12 (emphasis added).
- **Mapping to Soto:** This is not case law, but it is directly useful for the BP-9 itself: it shows that **even BOP's own historical procedural framework requires** (1) staff investigation of the inmate's stated reasons for any placement refusal, (2) a written memorandum documenting that rationale, (3) documentation of unit-team efforts to encourage participation, and (4) **signatures of both the Associate Warden (Programs) and the inmate** — not a unilateral, months-later pronouncement by a single case manager with no inmate signature, no AW sign-off, and no investigation of Soto's actual stated reason (that the "homeless" designation was false and he wanted it corrected, not that he refused placement). If the current version of this policy retains anything resembling this structure, the case manager's unilateral action departed from BOP's own required process — a strong administrative-remedy argument independent of the constitutional/statutory theories above.

## 6. First Amendment Retaliation / Bivens Avenue — Foreclosed for Damages, but Instructive

### Egbert v. Boule, 596 U.S. 482 (2022)
- **Court:** U.S. Supreme Court.
- **Holding:** The Court held **6-3** that no *Bivens* damages remedy exists for a First Amendment retaliation claim against a federal officer, reasoning that Congress — not courts — is better positioned to authorize such a remedy, and that recognizing such claims would let a plaintiff "turn practically any adverse action into grounds for a retaliation claim," with a chilling effect on officials' discharge of duties.
- **Mapping to Soto:** **Forecloses (not supports) a damages-based Bivens theory.** If Soto's BP-9/subsequent litigation strategy contemplated a Bivens suit against the case manager for retaliating against him for disputing the false "homeless" designation, Egbert is a significant, binding obstacle — the Supreme Court has now foreclosed First Amendment Bivens retaliation claims against federal officers generally, including BOP staff. This does **not** foreclose habeas (§ 2241) or administrative-remedy (BP-9/APA) avenues, which do not depend on Bivens. **Strategic implication:** frame Soto's claim as a statutory/administrative individualized-review violation (per Part 1, above) and/or a habeas claim regarding the execution of his sentence (per Part 4), not as a constitutional damages claim, which is very likely foreclosed post-Egbert.

### McGowan v. United States, 825 F.3d 118 (2d Cir. 2016)
- **Court:** U.S. Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit.
- **Procedural posture:** Appeal from dismissal of a suit by a former RRC resident against BOP, his case manager, the RRC facility manager, and U.S. Marshals, alleging false arrest/imprisonment, negligence, and violations of due process, equal protection, and the First Amendment arising from his post-blogging return to secure custody from an RRC.
- **Holding:** The district court (affirmed by the Second Circuit) dismissed the negligence claim for lack of subject-matter jurisdiction and dismissed the constitutional claims on the ground that "there is no private right of action for violation of a federal prisoner's First Amendment rights" — i.e., no Bivens remedy, consistent with (and predating) Egbert.
- **Mapping to Soto:** Confirms, pre-Egbert, that suits naming a BOP case manager individually over RRC-related conduct were already vulnerable to dismissal on Bivens grounds even before Egbert tightened the doctrine further. Reinforces the strategic point above: pursue the administrative/statutory/habeas track, not a Bivens damages track, against the case manager personally.

## 9. Recommended Use in the BP-9 and Beyond

- **For the BP-9 itself** (an internal administrative filing, not a court brief), the strongest and most defensible citations are: (a) the statutory text of 18 U.S.C. § 3621(b) and 18 U.S.C. § 3624(c) (undisputed); (b) the *Woodall/Fults/Wedelstedt/Rodriguez* line for the proposition that RRC/CCC consideration must be individualized and cannot be foreclosed by a non-statutory, categorical rule; and (c) BOP's own PS 7310.04 § 11 "Refusals" framework (subject to confirming the current governing policy) to show the case manager's unilateral, undocumented, unsigned recharacterization did not follow BOP's own required process for treating something as a genuine refusal.
- **For any follow-on § 2241 habeas petition** (if the BP-9/BP-10/BP-11 process does not resolve the issue), *Woodall* is the controlling Third Circuit authority establishing that this type of claim is cognizable, and the claim should be framed as a challenge to BOP's *failure to apply the § 3621(b) factors* (a process challenge) rather than a demand for a specific placement outcome, to avoid the jurisdictional trap illustrated by *Osorio-Calderon*.
- **Avoid** relying on a Bivens damages theory against the case manager individually — *Egbert v. Boule* and *McGowan v. United States* make that avenue very unlikely to survive a motion to dismiss.
- **Cite Misseldine v. Lillard** as persuasive (not precedential, not final) authority that federal courts take seriously allegations of case-manager conduct that punishes or leverages an inmate's engagement with the RRC-referral/BP-9 process — directly supportive of the "this was not a legitimate exercise of discretion" framing.
- **Do independently verify** (PACER/CourtListener, not secondary sources) the FCI Englewood litigation before citing it anywhere formal; it is currently sourced only from an advocacy blog summary.
