## 0. Headline finding that reframes the research request

The background brief for this track assumed that **34 U.S.C. § 60541** contains the "Second Chance Act factors" (facility resources, offense nature, prisoner history, sentencing-court statement, Sentencing Commission policy statement). **That is not correct, and it matters for how the BP-9 is drafted.** Those five factors live in **18 U.S.C. § 3621(b)**, which 18 U.S.C. § 3624(c)(6)(A) expressly cross-references and imports into RRC/prerelease-custody placement decisions. 34 U.S.C. § 60541 ("Federal Prisoner Reentry Initiative," formerly 42 U.S.C. § 17541, enacted as § 231 of the Second Chance Act of 2007) is a broader reentry-programming statute (skills assessment, ID-document assistance, employer outreach, an elderly/terminally-ill home-detention pilot program in subsection (g)) — it does **not** itself supply the individualized-placement-factor test. The controlling three-statute structure is:

- **18 U.S.C. § 3624(c)** — duty to provide prerelease custody (RRC and home confinement), capped durations, and a mandate that implementing regulations track § 3621(b).
- **18 U.S.C. § 3621(b)** — the five factors themselves, framed as constraints on *how* BOP exercises its placement/transfer discretion.
- **18 U.S.C. § 3625** — strips APA judicial-review causes of action (5 U.S.C. §§ 554, 555, 701–706) for determinations "under this subchapter" (which includes §§ 3621–3624), but does **not** bar habeas or constitutional review of *categorical* or *ultra vires* BOP action (see Part 3 below).

Recommend the BP-9 and any follow-on memo cite **18 U.S.C. § 3624(c)(6)(A)** as the textual hook that pulls § 3621(b)'s five factors into RRC placement decisions, rather than citing 34 U.S.C. § 60541 for that proposition. § 60541 can still be cited for the general congressional reentry policy (subsection (a)'s "Federal prisoner reentry initiative" findings) as persuasive context, but not as the source of the factor test.

## 1. 18 U.S.C. § 3624(c) — verbatim text

Verified against Cornell LII's rendering of the current U.S. Code (post–First Step Act of 2018, which amended this subsection via the "Second Chance Act Reauthorization Act of 2018," Title V of the FSA). [VERIFY BEFORE FILING: cross-check final punctuation/paragraph breaks against the official govinfo.gov PDF of the U.S. Code — govinfo's PDF-parsing tool could not render machine-readable text during this research session, so the verbatim capture below comes from Cornell LII, a reliable but non-official secondary compilation; uscode.house.gov attempts also returned paraphrase rather than raw text.]

### § 3624(c)(1) — In general

> "The Director of the Bureau of Prisons shall, to the extent practicable, ensure that a prisoner serving a term of imprisonment spends a portion of the final months of that term (not to exceed 12 months), under conditions that will afford that prisoner a reasonable opportunity to adjust to and prepare for the reentry of that prisoner into the community. Such conditions may include a community correctional facility."

Key features for Soto's argument:
- "**shall**, to the extent practicable, **ensure**" — mandatory verb; the "to the extent practicable" qualifier goes to practicability (bed space, timing), not to an unfettered veto.
- 12-month statutory ceiling on RRC placement.
- "Such conditions **may** include a community correctional facility" — RRC is one mechanism among the prerelease-custody options, not a separate all-or-nothing gate that staff can slam shut for reasons untethered to the statute.

### § 3624(c)(2) — Home confinement authority (partial, duration clause)

> "The authority under this subsection may be used to place a prisoner in home confinement for the shorter of 10 percent of the term of imprisonment of that prisoner or 6 months."

(Full paragraph also directs the Bureau to place lower-risk, lower-need prisoners on home confinement "to the extent practicable" for the maximum time permitted.)

### § 3624(c)(4) — No limitations

> "Nothing in this subsection shall be construed to limit or restrict the authority of the Director of the Bureau of Prisons under section 3621."

This is the textual bridge confirming that (c) operates as an overlay on, not a replacement for, § 3621's general designation authority — and § 3621(b) is where the factor-based discretion (and its limits) is defined.

### § 3624(c)(5) — Reporting (paraphrase; not load-bearing for the merits argument)

Requires an annual report to the House and Senate Judiciary Committees on community-corrections-facility utilization: numbers/percentages of prisoners placed, average placement lengths, and data on **reasons prisoners were not placed** in RRCs (a data point suggesting Congress expected BOP to be able to *articulate* reasons for non-placement — consistent with a reasoned, factor-based process rather than ad hoc staff gatekeeping).

### § 3624(c)(6) — Issuance of regulations (verbatim, confirmed)

> "(6) Issuance of regulations.—The Director of the Bureau of Prisons shall issue regulations pursuant to this subsection not later than 90 days after the date of the enactment of the Second Chance Reauthorization Act of 2018, which shall ensure that placement in a community correctional facility by the Bureau of Prisons is—
> (A) conducted in a manner consistent with section 3621(b) of this title;
> (B) determined on an individual basis; and
> (C) of sufficient duration to provide the greatest likelihood of successful reintegration into the community."

**This is the single most important sentence in the statutory scheme for the BP-9.** It does three things simultaneously:
1. Ties RRC placement decisions to § 3621(b) — i.e., the five enumerated factors, not open-ended staff discretion.
2. Mandates **individualized** ("determined on an individual basis") review — categorical, blanket, or status-based exclusion of an inmate from all further consideration is in tension with this command unless accomplished through the kind of formal rulemaking Lopez v. Davis contemplates (see Part 3).
3. Directs that whatever placement *is* made be "of sufficient duration" to maximize reintegration success — reinforcing that the statutory decision BOP is asked to make is about **duration and conditions**, not a threshold yes/no gate divorced from the factors.

## 2. 18 U.S.C. § 3621(b) — verbatim text (the actual "SCA factors")

Confirmed verbatim via Cornell LII, current U.S. Code. [VERIFY BEFORE FILING against govinfo.gov's official PDF for citation-perfect punctuation.]

> "The Bureau of Prisons shall designate the place of the prisoner's imprisonment, and shall, subject to bed availability, the prisoner's security designation, the prisoner's programmatic needs, the prisoner's mental and medical health needs, any request made by the prisoner related to faith-based needs, recommendations of the sentencing court, and other security concerns of the Bureau of Prisons, place the prisoner in a facility as close as practicable to the prisoner's primary residence, and to the extent practicable, in a facility within 500 driving miles of that residence. The Bureau shall, subject to consideration of the factors described in the preceding sentence and the prisoner's preference for staying at his or her current facility or being transferred, transfer prisoners to facilities that are closer to the prisoner's primary residence even if the prisoner is already in a facility within 500 driving miles of that residence. The Bureau may designate any available penal or correctional facility that meets minimum standards of health and habitability established by the Bureau, whether maintained by the Federal Government or otherwise and whether within or without the judicial district in which the person was convicted, that the Bureau determines to be appropriate and suitable, considering—

> (1) the resources of the facility contemplated;

> (2) the nature and circumstances of the offense;

> (3) the history and characteristics of the prisoner;

> (4) any statement by the court that imposed the sentence—
> (A) concerning the purposes for which the sentence to imprisonment was determined to be warranted; or
> (B) recommending a type of penal or correctional facility as appropriate; and

> (5) any pertinent policy statement issued by the Sentencing Commission pursuant to section 994(a)(2) of title 28.

> In designating the place of imprisonment or making transfers under this subsection, there shall be no favoritism given to prisoners of high social or economic status. The Bureau may at any time, having regard for the same matters, direct the transfer of a prisoner from one penal or correctional facility to another. The Bureau shall make available appropriate substance abuse treatment for each prisoner the Bureau determines has a treatable condition of substance addiction or abuse. Any order, recommendation, or request by a sentencing court that a convicted person serve a term of imprisonment in a community corrections facility shall have no binding effect on the authority of the Bureau under this section to determine or change the place of imprisonment of that person. **Notwithstanding any other provision of law, a designation of a place of imprisonment under this subsection is not reviewable by any court.**"

**Reading § 3624(c)(6)(A) together with § 3621(b):** the statutory list of considerations that may bear on an RRC decision is (i) facility resources/bed availability, (ii) security designation, (iii) programmatic/medical/mental-health needs, (iv) faith-based requests, (v) sentencing-court recommendations, (vi) other BOP security concerns, plus the five enumerated factors at the end. **Nowhere in this list is there a factor for "inmate declined to sign a referral form containing information he disputes as inaccurate."** That omission is the crux of Soto's statutory argument: Congress built a closed, enumerated list of considerations; staff cannot graft an extra-statutory disqualifying condition onto it and use that condition to remove an inmate from the individualized process altogether.

The final sentence — "a designation of a place of imprisonment under this subsection is not reviewable by any court" — is what makes § 3625 doctrinally significant (Part 3): it, together with § 3625, forecloses ordinary APA/merits review of *where* BOP places someone, but courts have distinguished that bar from review of whether BOP followed the statutorily *required process* at all (individualized consideration, non-categorical exclusion, factor-based decisionmaking).

## 3. 18 U.S.C. § 3625 — the judicial-review bar (verbatim, and why it doesn't kill this claim)

> "The provisions of sections 554 and 555 and 701 through 706 of title 5, United States Code, do not apply to the making of any determination, decision, or order under this subchapter."

This eliminates ordinary APA arbitrary-and-capricious review (5 U.S.C. § 706(2)(A)) of individual BOP placement *decisions* under §§ 3621–3624. That is a real limit on Track 4/5's original request to find "APA claims succeeding" — they generally do not, because § 3625 removes the APA cause of action for this subchapter. However:

- Multiple secondary sources (see Moreno v. Ives discussion, Part 5 below) describe an established distinction between (a) challenges to BOP's individualized, discretionary weighing of the factors on the merits — **barred** — and (b) challenges that BOP **categorically** excluded an inmate or class of inmates from the individualized process altogether, or acted **ultra vires**/inconsistently with its own governing statute or regulation — **not barred**, and reviewable via 28 U.S.C. § 2241 habeas or as constitutional claims.
- This is exactly Soto's theory: he is not asking a court (or the Warden, at BP-9 stage) to second-guess a discretionary weighing of the five factors. He is arguing BOP staff never got to that weighing at all — they substituted a non-statutory, unauthorized categorical exclusion (treating a paperwork dispute as a permanent bar) for the individualized process the statute requires.

## 4. 34 U.S.C. § 60541 — Federal Prisoner Reentry Initiative (structure and role)

Confirmed structure (Cornell LII / govinfo secondary rendering) — subsections **(a) through (h)**:

- **(a) In general** — "The Attorney General, in coordination with the Director of the Bureau of Prisons, shall, subject to the availability of appropriations, conduct the following activities to establish a Federal prisoner reentry initiative:" — followed by paragraphs on (1) a reentry strategy (skills assessment, individualized skills-development plans, priority for high-risk populations, interagency coordination, incentives for program participation) and (2) authorized incentives for participants (which may include maximum allowable community-confinement time, but expressly excludes sentence reductions).
- **(b)** Identification-document assistance and release-planning assistance.
- **(c)** Improved DOJ reentry procedures.
- **(d)** Bureau of Prisons duties — coding reentry needs, tracking prisoner progress, annual reporting to Congress, recidivism-reduction measurement.
- **(e)** Employer education/encouragement regarding hiring formerly incarcerated people.
- **(f)** Omitted (amended other Code sections upon enactment).
- **(g)** Pilot program for release of eligible elderly and terminally ill offenders to home detention (the provision the task brief correctly flagged as probably not the right subsection — confirmed correct instinct; this subsection is about a separate elderly/terminally-ill pilot, not the general five-factor test).
- **(h)** Appropriations authorization ($5,000,000/year, FY2019–2023 per some renderings — [VERIFY BEFORE FILING] current appropriations window, as the authorization period may have lapsed or been extended by later legislation not captured in this research pass).

**Usable citation from § 60541 for the BP-9:** subsection (a)'s statement of purpose — Congress explicitly created this initiative to require BOP to assess and act on individual prisoners' reentry needs, evidencing a general congressional design of individualized, needs-based reentry processing rather than administratively convenient categorical gatekeeping. Cite as policy-context corroboration, not as the source of the five-factor test (which is § 3621(b), imported via § 3624(c)(6)(A)).

Provisions most relevant to Soto's BP-9, as captured:

### (a) Placement criteria
Referrals are to be based on "assessments of inmate needs for services, public safety, and the necessity of the Bureau to manage its inmate population responsibly," with consideration of: individual transition needs and community resources; institutional adjustment (with an express caution that CCC placement is "not to be used as a reward"); sentence length; existing court-ordered CCC conditions; medical capability to participate; escape history/prior CCC failures; and proximity to release residence.

### (b) Inmate refusal of CCC/RRC placement — defined procedure
> "When an eligible inmate refuses CCC placement, staff shall investigate the inmate's reasons. Staff may honor an inmate's refusal of CCC placement."

Suitable reasons include prior CCC failure, resident conflict, or location/remoteness concerns.

> "When the inmate does not present a suitable reason, and the unit team believes that a placement would serve a correctional need, the unit team shall make every effort to encourage participation."

**Mandatory documentation requirement (quoted directly):**
> "When an inmate refuses placement, a memorandum, signed by the Associate Warden (Programs) and the inmate, shall be placed in the Inmate Central File. The memorandum should document the inmate's rationale for refusal and all unit team effort to encourage participation."

**Application to Soto:** Under the PS's own terms, a "refusal of CCC placement" triggers a defined, bilateral process — staff investigation, an encouragement effort, and a memorandum that must be **signed by both the Associate Warden (Programs) and the inmate**. Soto's case manager did none of this. There is no indication of (i) any AW-signed memorandum, (ii) any staff "effort to encourage participation," or (iii) Soto's own signature on any refusal memorandum — because Soto never refused placement. He refused to attest, in writing, to a factual assertion (homelessness) he disputes, and offered a correction. That is not the conduct PS 7310.04 defines as "refusal of CCC placement"; it is, at most, a dispute over the accuracy of referral paperwork, which the PS's refusal provision does not address at all.

### (c) Categorical exclusion from CCC programs — separately defined, and narrower than what happened here
The PS separately identifies a list of inmates who "shall not ordinarily participate in CCC programs" (Section 10, per the secondary summary), including those with Sex Offender or Deportable Alien Public Safety Factors, pending detainers, refusal to participate in *required programs* (a different concept from refusing to sign a disputed form), or those posing a significant threat to the community.

**Critically:** even this categorical-exclusion mechanism requires:
> "When an inmate is excluded under this subsection, a memorandum, signed by the Warden, shall be prepared and placed in the Inmate Central File to explain the rationale for exclusion from CC Programs."

**Application to Soto:** Two independent points follow. First, Soto's conduct (declining to sign a referral document he disputed as factually false, while requesting a correction) does not fall within any of the PS's enumerated categorical-exclusion grounds. Second, even if a case manager believed it did, the PS vests the authority to exclude an inmate from CCC/RRC programs **in the Warden**, exercised through a **written, signed memorandum explaining the rationale** — not in a case manager's unilateral, undocumented decision announced for the first time months later at a team meeting. Dennis's action is doubly unauthorized under the agency's own policy: wrong actor (case manager, not Warden) and wrong process (no signed memorandum of record, no stated rationale tied to an enumerated ground).

### (d) No provision addressing referral-packet accuracy disputes
The secondary summary found no explicit PS 7310.04 provision governing what happens when an inmate disputes the accuracy of information (e.g., address/homelessness status) in a referral packet. This is itself useful: it means there is no policy basis, anywhere in the PS, for treating a request to correct inaccurate information as equivalent to a "refusal" of placement — the two are conflated by the case manager's position, not by BOP's own written policy.

## 7. Case law

### 7.1 Woodall v. Federal Bureau of Prisons, 432 F.3d 235 (3d Cir. 2005) — BINDING (Third Circuit)

- **Court/Year:** U.S. Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit, 2005.
- **Posture:** § 2241 habeas challenge to a 2005 BOP regulation (28 C.F.R. §§ 570.20, 570.21) that categorically limited CCC/RRC placement to the last 10% of a sentence (not to exceed 6 months), regardless of the § 3621(b) factors.
- **Holding (as corroborated by multiple secondary sources — casemine.com summary and independent web-search corroboration; [VERIFY BEFORE FILING] exact quoted language against the official F.3d text, which this research session could not directly retrieve due to repeated 403 errors from Justia/Casemine/Leagle and unreadable PDF output from the Third Circuit's own site):** The Third Circuit held that 18 U.S.C. § 3621(b) requires BOP to exercise its placement discretion **using the factors Congress enumerated**, and that the 2005 regulation — which categorically excluded consideration of those factors for a swath of the sentence — created an "unavoidable conflict" with the statute's individualized-consideration mandate. The court invalidated the regulation and required BOP to give each prisoner "an individualized, good faith determination" regarding CCC placement, considering the statutory factors, without regard to the invalidated categorical rule.
- **Relevance to Soto:** This is the closest binding authority to Soto's core legal theory: BOP's placement discretion is **bounded by the enumerated factors**, and a categorical rule that forecloses individualized consideration — whether embodied in a formal regulation (as in Woodall) or, as here, in an ad hoc staff decision to treat a paperwork dispute as a permanent disqualifier — conflicts with §§ 3621(b) and 3624(c)(6). Soto's case is arguably a stronger version of Woodall: at least the 2005 regulation was promulgated through notice-and-comment rulemaking; Dennis's categorical exclusion was not adopted through any process at all.
- **[VERIFY BEFORE FILING]:** Obtain the official F.3d text (via Westlaw/Lexis, a law library, or a successful direct CourtListener/govinfo pull) before quoting Woodall's "unavoidable conflict" or "individualized, good faith determination" language in the filed BP-9 or any follow-on federal pleading, since this session could not confirm the exact wording from the primary opinion itself.

### 7.2 Rodriguez v. Smith, 541 F.3d 1180 (9th Cir. 2008) — PERSUASIVE (Ninth Circuit)

- **Court/Year:** U.S. Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit, 2008. Also reported at 2008 WL 4070264; 2008 U.S. App. LEXIS 18918.
- **Posture:** § 2241 habeas petition seeking an order directing BOP to consider the petitioner for RRC transfer under the § 3621(b) factors, without reference to BOP policies/regulations the petitioner argued contradicted the statute.
- **Holding (corroborated via multiple independent secondary sources; [VERIFY BEFORE FILING] exact page-pincited quotes — primary CourtListener opinion text could not be retrieved in this session):** The court held that BOP's categorical exercise of discretion, as embodied in 28 C.F.R. §§ 570.20–.21, violated Congress's intent regarding the individualized considerations required by § 3621(b), because rules of general applicability that categorically exclude inmates from RRC eligibility without reference to the mandatory factors conflict with the statute. The court affirmed an order directing BOP to promptly and individually consider the petitioner for RRC transfer, "without reference to" the invalid categorical regulations.
- **Relevance to Soto:** Reinforces, from a different circuit, the same core proposition as Woodall — an agency-imposed categorical bar to RRC consideration, untethered to individualized application of the § 3621(b) factors, is unlawful, and the remedy is an order compelling the individualized consideration the statute requires (which is exactly the remedy Soto's BP-9 should request: rescission of the "no longer being considered" determination and a compliant individualized SCA review).

### 7.3 Miller v. Whitehead, 527 F.3d 752 (8th Cir. 2008) — PERSUASIVE, MIXED (Eighth Circuit)

- **Court/Year:** U.S. Court of Appeals for the Eighth Circuit, 2008.
- **Holding (corroborated by FindLaw summary):** "[W]e do not read § 3621(b) to preclude the consideration of factors beyond those set forth in the statute" (quoting/citing Levine v. Apker, 455 F.3d 71 (2d Cir. 2006)). The court also stated § 3621(b) "requires that discretion be exercised on an individual basis," and held that a BOP program statement, as applied, did not automatically preclude consideration of RRC placement earlier than the final 11–13 months of a sentence — but the court also cautioned that an inmate is not entitled to "a full analysis" every time he makes a placement request.
- **Relevance to Soto — cuts both ways, use carefully:** The "does not preclude...factors beyond those set forth in the statute" language is a double-edged sword. It could be read to support BOP's position that staff may weigh things beyond the five factors. **However**, it should be distinguished on two grounds: (1) Miller addresses BOP's ability to consider *additional relevant reentry-related factors* within an individualized weighing process (e.g., program-statement-based durational guidelines) — it does not endorse using an unrelated, extra-statutory event (a paperwork-accuracy dispute) as a **total, permanent bar** to any consideration at all; and (2) Miller simultaneously reaffirms that discretion must be "exercised on an individual basis," which is the half of the holding that helps Soto. Recommend citing Miller narrowly, if at all, and primarily for the individual-basis requirement, while flagging the "factors beyond the statute" line for the government to potentially raise and being ready to distinguish it.

### 7.5 Lopez v. Davis, 531 U.S. 230 (2001) — BINDING (U.S. Supreme Court) — key distinguishing authority

- **Court/Year:** U.S. Supreme Court, 2001.
- **Holding (well-corroborated, widely cited proposition):** BOP has discretion under 18 U.S.C. § 3621 to adopt **categorical exclusions** from a discretionary early-release/placement benefit (there, the RDAP early-release incentive, for prisoners whose offense involved a firearm), **through rulemaking** — i.e., formal, promulgated regulations of general applicability — even where the underlying statutory scheme otherwise contemplates case-by-case, individualized review. The Court reasoned that an agency delegated case-by-case decisional authority may, absent a clear congressional statement to the contrary, use rulemaking to resolve categories of cases in advance.
- **Relevance to Soto — important distinguishing tool:** Lopez v. Davis is the government's most likely counter-authority ("BOP can categorically exclude people"). Soto's BP-9 should **distinguish, not ignore, Lopez**: Lopez authorizes categorical exclusion only when accomplished through **notice-and-comment rulemaking** — a formal, generally-applicable regulation, subject to its own procedural safeguards and public accountability — not through an unpublished, ad hoc, one-inmate decision by a case manager, applied retroactively to an event (declining to sign a disputed form) that BOP has never announced, in any regulation or Program Statement, as a categorical disqualifier. PS 7310.04 itself (Part 5, above) shows BOP knows how to write a categorical-exclusion rule when it wants one (Section 10's enumerated list, requiring a Warden-signed memorandum) — and the conduct here doesn't fit that rule either. Lopez's holding is about the *permissible form* categorical BOP action must take; it does not bless freestanding, individualized, undocumented staff-level gatekeeping of the kind at issue here.

### 7.6 Moreno v. Ives, No. 18-35888 (9th Cir. Dec. 23, 2020) — PERSUASIVE (Ninth Circuit)

- **Holding, as corroborated:** Reaffirms that individualized § 3621-type BOP determinations are generally unreviewable under the APA per § 3625, **but** "categorical challenges to how BOP interprets its own regulations are reviewable" via § 2241 habeas (citing Abbott v. Fed. This framing should be carried through the BP-9's merits section explicitly, so the Warden cannot dismiss the claim as "just a disagreement with a discretionary call."

## 8. Synthesis for the BP-9 merits section

Putting the statutory text, § 3621(b) factors, PS 7310.04, and case law together, the argument structure is:

1. **Congress created a mandatory, individualized process, not an optional one.** § 3624(c)(1) uses "shall... ensure," and § 3624(c)(6) requires regulations guaranteeing that RRC placement decisions are individualized and consistent with § 3621(b). (Woodall; Rodriguez v. Smith.)
2. **The universe of things BOP may weigh in that process is defined by statute** — § 3621(b)'s five factors plus the handful of considerations listed in § 3621(b)'s lead-in (bed availability, security designation, programmatic/medical/mental-health needs, faith-based requests, sentencing-court recommendations, other security concerns). A dispute over the factual accuracy of a referral form, or an inmate's refusal to attest to information he believes is false, appears nowhere on that list.
3. **BOP's own implementing policy, PS 7310.04, confirms the point.** It defines "refusal of CCC placement" as a specific, documented, bilateral process (staff investigation, encouragement effort, AW-and-inmate-signed memorandum) that did not occur here, and separately defines categorical exclusion from CC programs as a Warden-level decision, restricted to enumerated grounds and requiring a signed rationale memorandum — again, not what happened here, and not a ground that covers Soto's conduct in any event.
4. **To the extent BOP claims authority to categorically exclude Soto for reasons outside the statute and outside PS 7310.04's enumerated grounds, Lopez v. Davis does not save that action** — Lopez permits categorical exclusion only via rulemaking, not ad hoc, undocumented, single-inmate staff decisions.
5. **The remedy is not a demand for a particular placement outcome (which would run into § 3621(b)'s "not reviewable by any court" clause and § 3625's APA bar) — it is a demand for the individualized process the statute and BOP's own policy already promise**, mirroring the relief ordered in Woodall and Rodriguez v. Smith: rescind the "no longer being considered" determination and conduct the SCA/RRC review using the § 3621(b) factors.
