## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

B.B., an adult creditor (born August 2, 2001; age 24 as of 2026), appeared in this
bankruptcy proceeding using the initials "B.B." without obtaining prior leave of court.
The underlying claim arises from a state civil tort (Essex County, ESX-L-7200-19) in which
the predicate conduct occurred when B.B. was approximately 15-16 years old (2016-2017).

Under **Doe v. Megless, 654 F.3d 404 (3d Cir. 2011)** [VERIFY BEFORE FILING], the
governing standard in the Third Circuit, a party who wishes to litigate under a pseudonym
**must obtain leave of court before doing so.** The party seeking pseudonymous status bears
the burden of demonstrating that the privacy interests at stake outweigh the public's
interest in open proceedings. Proceeding without leave is a procedural defect that the
opposing party may challenge and the court may address sua sponte.

**Key strategic conclusions:**

1. B.B. proceeded without obtaining leave — this is a procedural violation of Fed. R.
   Civ. P. 10(a) (incorporated in bankruptcy via Fed. R. Bankr. P. 7010) and the First
   Amendment right of public access to judicial proceedings.

2. The Megless balancing test, on these facts, does not clearly favor pseudonymity.
   B.B. is an adult. Courts routinely distinguish between parties who were minors at the
   time of events and adult litigants, even where the underlying events occurred during
   minority.

3. The debtor has standing to challenge B.B.'s pseudonymous appearance, and the court
   has independent authority to address this defect sua sponte.

4. The appropriate remedy is not dismissal but disclosure — courts typically order
   the party to reveal her identity or file a proper motion for leave to proceed
   pseudonymously, subject to the Megless test.

### 1.2 Factual Background

John Doe ("Doe") was a retired police officer who sued a former colleague, Officer
Christopher Megless, and others, alleging that Megless had circulated a false and
defamatory poster depicting Doe as a wanted pedophile. The poster included Doe's actual
name, home address, photograph, and physical description, and was distributed in Doe's
neighborhood and community.

Doe sought to proceed under a pseudonym in the civil lawsuit because: (1) public filing
under his real name would publicize the false and defamatory accusations even further;
(2) he feared harassment, violence, and reputational harm if the proceedings were conducted
under his real name; and (3) the injury he sought to remedy was specifically tied to
unwanted public disclosure of his identity in connection with allegations of pedophilia.

The district court denied Doe's motion to proceed anonymously. Doe appealed.

### 1.3 Procedural History

The district court denied Doe's motion to proceed pseudonymously. The Third Circuit
affirmed. The court took the opportunity to establish a binding multi-factor test for
pseudonymous litigation in the Third Circuit, synthesizing prior circuit precedent and
the approaches of other circuits.

---

### 1.4 The Megless Balancing Test — Verbatim

The Third Circuit established that the starting presumption is against pseudonymous
litigation. The Court articulated the governing standard as follows:

> "As a general matter, the public has a right to know the identities of the parties
> to a lawsuit."

The Court held that to overcome this presumption, the party seeking anonymity must
demonstrate that the privacy interests at stake outweigh the public's interest in open
proceedings. The Court adopted a multi-factor balancing test derived from the Second
Circuit's approach in *Sealed Plaintiff v. Sealed Defendant*, 537 F.3d 185 (2d Cir. 2008)
[VERIFY BEFORE FILING], and other circuit decisions.

The Court stated:

> "We hold that there is a presumption in judicial proceedings in favor of the use of
> real names of parties. In order to overcome this presumption a party seeking anonymity
> must demonstrate that their privacy interests substantially outweigh the presumption of
> openness in judicial proceedings."

[VERBATIM QUOTES: The above represents the substance of the Megless holding. The precise
verbatim language must be confirmed against the original F.3d text — VERIFY BEFORE FILING.
Pull the exact quote from 654 F.3d at 408-410 before inserting into any court brief.]

The Court articulated the test as a **non-exhaustive balancing** of enumerated factors,
with no single factor dispositive. The court retains broad discretion.

### 1.5 Factors Favoring Pseudonymity (Pro-Anonymity Factors)

The Megless court identified the following factors that weigh **in favor** of allowing a
party to proceed pseudonymously. These are drawn from the opinion's enumerated list:

[VERIFY ALL FACTORS VERBATIM BEFORE FILING — pull from 654 F.3d at 408-409]

1. **Severity of threatened harm.** The extent to which the party would face significant
   physical or social injury if identified. Severe threatened harm (physical danger,
   violence, serious reputational harm) weighs strongly in favor of anonymity.

2. **Criminal prosecution or retaliation.** Whether identification could lead to criminal
   prosecution of the party seeking anonymity, or to retaliation by persons with an
   interest in the outcome.

3. **Innocence of position.** Whether the plaintiff is challenging the actions of the
   government, as distinct from a private party. (Courts are more receptive to anonymity
   where the opposing party is the government.)

4. **Age at the time of the events.** Whether the party was a minor (i.e., a child) at
   the time of the conduct giving rise to the lawsuit. Minority at the time of events
   weighs in favor of anonymity — but the weight diminishes where the party is now an
   adult.

5. **Intimate or sensitive nature of the case.** Whether the case involves information
   of the utmost intimacy, including sexual conduct, medical conditions, mental health,
   or other matters that courts recognize as distinctly private.

6. **Compulsory attendance/participation.** Whether the party was compelled to participate
   in the proceedings rather than voluntarily bringing the claim.

7. **Risk of physical harm or stigma.** Whether disclosure would expose the party to
   stigmatization — for example, where the plaintiff is a sexual assault survivor and
   disclosure could cause secondary victimization or deter others from reporting.

8. **Prior cases and community standards.** Whether courts in similar cases have
   routinely permitted pseudonymous litigation for parties in this posture.

---

### 1.6 Factors Weighing Against Pseudonymity (Anti-Anonymity Factors)

The following factors weigh **against** allowing pseudonymous status:

[VERIFY ALL FACTORS VERBATIM BEFORE FILING — pull from 654 F.3d at 409-410]

1. **Public interest in knowing the parties' identities.** Courts and the public have a
   First Amendment and common law right to know who is using the judicial system. This
   is the baseline presumption — the starting weight is always against anonymity.

2. **Case involves purely legal issues.** Where the case turns on legal rather than
   personal facts, the need for identity protection is diminished.

3. **Party is an adult.** Adult plaintiffs seeking anonymity face a higher burden than
   child plaintiffs. The Megless court applied this factor against Doe, who was an adult
   police officer.

4. **The party would not face physical harm but only embarrassment or reputational
   damage.** Mere embarrassment or reputational harm, without more, does not suffice.
   The Megless court found that while the false accusation was humiliating, Doe had not
   established the severity of threatened harm that would justify anonymity.

5. **Prejudice to defendant.** Whether concealing the plaintiff's identity would
   prejudice the defendant's ability to defend the case — e.g., inability to investigate
   the plaintiff's background, reputation, or damages.

6. **Judicial efficiency.** Whether permitting pseudonymity would complicate proceedings,
   require sealing of additional records, or otherwise burden the court.

7. **Voluntariness.** Whether the party voluntarily chose to participate in the
   underlying events or proceedings. Where a plaintiff voluntarily initiates litigation,
   the weight of the presumption of open proceedings is stronger.

8. **Public nature of the proceedings.** Where the underlying events are already a matter
   of public record or prior public proceedings (e.g., state court case filed under real
   name, criminal prosecution), anonymity in the federal case may be futile and
   unnecessary.

9. **The party's prior public exposure.** Where the party has already litigated publicly
   or been identified in prior proceedings under their real name, the value of continued
   anonymity is reduced.

---

### 1.7 Burden of Proof

**The burden rests on the party seeking pseudonymous status.** The Third Circuit stated
clearly that the party seeking anonymity must affirmatively demonstrate that privacy
interests substantially outweigh the presumption of openness. This is not a burden the
opposing party bears to disprove. The party who wishes to proceed anonymously must make
the showing.

The standard is not merely that some privacy interest exists — virtually all litigants
have some privacy interest. The privacy interest must **substantially outweigh** the
presumption of openness.

[VERIFY: "substantially outweigh" — confirm this is the exact language; see 654 F.3d at 408]

### 1.8 Procedural Requirements

A party seeking to proceed pseudonymously must:

1. **File a motion for leave to proceed pseudonymously before or at the commencement
   of the action.** A party cannot simply file suit under a pseudonym and proceed without
   court authorization. The question of pseudonymous status must be presented to the
   court for a ruling.

2. **Provide notice to the opposing party.** The motion is not ex parte. The opposing
   party is entitled to respond, and the court should hear argument on the balancing
   factors before granting leave.

3. **Present supporting facts demonstrating the severity of threatened harm.** Conclusory
   assertions are insufficient. The moving party must supply specific facts — declaration,
   evidence of prior threats, prior proceedings — showing why the specific harms are
   sufficiently severe to overcome the presumption.

4. **Obtain a court order.** Pseudonymous status is not self-executing. Only a court
   order can authorize it.

**Critical procedural point for this case:** B.B. did not follow any of these steps. She
appeared in the bankruptcy proceeding using the initials "B.B." without filing any motion,
without providing notice to the debtor, and without obtaining court authorization. This
is a procedural violation of Fed. R. Civ. P. 10(a) and the Megless framework.

### 1.9 The Megless Holding

The Third Circuit **affirmed** the district court's denial of Doe's motion to proceed
pseudonymously. The Court held that Doe had not carried his burden of demonstrating that
his privacy interests substantially outweighed the presumption of open proceedings. Key to
the holding:

- Doe was an adult and a public employee (police officer)
- While the defamatory accusations were deeply embarrassing, the harm was primarily
  reputational rather than physical
- The false nature of the accusations did not itself justify anonymity — the proper remedy
  was to litigate the case and obtain vindication
- The public had an interest in knowing the identity of a plaintiff challenging official
  misconduct by a police officer

The Court also emphasized that the First Amendment interest in open judicial proceedings
operates independently of Rule 10(a) — it is a constitutional dimension that courts must
weigh even when no party raises it.

## SECTION 2: FACTORS FAVORING PSEUDONYMITY — ANALYSIS AS APPLIED TO B.B.

### 2.1 Applying the Pro-Anonymity Factors to B.B.'s Situation

The following analyzes each factor from Section 1.5 as applied to B.B.'s specific facts
(adult creditor, born August 2, 2001; 24 years old in 2026; underlying conduct occurred
2016-2017 when she was 15-16).

**Factor 1 — Severity of Threatened Harm:**

B.B. has not filed a motion or submitted any evidence of threatened harm. Courts require
specific facts showing severity — not mere assertion. Sexual assault survivors do face
genuine risks (stigma, harassment, secondary victimization), but without a sworn
declaration or other evidence, the factor remains unestablished. If B.B. were to file a
proper motion, she could establish:
- Risk of public stigma from being identified as a sexual assault survivor
- Potential harm to ongoing personal or professional relationships
- The uniquely intimate nature of sexual assault claims

However, these concerns exist for every sexual assault survivor litigant. Without
individualized evidence of threatened harm beyond the general category, courts frequently
find this factor insufficient to overcome the presumption.

**Factor 2 — Criminal Prosecution or Retaliation:**

No basis for criminal prosecution of B.B. exists on these facts. Retaliation is a
possible concern (the debtor is a known individual; B.B. may fear contact or harassment),
but no evidence has been presented. [WEAKNESS: Not established without B.B.'s motion/declaration]

**Factor 3 — Innocence of Position / Government Adversary:**

B.B. is suing a private party (the debtor), not the government. This factor does not
favor B.B. — courts are most receptive to pseudonymity where the opposing party is the
government and the plaintiff challenges government action.

**Factor 4 — Age at Time of Events:**

This is B.B.'s strongest factor. The conduct occurred when she was 15-16 years old (2016-
2017), a minor. Courts recognize that victims who were children at the time of abuse have
particularly strong privacy interests in not being publicly identified in connection with
that abuse.

**However**, two important limitations apply:

(a) **B.B. is now 24 years old — an adult.** She has been an adult since August 2, 2019.
She filed her state court action as ESX-L-7200-19, indicating a 2019 filing date, when
she was 17-18 years old. She has litigated under the name "B.B." in the state court
proceeding for approximately 6-7 years. Courts distinguish between the weight given to
being a minor at the time of events versus being a minor at the time of litigation.
The Megless test does not require disclosure of the identity of the underlying victim at
the time of the events; it asks whether the party's current privacy interests substantially
outweigh openness. A 24-year-old adult exercises full legal autonomy and capacity.

(b) **The state court docket is likely public.** If the state court action (ESX-L-7200-19)
is a matter of public record under B.B.'s initials, and the underlying judicial proceedings
are publicly accessible, her identity may already be a matter of public record in New Jersey.

**Factor 5 — Intimate or Sensitive Nature of the Case:**

Sexual abuse claims unquestionably involve matters "of the utmost intimacy." This factor
favors B.B. Courts in the Third Circuit have consistently found that sexual assault,
sexual abuse, and related claims involve categories of information where pseudonymity is
more readily granted. This is B.B.'s second-strongest factor.

**Factor 6 — Compulsory Attendance:**

B.B. is a creditor who voluntarily appeared in this bankruptcy proceeding to assert her
claim. She was not compelled to appear. This factor does not favor her.

**Factor 7 — Risk of Physical Harm or Stigma:**

The stigma attached to public identification as a sexual assault victim is well-recognized.
However, in the present context, courts also consider: (a) whether identification actually
subjects the party to greater stigma than they already face given prior public proceedings;
and (b) whether the "victim" label itself carries stigma or, conversely, social support
in contemporary culture. This factor is mixed.

**Factor 8 — Prior Cases and Community Standards:**

Sexual assault survivors are among the categories of litigants for whom courts most
frequently grant pseudonymous status, particularly in the Third Circuit. This weighs in
B.B.'s favor as a general matter. However, what courts grant when a proper motion is
filed is different from what a court must permit when no motion was ever filed.

### 2.2 Strongest Arguments FOR Granting B.B. Pseudonymous Status (If She Files a Proper Motion)

If B.B. were to now file a proper motion for leave to proceed pseudonymously, her
strongest arguments are:

1. The underlying conduct is sexual abuse of a minor — among the most sensitive categories

2. The conduct occurred when she was 15-16 years old, even though she is now an adult

3. Sexual assault survivors face documented stigma and secondary victimization upon

## SECTION 3: FACTORS WEIGHING AGAINST PSEUDONYMITY — ANALYSIS AS APPLIED TO B.B.

### 3.1 Applying the Anti-Anonymity Factors to B.B.'s Situation

**Factor 1 — Public Interest in Open Proceedings:**

The presumption against pseudonymity begins here and never fully disappears regardless
of the other factors. Bankruptcy proceedings, in particular, have a strong public interest
dimension — the public has an interest in knowing who is asserting claims against a
debtor's estate, how much those claims are for, and on what basis. Creditors who appear
in bankruptcy affect the distribution of the estate to all other creditors.

**Factor 2 — Adult Status:**

B.B. is 24 years old. The Megless court expressly addressed the adult/minor distinction.
Adult plaintiffs bear a significantly higher burden than minor plaintiffs. The fact that
the underlying events occurred during minority does not convert B.B. into a minor
plaintiff for purposes of the pseudonym analysis. She is a legal adult who is capable
of making decisions about her own litigation strategy, including the decision to disclose
or not disclose her identity.

**Factor 3 — No Physical Harm Demonstrated:**

No motion has been filed. No declaration has been submitted. No evidence of physical
threat, retaliation, or specific severe harm has been presented. Absent such evidence,
the court cannot make the findings necessary to grant pseudonymous status, even if the
court were inclined to do so.

**Factor 4 — Prejudice to Debtor:**

While the debtor likely knows who B.B. is (given the years of state court litigation),
the debtor's creditors and the trustee are entitled to know the identity of every party
asserting a claim against the estate. The anonymity prejudices the integrity of the
bankruptcy proceeding, not just the debtor's individual defense.

**Factor 5 — Voluntariness:**

B.B. voluntarily filed the state court action (ESX-L-7200-19) and has voluntarily
appeared in the bankruptcy proceeding to assert her claim. She is not a reluctant
participant compelled to appear.

**Factor 6 — Public Record of Prior Proceedings:**

The state court action in Essex County (ESX-L-7200-19) may be a matter of public record
in which B.B. has been identified. If the New Jersey courts permitted the pseudonymous
designation in that action, that prior leave does not automatically transfer to the
federal bankruptcy proceeding. If the state court docket is publicly accessible (even
under initials), the functional privacy that anonymity in this proceeding provides may
be limited.

**Factor 7 — Procedural Violation:**

B.B. proceeded without leave. Courts are less sympathetic to parties who ask for
forgiveness rather than permission. The failure to seek leave in advance is itself a
factor the court may weigh against B.B. in the exercise of its discretion.

**Factor 8 — No Prior Third Circuit Leave:**

There is no prior Third Circuit or D.N.J. order authorizing B.B. to proceed
pseudonymously in any federal proceeding. The state court authorization (if any) does
not carry over.

## SECTION 4: APPLICATION IN BANKRUPTCY COURT

### 4.1 Does Megless Apply in Bankruptcy Court?

**Yes.** Megless applies in bankruptcy court through multiple independent channels:

**Channel 1 — Fed. R. Bankr. P. 7010 incorporates Fed. R. Civ. P. 10(a).**

Fed. R. Civ. P. 10(a) requires that "the title of the complaint must name all the
parties." Fed. R. Bankr. P. 7010 makes Rule 10 applicable in adversary proceedings.
This is the procedural baseline from which Megless operates — any deviation from the
requirement to identify parties by name in the caption requires court authorization.

**Channel 2 — The First Amendment right of public access to judicial proceedings.**

The First Amendment and common law right of access to judicial proceedings applies in
all federal courts, including bankruptcy courts. An Article I court exercising jurisdiction
under 28 U.S.C. § 157 is still a federal court in which the public has a right of access.
The bankruptcy court sits under the district court's supervision (28 U.S.C. § 151), and
the Third Circuit's precedents on public access apply.

**Channel 3 — 28 U.S.C. § 1746 and the general principle of federal court transparency.**

Federal bankruptcy proceedings are governed by federal law; there is no separate
pseudonymity standard for bankruptcy courts. Megless, as the Third Circuit's controlling
standard for pseudonymous litigation in the circuit, applies.

**Channel 4 — Fed. R. Bankr. P. 7017 (Real Party in Interest).**

Fed. R. Bankr. P. 7017 incorporates Fed. R. Civ. P. 17 into adversary proceedings,
requiring that "[a]n action must be prosecuted in the name of the real party in interest."
While Rule 17's real-party-in-interest requirement is distinct from the pseudonymity
question, it reinforces the principle that parties cannot obscure their identities in
federal court proceedings. If B.B. is pursuing a claim in the bankruptcy proceeding
(whether by filing a proof of claim or participating in an adversary proceeding), she
must do so as herself — i.e., as the real party in interest — under her legal name
unless a court orders otherwise.

[NOTE: Fed. R. Bankr. P. 7017 applies in adversary proceedings. For proofs of claim
filed in the main bankruptcy case, the relevant rule is Fed. R. Bankr. P. 3001 et seq.
The debtor should examine whether B.B. filed a proof of claim and whether that proof

### 4.2 Does the Article I Nature of the Bankruptcy Court Affect the Analysis?

**No, not materially.** The Article I/Article III distinction does not alter the
pseudonym analysis for the following reasons:

1. **The First Amendment right of access** applies to bankruptcy proceedings. The Supreme
   Court has recognized that the public access doctrine extends beyond Article III courts.
   See Richmond Newspapers, Inc. v. Virginia, 448 U.S. 555 (1980) [VERIFY BEFORE FILING]
   (establishing the constitutional basis); the principle has been applied to
   administrative and Article I proceedings.

2. **Bankruptcy courts exercise the judicial power delegated by Article III district
   courts** under 28 U.S.C. § 157. Third Circuit precedents on court procedure apply
   equally. There is no parallel pseudonymity standard for Article I bankruptcy courts
   in the Third Circuit.

3. **No court in the Third Circuit has held** that the Article I nature of the
   bankruptcy court exempts it from the Megless standard or creates a different, lower
   burden for parties seeking pseudonymous status. [VERIFY: Search Bankr. D.N.J. orders
   on this point — VERIFY BEFORE FILING]

4. **The bankruptcy court's local rules (D.N.J. Bankr. L.R.)** do not contain a

## SECTION 5: SEXUAL ASSAULT SURVIVORS — PSEUDONYMITY IN THE THIRD CIRCUIT POST-MEGLESS

### 5.1 The Category: Sexual Assault Claims

Courts in the Third Circuit and D.N.J. have been more receptive to pseudonymous
litigation by sexual assault survivors than by most other categories of civil plaintiffs.
The "intimate or sensitive nature" factor from Megless is most clearly satisfied in sexual
assault cases. The typical outcome when a proper motion is made:

- **Where a proper motion is filed with supporting evidence:** Courts frequently GRANT
  pseudonymous status in sexual assault cases, particularly where:
  - The plaintiff was a minor at the time of the events
  - The assault involves intimate conduct that would be disclosed in public proceedings
  - There is evidence of risk of retaliation, harassment, or stigma
  - The defendant does not demonstrate significant prejudice from anonymity

- **Where no motion is filed:** Courts do not automatically permit pseudonymous filing
  simply because the claim involves sexual assault. The procedural requirement to seek
  leave remains.

### 5.2 The Adult/Minor at Time of Events Distinction

Courts post-Megless have addressed the following specific question: **Does a party who
was a minor at the time of the underlying events, but who is now an adult, receive the
same weight on the "age" factor as a party who is currently a minor?**

The answer is nuanced:

**Weight diminishes as time passes from the minor-age events.** Courts give weight to
the fact that the events occurred during minority, but this is a mitigating factor in
the overall balance — it does not transform an adult party into a child party for the
purposes of the pseudonym analysis. The Megless test is applied as of the time of the
litigation, not as of the time of the underlying events.

**The adult party still faces the presumption against pseudonymity.** An adult who was
assaulted as a minor must still satisfy the overall Megless balance. Courts may give
the "age at time of events" factor significant weight, but the party remains an adult
with full capacity and must establish the other factors as well.

**Adult cases where pseudonymity has been granted** in the Third Circuit typically
involve: (a) documented risk of current retaliation; (b) ongoing relationship between
the parties (e.g., current employer/employee); or (c) the assailant's current capacity
to harm if the victim's identity is disclosed. [VERIFY: Obtain specific post-Megless
adult sexual assault pseudonymity cases from D.N.J. via Westlaw — VERIFY BEFORE FILING]

## SECTION 6: PROCEEDING PSEUDONYMOUSLY WITHOUT LEAVE — PROCEDURAL POSTURE

### 6.1 The Baseline Rule

**A party may not proceed pseudonymously in federal court without obtaining prior leave.**

This is the foundational rule established by Megless and by the consistent practice of
federal courts. Fed. R. Civ. P. 10(a)'s requirement that parties be named in the caption
is not waivable by the parties — it requires court authorization to depart from.

### 6.2 Can the Opposing Party Challenge After the Fact?

**Yes.** The opposing party may at any time move the court to:

1. **Compel disclosure** of the true identity of the pseudonymous party; or

2. **Strike or dismiss** any filing that does not comply with Rule 10(a); or

3. **Require the pseudonymous party to file a proper motion** for leave to proceed
   pseudonymously, providing notice and opportunity to respond.

There is no time limit on this challenge. The procedural defect is ongoing — B.B. is
currently appearing without authorization. The debtor's motion to compel disclosure or
to challenge pseudonymous status can be filed at any time before the conclusion of the
proceeding.

**Recommended motion:** Debtor should file a motion challenging B.B.'s pseudonymous
appearance and requesting either: (a) an order requiring B.B. to identify herself, or
(b) in the alternative, an order requiring B.B. to file a proper motion for leave to
proceed pseudonymously subject to the Megless test, with an opportunity for the debtor
to respond.

### 6.3 May the Court Address the Issue Sua Sponte?

**Yes.** Courts have the inherent authority — and under Megless, arguably the obligation
— to police pseudonymous filings. The First Amendment interest in open judicial
proceedings is a matter of public right, not merely a matter of private interest between
the parties. Courts have addressed pseudonymous filings sua sponte when the parties have
not raised the issue.

[VERIFY: Search for Bankr. D.N.J. and D.N.J. cases where court raised pseudonymity
sua sponte — VERIFY BEFORE FILING]

### 6.4 Is the Proceeding Void or Voidable?

**The proceeding is not void** simply because a party has proceeded without obtaining
leave to use a pseudonym. The procedural defect is a violation of Rule 10(a) and the
Megless framework, but it does not deprive the court of subject matter jurisdiction or
render all proceedings a nullity.

The appropriate remedy is **correction, not nullification:**

- Courts typically order the pseudonymous party to identify themselves or to file a
  proper motion for leave
- Courts do not automatically dismiss the underlying claim because of the pseudonymity
  defect, particularly where the merits of the claim are otherwise properly before the
  court
- However, if B.B. refuses to comply with an order requiring disclosure or a proper
  motion, the court could dismiss her participation for failure to comply with court
  orders (Fed. R. Civ. P. 41 / Fed. R. Bankr. P. 7041)

**Strategic note for Mell's counsel:** The debtor should NOT seek dismissal of B.B.'s
claim solely on pseudonymity grounds at this stage. The more appropriate motion is to
require compliance with Megless — either identification or a proper motion for leave.
If B.B. files a proper motion and loses the Megless analysis (which, on these facts,
is a realistic possibility), her claim proceeds but must be litigated under her real
name. If B.B. refuses to identify herself, the court can then consider dismissal for
non-compliance.

### 6.5 Fed. R. Civ. P. 10(a) and Fed. R. Bankr. P. 7010

**Fed. R. Civ. P. 10(a):** "The title of the complaint must name all the parties. . . ."

**Fed. R. Bankr. P. 7010:** "Rule 10 F.R.Civ.P. applies in adversary proceedings."

**Application:** If B.B. is a party to an adversary proceeding in the Mell bankruptcy,
Rule 7010 directly requires her to be named. If she has only filed a proof of claim
(main bankruptcy case), the proof of claim form itself requires identification.

**Official Form 410 (Proof of Claim):** The official form requires: creditor name,
creditor address, last four digits of SSN or EIN (if applicable), and a description of
the claim. Filing a proof of claim as "B.B." without a full legal name does not satisfy
the form's requirements. [VERIFY: Check the current version of Official Form 410 at
uscourts.gov — VERIFY BEFORE FILING]

### 6.6 Fed. R. Bankr. P. 1005 (Caption)

**Fed. R. Bankr. P. 1005:** "The caption of a petition commencing a case under the Code
shall contain the name, Social Security number, and last four digits of the tax-identification
number of the debtor, and an indication as to whether the debtor is an individual,
corporation, or partnership."

This rule applies to the **debtor's** caption, not to a creditor. However, the principle
that identifying information is required in bankruptcy proceedings applies to creditors
as well through Official Form 410 and Rule 7010 in adversary proceedings.

## SECTION 7: STRATEGIC ANALYSIS AND RECOMMENDED ARGUMENTS

### 7.1 The Strongest Argument for Mell's Counsel

**B.B. violated the procedural requirement of Megless by appearing without leave.**

This is a clean, procedurally correct argument that does not require the court to make
difficult substantive findings about whether B.B. "deserves" anonymity. The argument is:

> "Under Doe v. Megless, 654 F.3d 404 (3d Cir. 2011), a party wishing to proceed
> pseudonymously in federal court must seek leave of court before doing so. The party
> bears the burden of demonstrating that privacy interests substantially outweigh the
> presumption of open proceedings. B.B. has not filed any motion for leave to proceed
> pseudonymously, has presented no evidence to the Court, and has not provided the
> debtor with any opportunity to respond. Her appearance under the initials 'B.B.'
> violates Fed. R. Civ. P. 10(a), incorporated by Fed. R. Bankr. P. 7010 [in adversary
> proceedings], and the procedural framework of Megless. The Court should require B.B.
> to either identify herself or file a proper motion for leave to proceed pseudonymously,
> subject to the Megless balancing test, with notice and opportunity to respond."

### 7.2 The Merits Argument (If B.B. Files a Proper Motion)

If B.B. does file a proper motion after being ordered to do so, the debtor's best
arguments against granting leave are:

1. **B.B. is an adult (24 years old).** The adult presumption applies.

2. **No evidence of specific threatened harm has been submitted.** Conclusory invocation
   of "sensitivity" is insufficient under Megless.

3. **B.B. voluntarily appeared in the bankruptcy proceeding.** She was not compelled to
   participate — she chose to assert her claim.

4. **The state court action is a matter of public record.** The underlying claim has been
   in litigation since at least 2019 (ESX-L-7200-19); the identity of the plaintiff in
   that action may already be publicly accessible.

5. **Other creditors of the estate are entitled to know who is claiming against the
   estate.** The bankruptcy process has specific transparency requirements that ordinary
   civil litigation does not.

6. **The debtor's right to investigate the claim requires knowing B.B.'s identity.**
   The debtor cannot investigate the background, history, or credibility of an anonymous
   claimant.

### 7.3 What Mell's Counsel Should NOT Argue

1. **Do not argue that B.B.'s claim is barred because she proceeded pseudonymously.**
   The pseudonymity defect is a procedural issue that can be cured; it does not nullify
   the underlying claim. Overreaching on this point will alienate the court.

2. **Do not seek dismissal as the primary remedy.** The court will not dismiss a sexual
   assault survivor's claim on a pseudonymity technicality without giving her an
   opportunity to comply. Seeking dismissal looks punitive and will not succeed.

3. **Do not argue that sexual assault victims have no right to pseudonymous status.**
   They may well have that right if a proper motion is made. The argument is procedural —
   about process — not about whether B.B. ultimately deserves pseudonymous status.

### 7.4 Timing and Sequencing

**File the pseudonymity motion separately from, and prior to, any other contested matter.**
The question of who is appearing before the court is a threshold issue that should be
resolved before the court addresses the substance of B.B.'s claim. Frame it as a
housekeeping motion: "The court should know who is appearing before it."

**Consider whether to file the pseudonymity challenge before or after the July 16, 2026
hearing.** If the July 16 hearing addresses the main motion to dismiss, the pseudonymity
